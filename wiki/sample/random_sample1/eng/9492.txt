<p>An <b>extinct in the wild</b> (<b>EW</b>) <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> is one which has been <a href="/wiki/Conservation_status" title="Conservation status">categorized</a> by the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> as only known by living members kept in captivity or as a naturalized population outside its historic range due to massive habitat loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extinct_in_the_wild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Examples of species and subspecies that are extinct in the wild include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alagoas_curassow" title="Alagoas curassow">Alagoas curassow</a> (extinct in the wild since 1988)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Black_softshell_turtle" title="Black softshell turtle">Black soft-shell turtle</a> (extinct in the wild since 2002)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Escarpment_cycad" title="Escarpment cycad">Escarpment cycad</a> (extinct in the wild since 2006)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Guam_rail" title="Guam rail">Guam rail</a> (extinct in the wild since the 1980s)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_crow" title="Hawaiian crow">Hawaiian crow</a> or ʻalalā (extinct in the wild since 2002)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Scimitar_oryx" title="Scimitar oryx">Scimitar oryx</a> (extinct in the wild since 1998)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Socorro_dove" title="Socorro dove">Socorro dove</a> (extinct in the wild since 1972)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wyoming_toad" title="Wyoming toad">Wyoming toad</a> (extinct in the wild since 1991)</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pinta_Island_tortoise" title="Pinta Island tortoise">Pinta Island tortoise</a> (<i>Geochelone nigra abingdoni</i>) had only one living individual, named <a href="/wiki/Lonesome_George" title="Lonesome George">Lonesome George</a>, until his death in June 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planetark_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-planetark-3">[3]</a></sup> The tortoise was believed to be entirely extinct in the mid-20th century, until Hungarian <a href="/wiki/Malacology" title="Malacology">malacologist</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%B3zsef_V%C3%A1gv%C3%B6lgyi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="József Vágvölgyi (page does not exist)">József Vágvölgyi</a> spotted Lonesome George on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galapagos_Islands" title="Galapagos Islands">Galapagos island</a> of Pinta on 1 December 1971. Since then, Lonesome George has been a powerful symbol for conservation efforts in general and for the Galapagos Islands in particular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> With his death on 24 June 2012, the subspecies is again believed to be extinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> With the discovery of 17 hybrid Pinta tortoises located at nearby Wolf Volcano a plan has been made to attempt to breed the subspecies back into a pure state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Not all species that are extinct in the wild are rare. For example, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ameca_splendens" title="Ameca splendens">Ameca splendens</a></i>, though extinct in the wild,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> was a popular fish among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquarist" title="Aquarist">aquarists</a> for some time, but hobbyist stocks have declined quite a lot more recently, placing its survival in jeopardy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kelleyetal2006_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-kelleyetal2006-8">[8]</a></sup> However, the ultimate purpose of preserving biodiversity is to maintain ecological function. When a species exists only in captivity, it is <a href="/wiki/Ecological_extinction" title="Ecological extinction">ecologically extinct</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reintroduction">Reintroduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extinct_in_the_wild&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reintroduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reintroduction" title="Reintroduction">Reintroduction</a></div>
<p>Reintroduction is the deliberate release of species into the wild, from captivity or relocated from other areas where the species survives. This may be an option for certain species that are endangered or extinct in the wild. However, it may be difficult to reintroduce EW species into the wild, even if their natural habitats were restored, because survival techniques, which are often passed from parents to offspring during parenting, may be lost. While conservation efforts may preserve some of the <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a> of a species, the species may never fully recover due to the loss of the natural <a href="/wiki/Memetics" title="Memetics">memetics</a> of the species.</p>
