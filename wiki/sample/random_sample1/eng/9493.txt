<p><b>Jascha Heifetz</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>; 2 February  [<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> 20 January] 1901 – 10 December 1987) was a Lithuanian born <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violinist</a>. Many consider him to be the greatest violinist of all time. Born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilno" title="Wilno">Wilno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> (present-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vilnius,_Lithuania" title="Vilnius, Lithuania">Vilnius, Lithuania</a>), he moved as a teenager to the United States, where his <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Hall" title="Carnegie Hall">Carnegie Hall</a> debut was rapturously received. He was a virtuoso since childhood—<a href="/wiki/Fritz_Kreisler" title="Fritz Kreisler">Fritz Kreisler</a>, another leading violinist of the twentieth century, said on hearing Heifetz's debut, "We might as well take our fiddles and break them across our knees."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>He had a long and successful performing and recording career; after an injury to his right (bowing) arm, he focused on teaching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GodsFiddler_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-GodsFiddler-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jascha_Heifetz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Heifetz was born into a Russian Jewish family in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wilno" title="Wilno">Wilno</a>, then part of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> His father, Reuven Heifetz, son of Elie, was a local violin teacher and served as the concertmaster of the Vilnius Theatre Orchestra for one season before the theatre closed down. While Jascha was an infant, his father did a series of tests, observing how his son responded to his fiddling. This convinced him that Jascha had great potential, and before Jascha was two years old, his father bought him a small violin, and taught him bowing and simple fingering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahn1969_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kahn1969-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>At five years old, he started lessons with <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Auer" title="Leopold Auer">Leopold Auer</a>. He was a child prodigy, making his public debut at seven, in Kovno (now <a href="/wiki/Kaunas" title="Kaunas">Kaunas</a>, Lithuania) playing the <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Mendelssohn)" title="Violin Concerto (Mendelssohn)">Violin Concerto in E minor</a> by <a href="/wiki/Felix_Mendelssohn" title="Felix Mendelssohn">Felix Mendelssohn</a>. In 1910 he entered the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg_Conservatory" title="Saint Petersburg Conservatory">Saint Petersburg Conservatory</a> to study under <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Auer" title="Leopold Auer">Leopold Auer</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>He played in Germany and <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, and met <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Kreisler" title="Fritz Kreisler">Fritz Kreisler</a> for the first time in a Berlin private house, in a "private press matinee on May 20, 1912. The home was that of Arthur Abell, the pre-eminent Berlin music critic for the American magazine, Musical Courier. Among other noted violinists in attendance was Fritz Kreisler. After the 12-year-old Heifetz performed the Mendelssohn violin concerto, Abell reported that Kreisler said to all present, 'We may as well break our fiddles across our knees.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Heifetz visited much of Europe while still in his teens. In April 1911, Heifetz performed in an outdoor concert in St. Petersburg before 25,000 spectators; there was such a sensational reaction that police officers needed to protect the young violinist after the concert. In 1914, he performed with the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Philharmonic" title="Berlin Philharmonic">Berlin Philharmonic</a> conducted by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Nikisch" title="Arthur Nikisch">Arthur Nikisch</a>. The conductor was very impressed, saying he had never heard such an excellent violinist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jascha_Heifetz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Heifetz and his family left Russia in 1917, traveling by rail to the Russian far east and then by ship to the United States, arriving in San Francisco. On 27 October 1917, Heifetz played for the first time in the United States, at <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Hall" title="Carnegie Hall">Carnegie Hall</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>, and became an immediate sensation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahn1969p66_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kahn1969p66-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AykeBook_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-AykeBook-11">[11]</a></sup> Fellow violinist <a href="/wiki/Mischa_Elman" title="Mischa Elman">Mischa Elman</a> in the audience asked "Do you think it's hot in here?", whereupon the pianist <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Godowsky" title="Leopold Godowsky">Leopold Godowsky</a>, in the next seat, imperturbably replied, "Not for pianists."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1917, Heifetz was elected as an honorary member of <a href="/wiki/Phi_Mu_Alpha_Sinfonia" title="Phi Mu Alpha Sinfonia">Phi Mu Alpha Sinfonia</a>, the national fraternity for men in music, by the fraternity's Alpha chapter at the New England Conservatory of Music in Boston. As he was aged 16 at the time, he was perhaps the youngest person ever elected to membership in the organization. Heifetz remained in the country and became an American citizen in 1925. When he told admirer <a href="/wiki/Groucho_Marx" title="Groucho Marx">Groucho Marx</a> he had been earning his living as a musician since the age of seven, Groucho answered, "And I suppose before that you were just a bum."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1954, Heifetz began working with pianist Brooks Smith, who would serve as Heifetz's accompanist for many years until he chose Dr. Ayke Agus as his accompanist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> He was also accompanied in concert for more than 20 years by Emmanuel Bay, another immigrant from Russia and a personal friend.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Heifetz's musicianship was such that he would demonstrate to his accompanist how he wanted passages to sound on the piano, and would even suggest which fingerings to use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the seasons of 1955–56, Heifetz announced that he would sharply curtail his concert activity, saying "I have been playing for a very long time". In 1958, he tripped in his kitchen and fractured his right hip, resulting in hospitalisation at <a href="/wiki/Cedars-Sinai_Medical_Center" title="Cedars-Sinai Medical Center">Cedars of Lebanon Hospital</a>, and a near fatal staphylococcus infection. He was invited to play Beethoven at the United Nations General Assembly, and entered leaning on a cane. By 1967, Heifetz had considerably curtailed his concert performances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahn1969p68_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kahn1969p68-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technique_and_timbre">Technique and timbre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jascha_Heifetz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Technique and timbre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Heifetz was "regarded as the greatest violin <a href="/wiki/Virtuoso" title="Virtuoso">virtuoso</a> since Paganini," wrote Lois Timnick of the Los Angeles Times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> "He set all standards for 20th-century violin playing...everything about him conspired to create a sense of awe," wrote music critic Harold Schonberg of the New York Times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> "The goals he set still remain, and for violinists today it's rather depressing that they may never really be attained again," wrote violinist Itzhak Perlman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Virgil_Thomson" title="Virgil Thomson">Virgil Thomson</a>, possibly referencing Richard Wagner's reputed taste for silk next to his skin, called Heifetz's style of playing "silk underwear music", a term he did not intend as a compliment. Other critics argue that he infused his playing with feeling and reverence for the composer's intentions. His style of playing was highly influential in defining the way modern violinists approach the instrument. His use of rapid <a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">vibrato</a>, emotionally charged <a href="/wiki/Portamento" title="Portamento">portamento</a>, fast tempi, and superb bow control coalesced to create a highly distinctive sound that makes Heifetz's playing instantly recognizable to aficionados. The violinist <a href="/wiki/Itzhak_Perlman" title="Itzhak Perlman">Itzhak Perlman</a>, who himself is noted for his rich warm tone and expressive use of portamento, describes Heifetz's tone as like "a tornado" because of its emotional intensity. Perlman said that Heifetz preferred to be recorded relatively close to the microphone; as a result, one would perceive a somewhat different tone quality when listening to Heifetz during a concert hall performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Heifetz was very particular about his choice of strings. He used a silver wound Tricolore <a href="/wiki/Catgut" title="Catgut">gut</a> G string, plain gut unvarnished D and A strings, and a Goldbrokat steel E string medium including clear Hill brand rosin sparingly. Heifetz believed that playing on gut strings was important in rendering an individual sound.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_recordings">Early recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jascha_Heifetz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early recordings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Heifetz made his first recordings in Russia during 1910–11, while still a student of <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Auer" title="Leopold Auer">Leopold Auer</a>. The existence of these recordings was not widely known until after Heifetz's death, when several sides (most notably <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Schubert" title="François Schubert">François Schubert's</a> <i>L'Abeille</i>) were reissued on an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LP_album" title="LP album">LP</a> included as a supplement to <a href="/wiki/The_Strad" title="The Strad">The Strad</a> magazine.</p>
<p>Shortly after his Carnegie Hall debut on 7 November 1917, Heifetz made his first recordings for the <a href="/wiki/Victor_Talking_Machine_Company" title="Victor Talking Machine Company">Victor Talking Machine Company</a>; he would remain with Victor and its successor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RCA_Victor" title="RCA Victor">RCA Victor</a>, for most of his career. For several years, in the 1930s, Heifetz recorded primarily for <a href="/wiki/HMV" title="HMV">HMV</a> in the UK because RCA Victor cut back on expensive classical recording sessions during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>; these discs were issued in the US by RCA Victor. Heifetz often enjoyed playing <a href="/wiki/Chamber_music" title="Chamber music">chamber music</a>. Various critics have blamed his limited success in chamber ensembles to the fact that his artistic personality tended to overwhelm his colleagues. Some notable collaborations include his 1941 recordings of <a href="/wiki/Piano_trio" title="Piano trio">piano trios</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a>, Schubert, and <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Brahms</a> with cellist <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Feuermann" title="Emanuel Feuermann">Emanuel Feuermann</a> and pianist <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rubinstein" title="Arthur Rubinstein">Arthur Rubinstein</a> as well as a later collaboration with Rubinstein and cellist <a href="/wiki/Gregor_Piatigorsky" title="Gregor Piatigorsky">Gregor Piatigorsky</a>, with whom he recorded trios by <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ravel" title="Maurice Ravel">Maurice Ravel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky" title="Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky">Tchaikovsky</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Felix_Mendelssohn" title="Felix Mendelssohn">Felix Mendelssohn</a>. Both formations were sometimes referred to as the <i>Million Dollar Trio</i>. Heifetz also recorded some string quintets with violinist <a href="/wiki/Israel_Baker" title="Israel Baker">Israel Baker</a>, violists <a href="/wiki/William_Primrose" title="William Primrose">William Primrose</a> and Virginia Majewski, and Piatigorsky.</p>
<p>He recorded the Beethoven <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Beethoven)" title="Violin Concerto (Beethoven)">Violin Concerto</a> in 1940 with the <a href="/wiki/NBC_Symphony_Orchestra" title="NBC Symphony Orchestra">NBC Symphony Orchestra</a> conducted by <a href="/wiki/Arturo_Toscanini" title="Arturo Toscanini">Arturo Toscanini</a>, and again in stereo in 1955 with the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Boston Symphony Orchestra">Boston Symphony Orchestra</a> conducted by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Munch_(conductor)" title="Charles Munch (conductor)">Charles Munch</a>. A live performance from 9 April 1944, of Heifetz playing the Mendelssohn <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Mendelssohn)" title="Violin Concerto (Mendelssohn)">Violin Concerto</a>, again with Toscanini and the NBC Symphony, has also been released.</p>
<p>He performed and recorded <a href="/wiki/Erich_Wolfgang_Korngold" title="Erich Wolfgang Korngold">Erich Wolfgang Korngold's</a> <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Korngold)" title="Violin Concerto (Korngold)">Violin Concerto</a> at a time when Korngold's scoring of numerous films for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warner_Brothers" title="Warner Brothers">Warner Brothers</a> prompted many classical musicians to develop the scarcely warranted opinion that Korngold was not a "serious" composer and to avoid his music in order to avoid being associated with him.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wartime">Wartime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jascha_Heifetz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wartime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heifetz commissioned a number of pieces, perhaps most notably the <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Walton)" title="Violin Concerto (Walton)">Violin Concerto</a> by <a href="/wiki/William_Walton" title="William Walton">William Walton</a>. He also arranged a number of pieces, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Hora_staccato" title="Hora staccato">Hora Staccato</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Grigora%C8%99_Dinicu" title="Grigoraș Dinicu">Grigoraș Dinicu</a>, a Romanian whom Heifetz is rumoured to have called the greatest violinist he had ever heard. Heifetz also played and composed for the piano;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AykeBook_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-AykeBook-11">[11]</a></sup> he performed mess hall jazz for soldiers at Allied camps across Europe during the Second World War, and under the alias Jim Hoyl he wrote a hit song, "When You Make Love to Me (Don't Make Believe)", which was sung by <a href="/wiki/Bing_Crosby" title="Bing Crosby">Bing Crosby</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decca_recordings">Decca recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jascha_Heifetz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Decca recordings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1944 to 1946, largely as a result of the <a href="/wiki/American_Federation_of_Musicians" title="American Federation of Musicians">American Federation of Musicians</a> recording ban (which actually began in 1942), Heifetz went to American <a href="/wiki/Decca_Records" title="Decca Records">Decca Records</a> to make recordings because Decca settled with the union in 1943, well before RCA Victor resolved their dispute with the musicians. He recorded primarily short pieces, including his own arrangements of music by <a href="/wiki/George_Gershwin" title="George Gershwin">George Gershwin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Foster" title="Stephen Foster">Stephen Foster</a>; these were pieces he often played as encores in his recitals. He was accompanied on the piano by Emanuel Bay or Milton Kaye. Among the more uncommon discs featured one of Decca's most popular artists, Bing Crosby, in the "Lullaby" from <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Godard" title="Benjamin Godard">Benjamin Godard's</a> opera <i><a href="/wiki/Jocelyn_(opera)" title="Jocelyn (opera)">Jocelyn</a></i> and <i>Where My Caravan Has Rested</i> (arranged by Heifetz and Crosby) by Hermann Lohr (1872–1943); Decca's studio orchestra was conducted by <a href="/wiki/Victor_Young" title="Victor Young">Victor Young</a> in the 27 July 1946, session. Recorded mostly in small studios, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digitally_remastered" title="Digitally remastered">digitally remastered</a> performances (issued by <a href="/wiki/MCA_Records" title="MCA Records">MCA</a>) have remarkably clear, <a href="/wiki/High_fidelity" title="High fidelity">high fidelity</a> sound. However, Heifetz soon returned to RCA Victor, where he continued to make recordings until the early 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_recordings">Later recordings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jascha_Heifetz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Later recordings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Returning to RCA Victor in 1946, Heifetz continued to record extensively for the company, including solo, chamber, and concerto recordings, primarily with the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Boston Symphony Orchestra">Boston Symphony Orchestra</a> under <a href="/wiki/Charles_Munch_(conductor)" title="Charles Munch (conductor)">Charles Munch</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Chicago Symphony Orchestra">Chicago Symphony Orchestra</a> under <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Reiner" title="Fritz Reiner">Fritz Reiner</a>. In 2000, RCA released a double CD compilation entitled <i>Jascha Heifetz – The Supreme</i> which gives a sampling of Heifetz's major recordings, including the 1955 recording of <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Brahms's</a> <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Brahms)" title="Violin Concerto (Brahms)">Violin Concerto</a> with Reiner and the Chicago Symphony Orchestra; the 1957 recording of <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky" title="Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky">Tchaikovsky's</a> <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Tchaikovsky)" title="Violin Concerto (Tchaikovsky)">Violin Concerto</a> (with the same forces); the 1959 recording of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Sibelius" title="Jean Sibelius">Sibelius's</a> <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Sibelius)" title="Violin Concerto (Sibelius)">Violin Concerto</a> with <a href="/wiki/Walter_Hendl" title="Walter Hendl">Walter Hendl</a> and the Chicago Symphony Orchestra; the 1961 recording of <a href="/wiki/Max_Bruch" title="Max Bruch">Max Bruch's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Scottish_Fantasy" title="Scottish Fantasy">Scottish Fantasy</a></i> with Sir <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Sargent" title="Malcolm Sargent">Malcolm Sargent</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Symphony_Orchestra" title="New Symphony Orchestra">New Symphony Orchestra</a> of London; the 1963 recording of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Glazunov" title="Alexander Glazunov">Glazunov's</a> <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Glazunov)" title="Violin Concerto (Glazunov)">A minor Concerto</a> with Walter Hendl and the RCA Victor Symphony Orchestra (drawn from New York musicians); the 1965 recording of <a href="/wiki/George_Gershwin" title="George Gershwin">George Gershwin's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Preludes_(Gershwin)" title="Three Preludes (Gershwin)">Three Preludes</a></i> (transcribed by Heifetz) with pianist Brooks Smith; and the 1970 recording of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Bach's</a> unaccompanied <i>Chaconne</i> from the <a href="/wiki/Partita_for_Violin_No._2_(Bach)" title="Partita for Violin No. 2 (Bach)">Partita No. 2 in D minor</a>.</p>

<p>On his third tour to <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> in 1953, Heifetz included in his recitals the Violin Sonata by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Strauss" title="Richard Strauss">Richard Strauss</a>. At the time, Strauss and a number of other German intellectuals were considered by many to be Nazis, or at least Nazi sympathizers, and Strauss works were unofficially banned in Israel along with those of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>. Despite the fact that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocaust" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a> had occurred less than ten years earlier and a last-minute plea from the Israeli Minister of Education, the defiant Heifetz argued, "The music is above these factors … I will not change my program. I have the right to decide on my repertoire." Throughout his tour the performance of the Strauss sonata was followed by dead silence.</p>
<p>Heifetz was attacked after his recital in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> outside his hotel by a young man who struck Heifetz's violin case with a crowbar, prompting Heifetz to use his bow-controlling right hand to protect his priceless violins. As the attacker started to flee, Heifetz alerted his companions, who were armed, "Shoot that man, he tried to kill me." The attacker escaped and was never found. The attack has since been attributed to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Israel_(group)" title="Kingdom of Israel (group)">Kingdom of Israel</a> terrorist group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The incident made headlines in the press and Heifetz defiantly announced that he would not stop playing the Strauss. Threats continued to come, however, and he omitted the Strauss from his next recital without explanation. His last concert was cancelled after his swollen right hand began to hurt. He left Israel and did not return until 1970.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Immigration_to_the_U.S.">Immigration to the U.S.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jascha_Heifetz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Immigration to the U.S.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Soviet establishment considered Heifetz and his teacher <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Auer" title="Leopold Auer">Leopold Auer</a> as traitors to their home country for emigrating to the US. Meanwhile, musicians who remained, such as <a href="/wiki/David_Oistrakh" title="David Oistrakh">David Oistrakh</a>, were seen as patriots. Heifetz greatly criticized the Soviet regime; he condemned the <a href="/wiki/International_Tchaikovsky_Competition" title="International Tchaikovsky Competition">International Tchaikovsky Competition</a> for being biased against Western competitors. During the Carl Flesch Competition in London, Oistrakh tried to persuade <a href="/wiki/Erick_Friedman" title="Erick Friedman">Erick Friedman</a>, Heifetz's star student, to enter the Tchaikovsky Competition, of which he was the principal juror. Hearing of this, Heifetz strongly advised against it, warning Friedman, "You will see what will happen there."</p>
<p>Consequently, the competition received international outrage after Friedman, already a seasoned performer and recording artist for RCA, who had performed with the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Chicago Symphony Orchestra">Chicago Symphony Orchestra</a>, <a href="/wiki/London_Symphony_Orchestra" title="London Symphony Orchestra">London Symphony Orchestra</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Symphony_Orchestra" title="Boston Symphony Orchestra">Boston Symphony Orchestra</a>, among many others, was placed sixth behind players who had yet to establish themselves. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Szigeti" title="Joseph Szigeti">Joseph Szigeti</a> later informed Heifetz himself that he had given his student top scores.</p>

<p>After an only partially successful operation on his right shoulder in 1972, Heifetz ceased giving concerts and making records. Although his prowess as a performer remained intact and he continued to play privately until the end, his bow arm was affected and he could never again hold the bow as high as before.</p>

<p>Heifetz taught the violin extensively, holding master classes first at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UCLA" title="UCLA">UCLA</a>, then at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a>, where the faculty included renowned cellist <a href="/wiki/Gregor_Piatigorsky" title="Gregor Piatigorsky">Gregor Piatigorsky</a> and violist <a href="/wiki/William_Primrose" title="William Primrose">William Primrose</a>. For a few years in the 1980s he also held classes in his private studio at home in Beverly Hills. His teaching studio can be seen today in the main building of the <a href="/wiki/Colburn_School" title="Colburn School">Colburn School</a> and serves as an inspiration to the students there. During his teaching career Heifetz taught, among others, <a href="/wiki/Erick_Friedman" title="Erick Friedman">Erick Friedman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Amoyal" title="Pierre Amoyal">Pierre Amoyal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Koelman" title="Rudolf Koelman">Rudolf Koelman</a>, Endre Granat, <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Fodor_(violinist)" title="Eugene Fodor (violinist)">Eugene Fodor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Rosenthal_(violinist)" title="Paul Rosenthal (violinist)">Paul Rosenthal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ilkka_Talvi" title="Ilkka Talvi">Ilkka Talvi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ayke_Agus" title="Ayke Agus">Ayke Agus</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> See: <a href="/wiki/List_of_music_students_by_teacher:_G_to_J#Jascha_Heifetz" title="List of music students by teacher: G to J">List of music students by teacher: G to J#Jascha Heifetz</a>.</p>
<p>During the last ten years of his life, Heifetz visited Hans Benning at <a href="/wiki/Benning_Violins" title="Benning Violins">Benning Violins</a> for maintenance on his 1740 Guarneri violin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-latimes_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jascha_Heifetz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Heifetz died at <a href="/wiki/Cedars-Sinai_Medical_Center" title="Cedars-Sinai Medical Center">Cedars-Sinai Medical Center</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a> on December 10, 1987, at the age of 86 following a fall at his home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimesobit_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimesobit-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>Heifetz owned the 1714 <i><a href="/wiki/Dolphin_Stradivarius" title="Dolphin Stradivarius">Dolphin</a> <a href="/wiki/Stradivarius" title="Stradivarius">Stradivarius</a></i>, the 1731 "Piel" Stradivarius, the 1736 Carlo Tononi, and the 1742 <i>ex David</i> <a href="/wiki/Guarneri" title="Guarneri">Guarneri</a> del Gesù, the last of which he preferred and kept until his death. The Dolphin Strad is currently owned by the Nippon Music Foundation. The Heifetz Tononi violin, used at his 1917 Carnegie Hall debut, was left in his will to Sherry Kloss, his Master-Teaching Assistant, with "one of my four good bows". Violinist Kloss wrote <i>Jascha Heifetz Through My Eyes</i>, and is a co-founder of the Jascha Heifetz Society.</p>
<p>The famed Guarneri is now in the San Francisco Legion of Honor Museum, as instructed by Heifetz in his will, and may only be taken out and played "on special occasions" by deserving players. The instrument has recently been on loan to San Francisco Symphony's <a href="/wiki/Concertmaster" title="Concertmaster">concertmaster</a> <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Barantschik" title="Alexander Barantschik">Alexander Barantschik</a>, who featured it in 2006 with <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Gorbatenko" title="Andrei Gorbatenko">Andrei Gorbatenko</a> and the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Academy_Orchestra" title="San Francisco Academy Orchestra">San Francisco Academy Orchestra</a> in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In 1989, Heifetz received a posthumous <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Lifetime_Achievement_Award" title="Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award">Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award</a>.</p>

<p>Heifetz married silent motion picture actress <a href="/wiki/Florence_Vidor" title="Florence Vidor">Florence Vidor</a> (1895–1977), ex-wife of <a href="/wiki/King_Vidor" title="King Vidor">King Vidor</a>, in 1928, and adopted her seven-year-old daughter, Suzanne (1918–2003). The couple had two more children, Josefa (born 1930) and Robert (1932–2001) before divorcing in 1945. In 1947, Heifetz married Frances Spielberger Spiegelberg (1911-2000), with whom he had another son, Joseph (known as Jay). The second marriage ended in divorce in 1962.</p>
<p>Heifetz's son Jay is a professional photographer. He was formerly head of marketing for the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Philharmonic" title="Los Angeles Philharmonic">Los Angeles Philharmonic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Bowl" title="Hollywood Bowl">Hollywood Bowl</a>, and the Chief Financial Officer of <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a>' Worldwide Video Division. He lives and works in <a href="/wiki/Fremantle" title="Fremantle">Fremantle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>. Heifetz's daughter, Josefa Heifetz Byrne, is a lexicographer, the author of the <i>Dictionary of Unusual, Obscure and Preposterous Words</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Heifetz's grandson <a href="/wiki/Danny_Heifetz" title="Danny Heifetz">Danny Heifetz</a> is an accomplished drummer/percussionist. Heifetz's extended family was active in Los Angeles progressive political circles in addition to music and art: they include artist Frances Heifetz-Bloch and her husband <a href="/wiki/Kalman_Bloch" title="Kalman Bloch">Kalman Bloch</a> and daughter <a href="/wiki/Michele_Zukovsky" title="Michele Zukovsky">Michele Zukovsky</a>—co-principal clarinetists for the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Philharmonic" title="Los Angeles Philharmonic">Los Angeles Philharmonic</a>—and son Gregory Bloch, violinist for the Italian rock band <a href="/wiki/Premiata_Forneria_Marconi" title="Premiata Forneria Marconi">Premiata Forneria Marconi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/It%27s_A_Beautiful_Day" title="It's A Beautiful Day">It's A Beautiful Day</a>, and member of the <a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a> orchestra from 1978–80.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Although Heifetz had a "difficult" personality, and has even been described as "misanthropic", he enjoyed the company of selected friends who zealously guarded his privacy, he spoke several languages including flawless English, and was an avid bridge and ping-pong player. His childhood had been difficult; his father was an extremely stern man who, even after Jascha had become the family's sole breadwinner, would still roundly criticise every performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Filmography">Filmography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jascha_Heifetz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Filmography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heifetz played a featured role in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/They_Shall_Have_Music" title="They Shall Have Music">They Shall Have Music</a></i> (1939) directed by <a href="/wiki/Archie_Mayo" title="Archie Mayo">Archie Mayo</a> and written by John Howard Lawson and Irmgard von Cube. He played himself, stepping in to save a music school for poor children from foreclosure. He later appeared in the 1947 film, <i>Carnegie Hall</i>, performing an abridged version of the first movement of Tchaikovsky's <a href="/wiki/Violin_Concerto_(Tchaikovsky)" title="Violin Concerto (Tchaikovsky)">Violin Concerto</a>, with the orchestra led by <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Reiner" title="Fritz Reiner">Fritz Reiner</a>, and consoling the star of the picture, who had watched his performance. In 1951, he appeared in the film <i>Of Men and Music</i>. In 1962, he appeared in a televised series of his master classes, and, in 1971, <i>Heifetz on Television</i> aired, an hour-long color special that featured the violinist performing a series of short works, the <i><a href="/wiki/Scottish_Fantasy" title="Scottish Fantasy">Scottish Fantasy</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Max_Bruch" title="Max Bruch">Max Bruch</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Chaconne" title="Chaconne">Chaconne</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Partita_for_Violin_No._2_(Bach)" title="Partita for Violin No. 2 (Bach)">Partita No. 2</a> by <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">J.<span class="nowrap"> </span>S.<span class="nowrap"> </span>Bach</a>. Heifetz conducted the orchestra, as the surviving video recording documents.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The most recent film featuring Heifetz, <i>Jascha Heifetz: God's Fiddler</i>, premiered on 16 April 2011 at the <a href="/wiki/Colburn_School" title="Colburn School">Colburn School</a> of Music. It is described as "The only film biography of the world's most renowned violinist, featuring family home movies in Los Angeles and all over the world. The documentary-like film talks about Heifetz's life and accomplishments and gives an inside view of his personal life."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GodsFiddler_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-GodsFiddler-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
