<p><i><b>1600 Penn</b></i> is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single-camera" title="Single-camera">single-camera</a> <a href="/wiki/Sitcom" title="Sitcom">sitcom</a> series about a <a href="/wiki/Dysfunctional_family" title="Dysfunctional family">dysfunctional family</a> living in the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a>. The series stars <a href="/wiki/Jenna_Elfman" title="Jenna Elfman">Jenna Elfman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Pullman" title="Bill Pullman">Bill Pullman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Josh_Gad" title="Josh Gad">Josh Gad</a>. Gad, along with <a href="/wiki/Jason_Winer" title="Jason Winer">Jason Winer</a> and Jon Lovett jointly created the central characters (the Gilchrist family) and the sitcom core format. <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> placed a series order in May 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The series aired as a mid-season replacement from December 17, 2012, to March 28, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> On May 9, 2013, <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> canceled the series after one season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cast_and_characters">Cast and characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1600_Penn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cast and characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Standrich "Skip" Gilchrist Jr., portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Josh_Gad" title="Josh Gad">Josh Gad</a>, is the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a>' eldest son.</li>
<li>Emily Nash-Gilchrist, portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Jenna_Elfman" title="Jenna Elfman">Jenna Elfman</a>, is the President's second wife and former campaign manager.</li>
<li>Becca Gilchrist, portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Martha_MacIsaac" title="Martha MacIsaac">Martha MacIsaac</a>, is the President's eldest daughter.</li>
<li>Marshall Malloy, portrayed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andre_Holland" title="Andre Holland">Andre Holland</a>, is the <a href="/wiki/White_House_Press_Secretary" title="White House Press Secretary">White House Press Secretary</a>.</li>
<li>Marigold Gilchrist, portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Amara_Miller" title="Amara Miller">Amara Miller</a>, is the President's younger daughter.</li>
<li>Xander Gilchrist, portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Stockham" title="Benjamin Stockham">Benjamin Stockham</a>, is the President's younger son.</li>
<li>Standrich Dale Gilchrist, portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Pullman" title="Bill Pullman">Bill Pullman</a>, is the President of the United States. A widower, he is a former <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">Congressman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Nevada" title="List of Governors of Nevada">Governor of Nevada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zurawik20130108_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-zurawik20130108-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prudom20130110_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-prudom20130110-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Recurring star <a href="/wiki/Robbie_Amell" title="Robbie Amell">Robbie Amell</a> plays D.B, the presumed father to Becca's baby throughout the first season.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting">Casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1600_Penn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Brittany_Snow" title="Brittany Snow">Brittany Snow</a> had originally been cast as the eldest daughter Becca, but was replaced by MacIsaac.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This is the second time <a href="/wiki/Bill_Pullman" title="Bill Pullman">Bill Pullman</a> has played an American president, the first having been in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(1996_film)" title="Independence Day (1996 film)">Independence Day</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prudom20130110_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-prudom20130110-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1600_Penn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>1600 Penn</i> received mixed reviews from critics. <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> gives the series a rating of 43%, based on 35 reviews, with the site's critical consensus reading, "Broad but likeable, <i>1600 Penn</i> unfortunately doles out its jokes unevenly and lacks the cutting wit necessary to meet its satirical aims."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> the series has a score of 55 out of 100, based on 36 critics, indicating "mixed or average reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>David Hinkley of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daily_News_(New_York)" title="Daily News (New York)">New York Daily News</a></i> gave the series 1 out of 5 stars and said "[it] was clearly designed to be good silly fun. It nails one out of three. It's silly." He called the First Family "annoying... sitcom stereotypes" and said that it "mines none of the more subtle and satisfying possibilities of poking fun at a staid institution. It's more like a drug-fueled <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i> sketch that won't end. Fortunately, <i>1600 Penn</i> probably will."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Tim Surette of <a href="/wiki/TV.com" title="TV.com">TV.com</a> said that the show is "what happens when network executives think a screeching buffoon equals laughs" and that the jokes elicit responses of "mostly tumbleweeds and cricket chirps".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Paste_(magazine)" title="Paste (magazine)">Paste</a>'</i>s Ross Bonaime also criticized the characters and said, "please, oh please, make the show actually funny... maybe it should just be put out of its misery."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Vicki Hyman of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Star-Ledger" title="The Star-Ledger">The Star-Ledger</a></i> graded the show a "D" and said, "you'd be forgiven for thinking [it] was a relic of the 1980s or 1990s", adding that the show was looking for "viewers who have lax requirements about actual humor in their comedies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Paul Meekin of <i>Star Pulse</i> noted that the show was unable to escape the footprint of <i><a href="/wiki/The_West_Wing" title="The West Wing">The West Wing</a></i> and wondered if it was "a years-late <i>West Wing</i> parody, a humorous and fresh take on presidential politics, or somewhere in between?" answering, "it's neither. It's actually quite godawful."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>One less critical review came from Hank Stuever of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i>, who called it "formulaic" but said, "give a few points to <i>1600 Penn</i> for trying." He also noted that "there's no danger of a partisan storyline or any resemblance to the current administration."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Maureen Ryan of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a></i> called Josh Gad "by far the best thing about this show" and hoped that if the show were cancelled, he find a better vehicle for his talents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
