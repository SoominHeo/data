<p>The <b>Lockheed F-117 Nighthawk</b> is a single-seat, <a href="/wiki/Twinjet" title="Twinjet">twin-engine</a> stealth <a href="/wiki/Attack_aircraft" title="Attack aircraft">attack aircraft</a> that was developed by <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation" title="Lockheed Corporation">Lockheed's</a> secretive <a href="/wiki/Skunk_Works" title="Skunk Works">Skunk Works</a> division and operated by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> (USAF). The F-117 was based on the <i><a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Have_Blue" title="Lockheed Have Blue">Have Blue</a></i> technology demonstrator, and was the first operational aircraft to be designed around <a href="/wiki/Stealth_technology" title="Stealth technology">stealth technology</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Maiden_flight" title="Maiden flight">maiden flight</a> of the Nighthawk took place in 1981, and the aircraft achieved <a href="/wiki/Initial_operating_capability" title="Initial operating capability">initial operating capability</a> status in 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nat_Museum_factsht_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nat_Museum_factsht-1">[1]</a></sup> The Nighthawk was shrouded in secrecy until it was revealed to the public in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maxwell_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-maxwell-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The F-117 was widely publicized for its role in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Persian Gulf War</a> of 1991. Although it was commonly referred to as the "Stealth Fighter", it was strictly an attack aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eden_p240_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-eden_p240-5">[5]</a></sup> F-117s took part in the conflict in <a href="/wiki/Yugoslav_Wars" title="Yugoslav Wars">Yugoslavia</a>, where <a href="/wiki/1999_F-117A_shootdown" title="1999 F-117A shootdown">one was shot down</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Surface-to-air_missile" title="Surface-to-air missile">surface-to-air missile</a> (SAM) on 27 March 1999; it was the only Nighthawk to be lost in combat. The U.S. Air Force retired the F-117 on 22 April 2008, primarily due to the fielding of the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-22_Raptor" title="Lockheed Martin F-22 Raptor">F-22 Raptor</a>. Sixty-four F-117s were built, 59 of which were production versions, with the other five being demonstrators/prototypes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_Have_Blue">Background and <i>Have Blue</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background and Have Blue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Have_Blue" title="Lockheed Have Blue">Lockheed <i>Have Blue</i></a></div>
<p>In 1964, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyotr_Ufimtsev" title="Pyotr Ufimtsev">Pyotr Ufimtsev</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> mathematician, published a seminal paper titled <i>Method of Edge Waves in the Physical Theory of Diffraction</i> in the journal of the Moscow Institute for Radio Engineering, in which he showed that the strength of the <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> return from an object is related to its edge configuration, not its size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dtic_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-dtic-6">[6]</a></sup> Ufimtsev was extending theoretical work published by the German physicist <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld" title="Arnold Sommerfeld">Arnold Sommerfeld</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Centennial_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Centennial-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-diffraction_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-diffraction-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filling_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-filling-9">[9]</a></sup> Ufimtsev demonstrated that he could calculate the <a href="/wiki/Radar_cross-section" title="Radar cross-section">radar cross-section</a> across a wing's surface and along its edge. The obvious and logical conclusion was that even a large aircraft could be made difficult to see by radar by exploiting this principle. However, the airplane's design would make it <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamically_unstable" title="Aerodynamically unstable">aerodynamically unstable</a>, and the state of computer technology in the early 1960s could not provide the kinds of flight computers which would later allow aircraft such as the F-117 and <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_B-2_Spirit" title="Northrop Grumman B-2 Spirit">B-2 Spirit</a> to stay airborne. By the 1970s, when Lockheed analyst Denys Overholser found Ufimtsev's paper, computers and software had advanced significantly, and the stage was set for the development of a stealthy airplane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Advent_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Advent-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>The F-117 was born after combat experience in the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> when increasingly sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Surface-to-air_missile" title="Surface-to-air missile">surface-to-air missiles</a> (SAMs) downed heavy bombers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-air-attack_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-air-attack-11">[11]</a></sup> It was a <a href="/wiki/Black_project" title="Black project">black project</a>, an ultra-secret program for much of its life: very few people in <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">the Pentagon</a> knew the program even existed, until the F-117s were revealed to the public in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maxwell_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-maxwell-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-12">[12]</a></sup> The project began in 1975 with a model called the "Hopeless Diamond"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rich_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-rich-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-f117reunion_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-f117reunion-14">[14]</a></sup> (a wordplay on the <a href="/wiki/Hope_Diamond" title="Hope Diamond">Hope Diamond</a> because of its appearance). The following year, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" title="Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency">Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency</a> (DARPA) issued Lockheed Skunk Works a contract to build and test two Stealth Strike Fighters, under the code name "<i><a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Have_Blue" title="Lockheed Have Blue">Have Blue</a></i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodall_p.19_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodall_p.19-15">[15]</a></sup> These subscale aircraft incorporated jet engines of the <a href="/wiki/Northrop_T-38_Talon" title="Northrop T-38 Talon">Northrop T-38A</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fly-by-wire" title="Fly-by-wire">fly-by-wire</a> systems of the <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_F-16_Fighting_Falcon" title="General Dynamics F-16 Fighting Falcon">F-16</a>, landing gear of the <a href="/wiki/Fairchild_Republic_A-10_Thunderbolt_II" title="Fairchild Republic A-10 Thunderbolt II">A-10</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Built_environment" title="Built environment">environmental systems</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_C-130_Hercules" title="Lockheed C-130 Hercules">C-130</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodall_p.19_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goodall_p.19-15">[15]</a></sup> By bringing together existing technology and components, Lockheed built two demonstrators under budget, at $35 million for both aircraft, and in record time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodall_p.19_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Goodall_p.19-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Maiden_flight" title="Maiden flight">maiden flight</a> of the demonstrators occurred on 1 December 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eden_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-eden-16">[16]</a></sup> Although both aircraft were lost during the demonstration program, test data proved positive. The success of <i>Have Blue</i> led the government to increase funding for <a href="/wiki/Stealth_technology" title="Stealth technology">stealth technology</a>. Much of that increase was allocated towards the production of an operational stealth aircraft, the Lockheed F-117A, under the program code name "<i>Senior Trend</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goodall_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-goodall-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-f-117a_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-f-117a-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Senior_Trend"><i>Senior Trend</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Senior Trend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The decision to produce the F-117A was made on 1 November 1978, and a contract was awarded to Lockheed Advanced Development Projects, popularly known as the Skunk Works, in <a href="/wiki/Burbank,_California" title="Burbank, California">Burbank</a>, California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rich1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-rich1-19">[19]</a></sup> The program was led by <a href="/wiki/Ben_Rich" title="Ben Rich">Ben Rich</a>, with Alan Brown as manager of the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Rich called on Bill Schroeder, a Lockheed mathematician, and Denys Overholser, a computer scientist, to exploit Ufimtsev's work. The three designed a computer program called "Echo", which made it possible to design an airplane with flat panels, called facets, which were arranged so as to scatter over 99% of a radar's signal energy "painting" the aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Advent_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Advent-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discovery_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-discovery-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-air-attack2_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-air-attack2-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first YF-117A, serial number <i>79-0780</i>, made its maiden flight from <a href="/wiki/Area_51" title="Area 51">Groom Lake</a>, Nevada, on 18 June 1981,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodall_p.27_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodall_p.27-23">[23]</a></sup> only 31 months after the full-scale development decision. The first production F-117A was delivered in 1982, and operational capability was achieved in October 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Centennial_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Centennial-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodall_p.29_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodall_p.29-24">[24]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/4450th_Tactical_Group" title="4450th Tactical Group">4450th Tactical Group</a> stationed at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nellis_AFB" title="Nellis AFB">Nellis AFB</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a> were tasked with the operational development of the early F-117, and between 1981 (prior to the arrival of the first models) and 1989 they used <a href="/wiki/LTV_A-7_Corsair_II" title="LTV A-7 Corsair II">LTV A-7 Corsair IIs</a> for training, to bring all pilots to a common flight training baseline and later as chase planes for F-117A tests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holder.26Wallace_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holder.26Wallace-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Air Force denied the existence of the aircraft until 10 November 1988, when Assistant Secretary of Defense <a href="/wiki/J._Daniel_Howard" title="J. Daniel Howard">J. Daniel Howard</a> displayed a grainy photograph at a Pentagon press conference, disproving the many inaccurate rumors about the shape of the secret "F-19". After the announcement pilots could fly the F-117 during daytime and no longer needed to be associated with the A-7, flying the T-38 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a> trainer for travel and training instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmorep2003_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-crickmorep2003-26">[26]</a></sup> In April 1990, two F-117 aircraft were flown into <a href="/wiki/Nellis_Air_Force_Base" title="Nellis Air Force Base">Nellis Air Force Base</a>, Nevada, arriving during daylight and publicly displayed to a crowd of tens of thousands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dreamlandresort_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-dreamlandresort-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Five Full Scale Development (FSD) aircraft were built, designated "YF-117A".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-designation-systems_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-designation-systems-28">[28]</a></sup> The last of 59 production F-117s were delivered on 3 July 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodall_p.29_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goodall_p.29-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-donald_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-donald-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<p>As the Air Force has stated, "Streamlined management by Aeronautical Systems Center, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wright-Patterson_AFB" title="Wright-Patterson AFB">Wright-Patterson AFB</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, combined breakthrough stealth technology with concurrent development and production to rapidly field the aircraft... The F-117A program demonstrates that a stealth aircraft can be designed for reliability and maintainability."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nat_Museum_factsht_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nat_Museum_factsht-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Designation">Designation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Designation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The operational aircraft was officially designated "F-117A".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dtic3_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-dtic3-30">[30]</a></sup> Most modern U.S. military aircraft use <a href="/wiki/1962_United_States_Tri-Service_aircraft_designation_system" title="1962 United States Tri-Service aircraft designation system">post-1962 designations</a> in which the designation "F" is usually an <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">air-to-air fighter</a>, "B" is usually a <a href="/wiki/Bomber" title="Bomber">bomber</a>, "A" is usually a ground-attack aircraft, etc. (Examples include the <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F-15_Eagle" title="McDonnell Douglas F-15 Eagle">F-15</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_B-2_Spirit" title="Northrop Grumman B-2 Spirit">B-2</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Grumman_A-6_Intruder" title="Grumman A-6 Intruder">A-6</a>.) The F-117 is primarily an attack aircraft,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eden_p240_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-eden_p240-5">[5]</a></sup> so its "F" designation is inconsistent with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">DoD</a> system. This is an inconsistency that has been repeatedly employed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> with several of its attack aircraft since the late 1950s, including the <a href="/wiki/Republic_F-105_Thunderchief" title="Republic F-105 Thunderchief">Republic F-105 Thunderchief</a> and <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_F-111_Aardvark" title="General Dynamics F-111 Aardvark">General Dynamics F-111 Aardvark</a>. A televised documentary quoted a senior member of the F-117A development team as saying that the top-notch USAF fighter pilots required to fly the new aircraft were more easily attracted to an aircraft with an "F" designation for fighter, as opposed to a bomber ("B") or attack ("A") designation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>The designation "F-117" seems to indicate that it was given an official designation prior to the 1962 U.S. Tri-Service Aircraft Designation System and could be considered numerically to be a part of the earlier "Century series" of fighters. The assumption prior to the revealing of the aircraft to the public was that it would likely receive the <a href="/wiki/F-19" title="F-19">F-19</a> designation as that number had not been used. However, there were no other aircraft to receive a "100" series number following the F-111. Soviet fighters obtained by the U.S. via various means under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constant_Peg" title="Constant Peg">Constant Peg</a> program<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airforce-magazine_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-airforce-magazine-32">[32]</a></sup> were given F-series numbers for their evaluation by U.S. pilots, and with the advent of the <a href="/wiki/Teen_Series" title="Teen Series">Teen Series</a> fighters, most often <a href="/wiki/Century_Series" title="Century Series">Century Series</a> designations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merlin_groom_2011_p32_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Merlin_groom_2011_p32-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>As with other exotic military aircraft types flying in the southern Nevada area, such as captured fighters, an arbitrary radio call of "117" was assigned. This same radio call had been used by the enigmatic <a href="/wiki/4477th_Test_and_Evaluation_Squadron" title="4477th Test and Evaluation Squadron">4477th Test and Evaluation Squadron</a>, also known as the "Red Hats" or "Red Eagles", that often had flown expatriated <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan#List_of_MiG_aircraft" title="Mikoyan">MiG jet fighters</a> in the area, but there was no relationship to the call and the formal F-19 designation then being considered by the Air Force. Apparently, use of the "117" radio call became commonplace and when Lockheed released its first flight manual (i.e., the Air Force "dash one" manual for the aircraft), F-117A was the designation printed on the cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-miller-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:F-117_Front_View.jpg"><img alt="Closeup view of the nose of a black jet, emphasizing the many angled-surfaces" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1899" data-file-width="2859" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/F-117_Front_View.jpg/220px-F-117_Front_View.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/F-117_Front_View.jpg/330px-F-117_Front_View.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/F-117_Front_View.jpg/440px-F-117_Front_View.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:F-117_Front_View.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Front view of an F-117</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The F-117's unusual design surprised and puzzled experienced pilots; a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> pilot who flew it as an <a href="/wiki/Exchange_officer" title="Exchange officer">exchange officer</a> while still secret stated that when he first saw a photograph of the F-117, he "promptly giggled and thought to [himself] 'this clearly can't fly'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore-35">[35]</a></sup> Early stealth aircraft were designed with a focus on minimal <a href="/wiki/Radar_cross-section" title="Radar cross-section">radar cross-section</a> (RCS) rather than aerodynamic performance. Highly-stealth aircraft like the F-117 Nighthawk are aerodynamically unstable in all three <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_principal_axes" title="Aircraft principal axes">aircraft principal axes</a> and require constant flight corrections from a <a href="/wiki/Fly-by-wire" title="Fly-by-wire">fly-by-wire</a> (FBW) flight system to maintain controlled flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-janos_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-janos-36">[36]</a></sup> It is shaped to deflect radar signals and is about the size of an F-15 Eagle.</p>
<p>The single-seat Nighthawk is powered by two non-afterburning <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_F404" title="General Electric F404">General Electric F404</a> turbofan engines. It is <a href="/wiki/Aerial_refueling" title="Aerial refueling">air refuelable</a> and features a <a href="/wiki/V-tail" title="V-tail">V-tail</a>. The maximum speed is 623 mph (993 km/h) at high altitude, the max rate of climb is 2820 feet (860 m) per minute, and service ceiling is 43,000 (13,110 m) to 45,000 feet (13,716 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The cockpit was quite spacious, with ergonomic displays and controls, but the field of view was somewhat obstructed, with a large blind spot to the rear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Avionics">Avionics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Avionics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has quadruple-redundant <a href="/wiki/Fly-by-wire" title="Fly-by-wire">fly-by-wire flight controls</a>. To lower development costs, the avionics, fly-by-wire systems, and other parts are derived from the <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics_F-16_Fighting_Falcon" title="General Dynamics F-16 Fighting Falcon">General Dynamics F-16 Fighting Falcon</a>, <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F/A-18_Hornet" title="McDonnell Douglas F/A-18 Hornet">McDonnell Douglas F/A-18 Hornet</a> and <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_F-15E_Strike_Eagle" title="McDonnell Douglas F-15E Strike Eagle">McDonnell Douglas F-15E Strike Eagle</a>. The parts were originally described as spares on budgets for these aircraft, to keep the F-117 project secret.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_F-117A_cockpit_at_the_National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force,_Dayton,_Ohio,_USA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="1920" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Lockheed_F-117A_cockpit_at_the_National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force%2C_Dayton%2C_Ohio%2C_USA.jpg/220px-Lockheed_F-117A_cockpit_at_the_National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force%2C_Dayton%2C_Ohio%2C_USA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Lockheed_F-117A_cockpit_at_the_National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force%2C_Dayton%2C_Ohio%2C_USA.jpg/330px-Lockheed_F-117A_cockpit_at_the_National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force%2C_Dayton%2C_Ohio%2C_USA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Lockheed_F-117A_cockpit_at_the_National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force%2C_Dayton%2C_Ohio%2C_USA.jpg/440px-Lockheed_F-117A_cockpit_at_the_National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force%2C_Dayton%2C_Ohio%2C_USA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_F-117A_cockpit_at_the_National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force,_Dayton,_Ohio,_USA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cockpit</div>

<p>The aircraft is equipped with sophisticated navigation and attack systems integrated into a digital avionics suite. It navigates primarily by <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a> and high-accuracy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inertial_navigation" title="Inertial navigation">inertial navigation</a>. Missions are coordinated by an automated planning system that can automatically perform all aspects of an attack mission, including weapons release. Targets are acquired by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_imaging" title="Thermal imaging">thermal imaging</a> <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> system, slaved to a <a href="/wiki/Laser_rangefinder" title="Laser rangefinder">laser rangefinder</a>/<a href="/wiki/Laser_designator" title="Laser designator">laser designator</a> that finds the range and designates targets for <a href="/wiki/Laser-guided_bomb" title="Laser-guided bomb">laser-guided bombs</a>. The F-117A's split internal bay can carry 5,000 lb (2,300 kg) of ordnance. Typical weapons are a pair of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GBU-10" title="GBU-10">GBU-10</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GBU-12" title="GBU-12">GBU-12</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GBU-27" title="GBU-27">GBU-27</a> laser-guided bombs, two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BLU-109" title="BLU-109">BLU-109</a> penetration bombs, or two Joint Direct Attack Munitions (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JDAM" title="JDAM">JDAMs</a>), a GPS/INS guided stand-off bomb.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stealth">Stealth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Stealth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stealth_aircraft" title="Stealth aircraft">Stealth aircraft</a></div>
<p>The F-117 has a <a href="/wiki/Radar_cross-section" title="Radar cross-section">Radar cross-section</a> of about 0.001 m<sup>2</sup> (0.0108 sq ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-richardson_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-richardson-39">[39]</a></sup> Among the penalties for stealth are lower engine thrust, due to losses in the inlet and outlet, a very low wing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio_(wing)" title="Aspect ratio (wing)">aspect ratio</a>, and a high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweep_angle" title="Sweep angle">sweep angle</a> (50°) needed to deflect incoming radar waves to the sides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sweetman_AS_dj2008_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sweetman_AS_dj2008-40">[40]</a></sup> With these design considerations and no <a href="/wiki/Afterburner" title="Afterburner">afterburner</a>, the F-117 is limited to subsonic speeds.</p>
<p>The F-117A carries no radar, which lowers emissions and cross-section, and whether it carries any radar detection equipment is classified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sweetman_AS_dj2008_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sweetman_AS_dj2008-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The F-117A's <a href="/wiki/Faceting" title="Faceting">faceted</a> shape (made from 2-dimensional flat surfaces) resulted from the limitations of the 1970s-era computer technology used to calculate its radar cross-section. Later <a href="/wiki/Supercomputer" title="Supercomputer">supercomputers</a> made it possible for subsequent aircraft like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-2_bomber" title="B-2 bomber">B-2 bomber</a> to use curved surfaces while maintaining stealth, through the use of far more computational resources to perform the additional calculations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rich4_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-rich4-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>An exhaust plume contributes a significant <a href="/wiki/Infrared_signature" title="Infrared signature">infrared signature</a>. The F-117 reduces IR signature with a non-circular <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_system" title="Exhaust system">tail pipe</a> (a slit shape) to minimize the exhaust cross-sectional volume and maximize the mixing of hot exhaust with cool ambient air. The F-117 lacks <a href="/wiki/Afterburner" title="Afterburner">afterburners</a>, because the hot exhaust would increase the <a href="/wiki/Infrared_signature" title="Infrared signature">infrared signature</a>, and breaking the <a href="/wiki/Sound_barrier" title="Sound barrier">sound barrier</a> would produce an obvious <a href="/wiki/Sonic_boom" title="Sonic boom">sonic boom</a>, as well as surface heating of the aircraft skin which also increased the infrared footprint. As a result, its performance in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_combat_maneuvering" title="Air combat maneuvering">air combat maneuvering</a> required in a <a href="/wiki/Dogfight" title="Dogfight">dogfight</a> would never match that of a dedicated fighter aircraft. This was unimportant in the case of this aircraft since it was designed to be a bomber.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Passive_radar" title="Passive radar">Passive (multistatic) radar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bistatic_radar" title="Bistatic radar">bistatic radar</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bistatic_Radar_Sets_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bistatic_Radar_Sets-42">[42]</a></sup> and especially <a href="/wiki/Multistatic_radar" title="Multistatic radar">multistatic radar</a> systems detect some stealth aircraft better than conventional <a href="/wiki/Monostatic_radar" title="Monostatic radar">monostatic radars</a>, since first-generation stealth technology (such as the F-117) reflects energy away from the transmitter's <a href="/wiki/Line-of-sight_propagation" title="Line-of-sight propagation">line of sight</a>, effectively increasing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radar_cross_section" title="Radar cross section">radar cross section</a> (RCS) in other directions, which the passive radars monitor.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the program's early years, from 1984 to mid-1992, the F-117A fleet was based at <a href="/wiki/Tonopah_Test_Range_Airport" title="Tonopah Test Range Airport">Tonopah Test Range Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, where it served under the 4450th Tactical Group. Because the F-117 was classified during this time, the unit was officially located at <a href="/wiki/Nellis_Air_Force_Base" title="Nellis Air Force Base">Nellis Air Force Base</a>, Nevada, and equipped with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-7_Corsair_II" title="A-7 Corsair II">A-7 Corsair II</a> aircraft. All military personnel were permanently assigned to Nellis AFB, but most personnel and their families lived in Las Vegas. This required commercial air and trucking to transport personnel between Las Vegas and Tonopah each week. The 4450th was absorbed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/37th_Fighter_Wing" title="37th Fighter Wing">37th Tactical Fighter Wing</a> in 1989. In 1992, the entire fleet was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Holloman_Air_Force_Base" title="Holloman Air Force Base">Holloman Air Force Base</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, where it was placed under the command of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/49th_Fighter_Wing" title="49th Fighter Wing">49th Fighter Wing</a>. This move also eliminated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Key_Air" title="Key Air">Key Air</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Trans_Air" title="American Trans Air">American Trans Air</a> contract flights to Tonopah, which flew 22,000 passenger trips on 300 flights from Nellis to Tonopah per month.</p>
<p>F-117 pilots called themselves "Bandits". Each of the 558 Air Force pilots who have flown the F-117 have a Bandit number, such as "Bandit 52", that indicates the sequential order of their first flight in the F-117.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Topolsky_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Topolsky-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The F-117 has been used several times in war. Its first mission was during the <a href="/wiki/United_States_invasion_of_Panama" title="United States invasion of Panama">United States invasion of Panama</a> in 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tread_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tread-44">[44]</a></sup> During that invasion two F-117A Nighthawks dropped two bombs on Rio Hato airfield.</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a> in 1991, the F-117A flew approximately 1,300 sorties and scored direct hits on 1,600 high-value targets in Iraq<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nat_Museum_factsht_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nat_Museum_factsht-1">[1]</a></sup> over 6,905 flight hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frontline_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-frontline-45">[45]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaflet_drop" title="Leaflet drop">Leaflet drops</a> on Iraqi forces displayed the F-117 destroying ground targets and warned "Escape now and save yourselves".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmorep2003_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-crickmorep2003-26">[26]</a></sup> Initial claims of its effectiveness were later found to be overstated. For instance it was claimed that the F-117 made up 2.5% of <a href="/wiki/Coalition_of_the_Gulf_War" title="Coalition of the Gulf War">Coalition</a> tactical aircraft in Iraq and they attacked more than 40% of the strategic targets;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-46">[46]</a></sup> this ignored the fact that only 229 Coalition aircraft could drop and designate laser-guided bombs of which 36 F-117 represented 15.7%, and only the USAF had the I-2000 bombs intended for hardened targets, so the F-117 represented 32% of all coalition aircraft that could deliver such bombs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Initial reports of them hitting 80% of their targets were later scaled back to "41-60%".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> On the first night they failed to hit 40% of the air-defense targets they were assigned, including the Air Defense Operations Center in Baghdad, and 8 such targets remained functional out of 10 that could be assessed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> In their Desert Storm white paper the USAF claimed that "the F-117 was the only airplane that the planners dared risk over downtown Baghdad" and that this area was particularly well defended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GAO1378_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-GAO1378-50">[50]</a></sup> In fact most of the air defenses were on the outskirts of the city and many other aircraft hit targets in the downtown area, with minimal casualties when they attacked at night like the F-117.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GAO1378_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-GAO1378-50">[50]</a></sup> This meant they avoided the optically aimed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_cannon#AA_warfare_systems" title="Anti-aircraft cannon">AAA</a> and infra-red <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface-to-Air_Missile" title="Surface-to-Air Missile">SAMs</a> which were the biggest threat to Coalition aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>The aircraft was operated in secret from Tonopah for almost a decade, but after the Gulf War the aircraft moved to Holloman in 1992. Its integration with the USAF's non-stealth "iron jets" occurred slowly, however; because of ongoing secrecy, others continued to see the aircraft, one senior F-117A pilot later said, as "none of their business, a stand-alone system".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sweetman_AS_dj2008_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sweetman_AS_dj2008-40">[40]</a></sup> The F-117A and the men and women of the 49th Fighter Wing were deployed to Southwest Asia on multiple occasions. On their first deployment, with the aid of aerial refueling, pilots flew non-stop from Holloman to Kuwait, a flight of approximately 18.5 hours – a record for single-seat fighters that stands today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nat_Museum_factsht_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nat_Museum_factsht-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combat_loss">Combat loss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Combat loss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1999_F-117A_shootdown" title="1999 F-117A shootdown">1999 F-117A shootdown</a></div>
<p>One F-117 (AF ser. no. 82-0806) was lost to enemy action. It was shot down during a mission against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Army_of_Yugoslavia_(FRY)" title="Army of Yugoslavia (FRY)">Army of Yugoslavia</a> on 27 March 1999, during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1999_NATO_bombing_of_Yugoslavia" title="1999 NATO bombing of Yugoslavia">Operation Allied Force</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup> At approximately 8:15 pm local time, the aircraft was acquired by a fire control radar at a distance of 13 km and an altitude of 8 km: SA-3s were then launched by a Yugoslav version of the Soviet <a href="/wiki/S-125_Neva/Pechora" title="S-125 Neva/Pechora">Isayev S-125 "Neva"</a> (NATO name SA-3 "Goa") anti-aircraft missile system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strategypage_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-strategypage-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dani-54">[54]</a></sup> The launcher was run by the 3rd Battalion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/250th_Air_Defence_Missile_Brigade" title="250th Air Defence Missile Brigade">250th Air Defence Missile Brigade</a> under the command of Colonel <a href="/wiki/Zolt%C3%A1n_Dani" title="Zoltán Dani">Zoltán Dani</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-archive-55">[55]</a></sup> According to Dani in a 2007 interview, his troops spotted the aircraft on radar when its bomb-bay doors opened, raising its radar signature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive5_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-archive5-56">[56]</a></sup> One source states one of the missiles detonated by its <a href="/wiki/Proximity_fuze" title="Proximity fuze">proximity fuze</a> near the F-117.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup> Dani said he kept most of his missile sites intact by frequently moving them, and had spotters looking for F-117s and other NATO aircraft. He also stated that he oversaw the modification of his targeting radar to improve its detection capability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dani_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dani-54">[54]</a></sup></p>

<p>After the explosion, the aircraft became uncontrollable, forcing the pilot to eject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-3"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup> The pilot was recovered six hours later by a USAF pararescue team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-4"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Photos show that the aircraft struck the ground at low speed in an inverted position, and that the airframe remained relatively intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-5"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup> The Serbs invited Russian personnel to inspect the aircraft's remains, compromising the then 25-year-old U.S. stealth technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsmax_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-newsmax-58">[58]</a></sup> The F-117's pilot was initially misidentified. Though the name "Capt Ken 'Wiz' Dwelle" was painted on the canopy, it was revealed in 2007 that the pilot was Lt. Col. Dale Zelko.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airforces_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-airforces-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usatoday_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday-60">[60]</a></sup> The stealth technology from the downed F-117 may have been acquired by Russia and China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chinadigitaltimes_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-chinadigitaltimes-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some American sources state that a second F-117A was damaged during the same campaign, allegedly on 30 April 1999;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsbank_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-newsbank-62">[62]</a></sup> the aircraft returned to base, but it supposedly never flew again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-description_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-description-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airforces6_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-airforces6-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_service_and_retirement">Later service and retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Later service and retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Use of the aircraft as part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Allied_Force" title="Operation Allied Force">Operation Allied Force</a> continued, and it was later used in the <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%932014)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001–2014)">Operation Enduring Freedom</a> in 2001 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Iraqi_Freedom" title="Operation Iraqi Freedom">Operation Iraqi Freedom</a> in 2003. The F-117 was operated by the U.S. Air Force.</p>
<p>The loss in Serbia caused the USAF to create a subsection of their existing weapons school to improve tactics. More training was done with other units, and the F-117A began to participate in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Flag_exercise" title="Red Flag exercise">Red Flag exercise</a>. Though advanced for its time, the F-117's stealthy faceted airframe required a large amount of maintenance and was eventually superseded by streamlined shapes produced with <a href="/wiki/Computer-aided_design" title="Computer-aided design">computer-aided design</a>. Other weapon systems began to take on the F-117A's roles, such as the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-22_Raptor" title="Lockheed Martin F-22 Raptor">F-22 Raptor</a> gaining ability to drop guided bombs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> By 2005, the aircraft was used only for certain missions, such as if a pilot needed to verify that the correct target had been hit, or when minimal <a href="/wiki/Collateral_damage" title="Collateral damage">collateral damage</a> was vital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sweetman_AS_dj2008_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sweetman_AS_dj2008-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Air Force had once planned to retire the F-117 in 2011, but Program Budget Decision 720 (PBD 720), dated 28 December 2005, proposed retiring it by October 2008 to free up an estimated $1.07 billion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thehill_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-thehill-66">[66]</a></sup> to buy more F-22s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Topolsky_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Topolsky-43">[43]</a></sup> PBD 720 called for 10 F-117s to be retired in FY2007 and the remaining 42 in FY2008, stating that other Air Force planes and missiles could stealthily deliver precision ordnance, including the <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman_B-2_Spirit" title="Northrop Grumman B-2 Spirit">B-2 Spirit</a>, F-22 and <a href="/wiki/AGM-158_JASSM" title="AGM-158 JASSM">JASSM</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive7_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-archive7-67">[67]</a></sup> The planned introduction of the multirole <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_F-35_Lightning_II" title="Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II">F-35 Lightning II</a> also contributed to the retirement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boston_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-boston-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2006, the Air Force closed the F-117 formal training unit (FTU),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airforcetimes_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-airforcetimes-69">[69]</a></sup> and announced the retirement of the F-117.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bates_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bates-70">[70]</a></sup> The first six aircraft to be retired made their last flight on 12 March 2007 after a ceremony at Holloman AFB to commemorate the aircraft's career. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brigadier_General" title="Brigadier General">Brigadier General</a> <a href="/wiki/David_L._Goldfein" title="David L. Goldfein">David L. Goldfein</a>, commander of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/49th_Fighter_Wing" title="49th Fighter Wing">49th Fighter Wing</a>, said at the ceremony, "With the launch of these great aircraft today, the circle comes to a close – their service to our nation's defense fulfilled, their mission accomplished and a job well done. We send them today to their final resting place – a home they are intimately familiar with – their first, and only, home outside of Holloman."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atn070316_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-atn070316-71">[71]</a></sup></p>

<p>Unlike most other Air Force aircraft that are retired to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Davis-Monthan_AFB" title="Davis-Monthan AFB">Davis-Monthan AFB</a> for scrapping, or dispersal to museums, most of the F-117s were placed in "Type 1000" storage<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> in their original hangars at the <a href="/wiki/Tonopah_Test_Range_Airport" title="Tonopah Test Range Airport">Tonopah Test Range Airport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-6"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup> At Tonopah, their wings were removed and the aircraft are stored in their original climate-controlled hangars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atn070316_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-atn070316-71">[71]</a></sup> The decommissioning occurred in eight phases, with the operational aircraft retired to Tonopah in seven waves beginning on 13 March 2007, and ending with the last wave's arrival on 22 April 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATRetire_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-LATRetire-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-7"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup> Four aircraft were kept flying beyond April by the <a href="/wiki/410th_Flight_Test_Squadron" title="410th Flight Test Squadron">410th Flight Test Squadron</a> at Palmdale for flight test. By August, two were remaining. The last F-117 (AF Serial No. 86-0831) left Palmdale to fly to Tonopah on 11 August 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-8"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flightglobal_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-flightglobal-73">[73]</a></sup> With the last aircraft retired, the 410th was inactivated in a ceremony on 1 August 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edwards_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-edwards-74">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>Five aircraft were placed in museums, including the first four YF-117As and some remains of the F-117 shot down over Serbia. Through 2009, one F-117 has been scrapped. F-117 AF Serial No. 79-0784 was scrapped at the Palmdale test facility on 26 April 2008. It was the last F-117 at Palmdale and was scrapped to test an effective method for destroying F-117 airframes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-9"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup> Although officially retired, the F-117 fleet remains intact, and photos show the aircraft carefully mothballed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-10"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup> F-117s have been spotted flying in the Nellis Bombing Range as recently as July 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aroundtherange_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-aroundtherange-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jalopnik1_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-jalopnik1-77">[77]</a></sup> Some of the aircraft are flown periodically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>Congress had declared that all F-117s mothballed from 30 September 2006 onwards to be maintained "in a condition that would allow recall of that aircraft to future service" as part of the 2007 National Defense Authorization Act. By April 2016, lawmakers appeared ready to "remove the requirement that certain F-117 aircraft be maintained in a condition that would allow recall of those aircraft to future service," which would move them from storage to the aerospace maintenance and regeneration yard in Arizona to be scavenged for hard-to-find parts or torn apart entirely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FG_2016-04-26_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FG_2016-04-26-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="F-117N_.22Seahawk.22">F-117N "Seahawk"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title='Edit section: F-117N "Seahawk"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> tested the F-117 in 1984 but decided that it was not suitable for use on an aircraft carrier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmorep2003_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-crickmorep2003-26">[26]</a></sup> In the early 1990s, Lockheed proposed an upgraded, carrier-capable variant of the F-117 dubbed the "Seahawk" to the Navy as an alternative to the canceled A/F-X program. The unsolicited proposal was received poorly by the Department of Defense, which had little interest in the single mission capabilities of such an aircraft, particularly as it would take money away from the Joint Advanced Strike Technology program, which evolved into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Strike_Fighter" title="Joint Strike Fighter">Joint Strike Fighter</a>. The new aircraft would have differed from the land-based F-117 in several ways, including the addition "of elevators, a bubble canopy, a less sharply swept wing and reconfigured tail".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-not_inter_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-not_inter-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-f-117a9_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-f-117a9-81">[81]</a></sup> The "N" variant would also be re-engined to use <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_F414" title="General Electric F414">General Electric F414</a> turbofans instead of the older <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_F404" title="General Electric F404">General Electric F404s</a>. Furthermore, the aircraft would be optionally fitted with hardpoints, allowing for an additional 8,000 lb (3,600 kg) of payload, and a new ground attack radar with air-to-air capability. In that role the F-117N could carry <a href="/wiki/AIM-120_AMRAAM" title="AIM-120 AMRAAM">AIM-120 AMRAAM</a> air-to-air missiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-not_inter_80-1"><a href="#cite_note-not_inter-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-new_f117n_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-new_f117n-82">[82]</a></sup></p>
<p>After being rebuffed by the Navy, Lockheed submitted an updated proposal that included afterburning capability and a larger emphasis on the F-117N as a multi-mission aircraft, rather than just an attack aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-new_f117n_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-new_f117n-82">[82]</a></sup> In efforts to boost interest, Lockheed also proposed an <i>F-117B</i> land-based variant that shared most of the F-117N capabilities. This variant was proposed to the USAF and the Royal Air Force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-international_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-international-83">[83]</a></sup> (Several RAF exchange officers flew the F-117 during its service, two RAF pilots formally evaluated the aircraft in 1986 as a reward for British help with the <a href="/wiki/1986_United_States_bombing_of_Libya" title="1986 United States bombing of Libya">American bombing of Libya</a> that year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmorep2003_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-crickmorep2003-26">[26]</a></sup> and the British declined an offer during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reagan_administration" title="Reagan administration">Reagan administration</a> to purchase the aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogoway20170103_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-rogoway20170103-84">[84]</a></sup>) This renewed F-117N proposal was also known as the <i>A/F-117X</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aerospace_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-aerospace-85">[85]</a></sup> Neither the F-117N or the F-117B was ordered.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_accidents">Notable accidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Notable accidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>F-117, Air Force Serial Number 79-0785, was lost on 20 April 1982 during takeoff on its maiden flight. The cause was incorrect assembly following a major design change to the flight control computer's inputs sequence, which differed from that of previous aircraft. As a result, the aircraft went out of control on takeoff, and crashed. Pilot Robert L. Riedenauer was unable to eject in time, and was seriously injured, requiring eight months of hospitalization and was forced to retire from flying. The battered airframe was taken to Skunk Works plant at Burbank, California for use as a functional engineering testbed for component testing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-11"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>F-117, AF Ser. No. 80-0792, was lost on 11 July 1986 near <a href="/wiki/Bakersfield,_California" title="Bakersfield, California">Bakersfield, California</a>. The aircraft suffered a <a href="/wiki/Controlled_flight_into_terrain" title="Controlled flight into terrain">controlled descent into terrain</a>, and was destroyed on impact. The pilot, Maj Ross E. Mulhare, was killed in the crash. The cause was determined to be <a href="/wiki/Spatial_disorientation" title="Spatial disorientation">spatial disorientation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-12"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>F-117, AF Ser. No. 85-0815 was lost on 14 October 1987. The aircraft suffered a controlled descent into terrain, and was destroyed on impact about 100 miles north of <a href="/wiki/Nellis_Air_Force_Base" title="Nellis Air Force Base">Nellis Air Force Base</a>, just east of Tonopah. The pilot, Maj Michael C. Stewart, was killed in the crash. The cause was determined to be spatial disorientation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-13"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>F-117, AF Ser. No. 82-0801, <i>Perpetrator</i> was lost on 4 August 1992. The aircraft crashed approximately eight miles northeast of <a href="/wiki/Holloman_Air_Force_Base" title="Holloman Air Force Base">Holloman Air Force Base</a>, and was destroyed on impact. The pilot, Capt John B. Mills, 416th FS, ejected safely. The cause was determined to be an improperly installed bleed air duct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-14"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>F-117, AF Ser. No. 86-0822, was lost on 10 May 1995. The aircraft suffered a controlled descent into terrain, and was destroyed on impact approximately seven miles south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zuni,_New_Mexico" title="Zuni, New Mexico">Zuni</a>, New Mexico on the <a href="/wiki/Zuni_Indian_Reservation" title="Zuni Indian Reservation">Zuni Indian Reservation</a>. The pilot, Capt Kenneth Levens, was killed in the crash. The cause was determined to be spatial disorientation following autopilot failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-15"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
<li>F-117, AF Ser. No. 81-0793, was lost on 14 September 1997 during an airshow at <a href="/wiki/Martin_State_Airport" title="Martin State Airport">Martin State Airport</a> / <a href="/wiki/Warfield_Air_National_Guard_Base" title="Warfield Air National Guard Base">Warfield Air National Guard Base</a> in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a>, Maryland. The aircraft suffered a catastrophic wing failure, resulting in the port wing completely separating from the fuselage. The aircraft was destroyed on impact. The pilot ejected safely. The cause was determined to be missing wing bolts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logan.2C_Don_2009_52-16"><a href="#cite_note-Logan.2C_Don_2009-52">[52]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nicknames">Nicknames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_F-117_Nighthawk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Nicknames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The aircraft's official name is "Night Hawk",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dtic10_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-dtic10-90">[90]</a></sup> however the alternative form "Nighthawk" is frequently used.</p>
<p>As it prioritized stealth over aerodynamics, it earned the nickname "Wobblin' Goblin" due to its alleged instability at low speeds; according to F-117 pilots, the nickname is undeserved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airforce-magazine11_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-airforce-magazine11-91">[91]</a></sup> "Wobblin' (or Wobbly) Goblin" is likely a holdover from the early <i>Have Blue</i> / <i>Senior Trend</i> (FSD) days of the project when instability was a problem. In the USAF, "Goblin" (without wobbly) persists as a nickname because of the aircraft's appearance. During <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Desert_Storm" title="Operation Desert Storm">Operation Desert Storm</a>, Saudis dubbed the aircraft "Shaba", which is Arabic for "Ghost".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-defensemedianetwork_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-defensemedianetwork-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
