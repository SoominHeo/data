<p><b>Feldgrau</b> (English: field-grey, also field grey) has been the official basic color of military <a href="/wiki/Uniform" title="Uniform">uniforms</a> of the German armed forces since the early 20th century until 1945 or 1989 respectively. However, according to the color code there was no exact scientific definition, so slightly different grey tinctures were possible. Armed forces of other countries selected slight variations or shadows of that color according to the German <b>Feldgrau</b>. Metaphorically, <i>feldgrau</i> used to refer to the armies of Germany (the <a href="/wiki/German_Army_(German_Empire)" title="German Army (German Empire)">Imperial German Army</a> and the <i>Heer</i> [en: ground forces, or army] component of the <i>Reichswehr</i> and the <i>Wehrmacht</i>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abstracts">Abstracts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feldgrau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Abstracts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Colloquially, today <i>feldgrau</i> means plainly <i>the color of the German uniform</i>, especially for the time of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, but also for the East German <a href="/wiki/National_People%27s_Army" title="National People's Army">National People's Army</a> (here under the description <b>steingrau</b> [en: stone-grey, also stonegrey] ). Certainly, <b>feldgrau</b> was introduced to the Austrian <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Armed_Forces" title="Austrian Armed Forces">Bundesheer</a> (en: Federal Army) in line to the German pattern as well.</p>
<p>The word <i>feldgrau</i> means "field grey", and by <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> the color was a light grey-green, though there is no specific color, rather a color range of greys to browns, that was one of the first standardized uniforms suitable to the age of smokeless gunpowder.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feldgrau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1910 the so-called <b>field-grey peace uniform</b> (de: <i>feldgraue Friedensuniform</i>), with colored cuffs, facings, shoulder straps and gorgets was disposed by decree in <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>, followed by all other German countries and armies, last by the <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_Army" title="Bavarian Army">Bavarian Army</a> in April 1916. Formerly, the Germans wore a Prussian blue shade similar to that of the <a href="/wiki/French_Army" title="French Army">French</a>.</p>
<p>Simultaneous it characterised the end of a variety of different colored uniform in German states. With that new unique “field-grey peace uniform” the <i>Imperial German Army</i> (de: Deutsches Heer) started military campaigns in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.</p>
<p>Other countries selected <b>feldgrau</b> as basic color for uniforms, shoulder straps, sleeve insignia, or pieces of equipment etc. as well. So was <b>feldgrau</b> introduced by the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Armed_Forces" title="Swedish Armed Forces">Swedish Armed Forces</a> in 1923 in line to the German pattern.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Austria">Austria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feldgrau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Austria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>With formation of the Austrian 1st Federation's Armed Forces in 1929/21 (de: <i>Österreichisches Bundesheer der ersten Republik</i>, lit.: Austrian Federal Army of the first Republic) there was a close orientation to Germany. For instance the <b>feldgrau</b> uniform (providing some camouflage features) and the corps colors of rank insignia adopted. However, slightly different grey shades were possible as well. Pike-grey (de: <i>Hechtgrau</i>, see file "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austrian_service_cap" title="Austrian service cap">Austrian service cap</a> in 1918") did not prove to provide the anticipated camouflage features.</p>
<p>Today, in line to the national traditions, the textile color of the Austrian 2nd Federation's Armed Forces (de: <i>Österreichisches Bundesheer der zweiten Republik</i>, lit.: Austrian Federal Army of the second Republic) is named <b>feldgrau</b> (also <b>braungrau</b> <small>[en: brown-grey]</small> - uniform jacket), and <b>steingrau</b> (also <b>steingrau-oliv</b> <small>[en: stone-grey-olive]</small>, or more popularly <b>NATO-oliv</b> <small>[en: NATO-olive]</small> - uniform trousers).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chile">Chile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feldgrau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Chile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Chilean Army also wears a full dress uniform in <b>feldgrau</b>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feldgrau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Armed_Forces" title="Swedish Armed Forces">Swedish Armed Forces</a> used a very similar color for infantry uniforms, for example the grey <a href="/wiki/File:Vapenrock_m1939.jpg" title="File:Vapenrock m1939.jpg">m/39</a> and later on grey-green as the German ones. The last uniform to use the color was the woollen <a href="/wiki/File:Vapenrock_m1958.jpg" title="File:Vapenrock m1958.jpg">m/58</a> winter uniform.</p>
