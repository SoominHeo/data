<p>The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <b>Yakovlev Yak-7</b> was developed from the earlier <a href="/wiki/Yakovlev_Yak-1" title="Yakovlev Yak-1">Yak-1</a> <a href="/wiki/Fighter_aircraft" title="Fighter aircraft">fighter</a>, initially as a <a href="/wiki/Trainer_(aircraft)" title="Trainer (aircraft)">trainer</a> but converted into a fighter. As both a fighter and later reverting to its original training role, the Yak-7 proved to be a capable aircraft and was well liked by air crews. The Yak-7 was simpler, tougher and generally better than the Yak-1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_p._122._2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_p._122.-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yakovlev_Yak-7&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1939, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Sergeevich_Yakovlev" title="Alexander Sergeevich Yakovlev">Alexander Yakovlev</a> designed a tandem-seat advanced trainer, originally designated "I-27" and then "UTI-26", offered along with the original I-26 proposal that became the Yak-1. The "UTI" (<i>Uchebno Trenirovochnyi Istrebitel</i>, translated as: Training Fighter) was intended to give <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">pilots</a>-in-training experience on a high-performance aircraft before transitioning to a fighter. With development work started in 1940, the UTI-26 differed from its predecessor in its larger span wing being placed farther back for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centre_of_gravity" title="Centre of gravity">balance</a> as well as having two <a href="/wiki/Cockpit" title="Cockpit">cockpits</a> with dual controls and a rudimentary communication system. It was armed with a single 7.62 mm (0.30 in) <a href="/wiki/ShKAS_machine_gun" title="ShKAS machine gun">ShKAS machine gun</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cowling" title="Cowling">cowling</a>, mainly for use in training, but Yakovlev envisioned a multi-purpose aircraft that could also undertake courier and light transport duties at the front.</p>
<p>The first production aircraft known as Yak-7UTIs retained a retractable main <a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">landing gear</a>, but beginning in the summer of 1941, a fixed landing gear variant, the Yak-7V (Vyvozoni for Familiarization) was substituted. The factory reasoned that production would be simplified and that reduced performance would not be detrimental for a trainer. Yak-7UTIs and Yak-7Vs were also equipped with skis for winter operations.</p>
<p>A factory team from N° 301, headed by K.A. Sinelshchikov, was detached from the <a href="/wiki/OKB" title="OKB">Yakovlev OKB</a> to supervise production of Yak-7UTI. One of these aircraft (serial number 04-11) was fitted with an <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_armour" title="Vehicle armour">armored</a> backrest plate over the rear position, <a href="/wiki/Self-sealing_fuel_tank" title="Self-sealing fuel tank">self-sealing fuel tanks</a> which filled with inert gas as they emptied, three "RO" rocket launchers under each wing for as many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RS-82" title="RS-82">RS-82</a> rockets, an axial <i>motornaya pushka</i>-mount 20 mm (0.79 in) <a href="/wiki/ShVAK_cannon" title="ShVAK cannon">ShVAK cannon</a> firing through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propeller_(aircraft)" title="Propeller (aircraft)">propeller</a> spinner, with 120 rounds, and two 7.62 mm ShKAS machine guns under the <a href="/wiki/Cowling" title="Cowling">cowling</a>, each with 750 rounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leonard_p._60._3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leonard_p._60.-3">[3]</a></sup> The rear <a href="/wiki/Cockpit" title="Cockpit">cockpit</a> position was retained, allowing it to accommodate a second seat (without controls) for fast courier and transport duties or a fuel tank for extended range. The additional space could also house bombs or other gear. The engine was an M-105P and the model was designated <b>Yak-7/M-105P</b>.</p>
<p>Sinelshchikov did not inform Yakovlev about the conversion and when he learned about it, Yakovlev remained sceptical of the need for the changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leonard_p._60._3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leonard_p._60.-3">[3]</a></sup> After brief factory trials, the aircraft turned out to be better than the single-seat Yak-1, thanks to the modifications already applied to the UTI as well as revised <a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">undercarriage</a> with bigger tires and wheels, more efficient brakes and revised elevators, among other changes. Yakovlev submitted the Yak-7 to the authorities who approved it immediately. The firing tests at the scientific trials Polygon for aircraft armament (NIPAV) were a success and the armament was found to have no effect on the flight characteristics or the general performances of the new fighter. The aircraft's stability as a firing platform was judged far better than that of the Yak-1, the <a href="/wiki/Lavochkin-Gorbunov-Gudkov_LaGG-3" title="Lavochkin-Gorbunov-Gudkov LaGG-3">Lavochkin-Gorbunov-Gudkov LaGG-3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-3" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-3">Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-3</a>.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GKO" title="GKO">GKO</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NKAP" title="NKAP">NKAP</a> issued decrees in August 1941 for the Yak-7 to be produced by Factories N°301 and N°153, but the Factory 301 had to be evacuated to <a href="/wiki/Novosibirsk" title="Novosibirsk">Novosibirsk</a> where it merged with N° 153. So, just 62 aircraft were produced in 1941: 51 in September–October by Factory N° 301 and 11 by N°153 in December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leonard_p._60._3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Leonard_p._60.-3">[3]</a></sup> Test pilot A.N. Lazarev noted the good flight characteristics, how easy it was to get out of a spin, how well it behaved when diving, characteristics that he considered safer than those of the Yak-1. But the Yak-7 showed some defects: the M-105P engine piping, the landing gear locking system, the tires and the tail wheel were identified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leonard_pp._60-61._4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leonard_pp._60-61.-4">[4]</a></sup> The Yak-7 was introduced into the production line and the first batch of 60 reached operational squadrons by the end of 1941.</p>
<p>Another important variant was the Yak-7/M-105PA. On this model, the two ShKAS on the cowling had been replaced by two 12.7 mm (0.50 in) UBS machine guns with 400 rounds (260 for the left and 160 for the right). It was powered by an M-105PA engine with an axial ShVAK cannon with 120 rounds. Oil and glycol radiators had been widened and refined and slightly tilted downward. The insulation of the airframe was improved, tail wheel was totally retractable; joints and skin were more carefully made; panels on engine cowling fitted better; the propeller reduction gear worked better; an electro-pneumatic reloading system was installed; canopy frame was reinforced. The Yak-7B made 27 tests flights in January and February 1942. The reports noted that while the aircraft "was not inferior to the LaGG-3 and MiG-3 and to foreign fighters in service in the USSR", it was more stable and had better flight characteristics. Subsequently, the GKO authorised production at Factory N°153 in place of the Yak-7A, from April 1942 and 261 machines were built until July. After 20 May, the aircraft were equipped with a 68 l (18 US gal) tank behind the pilot’s seat, but the pilots that used the Yak-7 on Stalingrad and on the Kuban removed it as it was not protected and affected the flight characteristics. Generally, the Yak-7B pleased its pilots. They reported that it was easy to fly at all altitudes, stable and easy to maintain and although it did not climb as quickly as a <a href="/wiki/Messerschmitt_Bf_109" title="Messerschmitt Bf 109">Messerschmitt Bf 109</a>, it was as manoeuvrable and fast, except in the vertical plane. But defects were also noted: there was too much drag from the <a href="/wiki/Radiator" title="Radiator">radiators</a>, the canopy glass was of bad quality; the pilot was not protected enough, taking-off and landing distances were too long and, above all, it was underpowered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leonard_p._78._5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leonard_p._78.-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Yakovlev suggested Klimov, the engine builder, some modifications that resulted in the M-105PF which was 130 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">hp</a> (97 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">kW</a>) more powerful. With this modified engine, the Yak-7B top speed was of 599 km/h (372 mph), it climbed much faster up to 5,000 m (16,404 ft) and it was more manoeuvrable both in the horizontal and the vertical planes. But because the rear tank was removed, its range was reduced and the CG was moved too forward, while M-105 defects (glycol and oil overheating, oil leaks etc.) persisted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leonard_p._79._1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leonard_p._79.-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Among the engine and armament options was the <b>Yak-7-37</b> fitted with a 37 mm (1.457 in) <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=MPSh-37&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="MPSh-37 (page does not exist)">MPSh-37</a> cannon, (MPSh - <i>motornaya pushka Shpital'novo</i> - engine mounted Shpital'nyy cannon), mounted between the engine cylinder blocks, firing through the propeller spinner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GordonYAK_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-GordonYAK-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yakovlev_Yak-7&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Yak-7 proved to be an effective close support fighter although the first two-seaters were considered nose-heavy, consequently, the factory introduced a rear cockpit fuel tank. Pilots complained about the fuel tank's vulnerability since it was unarmored, and it was usually removed in the field. There were constant changes to the design based on combat observations including a definitive single-seat variant, the <b>Yak-7B</b>, which was produced in large numbers.</p>
<p>After the war, some Yak-7V trainers were provided to the Poles and a single Yak-7V was delivered to the Hungarians for familiarization with the Yak-9 fighter.</p>
<p>After trials in April–May 1942, a small batch of 22 Yak-7-37s was authorised, all of which were issued to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=42nd_IAP&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="42nd IAP (page does not exist)">42nd IAP</a> at the North-Western front, where they proved highly successful both in air-to-air combat and ground attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GordonYAK_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-GordonYAK-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<dt>Yak-7-37</dt>
<dd>After trials in April–May 1942, a small batch of 22 Yak-7-37s was authorised, fitted with a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=MPSh-37&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="MPSh-37 (page does not exist)">MPSh-37</a> 37 mm (1.457 in) cannon, (MPSh - <i>motornaya pushka Shpital'novo</i> - engine mounted Shpital'nyy cannon), mounted between the engine cylinder blocks, firing through the propeller spinner. Two 12.7 mm (0.500 in) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UBS_machine_gun" title="UBS machine gun">UBS machine guns</a> were also fitted in the forward fuselage firing above the engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GordonYAK_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-GordonYAK-6">[6]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Yak-7A</dt>
<dd>improved Yak-7, about 300 built in early 1942</dd>
<dt>Yak-7B</dt>
<dd>upgraded version of Yak-7A (reduced wingspan, simplified landing gear, better equipment), about 5,000 were built.</dd>
<dt>Yak-7D</dt>
<dd>long range prototype, development basis for the <a href="/wiki/Yakovlev_Yak-9" title="Yakovlev Yak-9">Yak-7DI/Yak-9</a>.</dd>
<dt>Yak-7K <i>courier</i></dt>
<dd>VIP transport version. converted from Yak-7B, 1944.</dd>
<dt>Yak-7U Mark</dt>
<dt>Yak-7PVRD</dt>
<dd>experimental - had two DM-4 <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjet</a> under wings, top speed: 800 km/h (500 mph). Two were built.</dd>
<dt>Yak-7DI</dt>
<dd>long range fighter, redesignated <a href="/wiki/Yakovlev_Yak-9" title="Yakovlev Yak-9">Yak-9</a>.</dd>
<dt>Yak-7 M-82</dt>
<dd>- new (M-82) engine version. tested in 1941.</dd>
<dt>Yak-7R</dt>
<dd>Jet project with one liquid fuel jet and two <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjets</a>. 1942.</dd>
<dt>Yak-7R</dt>
<dd>purported jet version of Yak-7 with <a href="/wiki/Junkers_Jumo_004" title="Junkers Jumo 004">Jumo 004</a> engine. Said to have been built in Tbilisi to fly over Red Square at the parade in 1947.</dd>
<dt>Yak-7R</dt>
<dd>Yak-3 with <a href="/wiki/Junkers_Jumo_004" title="Junkers Jumo 004">Jumo 004</a> turbojet. Development started not later than <a href="/wiki/1945_in_aviation" title="1945 in aviation">1945</a>. First flown in 1946?</dd>
<dt>Yak-7T</dt>
<dd>two aircraft for testing engine mounted heavy cannons (NS-37 and NS-45 37 mm (1.457 in) and 45 mm (1.772 in) calibre respectively).</dd>
</dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications_.28Yak-7A_M-105PA.29">Specifications (Yak-7A M-105PA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yakovlev_Yak-7&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Specifications (Yak-7A M-105PA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p style="font-size: 90%; margin:0;"><i>Data from</i> OKB Yakovlev<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GordonYAK_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-GordonYAK-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crew:</b> 1</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 8.48 m (27 ft 10 in)</li>
<li><b>Wingspan:</b> 10 m (32 ft 10 in)</li>
<li><b>Wing area:</b> 17.15 m<sup>2</sup> (184.6 sq ft)</li>
<li><b>Empty weight:</b> 2,450 kg (5,401 lb)</li>
<li><b>Gross weight:</b> 2,935 kg (6,471 lb)</li>
<li><b>Powerplant:</b> 1 × <a href="/wiki/Klimov_M-105" title="Klimov M-105">M-105PA</a> V-12 liquid-cooled piston engine, 780 kW (1,050 hp) at rated altitude</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Maximum speed:</b> 495 km/h (308 mph; 267 kn) at sea level<br/></li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>571 km/h (355 mph; 308 kn) at 5,000 m (16,000 ft)</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li><b>Range:</b> 643 km (400 mi; 347 nmi)</li>
<li><b>Service ceiling:</b> 9,500 m (31,168 ft)</li>
<li><b>Rate of climb:</b> 12 m/s (2,400 ft/min)</li>
<li><b>Time to altitude:</b> 5,000 m (16,000 ft) in 6.4 minutes</li>
<li><b>Wing loading:</b> 172.6 kg/m<sup>2</sup> (35.4 lb/sq ft)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Power-to-weight_ratio" title="Power-to-weight ratio">Power/mass</a>:</b> 0.26 kW/kg (0.16 hp/lb)<br/></li>
<li><b>Take-off run:</b> 410 m (1,350 ft)</li>
<li><b>Landing run:</b> 610 m (2,000 ft)</li>
<li><b>Sustained turn time:</b> 21 - 22 seconds</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Armament</b></span><br/></p>
<ul>
<li>1 × 20 mm (0.787 in) <a href="/wiki/ShVAK_cannon" title="ShVAK cannon">ShVAK cannon</a></li>
<li>2 × 7.62 mm (0.300 in) <a href="/wiki/ShKAS_machine_gun" title="ShKAS machine gun">ShKAS machine guns</a>. Later models like the "B", used x2 12.7mm <a href="/wiki/Berezin_UB" title="Berezin UB">Berezin UB</a> guns.</li>
</ul>
