<p><b>Yakov Grigorevich Sinai</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Я́ков Григо́рьевич Сина́й</span>; born September 21, 1935) is a <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> known for his work on <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_system" title="Dynamical system">dynamical systems</a>. He contributed to the modern metric theory of dynamical systems and connected the world of deterministic (dynamical) systems with the world of probabilistic (stochastic) systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-1">[1]</a></sup> He has also worked on <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_physics" title="Mathematical physics">mathematical physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">probability theory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemmers_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nemmers-2">[2]</a></sup> His efforts have provided the groundwork for advances in the physical sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sinai has won several awards, including the <a href="/wiki/Nemmers_Prize_in_Mathematics" title="Nemmers Prize in Mathematics">Nemmers Prize</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Prize_in_Mathematics" title="Wolf Prize in Mathematics">Wolf Prize in Mathematics</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Abel_Prize" title="Abel Prize">Abel Prize</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yakov_Sinai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yakov Grigorevich Sinai was born into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Jewish" title="Russian Jewish">Russian Jewish</a> academic family on September 21, 1935, in Moscow, <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> (now Russia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abel_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abel-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> His parents, Nadezda Kagan and Gregory Sinai, were both microbiologists. His grandfather, <a href="/wiki/Veniamin_Kagan" title="Veniamin Kagan">Veniamin Kagan</a>, headed the Department of Differential Geometry at <a href="/wiki/Moscow_State_University" title="Moscow State University">Moscow State University</a> and was a major influence on Sinai's life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abel_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Abel-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sinai received his bachelor's and master's degrees from Moscow State University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemmers_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nemmers-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1960, he earned his Ph.D., also from Moscow State; his adviser was <a href="/wiki/Andrey_Kolmogorov" title="Andrey Kolmogorov">Andrey Kolmogorov</a>. Together with Kolmogorov, he showed that even for "unpredictable" dynamic systems, the level of unpredictability of motion can be described mathematically. In their idea, which became known as <a href="/wiki/Measure-preserving_dynamical_system#Measure-theoretic_entropy" title="Measure-preserving dynamical system">Kolmogorov–Sinai entropy</a>, a system with zero entropy is entirely predictable, while a system with non-zero entropy has an unpredictability factor directly related to the amount of entropy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1963, Sinai introduced the idea of <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_billiards" title="Dynamical billiards">dynamical billiards</a>, also known as "Sinai Billiards". In this idealized system, a particle bounces around inside a square boundary without loss of energy. Inside the square is a circular wall, of which the particle also bounces off. He then proved that for most initial trajectories of the ball, this system is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergodic" title="Ergodic">ergodic</a>, that is, after a long time, the amount of that time the ball will have spent in any given region on the surface of the table is approximately proportional to the area of that region. It was the first time anyone proved a dynamic system was ergodic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>From 1960 to 1971, Sinai was a researcher in the Laboratory of Probabilistic and Statistical Methods at Moscow State University. In 1971, he was promoted to professor and named a senior researcher at the <a href="/wiki/Landau_Institute_for_Theoretical_Physics" title="Landau Institute for Theoretical Physics">Landau Institute for Theoretical Physics</a> in Russia. Since 1993, Sinai has been a professor of mathematics at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>, while maintaining his position at the Landau Institute. For the 1997–98 academic year, he was the Thomas Jones Professor at Princeton. In 2005, the Moore Distinguished Scholar at the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abel_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Abel-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sinai has supported the poet, mathematician and human rights activist <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Esenin-Volpin" title="Alexander Esenin-Volpin">Alexander Esenin-Volpin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002, Sinai won the <a href="/wiki/Nemmers_Prize_in_Mathematics" title="Nemmers Prize in Mathematics">Nemmers Prize</a> for his "revolutionizing" work on dynamical systems, statistical mechanics, probability theory, and statistical physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemmers_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nemmers-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2005, the <i><a href="/wiki/Moscow_Mathematical_Journal" title="Moscow Mathematical Journal">Moscow Mathematical Journal</a></i> dedicated an issue to Sinai writing "Yakov Sinai is one of the greatest mathematicians of our time ... his exceptional scientific enthusiasm inspire[d] several generations of scientists all over the world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abel_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Abel-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2013, Sinai received the <a href="/wiki/Leroy_P._Steele_Prize" title="Leroy P. Steele Prize">Leroy P. Steele Prize</a> for Lifetime Achievement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abel_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Abel-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Academy_of_Science_and_Letters" title="Norwegian Academy of Science and Letters">Norwegian Academy of Science and Letters</a> awarded him the <a href="/wiki/Abel_Prize" title="Abel Prize">Abel Prize</a>, for his contributions to dynamical systems, <a href="/wiki/Ergodic_theory" title="Ergodic theory">ergodic theory</a>, and mathematical physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Presenting the award, <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Ellenberg" title="Jordan Ellenberg">Jordan Ellenberg</a> said Sinai had solved real world physical problems "with the soul of a mathematician".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-1">[1]</a></sup> He praised the tools developed by Sinai which demonstrate how systems that look different may in fact have fundamental similarities. The prize comes with 6 million <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_krone" title="Norwegian krone">Norwegian krone</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-1">[1]</a></sup> equivalent at the time to $US 1 million or £600,000. He was also inducted into the Norwegian Academy of Science and Letters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other awards won by Sinai include the <a href="/wiki/Boltzmann_Medal" title="Boltzmann Medal">Boltzmann Medal</a> (1986), the <a href="/wiki/Dannie_Heineman_Prize_for_Mathematical_Physics" title="Dannie Heineman Prize for Mathematical Physics">Dannie Heineman Prize for Mathematical Physics</a> (1990), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dirac_Prize" title="Dirac Prize">Dirac Prize</a> (1992), the <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Prize_in_Mathematics" title="Wolf Prize in Mathematics">Wolf Prize in Mathematics</a> (1997), the <a href="/wiki/Lagrange_Prize" title="Lagrange Prize">Lagrange Prize</a> (2008) and the <a href="/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9_Prize" title="Henri Poincaré Prize">Henri Poincaré Prize</a> (2009).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemmers_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nemmers-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abel_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Abel-3">[3]</a></sup> He is a member of the United States <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Russian Academy of Sciences">Russian Academy of Sciences</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Hungarian Academy of Sciences">Hungarian Academy of Sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemmers_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nemmers-2">[2]</a></sup> He is an honorary member of the <a href="/wiki/London_Mathematical_Society" title="London Mathematical Society">London Mathematical Society</a> and, in 2012, he became a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/American_Mathematical_Society" title="American Mathematical Society">American Mathematical Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemmers_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Nemmers-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Sinai has been selected an honorary member of the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Brazilian Academy of Sciences">Brazilian Academy of Sciences</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Academia_Europaea" title="Academia Europaea">Academia Europaea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Polish_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Polish Academy of Sciences">Polish Academy of Sciences</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> of London. He holds honorary degrees from the <a href="/wiki/Budapest_University_of_Technology_and_Economics" title="Budapest University of Technology and Economics">Budapest University of Technology and Economics</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_University_of_Jerusalem" title="Hebrew University of Jerusalem">Hebrew University of Jerusalem</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warwick_University" title="Warwick University">Warwick University</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warsaw_University" title="Warsaw University">Warsaw University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abel_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Abel-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sinai has authored more than 250 papers and books. Concepts in mathematics named after him include Sinai's random walk, Sinai–Ruelle–Bowen measures, and Pirogov–Sinai theory. Sinai has overseen more than 50 Ph.D. candidates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abel_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Abel-3">[3]</a></sup> He has spoken at the <a href="/wiki/International_Congress_of_Mathematicians" title="International Congress of Mathematicians">International Congress of Mathematicians</a> four times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nemmers_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Nemmers-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2000, he was a plenary speaker at the First Latin American Congress in Mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abel_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Abel-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sinai is married to mathematician and physicist Elena B. Vul. The couple have written several joint papers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abel_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-Abel-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
