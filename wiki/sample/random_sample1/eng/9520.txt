<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Yakusugi</h1>

<p><b>Yakusugi</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">屋久杉</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> refers to "Japanese cedar" (sugi, or <i><a href="/wiki/Cryptomeria" title="Cryptomeria">Cryptomeria</a></i>) on the island of <a href="/wiki/Yakushima" title="Yakushima">Yakushima</a>, typically growing at altitudes 500 meters and higher. The term also extends to the lumber taken from the logging of these coniferous trees.</p>
<p>In the strict sense, the term <i>Yakusugi</i> is reserved for trees aged 1,000 years or more, and younger trees are referred to as <i>kosugi</i> ("small cedars"). Replanted cedars are called <i>jisugi</i> ("local cedars") by the Yakushima islanders, although <i>jisugi</i> also refers to trees less than a century old.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outline">Outline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yakusugi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Outline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In general, the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Cryptomeria" title="Cryptomeria">cedar</a> lives for about 500 years, but yakusugi trees live much longer. They grow on less nutritious <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> soil slowly and have a very tight grain. The wood contains a lot of resin due to <a href="/wiki/Yakushima" title="Yakushima">Yakushima</a>'s high rainfall and high humidity, making it resistant to rotting. As a result, these trees tend to have longer lives, and many larger trees have survived for more than 2,000 years. Famous examples include the <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Dmon_Sugi" title="Jōmon Sugi">Jōmon Sugi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kigen" title="Kigen">Kigen</a>-sugi and <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Henry_Wilson" title="Ernest Henry Wilson">Wilson</a> stump.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Logging">Logging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yakusugi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Logging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A stone monument from <a href="/wiki/Kagoshima_Shrine" title="Kagoshima Shrine">Kagoshima Shrine</a> states that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugi" title="Sugi">sugi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hinoki" title="Hinoki">hinoki</a> lumber from <a href="/wiki/Yakushima" title="Yakushima">Yakushima</a> were transported here to rebuild the shrine around 1560. This is the earliest known record of yakusugi being used for <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a>.</p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Toyotomi_Hideyoshi" title="Toyotomi Hideyoshi">Hideyoshi</a>'s subjugation of <a href="/wiki/Kyushu" title="Kyushu">Kyushu</a>, his <a href="/wiki/Go-Bugy%C5%8D" title="Go-Bugyō">commissioner</a> <a href="/wiki/Ishida_Mitsunari" title="Ishida Mitsunari">Ishida Mitsunari</a> ordered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daimyo" title="Daimyo">daimyo</a> <a href="/wiki/Shimazu_Yoshihisa" title="Shimazu Yoshihisa">Shimazu Yoshihisa</a> who governed Kagoshima (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satsuma_province" title="Satsuma province">Satsuma province</a>) to conduct a survey of timber resources found in Yakushima, some time around 1590. Yakushima <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timbers</a> carried on 11 vessels from <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Ddoshima" title="Shōdoshima">Shōdoshima</a> (an island in the <a href="/wiki/Seto_Inland_Sea" title="Seto Inland Sea">Inland Sea</a>) were sent to Kyoto for erecting the <i><a href="/wiki/Daibutsu" title="Daibutsu">Daibutsu</a>-den</i> or "Great Buddha Hall" at the <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dk%C5%8D-ji_(Kyoto)" title="Hōkō-ji (Kyoto)">Hōkō-ji</a> temple built by Hideyoshi. The "Wilson stump" is considered the result of logging at this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnonymous1987.27.22Yakushima:_Cedarn_Isle.22.2C_pp.43.E2.80.9346_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnonymous1987.27.22Yakushima:_Cedarn_Isle.22.2C_pp.43.E2.80.9346-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a> (1603–1868), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tomari_Jochiku&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tomari Jochiku (page does not exist)">Tomari Jochiku</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nichiren" title="Nichiren">Nichiren</a> sect <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian scholar</a> born in Yakushima sought to lift his fellow islanders from economic plight by submitting a proposal to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satsuma_domain" title="Satsuma domain">Satsuma domain</a> whom he served, begging to allow commercial exploitation of the cedar on the island. Full-scale logging began around 1640.</p>
<p>Yakusugi was used in shipbuilding and architecture, although the bulk of them were manufactured into roof tiles called <i>hiragi</i> (lit. "flat wood"). The islanders sent <i>hiragi</i> to the Satsuma domain as annual tribute. The surplus yakusugi beyond the tribute quota were purchased by the Satsuma clan in exchange for rice and other supplies.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">Meiji</a> government (1868–1912) prohibited residents from cutting trees. The islanders sued unsuccessfully to gain back the woods, now designated [[Ja<sup class="plainlinks noprint Inline-Template" style="vertical-align:text-top;white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="external text" href="http://dispenser.homenet.org/~dispenser/cgi-bin/dab_solver.py?page=Yakusugi&amp;editintro=Template:Disambiguation_needed/editintro&amp;client=Template:Dn" rel="nofollow"><span title="Link needs disambiguation. (October 2015)">disambiguation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>]]<span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> ([[:日本の国有林:Ja<sup class="plainlinks noprint Inline-Template" style="vertical-align:text-top;white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="external text" href="http://dispenser.homenet.org/~dispenser/cgi-bin/dab_solver.py?page=Yakusugi&amp;editintro=Template:Disambiguation_needed/editintro&amp;client=Template:Dn" rel="nofollow"><span title="Link needs disambiguation. (October 2015)">disambiguation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>|日本の国有林]])</span>. But ensuing poverty in Yakushima prompted the national Forestry Bureau's local office to announce "the plan for forest management in Yakushima" in 1921. Some of the main points included:</p>
<ul>
<li>The front of the mountain is solely for local profit.</li>
<li>Local people may harvest trees for <a href="/wiki/Firewood" title="Firewood">firewood</a> and charcoal.</li>
<li>Local hiring for logging, forestation, and gathering <i><a href="/wiki/Trochodendron" title="Trochodendron">Trochodendron</a></i> wood, the natural source of <a href="/wiki/Birdlime" title="Birdlime">birdlime</a>.</li>
<li>Government funded <a href="/wiki/Gravel_road" title="Gravel road">gravel roads</a> around <a href="/wiki/Yakushima" title="Yakushima">Yakushima</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The court ruling finalized the decision to nationalize the forest, but logging commenced full swing. By 2001, all of the old growth wood in the National Forest had been cut down except in the protected zones, and commercial logging of natural yakusugi cedars has come to an end.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Domaiboku"><i>Domaiboku</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yakusugi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Domaiboku">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since it is no longer permitted to cut Yakusugi today, <a href="/wiki/Souvenir" title="Souvenir">souvenirs</a> etc. are made from stumps of previously felled trees and trees that fell naturally in <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoons</a>. These trees are called <i>domaiboku</i>, which literally means buried trees in the ground.</p>
