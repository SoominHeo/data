<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Yakiniku</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Yakiniku</b> (焼き肉 or 焼肉), meaning "<a href="/wiki/Grilling" title="Grilling">grilled</a> <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a>", is a <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> term which, in its broadest sense, refers to grilled meat dishes. "Yakiniku" originally referred to the "barbecue" of western food, which was given by Japanese writer <a href="/wiki/Kanagaki_Robun" title="Kanagaki Robun">Kanagaki Robun</a> (仮名垣魯文) in his literature, "Seiyo Ryoritsu" (meaning "western food handbook"), in 1872 (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Meiji_period" title="The Meiji period">The Meiji period</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Meat has been eaten in Japan since the <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Dmon_period" title="Jōmon period">Jōmon period</a>. However, the rise of Buddhism made the eating of meat tabooed, and consequently some people have theorized that meat "disappeared" from the table from the Middle Ages to the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The term "yakiniku" became associated with <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korean</a>-derived cuisine during the early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Showa_period" title="Showa period">Showa period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Due to the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, Korean restaurants in Japan were divided into North Korean (Kita Chōsen) and South Korean (Kankoku); "yakiniku restaurant" arose as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Politically_correct" title="Politically correct">politically correct</a> term to refer to restaurants of either type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Today, it commonly refers to a <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> style of cooking bite-sized meat (usually <a href="/wiki/Beef" title="Beef">beef</a> and <a href="/wiki/Offal" title="Offal">offal</a>) and vegetables on <a href="/wiki/Gridiron_(cooking)" title="Gridiron (cooking)">gridirons</a> or <a href="/wiki/Griddle" title="Griddle">griddles</a> over flame of wood charcoals carbonized by <a href="/wiki/Dry_distillation" title="Dry distillation">dry distillation</a> (<i>sumibi</i>, 炭火) or gas/electric grill. In North America, China, and Taiwan, <i>Yakiniku</i> is also referred to as "Japanese barbecue"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> while in Japan the origin has become a subject of debate, though it is conventionally considered to be <a href="/wiki/Korean_cuisine" title="Korean cuisine">Korean cuisine</a>. In 2002 the <a href="/wiki/NHK" title="NHK">NHK</a> program <i>NHK Ningen Kouza</i> (NHK人間講座, literally <i>NHK Humanity Lecture</i>) stated: "While some tend to think that yakiniku came from Korea, it was born in post war Japan."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Yet there are those who say that while yakiniku may have its beginnings in Japan, they believe it was first made by <a href="/wiki/Koreans_in_Japan" title="Koreans in Japan">Zainichi Koreans</a> (long-term Korean residents), and should therefore be considered Korean cuisine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title=" Dead link since December 2016">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup></p>
<p><i>Yakiniku</i> is a variant of <a href="/wiki/Bulgogi" title="Bulgogi">bulgogi</a> that has been modified by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zainichi_Korean" title="Zainichi Korean">Zainichi Koreans</a> to appeal to Japanese tastes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The present style of yakiniku restaurants are also derived from the Korean restaurants in Osaka and Tokyo which were opened around 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proper_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-proper-14">[14]</a></sup> In a yakiniku restaurant, diners order several types of prepared raw ingredients (either individually or as a set) which are brought to the table. The ingredients are cooked by the diners on a grill built into the table throughout the duration of the meal, several pieces at a time. The ingredients are then dipped in sauces known as <i><a href="/wiki/Tare_sauce" title="Tare sauce">tare</a></i> before being eaten. The most common sauce is made of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_soy_sauce" title="Japanese soy sauce">Japanese soy sauce</a> mixed with <a href="/wiki/Sake" title="Sake">sake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mirin" title="Mirin">mirin</a>, sugar, garlic, fruit juice and sesame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">Garlic</a>-and-<a href="/wiki/Shallot" title="Shallot">shallot</a> or <a href="/wiki/Miso" title="Miso">miso</a>-based dips are sometimes used. Different kinds of <a href="/wiki/Banchan" title="Banchan">Korean side dishes</a> like <a href="/wiki/Kimchi" title="Kimchi">kimchi</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nameul" title="Nameul">nameul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bibimbap" title="Bibimbap">bibimbap</a> are also served alongside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<p>After officially being prohibited for many years, eating beef was legalised in 1871 following the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> as part of an effort to introduce western culture to the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Meiji" title="Emperor Meiji">Emperor Meiji</a> became part of a campaign to promote beef consumption, publicly eating beef on January 24, 1873.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <i>Steak</i> and <i>roasted meat</i> were translated as <i>yakiniku</i> (焼肉) and <i>iriniku</i> (焙肉), respectively, as proposed western-style menus in <i>Seiyō Ryōri Shinan</i> published in 1872,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> although this usage of the former word was eventually replaced by the loanword <i>sutēki</i>.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Jingisukan" title="Jingisukan">Jingisukan</a></i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%82%B8%E3%83%B3%E3%82%AE%E3%82%B9%E3%82%AB%E3%83%B3_(%E6%96%99%E7%90%86)" title="ja:ジンギスカン (料理)">ja:ジンギスカン</a>, the Japanese transliteration of <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Genghis Khan</a>), is a style of grilling mutton, which is also referred to as a type of yakiniku. The dish was conceived in Hokkaidō, where it has ever since been a popular blue-collar dish, but has only recently gained nationwide popularity. The name Jingisukan is thought to have been invented by Sapporo-born Tokuzo Komai, who was inspired by the grilled mutton dishes of <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_Chinese_cuisine" title="Northeastern Chinese cuisine">Northeastern Chinese cuisine</a>. The first written mention of the dish under this name was in 1931.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yakiniku&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Common Japanese style of <i>Yakiniku</i>, drawing influences from Korean dishes such as <a href="/wiki/Bulgogi" title="Bulgogi">bulgogi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galbi" title="Galbi">galbi</a>, became widespread in Japan during the 20th century, most notably after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>. Restaurants serving this dish either advertised themselves as <i><a href="/wiki/Horumonyaki" title="Horumonyaki">horumonyaki</a></i> (ja:ホルモン焼き, offal-grill) or simply <a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Joseon</a> (Korean) cuisine <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">朝鮮料理</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>chōsen ryori</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>. The division of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_peninsula" title="Korean peninsula">Korean peninsula</a> led to disagreements in the mid-1960s in the naming of "Korean food", with pro-South businesses changing their signs to "<i>kankoku ryori</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">韓国料理</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>" (named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Korea" title="Republic of Korea">Republic of Korea</a>) rather than retaining the term <i>chōsen</i> (<a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Joseon</a>), the name of the old, undivided Korea which by then had been appropriated by the Northern side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proper_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-proper-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ventilated barbecue systems, introduced by Shinpo Co., Ltd. in March 1980,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> quickly spread throughout Japan as it enabled diners to eat Yakiniku in a smoke-free environment and thus greatly extended the clientele.</p>
<p>The popularity of yakiniku was given a further boost in 1991 when the easing of beef import restrictions led to a drop in the price of beef.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> However the industry was dealt an unprecedented blow in 2001 with the occurrence of <a href="/wiki/Bovine_spongiform_encephalopathy" title="Bovine spongiform encephalopathy">BSE</a> (mad cow disease) within Japan.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Typical_ingredients">Typical ingredients</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yakiniku&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Typical ingredients">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Typical ingredients include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Beef
<ul>
<li>Rōsu - loin and chuck slices</li>
<li>Karubi or baraniku - short ribs. From the Korean word "<a href="/wiki/Galbi" title="Galbi">galbi</a>". In Japan it is usually served without the bones, unless it is specified as <i>hone-tsuki-karubi</i>.</li>
<li>Harami - tender meat around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diaphragm_(anatomy)" title="Diaphragm (anatomy)">diaphragm</a>.</li>
<li>Tan - beef <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">tongue</a>. From the English word "tongue". Often served with crushed Welsh onion, salt and lemon juice.</li>
<li>Misuji - tender meat around the shoulder.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Pork
<ul>
<li>Butabara - pork belly.</li>
<li>P-toro / Tontoro - fatty meat around the cheek and the neck.From the word "Pork <a href="/wiki/List_of_sushi_and_sashimi_ingredients#Seafood" title="List of sushi and sashimi ingredients">toro</a>".</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><i>Horumon</i> or <i>motsu</i> - Offal. <i>Horumon</i> means "discarded items" and comes from the <a href="/wiki/Kansai_dialect" title="Kansai dialect">Kansai dialect</a>.
<ul>
<li>Rebā - beef <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>. From the German word "<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leber_(Lebensmittel)" title="de:Leber (Lebensmittel)">Leber</a>".</li>
<li>Tetchan - <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestine" title="Intestine">intestine</a>. From the Chinese word "大肠" (da chang). May simply be referred to as <i>horumon</i>.</li>
<li>Hatsu - <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>. From the English word "heart".</li>
<li>Kobukuro - Pork uterus. Enjoyed for its gristly texture.</li>
<li>Tēru - From the English word "tail". Slices of beef tail cut crosswise, bone attached.</li>
<li>Mino / Hachinosu - beef <a href="/wiki/Tripe" title="Tripe">tripe</a></li>
<li>Gatsu - Pork stomach. From the English word "gut"</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Chicken</li>
<li>Seafood - squid, shellfish, shrimp.</li>
<li>Vegetables - bell pepper, carrots, <a href="/wiki/Shiitake" title="Shiitake">shiitake</a> and other mushrooms, onions, cabbage, eggplant, <a href="/wiki/Bean_sprout" title="Bean sprout">bean sprout</a> (moyashi), garlic and <i><a href="/wiki/Kabocha" title="Kabocha">kabocha</a></i> squash are common.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Yakiniku_Day">Yakiniku Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yakiniku&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Yakiniku Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1993, the <a href="/wiki/All_Japan_Yakiniku_Association" title="All Japan Yakiniku Association">All Japan Yakiniku Association</a> proclaimed 29 August as official "Yakiniku Day" (<i>yakiniku no hi</i>), a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goroawase" title="Goroawase">goroawase</a> (numerical wordplay), as the date 8月29 can be (roughly) read as ya-(tsu)ki-ni-ku (8 = ya, 2 = ni, 9 = ku).</p>
