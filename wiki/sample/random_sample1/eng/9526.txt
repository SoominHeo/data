<p>A <b>field gun</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a> piece. Originally the term referred to smaller <a href="/wiki/Gun" title="Gun">guns</a> that could accompany a field army on the march and when in combat could be moved about the battlefield in response to changing circumstances (<a href="/wiki/Field_artillery" title="Field artillery">field artillery</a>), as opposed to guns installed in a fort (<a href="/wiki/Royal_Garrison_Artillery" title="Royal Garrison Artillery">garrison artillery</a>/<a href="/wiki/Coastal_artillery" title="Coastal artillery">coastal artillery</a>), or to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_cannon" title="Siege cannon">siege cannon</a> or mortars which were too large to be moved quickly, and would be used only in a prolonged <a href="/wiki/Siege" title="Siege">siege</a>.</p>
<p>Perhaps the most famous use of the field gun in terms of advanced tactics was <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>'s use of very large wheels on the guns that allowed them to be moved quickly even during a battle. By moving the guns from point to point during the battle, enemy formations could be broken up to be handled by the <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> wherever they were massing, dramatically increasing the overall effectiveness of the infantry.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_I">World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Field_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Field_guns_captured_by_New_Zealanders_in_World_War_I_on_display_in_London,_1918.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="497" data-file-width="644" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Field_guns_captured_by_New_Zealanders_in_World_War_I_on_display_in_London%2C_1918.jpg/220px-Field_guns_captured_by_New_Zealanders_in_World_War_I_on_display_in_London%2C_1918.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Field_guns_captured_by_New_Zealanders_in_World_War_I_on_display_in_London%2C_1918.jpg/330px-Field_guns_captured_by_New_Zealanders_in_World_War_I_on_display_in_London%2C_1918.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Field_guns_captured_by_New_Zealanders_in_World_War_I_on_display_in_London%2C_1918.jpg/440px-Field_guns_captured_by_New_Zealanders_in_World_War_I_on_display_in_London%2C_1918.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Field_guns_captured_by_New_Zealanders_in_World_War_I_on_display_in_London,_1918.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
German field guns captured by the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Expeditionary_Force" title="New Zealand Expeditionary Force">NZEF</a> displayed in London, 1918</div>

<p>As the evolution of artillery continued, almost all guns of any size became capable of being moved at some speed. With few exceptions, even the largest siege weapons had become mobile by road or rail by the start of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, and evolution after that point tended to be towards smaller weapons with increased mobility. Even the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Germany_during_World_War_II" title="History of Germany during World War II">German</a> super-heavy guns in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> were rail or caterpillar-track mobile.</p>
<p>In British use, a <i>field gun</i> was anything up to around 4.5 inches in calibre—larger guns were <i>medium</i> and the largest of all <i>heavy</i>. Their largest gun (as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Howitzer" title="Howitzer">howitzer</a>) was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BL_5.5_inch_Medium_Gun" title="BL 5.5 inch Medium Gun">5.5 inch (140 mm) Medium</a>, with a range of about 15,000\16,000 yards.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Field_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since about the start of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the term has been applied to long-range artillery pieces that fire at a relatively low angle, as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Howitzer" title="Howitzer">howitzers</a> which can fire at higher angles. Field guns also lack a specialized purpose, such as anti-tank or coastal artillery. By the later stages of World War II the majority of artillery in use was either in the form of howitzers of 105 mm to 155 mm, or in form of hybrid anti-tank/field guns that had high enough muzzle velocity to be used in both roles. The most common field guns of the era were the British 5.5 inch, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/155_mm_Long_Tom" title="155 mm Long Tom">155 mm Long Tom</a> (a development of a <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> World War I weapon) and Soviet <a href="/wiki/100_mm_field_gun_M1944_(BS-3)" title="100 mm field gun M1944 (BS-3)">BS-3</a> - an artillery piece adopted from a Naval gun and designed to double up as an anti-tank weapon.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1960s">The 1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Field_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a> tried the long-range gun again in the 1960s with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M107_Self-Propelled_Gun" title="M107 Self-Propelled Gun">M107 175 mm gun</a>. The M107 was used extensively in the Vietnam War and proved effective in artillery duels with the North Vietnamese forces. It was considered a high-maintenance item and was removed from service with U.S. forces after a rash of cracked barrels. Production of the M107 continued through the 1980s to support, and the gun continues to be in service with Israeli forces and reserve stocks are held by other former users such as the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Army_of_Vietnam" title="People's Army of Vietnam">People's Army of Vietnam</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_times">Modern times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Field_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Today the gun finds itself in an area that seems to be gone for good. The class of small and highly mobile artillery has been filled with increasing capacity by the man-portable <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(weapon)" title="Mortar (weapon)">mortar</a>, which replaced almost every artillery piece smaller than 105 mm. Gun-howitzers fill the middle ground, with the world rapidly standardizing on the 155 mm <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> or 152 mm former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a> standards. The need for a long-range weapon is filled by <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> artillery, or <a href="/wiki/Military_aircraft" title="Military aircraft">aircraft</a>. Modern gun-artillery such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L118" title="L118">L118</a> 105mm light gun is used to provide fire support for infantry and armour at ranges where mortars are impractical. Man-packed mortars lack the range or hitting power of gun-artillery. In between is the rifled towed mortar; this weapon (usually in 120mm calibre) is light enough to be towed by a <a href="/wiki/Land_Rover" title="Land Rover">Land Rover</a>, has a range of over 6,000m and fires a bomb comparable in weight to an artillery shell.</p>
