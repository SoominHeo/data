<p><b>Bhaiṣajyaguru</b>, formally <b>Bhaiṣajya-guru-vaiḍūrya-prabhā-rāja</b> ("King of Medicine Master and <a href="/wiki/Lapis_lazuli" title="Lapis lazuli">Lapis Lazuli</a> Light"), is the <a href="/wiki/Buddhahood" title="Buddhahood">Buddha</a> of healing and medicine in <a href="/wiki/Mahayana" title="Mahayana">Mahāyāna Buddhism</a>. Commonly referred to as the "Medicine Buddha", he is described as a doctor who cures <a href="/wiki/Dukkha" title="Dukkha">dukkha</a> (suffering) using the medicine of his teachings.</p>
<p>Bhaiṣajyaguru's original name and title was <i>rāja</i> (King), but <a href="/wiki/Xuanzang" title="Xuanzang">Xuanzang</a> translated it as <a href="/wiki/Tath%C4%81gata" title="Tathāgata">Tathāgata</a> (Buddha). Subsequent translations and commentaries followed Xuanzang in describing him as a Buddha. The image of Bhaiṣajyaguru is usually expressed with a canonical Buddha-like form holding a <a href="/wiki/Gallipot" title="Gallipot">gallipot</a> and, in some versions, possessing blue skin. Though also considered to be a guardian of the East, in most cases <a href="/wiki/Akshobhya" title="Akshobhya">Akshobhya</a> is given that role. As an exceptional case, the <a href="/wiki/Honzon" title="Honzon">honzon</a> of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koya-san" title="Koya-san">Kōya-san</a> <a href="/wiki/Kong%C5%8Dbu-ji" title="Kongōbu-ji">Kongōbu-ji</a>" was changed from Akshobhya to Bhaiṣajyaguru.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bhaisajyaguru&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bhaiṣajyaguru is described in the eponymous <i>Bhaiṣajya-guru-vaiḍūrya-prabhā-rāja Sūtra</i>, commonly called the <i>Medicine Buddha Sutra</i>, as a <a href="/wiki/Bodhisattva" title="Bodhisattva">bodhisattva</a> who made 12 great vows. On achieving <a href="/wiki/Buddhahood" title="Buddhahood">Buddhahood</a>, he became the Buddha of the eastern <a href="/wiki/Pure_land" title="Pure land">pure land</a> of Vaiḍūryanirbhāsa "Pure Lapis Lazuli".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> There, he is attended to by two <a href="/wiki/Bodhisattva" title="Bodhisattva">bodhisattvas</a> symbolizing the light of the sun and the light of the moon respectively:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Suryaprabha" title="Suryaprabha">Suryaprabha</a> (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">日光遍照菩薩</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">rìguāng biànzhào púsà</span></i>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Candraprabha" title="Candraprabha">Candraprabha</a> (Chinese: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">月光遍照菩薩</span>; pinyin: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">yuèguāng biànzhào púsà</span></i>)</li>
</ul>
<p>A Sanskrit manuscript of the <i>Bhaiṣajya-guru-vaiḍūrya-prabhā-rāja Sūtra</i> was among the texts attesting to the popularity of Bhaiṣajyaguru in the ancient northwest Indian kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Gandhara" title="Gandhara">Gandhāra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bakshi.2C_S.R._1998._p._194_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bakshi.2C_S.R._1998._p._194-4">[4]</a></sup> The manuscripts in this find are dated before the 7th century, and are written in the upright <a href="/wiki/Gupta_script" title="Gupta script">Gupta script</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bakshi.2C_S.R._1998._p._194_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bakshi.2C_S.R._1998._p._194-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Chinese Buddhist monk <a href="/wiki/Xuanzang" title="Xuanzang">Xuanzang</a> visited a <a href="/wiki/Mah%C4%81s%C4%81%E1%B9%83ghika" title="Mahāsāṃghika">Mahāsāṃghika</a> monastery at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bamiyan" title="Bamiyan">Bamiyan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, in the 7th century CE, and the site of this monastery has been rediscovered by archaeologists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sch.C3.B8yen_Collection:_Buddhism_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sch.C3.B8yen_Collection:_Buddhism-5">[5]</a></sup> Birchbark manuscript fragments from several Mahāyāna sūtras have been discovered at the site, including the <i>Bhaiṣajya-guru-vaidūrya-prabha-rāja Sūtra</i> (MS 2385).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sch.C3.B8yen_Collection:_Buddhism_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sch.C3.B8yen_Collection:_Buddhism-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Twelve_Vows">The Twelve Vows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bhaisajyaguru&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Twelve Vows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Twelve Vows of the Medicine Buddha upon attaining Enlightenment, according to the Medicine Buddha Sutra<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sutra_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-sutra-6">[6]</a></sup> are:</p>
<ol>
<li>To illuminate countless realms with his radiance, enabling anyone to become a Buddha just like him.</li>
<li>To awaken the minds of sentient beings through his light of <a href="/wiki/Lapis_lazuli" title="Lapis lazuli">lapis lazuli</a>.</li>
<li>To provide the sentient beings with whatever material needs they require.</li>
<li>To correct heretical views and inspire beings toward the path of the <a href="/wiki/Bodhisattva" title="Bodhisattva">Bodhisattva</a>.</li>
<li>To help beings follow the Moral Precepts, even if they failed before.</li>
<li>To heal beings born with deformities, illness or other physical sufferings.</li>
<li>To help relieve the destitute and the sick.</li>
<li>To help women who wish to be reborn as men achieve their desired rebirth.</li>
<li>To help heal mental afflictions and delusions.</li>
<li>To help the oppressed be free from suffering.</li>
<li>To relieve those who suffer from terrible hunger and thirst.</li>
<li>To help clothe those who are destitute and suffering from cold and mosquitoes.</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dharani_and_Mantra">Dharani and Mantra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bhaisajyaguru&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dharani and Mantra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <i>Bhaiṣajya-guru-vaiḍūrya-prabhā-rāja Sūtra</i>, the Medicine Buddha is described as having entered into a state of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sam%C4%81dhi_(Buddhism)" title="Samādhi (Buddhism)">samadhi</a> called "Eliminating All the Suffering and Afflictions of Sentient Beings." From this samadhi state he spoke the Medicine Buddha Dharani.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sutra_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-sutra-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">namo bhagavate bhaiṣajyaguru vaiḍūryaprabharājāya</span></i></dd>
<dd><i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">tathāgatāya arahate samyaksambuddhāya tadyathā:</span></i></dd>
<dd><i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">oṃ bhaiṣajye bhaiṣajye bhaiṣajya-samudgate svāhā.</span></i></dd>
</dl>
<p>The last line of the dharani is used as Bhaisajyaguru's short form mantra. There are several other mantras for the Medicine Buddha as well that are used in different schools of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vajrayana_Buddhism" title="Vajrayana Buddhism">Vajrayana Buddhism</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iconography">Iconography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bhaisajyaguru&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Iconography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bhaiṣajyaguru is typically depicted seated, wearing the three robes of a Buddhist monk, holding a lapis-colored jar of medicine nectar in his left hand and the right hand resting on his right knee, holding the stem of the Aruna fruit or <a href="/wiki/Terminalia_chebula" title="Terminalia chebula">Myrobalan</a> between thumb and forefinger. In the sutra, he is also described by his aura of lapis lazuli-colored light. In Chinese depictions, he is sometimes holding a <a href="/wiki/Pagoda" title="Pagoda">pagoda</a>, symbolising the ten thousand Buddhas of the three periods of time. He is also depicted standing on a Northern Wei stele from approximately 500 AD now housed in the Metropolitan Museum of Art, accompanied by his two attendants, Suryaprabha and Candraprabha. Within the halo are depicted the <i>Seven Bhaiṣajyaguru Buddhas</i> and seven apsaras.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_Chinese_Buddhism">Role in Chinese Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bhaisajyaguru&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Role in Chinese Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pure_Land_of_Bhaisajyaguru,_Yuan_Dynasty.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Pure_Land_of_Bhaisajyaguru%2C_Yuan_Dynasty.JPG/220px-Pure_Land_of_Bhaisajyaguru%2C_Yuan_Dynasty.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Pure_Land_of_Bhaisajyaguru%2C_Yuan_Dynasty.JPG/330px-Pure_Land_of_Bhaisajyaguru%2C_Yuan_Dynasty.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Pure_Land_of_Bhaisajyaguru%2C_Yuan_Dynasty.JPG/440px-Pure_Land_of_Bhaisajyaguru%2C_Yuan_Dynasty.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pure_Land_of_Bhaisajyaguru,_Yuan_Dynasty.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>The Pure Land of Bhaisajyaguru</i>, a wall mural made circa 1319 AD, <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The practice of veneration of the Medicine Buddha is also popular in China, as he is depicted as one of the trinity of Buddhas, the others being the founder <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Śākyamuni</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amitabha" title="Amitabha">Amitabha</a>. He can also be viewed as the healing attribution to Śākyamuni, as he is often called the "Medicine King" in sutras. There are two popular Chinese translations of this sutra: one by Xuanzang <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and the other by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Ching_(monk)" title="I Ching (monk)">Yijing</a> both translated in the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taisho_Tripitaka" title="Taisho Tripitaka">Taisho Tripitaka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Qianlong_Emperor" title="Qianlong Emperor">Qianlong</a> Tripitaka (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">乾隆大藏經</span>) each have three translations of the sutra:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>By Dharmagupta in 615 CE (Taisho: vol. 14, no. 449; Qianlong: no. 166)</li>
<li>By Xuanzang in 650 CE (Taisho: vol. 14, no. 450; Qianlong: no. 167)</li>
<li>By Yijing in 707 CE (Taisho: vol. 14, no. 451; Qianlong: no. 168)</li>
</ol>
<p>These three versions have different titles:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li>Dharmagupta: <i>Sutra of the Vows of the Medicine Buddha</i><br/>
<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">藥師如來本願經</span></li>
<li>Xuanzang: <i>Sutra of the Vows of the Medicine Buddha of Lapis Lazuli Crystal Radiance</i><br/>
<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">藥師琉璃光如來本願功德經</span></li>
<li>Yijing: <i>Sutra of the Vows of the Medicine Buddha of Lapis Lazuli Crystal Radiance and Seven Past Buddhas</i><br/>
<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">藥師琉璃光七佛本願功德經</span> (no. 168, two scrolls).</li>
</ol>
<p>The version translated by Yijing includes not only the vows of the Medicine Buddha but also the vows of the Seven Past Buddhas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like Tibetan Buddhists, Chinese Buddhists recite the mantra of the Medicine Buddha to overcome mental, physical and spiritual sickness. The <i>Bhaiṣajyaguruvaiḍūryaprabhārāja Sūtra</i>, which the Medicine Buddha is associated with and described in great detail in, is a common sutra to recite in Chinese temples as well. Furthermore, much like the <a href="/wiki/Nianfo" title="Nianfo">nianfo</a> path of Amitabha, the name of Medicine Buddha is also recited for the benefit of being reborn in the Eastern Pure Lands, though this is deemphasized in favor of the Medicine Buddha's role for the living.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_Japanese_Buddhism">Role in Japanese Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bhaisajyaguru&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Role in Japanese Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yakushi_(Bhaishajaguru)_by_Enku_(1628-95).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="1121" height="301" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Yakushi_%28Bhaishajaguru%29_by_Enku_%281628-95%29.jpg/220px-Yakushi_%28Bhaishajaguru%29_by_Enku_%281628-95%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Yakushi_%28Bhaishajaguru%29_by_Enku_%281628-95%29.jpg/330px-Yakushi_%28Bhaishajaguru%29_by_Enku_%281628-95%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Yakushi_%28Bhaishajaguru%29_by_Enku_%281628-95%29.jpg/440px-Yakushi_%28Bhaishajaguru%29_by_Enku_%281628-95%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yakushi_(Bhaishajaguru)_by_Enku_(1628-95).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Yakushi (Bhaishajaguru, The Buddha of Healing) by Enkū (1628-95). <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Museum" title="Brooklyn Museum">Brooklyn Museum</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Starting in the 7th century in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, Yakushi was prayed to in the place of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ashuku" title="Ashuku">Ashuku</a> (Akshobhya). Some of Yakushi's role has been taken over by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jiz%C5%8D" title="Jizō">Jizō</a> (Ksitigarbha), but Yakushi is still invoked in the traditional memorial services for the dead.</p>
<p>Older temples, those mostly found in the <a href="/wiki/Tendai" title="Tendai">Tendai</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shingon" title="Shingon">Shingon</a> sects, especially those around <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nara,_Nara" title="Nara, Nara">Nara</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinki" title="Kinki">Kinki</a> region often have Yakushi as the center of devotion, unlike later Buddhist sects which focus on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amitabha" title="Amitabha">Amitabha</a> Buddha or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kannon" title="Kannon">Kannon</a> Bodhisattva almost exclusively. Often, when Yakushi is the center of devotion in a Buddhist temple, he is flanked by the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Heavenly_Generals" title="Twelve Heavenly Generals">Twelve Heavenly Generals</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">十二神将</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Jūni-shinshō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, who were twelve <a href="/wiki/Yaksha" title="Yaksha">yaksha</a> generals who had been converted through hearing the <i>Bhaiṣajyaguruvaiḍūryaprabhārāja Sūtra</i>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sutra_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-sutra-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Wherever this sutra circulates or wherever there are sentient beings who hold fast to the name of the Medicine Buddha [Yakushi Buddha] and respectfully make offerings to him, whether in villages, towns, kingdoms or in the wilderness, we [the Twelve Generals] will all protect them. We will release them from all suffering and calamities and see to it that all their wishes are fulfilled.</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_Tibetan_Buddhism">Role in Tibetan Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bhaisajyaguru&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Role in Tibetan Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The practice of Medicine Buddha, the Supreme Healer (or Sangye Menla in <a href="/wiki/Standard_Tibetan" title="Standard Tibetan">Tibetan</a>) is not only a very powerful method for healing and increasing healing powers both for oneself and others, but also for overcoming the inner sickness of attachment, hatred, and ignorance, thus to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meditate" title="Meditate">meditate</a> on the Medicine Buddha can help decrease physical and mental illness and suffering.</p>
<p>The Medicine Buddha mantra is held to be extremely powerful for healing of physical illnesses and purification of negative <a href="/wiki/Karma" title="Karma">karma</a>. One form of practice based on the Medicine Buddha is done when one is stricken by disease. The patient is to recite the long Medicine Buddha mantra 108 times over a glass of water. The water is now believed to be blessed by the power of the mantra and the blessing of the Medicine Buddha himself, and the patient is to drink the water. This practice is then repeated each day until the illness is cured.</p>
