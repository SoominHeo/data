<p><b>Jan Palach</b> (11 August 1948 – 19 January 1969; <small>Czech pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Czech" title="Help:IPA for Czech">[jan ˈpalax]</a></span>) was a Czech student of history and political economy at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_University_in_Prague" title="Charles University in Prague">Charles University in Prague</a>. He committed <a href="/wiki/Self-immolation" title="Self-immolation">self-immolation</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_protest" title="Political protest">political protest</a> against the end of the <a href="/wiki/Prague_Spring" title="Prague Spring">Prague Spring</a> resulting from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1968_invasion_of_Czechoslovakia" title="1968 invasion of Czechoslovakia">1968 invasion of Czechoslovakia</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> armies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jan_Palach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In August 1968, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact_invasion_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Warsaw Pact invasion of Czechoslovakia">invaded</a> <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> to crush the <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberalising</a> reforms of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek" title="Alexander Dubček">Alexander Dubček</a>'s government during what was known as the <a href="/wiki/Prague_Spring" title="Prague Spring">Prague Spring</a>. <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>-born Palach decided to sacrifice himself in protest of the invasion and set himself on fire, in <a href="/wiki/Wenceslas_Square" title="Wenceslas Square">Wenceslas Square</a>, on 16 January 1969. According to a letter he sent to several public figures, an entire clandestine resistance organization had been established with the purpose of practicing self-immolation until their demands were met; however, it seems that such a group never existed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The demands declared in the letter were the abolition of censorship and a halt to the distribution of <i>Zprávy</i>, the official newspaper of the Soviet occupying forces. In addition, the letter called for the Czech and the Slovak peoples to go on a <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">general strike</a> in support of these demands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> An earlier draft of the letter that Palach wrote also called for the resignation of a number of pro-Soviet politicians,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but that demand did not make it into the final version, which included the remark that "our demands are not extreme, on the contrary". Palach died from his burns several days after his act, at the hospital. On his deathbed, he was visited by a female acquaintance from his college and by a student leader, to whom he had addressed one of the copies of his letter. It was reported that he had pleaded for others not to do what he had done but instead to continue the struggle by other means, although it has been doubted whether he really said that.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Jaroslava_Moserov%C3%A1" title="Jaroslava Moserová">Jaroslava Moserová</a>, a burns specialist who was the first to provide care to Palach at the Charles University Faculty Hospital, Palach did not set himself on fire to protest against the Soviet occupation, but did so to protest against the "demoralization" of Czechoslovak citizens caused by the occupation.</p>

<p>The funeral of Palach turned into a major protest against the occupation. A month later (on 25 February), another student, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Zaj%C3%ADc" title="Jan Zajíc">Jan Zajíc</a>, burned himself to death in the same place. This was followed in April of the same year by <a href="/wiki/Ev%C5%BEen_Plocek" title="Evžen Plocek">Evžen Plocek</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jihlava" title="Jihlava">Jihlava</a>, and by others.</p>
<p>Palach's self-immolation was the second act of that kind after that of the Pole <a href="/wiki/Ryszard_Siwiec" title="Ryszard Siwiec">Ryszard Siwiec</a>, which was successfully suppressed by the authorities and was mostly forgotten until the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_communism" title="Fall of communism">fall of communism</a>. Palach was not aware of Siwiec's protest.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Posthumous_recognition">Posthumous recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jan_Palach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Posthumous recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Palach was initially interred in <a href="/wiki/Ol%C5%A1any_Cemetery" title="Olšany Cemetery">Olšany Cemetery</a>. As his gravesite was growing into a national shrine, the Czechoslovak secret police (<a href="/wiki/StB" title="StB">StB</a>) set out to destroy any memory of Palach's deed and exhumed his remains on the night of 25 October 1973. His body was then cremated and sent to his mother in Palach's native town of <a href="/wiki/V%C5%A1etaty_(M%C4%9Bln%C3%ADk_District)" title="Všetaty (Mělník District)">Všetaty</a> while an anonymous old woman from a rest home was laid in the grave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Palach's mother was not allowed to deposit the urn in the local cemetery until 1974. On 25 October 1990 the urn was officially returned to its initial site in Prague.</p>
<p>On the 20th anniversary of Palach's death, protests ostensibly in memory of Palach (but intended as criticism of the regime) escalated into what would be called "Palach Week". The series of anticommunist demonstrations in Prague between 15 and 21 January 1989 were suppressed by the police, who beat demonstrators and used water cannons, often catching passers-by in the fray. Palach Week is considered one of the catalyst demonstrations which preceded the fall of communism in Czechoslovakia 10 months later.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Velvet_Revolution" title="Velvet Revolution">Velvet Revolution</a>, Palach (along with Zajíc) was commemorated in Prague by a bronze cross embedded at the spot where he fell outside the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_(Prague)" title="National Museum (Prague)">National Museum</a>, as well as a <a href="/wiki/Jan_Palach_Square" title="Jan Palach Square">square</a> named in his honour. The Czech astronomer <a href="/wiki/Lubo%C5%A1_Kohoutek" title="Luboš Kohoutek">Luboš Kohoutek</a>, who left Czechoslovakia the following year, named an <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> which had been discovered on 22 August 1969, after Jan Palach (<a href="/wiki/1834_Palach" title="1834 Palach">1834 Palach</a>). There are several other memorials to Palach in cities throughout Europe, including a small memorial inside the glacier tunnels beneath the Jungfraujoch in Switzerland.</p>
<p>Several later incidents of self-immolation may <a href="/wiki/Copycat_suicide" title="Copycat suicide">have been influenced</a> by the example of Palach and his media popularity. In the spring of 2003, a total of six young Czechs burned themselves to death,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> notably <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zden%C4%9Bk_Adamec_(Czech_demonstrator)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zdeněk Adamec (Czech demonstrator) (page does not exist)">Zdeněk Adamec</a>, a 19-year-old student from <a href="/wiki/Humpolec" title="Humpolec">Humpolec</a> who burned himself on 6 March 2003 on almost the same spot in front of the National Museum where Palach burnt himself, leaving a suicide note explicitly referring to Palach and the others who had committed suicide in the 1969 <a href="/wiki/Prague_Spring" title="Prague Spring">Prague Spring</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Just walking distance from the site of Palach's self-immolation, a statuary in Prague's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Town_Square_(Prague)" title="Old Town Square (Prague)">Old Town Square</a> honours iconic Bohemian religious thinker <a href="/wiki/Jan_Hus" title="Jan Hus">Jan Hus</a>, who was burned at the stake for his beliefs in 1415. Himself celebrated as a national hero for many centuries, some commentary has linked Palach's self-immolation to the execution of Hus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>The music video for the song "<a href="/wiki/Club_Foot_(song)" title="Club Foot (song)">Club Foot</a>" by the band <a href="/wiki/Kasabian" title="Kasabian">Kasabian</a> is dedicated to Palach. The composition "The Funeral of Jan Palach" performed by The Zippo Band and composed by <a href="/wiki/Phil_Kline" title="Phil Kline">Phil Kline</a> is a tribute. He is mentioned in <a href="/wiki/The_Stranglers" title="The Stranglers">The Stranglers</a>' bassist, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Burnel" title="Jean-Jacques Burnel">Jean-Jacques Burnel</a>'s 1979 solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/Euroman_Cometh" title="Euroman Cometh">Euroman Cometh</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1969, the <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenian</a> poet <a href="/wiki/Edvard_Kocbek" title="Edvard Kocbek">Edvard Kocbek</a> published a poem entitled "Rocket", in which he juxtaposed two events from that year: the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> landing, "a senseless act of technological nihilism", and "a rocket named Palach that launched itself into history, its smoky message was seen even through the darkest glasses".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>After seeking political asylum in the United States, Polish artist <a href="/wiki/Wiktor_Szostalo" title="Wiktor Szostalo">Wiktor Szostalo</a> commemorated Jan Palach in his <i>"Performance for Freedom"</i> proclaiming <i>"I am Jan Palach. I'm a Czech, I'm a Pole, a <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuanian</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnamese</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghani</a>, a betrayed You. After I've burnt myself a thousand times, perhaps we'll win"</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the occasion of the 40th anniversary of the death of Jan Palach, a statue sculpted by <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A1s_Beck" title="András Beck">András Beck</a> as a tribute to the student was transported from France to the Czech Republic. The statue was installed in <a href="/wiki/M%C4%9Bln%C3%ADk" title="Mělník">Mělník</a>, the city where Jan Palach did his studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Italian songwriter <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Guccini" title="Francesco Guccini">Francesco Guccini</a> wrote a song "La Primavera di Praga" in dedication to Jan Palach, compared to religious scholar <a href="/wiki/Jan_Hus" title="Jan Hus">Jan Hus</a>: "Once again Jan Hus is burning alive". Polish singer <a href="/wiki/Jacek_Kaczmarski" title="Jacek Kaczmarski">Jacek Kaczmarski</a> wrote a song about Palach's suicide, called "Pochodnie" ("Torches"). The Italian far-right Folk group, ”La Compagnia dell'Anello” released a song dedicated to him, titled Jan Palach. The Kasabian, British band, released the "Club Foot" on their 2004 debut album Kasabian on 17 May 2004 in the UK. The video of this song is dedicated to Jan Palach. A Pakistani poet Qazi Zafar Iqbal paid tribute to Jan Palach in the form of a Poem in Urdu. The poem is included in his book named "Ghurfa-e-Shab (The Window of Night)" published in 2006 in the city of Lahore.</p>
<p>The Luxembourg-based Welsh composer Dafydd Bullock was commissioned to write "Requiem for Jan Palach" (op 182) to commemorate the fortieth anniversary of Palach's suicide. It includes a setting of words which appeared briefly on a statue in Wenceslas Square after the event, before being erased by the authorities: "Do not be indifferent to the day when the light of the future was carried forward by a burning body".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In their 1983 song "Nuuj Helde" the Janse Bagge Bend (from the Netherlands) asks whether people know why Jan Palach burned. This song was meant to make the general public aware of heroes.</p>
<p>Palach featured in a monologue radio play entitled "Torch No 1" on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a>, directed by Martin Jenkins, and written by <a href="/wiki/David_Pownall" title="David Pownall">David Pownall</a>. Palach was played by Karl Davies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>French documentary filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Depardon" title="Raymond Depardon">Raymond Depardon</a> directed a 1969 film about Jan Palach.</p>
<p>Norwegian songwriter <a href="/wiki/Hans_Rotmo" title="Hans Rotmo">Hans Rotmo</a> mentioned Palach's name among other notable political activists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victor_Jara" title="Victor Jara">Victor Jara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Biko" title="Steve Biko">Steve Biko</a> in his 1989 song "Lennon Street".</p>
<p>Norwegian songwriter and singer <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ge_Aleksandersen" title="Åge Aleksandersen">Åge Aleksandersen</a> mentioned Palach's name in his 1984 song "Va det du Jesus".</p>
<p>A sequence of poems exploring the implications of Palach's death called <i>One Match</i> by the poet Sheila Hamilton were published in issue 51 of the Dorset-based poetry serial, <i>Tears in the Fence</i> (ed. David Caddy) in 2010.</p>
<p>A three-part 2013 Czech-Polish television show "<a href="/wiki/Burning_Bush_(mini-series)" title="Burning Bush (mini-series)">Burning Bush</a>", directed by <a href="/wiki/Agnieszka_Holland" title="Agnieszka Holland">Agnieszka Holland</a>, is situated around the events that happened after Jan Palach's self-immolation.</p>
<p>American Metal band <a href="/wiki/Lamb_of_God_(band)" title="Lamb of God (band)">Lamb of God</a> wrote a song on their studio album <a href="/wiki/VII:_Sturm_und_Drang" title="VII: Sturm und Drang">VII: Sturm und Drang</a>, entitled <i>"Torches"</i>, that was inspired by Palach's actions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Place_names">Place names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jan_Palach&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Place names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the Czech Republic, many towns have streets or squares named after Palach, of which perhaps most notable is the <a href="/wiki/Jan_Palach_Square" title="Jan Palach Square">Jan Palach Square</a> in central Prague. He also had streets named after him in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luxembourg_(city)" title="Luxembourg (city)">Luxembourg city</a> (Luxembourg), <a href="/wiki/Angers" title="Angers">Angers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parthenay" title="Parthenay">Parthenay</a> (France), <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a> (Poland), <a href="/wiki/Assen" title="Assen">Assen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haarlem" title="Haarlem">Haarlem</a> (Netherlands), <a href="/wiki/Varna" title="Varna">Varna</a> (Bulgaria) and <a href="/wiki/Nantwich" title="Nantwich">Nantwich</a> (United Kingdom). In <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> (Italy) (as well as in many other Italian towns), there is a central square named after Palach with a commemorative statue.</p>
<p>The oldest rock club in <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a> is named Palach. It is situated in <a href="/wiki/Rijeka" title="Rijeka">Rijeka</a> since 1969 to this day. There is a bus station in the town of <a href="/wiki/Curepipe" title="Curepipe">Curepipe</a>, Mauritius named after Jan Palach. A student hall in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, Italy on the <a href="/wiki/Giudecca" title="Giudecca">Giudecca island</a> has also been given the name of Jan Palach.</p>
