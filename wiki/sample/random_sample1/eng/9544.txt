<p>The <b>Yanggakdo International Hotel</b> is the largest operating hotel and the second tallest building in <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, after the <a href="/wiki/Ryugyong_Hotel" title="Ryugyong Hotel">Ryugyong Hotel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessinsider2013_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-businessinsider2013-1">[1]</a></sup> The hotel is located on <a href="/wiki/Yanggakdo" title="Yanggakdo">Yanggak Island</a> in the river Taedong, two kilometres (1.2 mi) to the south-east of the centre of <a href="/wiki/Pyongyang" title="Pyongyang">Pyongyang</a>, the nation's capital. It rises to an overall height of 170 metres (560 ft) and has a slowly <a href="/wiki/Revolving_restaurant" title="Revolving restaurant">revolving restaurant</a> on the 47th floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The hotel is said to contain 1,000 rooms and a total floor space of 87,870 square metres (945,800 sq ft). The structure was built between 1986 and 1992 by France's <a href="/wiki/Vinci_SA" title="Vinci SA">Campenon Bernard Construction Company</a> and opened in 1995.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yanggakdo_International_Hotel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Besides housing the reception, the ground floor offers the purchase of North Korean currency sets, postcards and letters, and basic commodities at Western prices. There is a bar, and a bookshop<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which stocks North Korean reading material including back-issues of the local English-language newspaper, the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pyongyang_Times" title="The Pyongyang Times">Pyongyang Times</a></i>, treatises and photos of <a href="/wiki/Kim_Il-sung" title="Kim Il-sung">Kim Il-sung</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-il" title="Kim Jong-il">Kim Jong-il</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-un" title="Kim Jong-un">Kim Jong-un</a>, children's books and material which describes the North Korean understanding of the history of the region.</p>
<p>In addition to the revolving restaurant, the hotel guide issued to guests indicates that the hotel contains four further restaurants on the second floor: dining-rooms one and two, the main banquet hall, and the Japanese, Chinese and Korean food dining-rooms.</p>
<p>The fifth floor is off limits to hotel guests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-businessinsider2013_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-businessinsider2013-1">[1]</a></sup> The elevators do not stop on the fifth floor, hence there is no fifth floor button on the elevator panel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The basement contains a bowling alley,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> a pool room, a sauna, a swimming pool, a barber shop, a casino and a massage club<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> run by a Chinese company with an exclusively female staff.</p>
<p>The hotel's grounds originally included a 9,000-square-metre nine-hole golf course. In 2011 the golf course was demolished to make space for a Chinese-funded health complex to be built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Also located on Yanggak Island, next to the hotel's grounds, is the Pyongyang International Cinema Hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The opening and closing ceremonies of the <i><a href="/wiki/Pyongyang_International_Film_Festival" title="Pyongyang International Film Festival">Pyongyang International Film Festival</a></i> take place here.</p>
<p>The Yanggakdo International Hotel is a standard stop on most tours of North Korea, and was featured in the graphic novel <i><a href="/wiki/Pyongyang:_A_Journey_in_North_Korea" title="Pyongyang: A Journey in North Korea">Pyongyang</a></i>.</p>
<p>On January 2, 2016, a visiting American college student, <a href="/wiki/Otto_Warmbier" title="Otto Warmbier">Otto Warmbier</a>, was arrested for stealing a political propaganda banner from the hotel and was sentenced on March 16, 2016 to fifteen years of hard labor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Staff members from the hotel testified against Warmbier at his trial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
