<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Electron hole</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Electron-hole.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="234" data-file-width="349" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Electron-hole.svg/220px-Electron-hole.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Electron-hole.svg/330px-Electron-hole.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Electron-hole.svg/440px-Electron-hole.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Electron-hole.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
When an electron leaves a <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> atom, it leaves an electron hole in its place. This causes the helium atom to become positively charged.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_engineering" title="Electronic engineering">electronic engineering</a>, an <b>electron hole</b> (often simply called a <b>hole</b>) is the lack of an electron at a position where one could exist in an <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> or <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">atomic lattice</a>. Since in a normal atom or crystal lattice the negative charge of the electrons is balanced by the positive charge of the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">atomic nuclei</a>, the absence of an electron leaves a net positive charge at the hole's location. Holes in a <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystal_lattice" title="Crystal lattice">crystal lattice</a> can move through the lattice as electrons can, and act similarly to <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">positively-charged</a> particles. They play an important role in the operation of <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_device" title="Semiconductor device">semiconductor devices</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diode" title="Diode">diodes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a>. However they are not actually <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">particles</a>, but rather <a href="/wiki/Quasiparticle" title="Quasiparticle">quasiparticles</a>; they are different from the <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positron</a>, which is the <a href="/wiki/Antiparticle" title="Antiparticle">antiparticle</a> of the electron. (See also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dirac_Sea" title="Dirac Sea">Dirac Sea</a>.)</p>
<p>If an electron is excited into a higher state it leaves a hole in its old state. This meaning is used in <a href="/wiki/Auger_electron_spectroscopy" title="Auger electron spectroscopy">Auger electron spectroscopy</a> (and other <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">x-ray</a> techniques), in <a href="/wiki/Computational_chemistry" title="Computational chemistry">computational chemistry</a>, and to explain the low electron-electron scattering-rate in crystals (<a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>, semiconductors).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_band_structure" title="Electronic band structure">electronic band structure</a> calculations lead to an <a href="/wiki/Effective_mass_(solid-state_physics)" title="Effective mass (solid-state physics)">effective mass</a> for the electrons, which typically is negative at the top of a band. The <a href="/wiki/Negative_mass" title="Negative mass">negative mass</a> is an unintuitive concept,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and in these situations a more familiar picture is found by considering a positive charge with a positive mass.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid-state_physics">Solid-state physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electron_hole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Solid-state physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_physics" title="Solid-state physics">solid-state physics</a>, an <b>electron hole</b> (usually referred to simply as a <b>hole</b>) is the absence of an electron from a full <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valence_band" title="Valence band">valence band</a>. A hole is essentially a way to conceptualize the interactions of the electrons within a nearly <i>full</i> system, which is <i>missing</i> just a few electrons. In some ways, the behavior of a hole within a semiconductor <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal lattice</a> is comparable to that of the bubble in a full bottle of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Simplified_analogy:_Empty_seat_in_an_auditorium">Simplified analogy: Empty seat in an auditorium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electron_hole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Simplified analogy: Empty seat in an auditorium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:15-puzzle-02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1480" data-file-width="1472" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/15-puzzle-02.jpg/220px-15-puzzle-02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/15-puzzle-02.jpg/330px-15-puzzle-02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/15-puzzle-02.jpg/440px-15-puzzle-02.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:15-puzzle-02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A children's puzzle which illustrates the mobility of holes in an atomic lattice. The tiles are analogous to electrons, while the missing tile is analogous to a hole. Just as the position of the missing tile can be moved to different locations by moving the tiles, a hole in a crystal lattice can move to different positions in the lattice by the motion of the surrounding electrons.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Hole conduction in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valence_band" title="Valence band">valence band</a> can be explained by the following analogy. Imagine a row of people seated in an auditorium, where there are no spare chairs. Someone in the middle of the row wants to leave, so he jumps over the back of the seat into an empty row, and walks out. The empty row is analogous to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conduction_band" title="Conduction band">conduction band</a>, and the person walking out is analogous to a free electron.</p>
<p>Now imagine someone else comes along and wants to sit down. The empty row has a poor view; so he does not want to sit there. Instead, a person in the crowded row moves into the empty seat the first person left behind. The empty seat moves one spot closer to the edge and the person waiting to sit down. The next person follows, and the next, et cetera. One could say that the empty seat moves towards the edge of the row. Once the empty seat reaches the edge, the new person can sit down.</p>
<p>In the process everyone in the row has moved along. If those people were negatively charged (like electrons), this movement would constitute <a href="/wiki/Electrical_resistivity_and_conductivity" title="Electrical resistivity and conductivity">conduction</a>. If the seats themselves were positively charged, then only the vacant seat would be positive. This is a very simple model of how hole conduction works.</p>
<p>In reality, due to the crystal structure properties, the hole is not localized to a single position as described in the previous example. Rather, the hole spans an area in the crystal lattice covering many hundreds of <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">unit cells</a>. This is equivalent to being unable to tell which broken bond corresponds to the "missing" electron.</p>
<p>Instead of analyzing the movement of an empty state in the valence band as the movement of many separate electrons, a single equivalent imaginary particle called a "hole" is considered. In an applied <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a>, the electrons move in one direction, corresponding to the hole moving in the other. If a hole associates itself with a neutral atom, that atom loses an electron and becomes positive. Therefore, the hole is taken to have positive <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">charge</a> of +e, precisely the opposite of the electron charge.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detailed_picture:_A_hole_is_the_absence_of_a_negative-mass_electron">Detailed picture: A hole is the absence of a negative-mass electron</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electron_hole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Detailed picture: A hole is the absence of a negative-mass electron">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BandDiagram-Semiconductors-E.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="303" data-file-width="441" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/BandDiagram-Semiconductors-E.PNG/220px-BandDiagram-Semiconductors-E.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/BandDiagram-Semiconductors-E.PNG/330px-BandDiagram-Semiconductors-E.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/BandDiagram-Semiconductors-E.PNG/440px-BandDiagram-Semiconductors-E.PNG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BandDiagram-Semiconductors-E.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A semiconductor <a href="/wiki/Electronic_band_structure" title="Electronic band structure">electronic band structure</a> (right) includes the dispersion relation of each band, i.e. the energy of an electron <i>E</i> as a function of the electron's <a href="/wiki/Wave_vector" title="Wave vector">wavevector</a> <i>k</i>. The "unfilled band" is the semiconductor's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conduction_band" title="Conduction band">conduction band</a>; it curves upward indicating positive <a href="/wiki/Effective_mass_(solid-state_physics)" title="Effective mass (solid-state physics)">effective mass</a>. The "filled band" is the semiconductor's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valence_band" title="Valence band">valence band</a>; it curves downward indicating negative effective mass.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The analogy above is quite simplified, and cannot explain why holes create an opposite effect to electrons in the <a href="/wiki/Hall_effect" title="Hall effect">Hall effect</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermoelectric_effect#Seebeck_effect" title="Thermoelectric effect">Seebeck effect</a>. A more precise and detailed explanation follows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kittel_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kittel-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>The <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_relation" title="Dispersion relation">dispersion relation</a> determines how electrons respond to forces (via the concept of <a href="/wiki/Effective_mass_(solid-state_physics)" title="Effective mass (solid-state physics)">effective mass</a>).</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kittel_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kittel-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>A dispersion relation is the relationship between <a href="/wiki/Wave_vector" title="Wave vector">wavevector</a> (k-vector) and energy in a band, part of the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_band_structure" title="Electronic band structure">electronic band structure</a>. In quantum mechanics, the electrons are waves, and energy is the wave frequency. A localized electron is a <a href="/wiki/Wave_packet" title="Wave packet">wavepacket</a>, and the motion of an electron is given by the formula for the <a href="/wiki/Group_velocity" title="Group velocity">group velocity of a wave</a>. An electric field affects an electron by gradually shifting all the wavevectors in the wavepacket, and the electron moves because its wave group velocity changes. Therefore, again, the way an electron responds to forces is entirely determined by its dispersion relation. An electron floating in space has the dispersion relation <i>E</i>=ℏ<sup>2</sup><i>k</i><sup>2</sup>/(2<i>m</i>), where <i>m</i> is the (real) <a href="/wiki/Electron_rest_mass" title="Electron rest mass">electron mass</a> and ℏ is <a href="/wiki/Planck_constant" title="Planck constant">reduced Planck constant</a>. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conduction_band" title="Conduction band">conduction band</a> of a semiconductor, the dispersion relation is instead <i>E</i>=ℏ<sup>2</sup><i>k</i><sup>2</sup>/(2<i>m</i><sup>*</sup>) (<i>m</i><sup>*</sup> is the <i><a href="/wiki/Effective_mass_(solid-state_physics)" title="Effective mass (solid-state physics)">effective mass</a></i>), so a conduction-band electron responds to forces <i>as if</i> it had the mass <i>m</i><sup>*</sup>.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Electrons near the top of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valence_band" title="Valence band">valence band</a> behave as if they have <a href="/wiki/Negative_mass" title="Negative mass">negative mass</a>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kittel_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kittel-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The dispersion relation near the top of the valence band is <i>E</i>=ℏ<sup>2</sup>k<sup>2</sup>/(2<i>m</i><sup>*</sup>) with <i>negative</i> effective mass. So electrons near the top of the valence band behave like they have <a href="/wiki/Negative_mass" title="Negative mass">negative mass</a>. When a force pulls the electrons to the right, these electrons actually move left. This is solely due to the shape of the valence band, and is unrelated to whether the band is full or empty. If you could somehow empty out the valence band and just put one electron near the valence band maximum (an unstable situation), this electron would move the "wrong way" in response to forces.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Positively-charged holes as a shortcut for calculating the total current of an almost-full band.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kittel_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kittel-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>A perfectly full band always has zero current. One way to think about this fact is that the electron states near the top of the band have negative effective mass, and those near the bottom of the band have positive effective mass, so the net motion is exactly zero. If an otherwise-almost-full valence band has a state <i>without</i> an electron in it, we say that this state is occupied by a hole. There is a mathematical shortcut for calculating the current due to every electron in the whole valence band: Start with zero current (the total if the band were full), and <i>subtract</i> the current due to the electrons that <i>would</i> be in each hole state if it wasn't a hole. Since <i>subtracting</i> the current caused by a <i>negative</i> charge in motion is the same as <i>adding</i> the current caused by a <i>positive</i> charge moving on the same path, the mathematical shortcut is to pretend that each hole state is carrying a positive charge, while ignoring every other electron state in the valence band.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>A hole near the top of the valence band moves the same way as an electron near the top of the valence band <b>would</b> move</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kittel_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kittel-3">[3]</a></sup> (which is in the opposite direction compared to conduction-band electrons experiencing the same force.)</li>
</ul>
<p>This fact follows from the discussion and definition above. This is an example where the auditorium analogy above is misleading. When a person moves left in a full auditorium, an empty seat moves right. But in this section we are imagining how electrons move through k-space, not real space, and the effect of a force is to move all the electrons through k-space in the same direction at the same time. In this context, a better analogy is a bubble underwater in a river: The bubble moves the same direction as the water, not opposite.</p>
<p>Since force = mass × acceleration, a negative-effective-mass electron near the top of the valence band would move the opposite direction as a positive-effective-mass electron near the bottom of the conduction band, in response to a given electric or magnetic force. Therefore, a hole moves this way as well.</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Conclusion: Hole is a positive-charge, positive-mass <a href="/wiki/Quasiparticle" title="Quasiparticle">quasiparticle</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
<p>From the above, a hole (1) carries a positive charge, and (2) responds to electric and magnetic fields as if it had a positive charge and positive mass. (The latter is because a particle with positive charge and positive mass responds to electric and magnetic fields in the same way as a particle with negative charge and negative mass.) That explains why holes can be treated in all situations as ordinary positively charged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quasiparticles" title="Quasiparticles">quasiparticles</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_semiconductor_technology">Role in semiconductor technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electron_hole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Role in semiconductor technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some semiconductors, such as silicon, the hole's effective mass is dependent on direction (<a href="/wiki/Anisotropy" title="Anisotropy">anisotropic</a>), however a value averaged over all directions can be used for some macroscopic calculations.</p>
<p>In most semiconductors, the effective mass of a hole is much larger than that of an <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>. This results in lower <a href="/wiki/Electron_mobility" title="Electron mobility">mobility</a> for holes under the influence of an <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a> and this may slow down the speed of the electronic device made of that semiconductor. This is one major reason for adopting electrons as the primary charge carriers, whenever possible in semiconductor devices, rather than holes.</p>
<p>However, in many semiconductor devices, both electrons <i>and</i> holes play an essential role. Examples include <a href="/wiki/P%E2%80%93n_diode" title="P–n diode">p–n diodes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_junction_transistor" title="Bipolar junction transistor">bipolar transistors</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Holes_in_quantum_chemistry">Holes in quantum chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electron_hole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Holes in quantum chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An alternate meaning for the term <b>electron hole</b> is used in <a href="/wiki/Computational_chemistry" title="Computational chemistry">computational chemistry</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Coupled_cluster" title="Coupled cluster">coupled cluster</a> methods, the ground (or lowest energy) state of a molecule is interpreted as the "vacuum state"—conceptually, in this state there are no electrons. In this scheme, the absence of an electron from a normally filled state is called a "hole" and is treated as a particle, and the presence of an electron in a normally empty state is simply called an "electron". This terminology is almost identical to that used in solid-state physics.</p>
