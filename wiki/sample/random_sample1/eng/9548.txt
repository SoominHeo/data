<p><i><b>Papaver somniferum</b></i>, the <b>opium poppy</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSBI07_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BSBI07-2">[2]</a></sup> is a species of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plant</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Papaveraceae" title="Papaveraceae">Papaveraceae</a>. It is the species of plant from which <a href="/wiki/Opium" title="Opium">opium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poppy_seed" title="Poppy seed">poppy seeds</a> are derived and is a valuable ornamental plant, grown in gardens. Its native range is probably the eastern Mediterranean, but is now obscured by ancient introductions and cultivation.</p>
<p>The opium poppy is the only species of <a href="/wiki/Papaveraceae" title="Papaveraceae">Papaveraceae</a> that is grown as an agricultural crop on a large scale. Other poppy species, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Papaver_rhoeas" title="Papaver rhoeas">Papaver rhoeas</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Papaver_argemone" title="Papaver argemone">Papaver argemone</a></i>, are important agricultural weeds, and may be mistaken for the crop.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papaver_somniferum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><i>Papaver somniferum</i> is an annual herb growing to about 100 cm. All parts of the plant are strongly <a href="/wiki/Glaucous" title="Glaucous">glaucous</a>, giving a greyish-green appearance, and the stem and leaves are sparsely covered with coarse hairs. The leaves are lobed and clasp the stem at the base. The flowers are up to 120mm diameter, normally with four white, mauve or red petals, sometimes with dark markings at the base. The fruit is a hairless, rounded capsule topped with 12–18 radiating <a href="/wiki/Stigma_(botany)" title="Stigma (botany)">stigmatic</a> rays. All parts of the plant exude white latex when wounded. The annual habit in combination with stem-clasping, glaucous, glabrous (or almost so) leaves are diagnostic for the species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stace_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stace-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:87</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blamey_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blamey-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:32</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties_and_cultivars">Varieties and cultivars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papaver_somniferum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Varieties and cultivars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Papaver somniferum</i> has many <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> or <a href="/wiki/Variety_(botany)" title="Variety (botany)">varieties</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a>. Colors of the flower vary widely, as do other physical characteristics, such as number and shape of petals, number of flowers and fruits, number of seeds, color of seeds, production of opium, etc.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><i>Papaver somniferum</i> Paeoniflorum Group (sometimes called <i>Papaver paeoniflorum</i>) is a subtype of opium poppy whose flowers are highly <a href="/wiki/Double-flowered" title="Double-flowered">double</a>, and are grown in many colors. <i>P. somniferum</i> Laciniatum Group (sometimes called <i>Papaver laciniatum</i>) is a subtype of opium poppy whose flowers are highly double and deeply lobed, to the point of looking like a ruffly <a href="/wiki/Pom-pom" title="Pom-pom">pom-pom</a>.</p>
<p>A few of the varieties and cultivars, notably the cultivars "Norman" and "Przemko",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> have low morphine content (less than 1%), and much higher concentrations of other alkaloids. Most varieties, however, including those most popular for ornamental use or seed production, have a higher morphine content, with the average content being 10%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papaver_somniferum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The native range of opium poppy is probably the Eastern Mediterranean, but extensive cultivation and introduction of the species throughout Europe since ancient times have obscured its origin. It has escaped from cultivation, or has been introduced and become naturalized extensively in all regions of the British Isles, particularly in the south and east<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSBImap_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-BSBImap-7">[7]</a></sup> and in almost all other countries of the world with suitable, temperate climates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GBIF_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-GBIF-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opiates">Opiates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papaver_somniferum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Opiates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The opium poppy, as its name indicates, is the principal source of <a href="/wiki/Opium" title="Opium">opium</a>, the dried <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a> produced by the <a href="/wiki/Ovary_(botany)" title="Ovary (botany)">seed pods</a>. Opium contains a class of naturally occurring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaloids" title="Alkaloids">alkaloids</a> known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opiates" title="Opiates">opiates</a>, that include <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thebaine" title="Thebaine">thebaine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Codeine" title="Codeine">codeine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papaverine" title="Papaverine">papaverine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noscapine" title="Noscapine">noscapine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oripavine" title="Oripavine">oripavine</a>. The Latin epithet <i>somniferum</i> means "sleep-bringing", referring to the <a href="/wiki/Sedative" title="Sedative">sedative</a> properties of some of these opiates.</p>
<p>The opiate drugs are extracted from opium. The latex oozes from incisions made on the green seed pods and is collected once dry. Tincture of opium or <a href="/wiki/Laudanum" title="Laudanum">laudanum</a>, consisting of opium dissolved in alcohol or a mixture of alcohol and water, is one of many unapproved drugs regulated by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA). Its marketing and distribution persists because its historical use preceded the Federal Food, Drug &amp; Cosmetic Act of 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unapproved_Drugs_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Unapproved_Drugs-9">[9]</a></sup> Tincture of opium B.P., containing 1% w/v of anhydrous morphine, also remains in the <a href="/wiki/British_Pharmacopoeia" title="British Pharmacopoeia">British Pharmacopoeia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_controlled_drugs_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of controlled drugs in the United Kingdom">listed as a Class A</a> substance under the <a href="/wiki/Misuse_of_Drugs_Act_1971" title="Misuse of Drugs Act 1971">Misuse of Drugs Act 1971</a>.</p>
<p>Morphine is the predominant alkaloid found in the cultivated varieties of opium poppy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INCB_TR_2008_IV_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-INCB_TR_2008_IV-11">[11]</a></sup> Raw opium contains about 8–14% morphine by dry weight, or more in high-yield <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kapoor_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kapoor-12">[12]</a></sup> It may be used directly or chemically modified to produce synthetic opioids such as <a href="/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poppy_seeds_and_oil">Poppy seeds and oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papaver_somniferum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Poppy seeds and oil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Poppy_seed" title="Poppy seed">Poppy seed</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Makowiec.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1567" data-file-width="2208" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Makowiec.jpg/220px-Makowiec.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Makowiec.jpg/330px-Makowiec.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Makowiec.jpg/440px-Makowiec.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Makowiec_(pastry)" title="Makowiec (pastry)"><i>makowiec</i></a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nut_roll" title="Nut roll">nut roll</a> filled with poppy seed paste, used commonly in Poland, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a></div>

<p>Poppy seeds from <i>Papaver somniferum</i> are an important food item and the source of <a href="/wiki/Poppyseed_oil" title="Poppyseed oil">poppyseed oil</a>, an edible oil that has many uses. The seeds contain very low levels of opiates and the oil extracted from them contains even less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Both the oil and the seed residue also have commercial uses.</p>
<p>Poppy seeds are used as a food in many cultures. They may be used entire by bakers to decorate their products or milled and mixed with sugar as a sweet filling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amazon.com_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-amazon.com-14">[14]</a></sup> They have a creamy and nut-like flavor, and when used with ground coconut, the seeds provide a unique and flavour-rich curry base. They can be dry roasted and ground to be used in wet curry (curry paste) or dry curry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papaver_somniferum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Use of the opium poppy predates written history. Images of opium poppies have been found in ancient <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> artifacts (<i>circa</i> 4000 BC). The making and use of opium was known to the <a href="/wiki/Minoan_civilization" title="Minoan civilization">ancient Minoans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Its sap was later named <i>opion</i> by the ancient <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greeks</a>, from whence it gained its modern name of <i>opium</i>.</p>
<p>Opium was used for treating <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, stomach illnesses, and bad eyesight.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/First_Opium_War" title="First Opium War">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Opium_War" title="Second Opium War">Second Opium Wars</a> among China, the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> and France took place in the late 1830s through the early 1860s, when the Chinese attempted to stop western traders from selling and later smuggling opium into their country from the large crops grown in India. The British in particular had a deep trade deficit with China, and the sale of British-owned Indian opium helped balance it.</p>
<p>Many modern writers, particularly in the 19th century, have written on the opium poppy and its effects, notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_de_Quincey" title="Thomas de Quincey">Thomas de Quincey</a> in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confessions_of_an_English_Opium_Eater" title="Confessions of an English Opium Eater">Confessions of an English Opium Eater</a></i>.</p>
<p>The French Romantic composer <a href="/wiki/Hector_Berlioz" title="Hector Berlioz">Hector Berlioz</a> used opium for inspiration, subsequently producing his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symphonie_Fantastique" title="Symphonie Fantastique">Symphonie Fantastique</a>. In this work, a young artist overdoses on opium and experiences a series of visions of his unrequited love.</p>
<p>Opium poppies (flower and fruit) appear on the coat of arms of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Anaesthetists" title="Royal College of Anaesthetists">Royal College of Anaesthetists</a>.</p>

<ul>
<li>In most of <b>Central Europe</b> (Austria, Croatia, Czech Republic, Germany, Hungary, Poland, Serbia, Slovakia, Slovenia, Switzerland) poppy seed is commonly used for traditional pastries and cakes. It is legal to grow poppies in all of these countries, though Germany requires a licence.</li>
<li>The <b>United Kingdom</b> does not require a licence for opium poppy cultivation, but does for extracting opium for medicinal products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Canada</b> forbids possessing, seeking or obtaining opium poppy <i>(Papaver somniferum)</i>, its preparations, derivatives, alkaloids and salts, although an exception is made for poppy seeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Italy</b> forbids cultivation of <i>P. somniferum</i> to extract the alkaloids, but small numbers of specimens can be grown without special permits for purely ornamental purposes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>In <b>Australia</b> <i>P. somniferum</i> is illegal to cultivate. 100 plants are required for determining a court of trial and 25 plants is considered intent to sell and supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a></b>: cultivation of the opium poppy is illegal, as is possession of poppy seeds. At least one man has been imprisoned for possessing poppy seeds obtained from a bread roll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <b><a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a></b> it is legal to cultivate opium poppy as long as it is not used to produce banned drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma</a></b> bans cultivation in certain provinces. In northern Burma bans have ended a century-old tradition of growing opium poppy. Between 20,000 and 30,000 former poppy farmers left the <a href="/wiki/Kokang" title="Kokang">Kokang</a> region as a result of the ban in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> People from the <a href="/wiki/Wa_State" title="Wa State">Wa</a> region, where the ban was implemented in 2005, fled to areas where growing opium is still possible.</li>
<li>In the <b>United States</b> cultivation of poppies for food is illegal. However, this is not typically enforced for poppies grown or sold for food or ornamental purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amazon.com_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-amazon.com-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (July 2016)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup> In addition, "Opium poppy and poppy straw" are also prohibited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> As the opium poppy is legal for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Culinary" title="Culinary">culinary</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esthetic" title="Esthetic">esthetic</a> reasons, poppies were once grown as a cash crop by farmers in California. The law of poppy cultivation in the United States is somewhat ambiguous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The reason for the ambiguity is because the Opium Poppy Control Act of 1942 (now <a href="/wiki/Repeal" title="Repeal">repealed</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> stated that any opium poppy should be declared illegal, even if the farmers were issued a state <a href="/wiki/License" title="License">permit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> § 3 of the Opium Poppy Control Act stated:
<blockquote>
<p>It shall be unlawful for any person who is not the holder of a license authorizing him to produce the opium poppy, duly issued to him by the Secretary of the Treasury in accordance with the provisions of this Act, to produce the opium poppy, or to permit the production of the opium poppy in or upon any place owned, occupied, used, or controlled by him.</p>
</blockquote>
This led to the Poppy Rebellion, and to the Narcotics Bureau arresting anyone planting opium poppies and forcing the destruction of poppy fields of anyone who defied the prohibition of poppy cultivation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Though the press of those days favored the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Narcotics" title="Federal Bureau of Narcotics">Federal Bureau of Narcotics</a>, the state of California supported the farmers who grew opium poppies for their seeds for uses in foods such as <a href="/wiki/Poppyseed_muffin" title="Poppyseed muffin">poppyseed muffins</a>. Today, this area of law has remained vague and remains somewhat controversial in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The Opium Poppy Control Act of 1942 was repealed on 27 October 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papaver_somniferum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Australia (Tasmania), Turkey and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> are the major producers of poppy for medicinal purposes and poppy-based drugs, such as <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Codeine" title="Codeine">codeine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The USA has a policy of sourcing 80% of its narcotic raw materials from the traditional producers, India and Turkey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>A recent initiative to extend opium production for medicinal purposes called Poppy for Medicine was launched by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Senlis_Council" title="The Senlis Council">The Senlis Council</a> which proposes that <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> could produce medicinal opium under a scheme similar to that operating in Turkey and India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The Council proposes licensing poppy production in Afghanistan, within an integrated control system supported by the Afghan government and its international allies, to promote economic growth in the country, create vital drugs and combat poverty and the diversion of illegal opium to drug traffickers and terrorist elements. Senlis is on record advocating reintroduction of poppy into areas of Afghanistan, specifically Kunduz, which has been poppy free for some time.</p>
<p>The Senlis proposal is based in part on the assertion that there is an acute global shortage of opium poppy-based medicines some of which (morphine) are on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Health_Organisation" title="World Health Organisation">World Health Organisation</a>'s list of <a href="/wiki/WHO_Model_List_of_Essential_Medicines" title="WHO Model List of Essential Medicines">essential drugs</a> as they are the most effective way of relieving severe pain. This assertion is contradicted by the <a class="external text" href="http://www.incb.org" rel="nofollow">International Narcotics Control Board (INCB)</a>, the "independent and quasi-judicial control organ monitoring the implementation of the United Nations drug control conventions". INCB reports that the supply of opiates is greatly in excess of demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2010, researchers from the Department of Biological Sciences at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Calgary" title="University of Calgary">University of Calgary</a> published an article in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_Chemical_Biology" title="Nature Chemical Biology">Nature Chemical Biology</a></i> about their discovery of two enzymes and their encoding genes, thebaine 6-O-demethylase (T6ODM) and codeine O-demethylase (CODM), involved in morphine <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">biosynthesis</a> derived from the opium poppy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCenzymes_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-UCenzymes-39">[39]</a></sup> The enzymes were identified as non-<a href="/wiki/Heme" title="Heme">heme</a> di<a href="/wiki/Oxygenase" title="Oxygenase">oxygenases</a>, and were isolated using <a href="/wiki/Functional_genomics" title="Functional genomics">functional genomics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCenzymes_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-UCenzymes-39">[39]</a></sup> Codeine O-demethylase produces the enzyme that converts codeine into morphine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-convert_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-convert-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2007, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_government" title="British government">British government</a> permitted the pharmaceutical company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macfarlan_Smith" title="Macfarlan Smith">Macfarlan Smith</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Matthey" title="Johnson Matthey">Johnson Matthey</a> company, FTSE 100) to cultivate opium poppies in England for medicinal reasons<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berr.gov.uk_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-berr.gov.uk-41">[41]</a></sup> after Macfarlan Smith's primary source, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, decided to increase the price of export opium latex. This move is well received by British farmers,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> with a major opium poppy field based in <a href="/wiki/Didcot" title="Didcot">Didcot</a>, England. As of 2012, they were growing in Dorset, Hampshire, Oxfordshire and Lincolnshire as a spring-sown breakcrop recognised under the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Single_payment_scheme&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Single payment scheme (page does not exist)">single payment scheme</a> farm subsidy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Fair_Trading" title="Office of Fair Trading">Office of Fair Trading</a> has alerted the government to their monopoly position on growing in the UK and worldwide production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamorphine" title="Diamorphine">diamorphine</a> and recommended consideration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berr.gov.uk_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-berr.gov.uk-41">[41]</a></sup> The government's response advocated the <i>status quo</i>, being concerned interference might cause the company to stop production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ornamental_cultivation">Ornamental cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papaver_somniferum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ornamental cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Poppy_from_above.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Poppy_from_above.JPG/220px-Poppy_from_above.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Poppy_from_above.JPG/330px-Poppy_from_above.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Poppy_from_above.JPG/440px-Poppy_from_above.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

A red opium poppy flower used for ornamental purposes</div>

<p>Once known as the "common garden poppy",<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> live plants and seeds of the opium poppy are widely sold by seed companies and nurseries in most of the western world, including the United States. Poppies are sought after by gardeners for the vivid coloration of the blooms, the hardiness and reliability of the poppy plants, the exotic chocolate-vegetal fragrance note of some cultivars, and the ease of growing the plants from purchased flats of seedlings or by direct sowing of the seed. Poppy seed pods are also sold for dried flower arrangements.</p>
<p>Since "opium poppy and poppy straw" are listed in Schedule II of the United States' <a href="/wiki/Controlled_Substances_Act" title="Controlled Substances Act">Controlled Substances Act</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DEA" title="DEA">DEA</a> license may be required to grow poppies in ornamental or display gardens. In fact, the legal status of strictly ornamental poppy gardens is more nuanced, and destruction of ornamental poppy installations or prosecution of gardeners (except those caught extracting opium via capsule scarification or tea extraction) are virtually unheard of.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> During the summer, opium poppies can be seen flowering in gardens throughout North America and Europe, and displays are found in many private plantings, as well as in public botanical and museum gardens such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Botanical_Garden" title="United States Botanical Garden">United States Botanical Garden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Botanical_Garden" title="Missouri Botanical Garden">Missouri Botanical Garden</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Botanical_Garden" title="North Carolina Botanical Garden">North Carolina Botanical Garden</a>.</p>
<p>Many countries grow the plants, and some rely heavily on the commercial production of the drug as a major source of income. As an additional source of profit, the seeds of the same plants are sold for use in foods, so the cultivation of the plant is a significant source of income. This international trade in seeds of <i>P. somniferum</i> was addressed by a UN resolution "to fight the international trade in illicit opium poppy seeds" on 28 July 1998.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papaver_somniferum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Afghanistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Opium_production_in_Afghanistan" title="Opium production in Afghanistan">Opium production in Afghanistan</a></div>
<p>After the ousting of the Taliban from the town of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marja,_Afghanistan" title="Marja, Afghanistan">Marja</a> in the southern Afghan province <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helmand" title="Helmand">Helmand</a> by <a href="/wiki/Operation_Moshtarak" title="Operation Moshtarak">Operation Moshtarak</a>, American and NATO commanders were confronted with the dilemma of, on the one hand, the need to "win the hearts and minds" of the local population and, on the other, the need to eradicate poppy fields and destroy the opium economy that allegedly financed the Taliban insurgency. It has been speculated that US Marines were initially ordered to ignore the crops to avoid trampling the local farmers' livelihood, and that this might have been because there were no significant poppy fields there before the first US forces arrived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
