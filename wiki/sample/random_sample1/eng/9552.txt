<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lift (force)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluid</a> flowing past the surface of a body exerts a <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> on it. <b>Lift</b> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_(geometric)#Vector_components" title="Vector (geometric)">component</a> of this force that is perpendicular to the oncoming flow direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It contrasts with the <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a> force, which is the component of the surface force parallel to the flow direction. If the fluid is air, the force is called an <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_force" title="Aerodynamic force">aerodynamic force</a>. In water, it is called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrodynamic" title="Hydrodynamic">hydrodynamic force</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2484" data-file-width="4504" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg/300px-Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg/450px-Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg/600px-Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Airfoil_lift_and_drag.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Lift is defined as the component of the total aerodynamic force perpendicular to the flow direction, and drag is the component parallel to the flow direction</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Lift is most commonly associated with the <a href="/wiki/Wing" title="Wing">wing</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Fixed-wing_aircraft" title="Fixed-wing aircraft">fixed-wing aircraft</a>, although lift is also generated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propeller_(aircraft)" title="Propeller (aircraft)">propellers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kite_types" title="Kite types">kites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helicopter_rotor" title="Helicopter rotor">helicopter rotors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">rudders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sail" title="Sail">sails</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keel" title="Keel">keels</a> on <a href="/wiki/Sailboat" title="Sailboat">sailboats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrofoil" title="Hydrofoil">hydrofoils</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wing_(automotive)" title="Wing (automotive)">wings</a> on <a href="/wiki/Auto_racing" title="Auto racing">auto racing</a> cars, <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a>, and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Streamlined" title="Streamlined">streamlined</a> objects. Lift is also exploited in the animal world, and even in the plant world by the seeds of certain trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> While the common meaning of the word "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lift#English" title="wikt:lift">lift</a>" assumes that lift opposes weight, lift in the technical sense used in this article can be in any direction with respect to gravity, since it is defined with respect to the direction of flow rather than to the direction of gravity. When an aircraft is flying straight and level (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cruise_(flight)" title="Cruise (flight)">cruise</a>) most of the lift opposes gravity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, when an aircraft is <a href="/wiki/Climb_(aeronautics)" title="Climb (aeronautics)">climbing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descent_(aircraft)" title="Descent (aircraft)">descending</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Banked_turn#Aviation" title="Banked turn">banking</a> in a turn the lift is tilted with respect to the vertical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Lift may also be entirely downwards in some <a href="/wiki/Aerobatics" title="Aerobatics">aerobatic manoeuvres</a>, or on the wing on a racing car. In this last case, the term <a href="/wiki/Downforce" title="Downforce">downforce</a> is often used. Lift may also be largely horizontal, for instance on a sail on a sailboat.</p>
<p>Aerodynamic lift is distinguished from other kinds of lift in fluids. Aerodynamic lift requires relative motion of the fluid which distinguishes it from <a href="/wiki/Aerostatics" title="Aerostatics">aerostatic</a> lift or <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> lift as used by balloons, blimps, and dirigibles. Aerodynamic lift usually refers to situations in which the body is completely immersed in the fluid, and is thus distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Planing_(boat)" title="Planing (boat)">planing lift</a> as used by motorboats, surfboards, and water-skis, in which only a lower portion of the body is immersed in the lifting fluid flow.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Simplified_physical_explanations_of_lift_on_an_airfoil">Simplified physical explanations of lift on an airfoil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Simplified physical explanations of lift on an airfoil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Airfoil_cross_section.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1626" data-file-width="2224" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Airfoil_cross_section.jpg/200px-Airfoil_cross_section.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Airfoil_cross_section.jpg/300px-Airfoil_cross_section.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Airfoil_cross_section.jpg/400px-Airfoil_cross_section.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Airfoil_cross_section.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A cross-section of a wing defines an airfoil shape</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">airfoil</a> is a streamlined shape that is capable of generating significantly more lift than drag.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A flat plate can generate lift, but not as much as a streamlined airfoil, and with somewhat higher drag.</p>
<p>There are several ways to explain how an airfoil generates lift. Some are more complicated or more mathematically rigorous than others; some have been shown to be incorrect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_Incorrect_Theory1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_Incorrect_Theory1-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swartz_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-swartz-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gentry_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gentry-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alphatrainer.com_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-alphatrainer.com-10">[10]</a></sup> For example, there are explanations based directly on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newton%E2%80%99s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton’s laws of motion">Newton’s laws of motion</a> and explanations based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernoulli%E2%80%99s_principle" title="Bernoulli’s principle">Bernoulli’s principle</a>. Either can be used to explain lift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flow_deflection_and_Newton.27s_laws">Flow deflection and Newton's laws</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flow deflection and Newton's laws">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NASANewtons3rdGlennResearchCenter.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="180" data-file-width="318" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/66/NASANewtons3rdGlennResearchCenter.gif/220px-NASANewtons3rdGlennResearchCenter.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/6/66/NASANewtons3rdGlennResearchCenter.gif 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NASANewtons3rdGlennResearchCenter.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Newton's third law says that for every action there is an equal and opposite re-action. When an airfoil deflects air downwards, the air exerts an upward force on the airfoil.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An airfoil generates lift by exerting a downward force on the air as it flows past. According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newton%27s_third_law" title="Newton's third law">Newton's third law</a>, the air must exert an equal and opposite (upward) force on the airfoil, which is the lift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HR_378a_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-HR_378a-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_and_Eberhardt_2001_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson_and_Eberhardt_2001-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langewiesche_1944_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langewiesche_1944-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morwood.60_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morwood.60-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The air flow changes direction as it passes the airfoil and follows a path that is curved downward. According to Newton's second law, this change in flow direction requires a downward force applied to the air by the airfoil. Then, according to Newton's third law, the air must exert an upward force on the airfoil. The overall result is that a reaction force, the lift, is generated opposite to the directional change. In the case of an airplane wing, the wing exerts a downward force on the air and the air exerts an upward force on the wing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LL_68_69_153_155_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-LL_68_69_153_155-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langewiesche_6_10_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langewiesche_6_10-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>The downward turning of the flow is not produced solely by the lower surface of the airfoil, and the air flow above the foil accounts for much of the downward-turning action.</p>
<p>This simple application of Newton's laws does not provide a complete theory of lift. In particular, it does not explain how the downforce on the air arises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Increased_flow_speed_and_Bernoulli.27s_principle">Increased flow speed and Bernoulli's principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Increased flow speed and Bernoulli's principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bernoulli%27s_principle" title="Bernoulli's principle">Bernoulli's principle</a> states that within a steady airflow of constant energy, when the air flows through a region of lower pressure it speeds up and vice versa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Thus, there is a direct mathematical relationship between the pressure and the speed, so if one knows the speed at all points within the airflow one can calculate the pressure, and vice versa. For any airfoil generating lift, there must be a pressure imbalance, i.e. lower average air pressure on the top than on the bottom. Bernoulli's principle states that this pressure difference must be accompanied by a speed difference.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_of_mass">Conservation of mass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation of mass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:304px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Streamlines_around_a_NACA_0012.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="164" data-file-width="302" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Streamlines_around_a_NACA_0012.svg/302px-Streamlines_around_a_NACA_0012.svg.png" width="302"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">Streamlines and streamtubes around a <a href="/wiki/NACA_airfoil" title="NACA airfoil">NACA 0012 airfoil</a> at moderate angle of attack. Note the overall downward deflection of the air, as well as narrower streamtubes above and wider streamtubes below the foil.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Starting with the flow pattern observed in both theory and experiments, the increased flow speed over the upper surface can be explained in terms of streamtube pinching and <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_mass" title="Conservation of mass">conservation of mass</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_Flight_5.19_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson_Flight_5.19-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Streamlines,_streaklines,_and_pathlines" title="Streamlines, streaklines, and pathlines">streamlines</a> divide the flow around the airfoil into <i>streamtubes</i> as depicted by the spaces between the streamlines in the adjacent diagram. By definition, fluid never crosses a streamline in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steady_flow" title="Steady flow">steady flow</a>. Assuming that the air is incompressible, the rate of volume flow (e.g. liters or gallons per minute) must be constant within each streamtube since matter is not created or destroyed. If a streamtube becomes narrower, the flow speed must increase in the narrower region to maintain the constant flow rate. This is an application of the principle of <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_mass" title="Conservation of mass">conservation of mass</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The picture shows that the upper stream tubes constrict as they flow up and around the airfoil. Conservation of mass says that the flow speed must increase as the stream tube area decreases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_Flight_5.19_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson_Flight_5.19-24">[24]</a></sup> Similarly, the lower stream tubes expand and the flow slows down.</p>
<p>From Bernoulli's principle, the pressure on the upper surface where the flow is moving faster is lower than the pressure on the lower surface where it is moving slower. This pressure difference creates a net <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_force" title="Aerodynamic force">aerodynamic force</a>, pointing upward.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_of_explanations_based_on_Bernoulli.27s_principle">Limitations of explanations based on Bernoulli's principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Limitations of explanations based on Bernoulli's principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>The explanation above does not explain why the streamtubes change size. To see why the air flows the way it does requires more sophisticated analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>Sometimes a geometrical argument is offered to demonstrate why the streamtubes change size: it is asserted that the top "obstructs" or "constricts" the air more than the bottom, hence narrower streamtubes. For conventional wings that are flat on the bottom and curved on top this makes some intuitive sense. But it does not explain how flat plates, symmetric airfoils, sailboat sails, or conventional airfoils flying upside down can generate lift, and attempts to calculate lift based on the amount of constriction do not predict experimental results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lift_force#False_explanation_based_on_equal_transit-time" title="Lift force">common explanation</a> using Bernoulli's principle asserts that the air must traverse both the top and bottom in the same amount of time and that this explains the increased speed on the (longer) top side of the wing. But this assertion is false; it is typically the case that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_parcel" title="Air parcel">air parcels</a> traveling over the upper surface will reach the trailing edge before those traveling over the bottom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_attributes_of_lift">Basic attributes of lift</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Basic attributes of lift">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lift is a result of pressure differences and depends on angle of attack, airfoil shape, air density, and airspeed.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pressure_differences">Pressure differences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pressure differences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">Pressure</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Stress_(mechanics)#Normal_and_shear_stresses" title="Stress (mechanics)">normal force</a> per unit area exerted by the air on itself and on surfaces that it touches. The lift force is transmitted through the pressure, which acts perpendicular to the surface of the airfoil. The air maintains physical contact at all points. Thus, the net force manifests itself as pressure differences. The direction of the net force implies that the average pressure on the upper surface of the airfoil is lower than the average pressure on the underside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>These pressure differences arise in conjunction with the curved air flow. Whenever a fluid follows a curved path, there is a pressure <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">gradient</a> perpendicular to the flow direction with higher pressure on the outside of the curve and lower pressure on the inside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Babinsky_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Babinsky-38">[38]</a></sup> This direct relationship between curved streamlines and pressure differences was derived from Newton's second law by <a href="/wiki/Leonhard_Euler" title="Leonhard Euler">Leonhard Euler</a> in 1754:</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Angle_of_attack">Angle of attack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Angle of attack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Airfoil_angle_of_attack.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="246" data-file-width="800" height="92" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Airfoil_angle_of_attack.jpg/300px-Airfoil_angle_of_attack.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Airfoil_angle_of_attack.jpg/450px-Airfoil_angle_of_attack.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Airfoil_angle_of_attack.jpg/600px-Airfoil_angle_of_attack.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Airfoil_angle_of_attack.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Angle of attack of an airfoil</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">angle of attack</a> is the angle between the <a href="/wiki/Chord_(aeronautics)" title="Chord (aeronautics)">chord line</a> of an airfoil and the oncoming air. A symmetrical airfoil will generate zero lift at zero angle of attack. But as the angle of attack increases, the air is deflected through a larger angle and the vertical component of the airstream velocity increases, resulting in more lift. For small angles a symmetrical airfoil will generate a lift force roughly proportional to the angle of attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the angle of attack grows larger, the lift reaches a maximum at some angle; increasing the angle of attack beyond this <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack#Critical_angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">critical angle of attack</a> causes the upper-surface flow to separate from the wing; there is less deflection downward so the airfoil generates less lift. The airfoil is said to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stall_(flight)" title="Stall (flight)">stalled</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airfoil_shape">Airfoil shape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Airfoil shape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Airfoil_camber.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2024" data-file-width="4170" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Airfoil_camber.jpg/300px-Airfoil_camber.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Airfoil_camber.jpg/450px-Airfoil_camber.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Airfoil_camber.jpg/600px-Airfoil_camber.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Airfoil_camber.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An airfoil with camber compared to a symmetrical airfoil</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The lift force depends on the shape of the airfoil, especially the amount of <a href="/wiki/Camber_(aerodynamics)" title="Camber (aerodynamics)">camber</a> (curvature such that the upper surface is more convex than the lower surface, as illustrated at right). Increasing the camber generally increases lift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cambered airfoils will generate lift at zero angle of attack. When the chord line is horizontal, the trailing edge has a downward direction and since the air follows the trailing edge it is deflected downward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> When a cambered airfoil is upside down, the angle of attack can be adjusted so that the lift force is upwards. This explains how a plane can fly upside down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The wings of birds and most subsonic aircraft have <a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">spans</a> much larger than their chords. For wings of this general shape (often referred to as having a high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspect-ratio" title="Aspect-ratio">aspect-ratio</a>), the most important features of the lifting flow can be explained in terms of the two-dimensional flow around an <a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">airfoil</a>, which is just the shape of a cross-section of the wing, as illustrated in the drawing at right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Most of the discussion in this article concentrates on two-dimensional airfoil flow. However, the flow around a three-dimensional wing involves significant additional issues, and these are discussed below under <a href="#Lift_of_three-dimensional_wings">Lift of three dimensional wings</a>. For a wing of low aspect ratio, such as a <a href="/wiki/Delta_wing" title="Delta wing">delta wing</a>, two-dimensional airfoil flow is not relevant, and three-dimensional flow effects dominate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_speed_and_density">Air speed and density</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Air speed and density">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The flow conditions also affect lift. Lift is proportional to the density of the air and approximately proportional to the square of the flow speed. Lift also depends on the size of the wing, being generally proportional to the wing's area projected in the lift direction. In aerodynamic theory and engineering calculations it is often convenient to quantify lift in terms of a "Lift coefficient" defined in a way that makes use of these proportionalities.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="A_more_comprehensive_physical_explanation">A more comprehensive physical explanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: A more comprehensive physical explanation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As described above, there are two main popular explanations of lift, one based on downward deflection of the flow combined with Newton's laws, and one based on changes in flow speed combined with Bernoulli's principle. Either of these, by itself, correctly identifies some aspects of the lifting flow but leaves other important aspects of the phenomenon unexplained. A more comprehensive explanation involves both downward deflection and changes in flow speed, and requires looking at the flow in more detail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McLean_2012.2C_Section_7.3.3_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-McLean_2012.2C_Section_7.3.3-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lift_involves_action_and_reaction_at_the_airfoil_surface_and_is_felt_as_a_pressure_difference">Lift involves action and reaction at the airfoil surface and is felt as a pressure difference</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Lift involves action and reaction at the airfoil surface and is felt as a pressure difference">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The airfoil shape and angle of attack work together so that the airfoil exerts a downward force on the air as it flows past. According to Newton's third law, the air must then exert an equal and opposite (upward) force on the airfoil, which is the lift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_and_Eberhardt_2001_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson_and_Eberhardt_2001-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langewiesche_1944_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Langewiesche_1944-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The force is exerted by the air as a pressure difference on the airfoil's surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milne-Thomson_1966.2C_Section_1.41_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Milne-Thomson_1966.2C_Section_1.41-54">[54]</a></sup> Pressure in a fluid is always positive in an absolute sense,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeans_1967.2C_Section_33_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jeans_1967.2C_Section_33-55">[55]</a></sup> so that pressure must always be thought of as pushing, and never as pulling. The pressure thus pushes inward on the airfoil everywhere on both the upper and lower surfaces. The flowing air reacts to the presence of the wing by reducing the pressure on the wing's upper surface and increasing the pressure on the lower surface. The pressure on the lower surface pushes up harder than the reduced pressure on the upper surface pushes down, and the net result is upward lift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milne-Thomson_1966.2C_Section_1.41_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Milne-Thomson_1966.2C_Section_1.41-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The pressure difference that exerts lift acts directly on the airfoil surfaces. But understanding how the pressure difference is produced requires understanding what the flow does over a wider area.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_airfoil_affects_the_flow_over_a_wide_area_around_it">The airfoil affects the flow over a wide area around it</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: The airfoil affects the flow over a wide area around it">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>An airfoil affects the speed and direction of the flow over a wide area. When an airfoil produces lift, the flow ahead of the airfoil is deflected upward, the flow above and below the airfoil is deflected downward, and the flow behind the airfoil is deflected upward again, leaving the air far behind the airfoil flowing in the same direction as the oncoming flow far ahead. The flow above the upper surface is always speeded up, and the flow below the airfoil is usually slowed down. The downward deflection and the changes in flow speed are pronounced and extend over a wide area, as can be seen in the flow animation on the right. These differences in the direction and speed of the flow are greatest close to the airfoil and decrease gradually far above and below. All of these features of the velocity field also appear in theoretical models for lifting flows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clancy_1975.2C_Section_4.5_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clancy_1975.2C_Section_4.5-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>The pressure is also affected over a wide area. When an airfoil produces lift, there is always a diffuse region of low pressure above the airfoil, and there is usually a diffuse region of high pressure below, as illustrated by the isobars (curves of constant pressure) in the drawing. The pressure difference that acts on the surface is just part of this spread-out pattern of non-uniform pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McLean_2012.2C_Section_7.3.3_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-McLean_2012.2C_Section_7.3.3-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_pressure_differences_and_the_changes_in_flow_speed_and_direction_support_each_other_in_a_mutual_interaction">The pressure differences and the changes in flow speed and direction support each other in a mutual interaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: The pressure differences and the changes in flow speed and direction support each other in a mutual interaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The non-uniform pressure exerts forces on the air in the direction from higher pressure to lower pressure. The direction of the force is different at different locations around the airfoil, as indicated by the block arrows in the <i>pressure distribution with isobars</i> figure. Air above the airfoil is pushed toward the center of the low-pressure region, and air below the airfoil is pushed outward from the center of the high-pressure region.</p>
<p>According to <i>Newton's second law</i>, a force causes air to accelerate in the direction of the force. Thus the vertical arrows in the <i>pressure distribution with isobars</i> figure indicate that air above and below the airfoil is accelerated, or turned, downward, and that the non-uniform pressure is thus the cause of the downward deflection of the flow visible in the flow animation. To produce this downward turning, the airfoil must have a positive angle of attack or have its rear portion curved downward as on an airfoil with camber. Note that the downward turning of the flow over the upper surface is the result of the air being pushed downward by higher pressure above it than below it.</p>
<p>The arrows ahead of the airfoil indicate that the flow ahead of the airfoil is deflected upward, and the arrows behind the airfoil indicate that the flow behind is deflected upward again, after being deflected downward over the airfoil. These defections are also visible in the flow animation.</p>
<p>The arrows ahead of the airfoil and behind also indicate that air passing through the low-pressure region above the airfoil is speeded up as it enters, and slowed back down as it leaves. Air passing through the high-pressure region below the airfoil sees the opposite: It is slowed down and then speeded back up. Thus the non-uniform pressure is also the cause of the changes in flow speed visible in the flow animation. The changes in flow speed are consistent with <i>Bernoulli's principle</i>, which states that in a steady flow without <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a>, lower pressure means higher speed, and higher pressure means lower speed.</p>
<p>Thus changes in flow direction and speed are directly caused by the non-uniform pressure. But this cause-and-effect relationship is not just one-way; it works in both directions simultaneously. The air's motion is affected by the pressure differences, but the existence of the pressure differences depends on the air's motion. The relationship is thus a mutual, or reciprocal, interaction: Air flow changes speed or direction in response to pressure differences, and the pressure differences are sustained by the air's resistance to changing speed or direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> A pressure difference can exist only if something is there for it to push against. In the case of an aerodynamic flow, what a pressure difference pushes against is the inertia of the air, as the air is accelerated by the pressure difference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McLean_2012.2C_Section_7.3.3_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-McLean_2012.2C_Section_7.3.3-53">[53]</a></sup> And this is why the mass of the air is important, and why lift depends on air density.</p>
<p>In summary, sustaining the pressure difference that exerts the lift force on the airfoil surfaces requires sustaining a pattern of non-uniform pressure spread over a wide area around the airfoil. This requires maintaining pressure differences in both the vertical and horizontal directions, and thus requires both downward turning of the flow and changes in flow speed according to Bernoulli's principle. The pressure differences and the changes in flow direction and speed sustain each other in a mutual interaction. The pressure differences follow naturally from Newton's second law and from the fact that the flow along the surface naturally follows the predominantly downward-sloping contours of the airfoil. And the fact that the air has mass is crucial to the interaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McLean_2012.2C_Section_7.3.3_53-3"><a href="#cite_note-McLean_2012.2C_Section_7.3.3-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_understanding_of_lift_as_a_physical_phenomenon">The understanding of lift as a physical phenomenon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: The understanding of lift as a physical phenomenon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scientific understanding of lift is based on mathematical theories of <i>continuum fluid mechanics</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> that are in turn based on established principles of physics, as discussed below under "<a href="#Mathematical_theories_of_lift">Mathematical theories of lift</a>". The applications of these theories to aerodynamic flows have been agreed upon by the scientific and engineering communities since the early 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Starting with just the shape of the lifting surface and the general flow conditions (airspeed, density, and angle of attack), the existence of lift, the amount of lift, and all of the important details of the lifting flow have been predicted successfully by the theories. Thus lift can be considered to be thoroughly understood in a scientific sense. Furthermore, the quantitative theories provide useful quantitative information for engineering purposes.</p>
<p>Simplified physical explanations of lift, without mathematics, are also useful for purposes of understanding, especially by non-technical audiences. These qualitative explanations are by their nature less rigorous and are thus not as well established as the mathematical theories, and they cannot provide quantitative information for engineering. A difficulty in devising such explanations is finding a satisfactory balance between completeness on one hand, and simplicity and brevity on the other. Fluid flows in general are complex phenomena, and simplified explanations are seldom completely satisfactory. Many different explanations of lift have been proposed, reflecting different choices of what aspect of the flow to emphasize. In many cases, oversimplification has led to incompleteness and/or outright errors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_Incorrect_Theory1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_Incorrect_Theory1-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gentry_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gentry-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alphatrainer.com_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-alphatrainer.com-10">[10]</a></sup> There has been a long history of disagreement and controversy, even into recent years, but only regarding the qualitative explanations, not the science itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_theories_of_lift">Mathematical theories of lift</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Mathematical theories of lift">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The mathematical theories are based on continuum fluid mechanics, in which it is assumed that air flows as if it were a continuous fluid. Lift is generated in accordance with the fundamental principles of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, the most relevant being the following three principles:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_momentum" title="Conservation of momentum">Conservation of momentum</a>, which is a direct consequence of <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">Newton's laws of motion</a>, especially Newton's second law which relates the net <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> on an element of air to its rate of <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> change,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Continuity_equation#Fluid_dynamics" title="Continuity equation">Conservation of Mass</a>, including the common assumption that the airfoil's surface is impermeable for the air flowing around, and</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">Conservation of energy</a>, which says that energy is neither created nor destroyed.</li>
</ul>
<p>Because an airfoil affects the flow in a wide area around it, these physical principles must be enforced at all points throughout an extended region. To do this requires expressing the conservation principles in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_differential_equations" title="Partial differential equations">partial-differential equations</a> combined with a set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boundary_conditions" title="Boundary conditions">boundary conditions</a> (requirements the flow has to satisfy at the airfoil surface and far away from the airfoil).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>To predict lift requires solving the equations for a particular airfoil shape and flow condition, which generally requires calculations that are so voluminous that they are practical only on a computer, through the methods of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computational_Fluid_Dynamics" title="Computational Fluid Dynamics">Computational Fluid Dynamics</a> (CFD). Determining the net aerodynamic pressure from a CFD solution requires "adding up" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integration_(mathematics)" title="Integration (mathematics)">integrating</a>) the pressures determined by the CFD over the surface of the airfoil as described under "<a href="#Pressure_integration">Pressure integration</a>".</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navier-Stokes_equations" title="Navier-Stokes equations">Navier-Stokes equations</a> (NS) provide the potentially most accurate theory of lift, but in practice, capturing the effects of turbulence in the boundary layer on the airfoil surface requires sacrificing some accuracy and using the <a href="/wiki/Reynolds-averaged_Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations" title="Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes equations">Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes equations</a> (RANS), as explained below. Simpler but less accurate theories have also been developed and are described below.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Navier-Stokes_.28NS.29_equations">Navier-Stokes (NS) equations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Navier-Stokes (NS) equations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These equations represent conservation of mass, Newton's second law (conservation of momentum), conservation of energy, the <a href="/wiki/Viscosity#Newtonian_and_non-Newtonian_fluids" title="Viscosity">Newtonian law for the action of viscosity</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourier_heat_conduction_equation#Fourier.27s_law" title="Fourier heat conduction equation">Fourier heat conduction law</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_state" title="Equation of state">equation of state</a> relating density, temperature, and pressure, and formulas for the viscosity and thermal conductivity of the fluid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>In principle, the NS equations, combined with boundary conditions of no through-flow and <a href="/wiki/No-slip_condition" title="No-slip condition">no slip</a> at the airfoil surface, could be used to predict lift in any situation in ordinary atmospheric flight with high accuracy. However, lifting flows in practical situations always involve turbulence in the boundary layer next to the airfoil surface, at least over the aft portion of the airfoil. Predicting lift by solving the NS equations in their raw form would require the calculations to resolve the details of the turbulence, down to the smallest eddy. This is not yet possible, even on the most powerful current computer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spalart_2000_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spalart_2000-71">[71]</a></sup> So in principle the NS equations provide a complete and very accurate theory of lift, but practical prediction of lift requires that the effects of turbulence be modeled in the RANS equations rather than computed directly.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reynolds-Averaged_Navier-Stokes_.28RANS.29_equations">Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These are the NS equations with the turbulence motions averaged over time, and the effects of the turbulence on the time-averaged flow represented by <a href="/wiki/Turbulence_modeling" title="Turbulence modeling">turbulence modeling</a> (an additional set of equations based on a combination of <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensional analysis</a> and empirical information on how turbulence affects a boundary layer in a time-averaged average sense).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> A RANS solution consists of the time-averaged velocity vector, pressure, density, and temperature defined at a dense grid of points surrounding the airfoil.</p>
<p>The amount of computation required is a minuscule fraction (billionths)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spalart_2000_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spalart_2000-71">[71]</a></sup> of what would be required to resolve all of the turbulence motions in a raw NS calculation, and with large computers available it is now practical to carry out RANS calculations for complete airplanes in three dimensions. Because turbulence models are not perfect, the accuracy of RANS calculations is imperfect, but it is good enough to be very helpful to airplane designers. Lift predicted by RANS is usually within a few percent of the actual lift.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inviscid-flow_equations_.28Euler_or_potential.29">Inviscid-flow equations (Euler or potential)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Inviscid-flow equations (Euler or potential)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Euler_equations_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Euler equations (fluid dynamics)">Euler equations</a> are the NS equations with the viscosity, heat conduction, and turbulence effects deleted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> As with a RANS solution, an Euler solution consists of the velocity vector, pressure, density, and temperature defined at a dense grid of points surrounding the airfoil. While the Euler equations are simpler than the NS equations, they still do not lend themselves to exact analytic solutions. Further simplification is available through <a href="/wiki/Potential_flow" title="Potential flow">potential flow</a> theory, which reduces the number of unknowns that must be solved for and makes analytic solutions possible in some cases, as described below.</p>
<p>Either Euler or potential-flow calculations predict the pressure distribution on the airfoil surfaces roughly correctly for angles of attack below stall, where they might miss the total lift by as much as 10-20%. At angles of attack above stall, inviscid calculations do not predict that stall has happened, and as a result they grossly overestimate the lift.</p>
<p>In potential-flow theory, the flow is assumed to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irrotational" title="Irrotational">irrotational</a>, i.e. that small fluid parcels have no net rate of rotation. Mathematically, this is expressed by the statement that the <a href="/wiki/Curl_(mathematics)" title="Curl (mathematics)">curl</a> of the velocity vector field is everywhere equal to zero. Irrotational flows have the convenient property that the velocity can be expressed as the <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">gradient</a> of a scalar function called a <a href="/wiki/Potential" title="Potential">potential</a>. A flow represented in this way is called <a href="/wiki/Potential_flow" title="Potential flow">potential flow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>In potential-flow theory, the flow is usually further assumed to be incompressible. Incompressible potential-flow theory has the advantage that the equation (<a href="/wiki/Laplace%27s_equation" title="Laplace's equation">Laplace's equation</a>) to be solved for the potential is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear#Physics" title="Linear">linear</a>, which allows solutions to be constructed by <a href="/wiki/Superposition_principle" title="Superposition principle">superposition</a> of other known solutions. The incompressible-potential-flow equation can also be solved by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conformal_mapping" title="Conformal mapping">conformal mapping</a>, a method based on the theory of functions of a complex variable. In the early 20th century, before computers were available, conformal mapping was used to generate solutions to the incompressible potential-flow equation for a class of idealized airfoil shapes, providing some of the first practical theoretical predictions of the pressure distribution on a lifting airfoil.</p>
<p>A solution of the potential equation directly determines only the velocity field. The pressure field is deduced from the velocity field through Bernoulli's equation.</p>

<p>Applying potential-flow theory to a lifting flow requires special treatment and an additional assumption. The problem arises because lift on an airfoil in inviscid flow requires <a href="/wiki/Circulation_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Circulation (fluid dynamics)">circulation</a> in the flow around the airfoil (See "<a href="#Circulation_and_the_Kutta-Joukowski_theorem">Circulation and the Kutta-Joukowski theorem</a>" below), but a single potential function that is continuous throughout the domain around the airfoil cannot represent a flow with nonzero circulation. The solution to this problem is to introduce a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Branch_cut" title="Branch cut">branch cut</a>, a curve or line from some point on the airfoil surface out to infinite distance, and to allow a jump in the value of the potential across the cut. The jump in the potential imposes circulation in the flow equal to the potential jump and thus allows nonzero circulation to be represented. However, the potential jump is a free parameter that is not determined by the potential equation or the other boundary conditions, and the solution is thus indeterminate. A potential-flow solution exists for any value of the circulation and any value of the lift. One way to resolve this indeterminacy is to impose the <a href="/wiki/Kutta_condition" title="Kutta condition">Kutta condition</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> which is that, of all the possible solutions, the physically reasonable solution is the one in which the flow leaves the trailing edge smoothly. The streamline sketches illustrate one flow pattern with zero lift, in which the flow goes around the trailing edge and leaves the upper surface ahead of the trailing edge, and another flow pattern with positive lift, in which the flow leaves smoothly at the trailing edge in accordance with the Kutta condition.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linearized_potential_flow">Linearized potential flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Linearized potential flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This is potential-flow theory with the further assumptions that the airfoil is very thin and the angle of attack is small.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> The linearized theory predicts the general character of the airfoil pressure distribution and how it is influenced by airfoil shape and angle of attack, but is not accurate enough for design work. For a 2D airfoil, such calculations can be done in a fraction of a second in a spreadsheet on a PC.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Circulation_and_the_Kutta-Joukowski_theorem">Circulation and the Kutta-Joukowski theorem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Circulation and the Kutta-Joukowski theorem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kutta%E2%80%93Joukowski_theorem" title="Kutta–Joukowski theorem">Kutta-Joukowski theorem</a> relates the lift on an airfoil to a circulatory component (<a href="/wiki/Circulation_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Circulation (fluid dynamics)">circulation</a>) of the flow around the airfoil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clancy_1975.2C_Section_4.5_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Clancy_1975.2C_Section_4.5-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-von_Mises_1959.2C_Section_VIII.2_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-von_Mises_1959.2C_Section_VIII.2-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson1991.2C_Section_3.15_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson1991.2C_Section_3.15-83">[83]</a></sup> Kutta-Joukowski is not a complete theory of lift in the same sense as those listed above because it does not predict how much circulation or lift a given airfoil will produce. Calculating the lift from Kutta-Joukowski requires a known value for the circulation.</p>

<p>Several components of the overall velocity field contribute to the circulation: the upward flow ahead of the airfoil, the accelerated flow above the airfoil, the decelerated flow below the airfoil, and the downward flow behind the airfoil. One derivation of the Kutta-Joukowski theorem involves integrating the fluxes of vertical momentum ahead of the airfoil and behind (on vertical planes extending to large distances above and below), taking the difference, and showing that the result is related to both the lift and the circulation. The flux of upward momentum ahead of the airfoil is found to account for half the lift, and the flux of downward momentum behind the airfoil is found to account for the other half, a result that also applies to three-dimensional wings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clancy_1975.2C_Section_4.5_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-Clancy_1975.2C_Section_4.5-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-von_Mises_1959.2C_Section_VIII.2_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-von_Mises_1959.2C_Section_VIII.2-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson1991.2C_Section_3.15_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson1991.2C_Section_3.15-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Kutta-Joukowski theorem is a key element in an explanation of lift that follows the development of the flow around an airfoil as the airfoil starts its motion from rest and a starting vortex is formed and left behind, leading to the formation of circulation around the airfoil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> Lift is then inferred from the Kutta-Joukowski theorem. This explanation is largely mathematical, and its general progression is based on logical inference, not physical cause-and-effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>The circulation around a conventional airfoil, and hence the lift it generates, is dictated by both its design and the flight conditions, such as forward velocity and angle of attack. Lift can be increased by artificially increasing the circulation, for example by boundary-layer blowing or the use of <a href="/wiki/Blown_flap" title="Blown flap">blown flaps</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Flettner_rotor" title="Flettner rotor">Flettner rotor</a> the entire airfoil is circular and spins about a spanwise axis to create the circulation.</p>

<p>The flow around a lifting airfoil must satisfy Newton's second law, or conservation of momentum, both locally at every point in the flow field, and in an integrated sense over any extended region of the flow. For an extended region, Newton's second law takes the form of the <i>momentum theorem for a control volume</i>, where a <a href="/wiki/Control_volume" title="Control volume">control volume</a> can be any region of the flow chosen for analysis. The momentum theorem states that the integrated force exerted at the boundaries of the control volume (a surface integral), is equal to the integrated time rate of change (<a href="/wiki/Material_derivative" title="Material derivative">material derivative</a>) of the momentum of fluid parcels passing through the interior of the control volume (a volume integral).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2015)">not in citation given</span></a> (<a href="/wiki/Talk:Lift_(force)#Suggested_revisions" title="Talk:Lift (force)">See discussion.</a>)</i>]</sup> For a steady flow, the volume integral can be replaced by the net surface integral of the flux of momentum through the boundary.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2015)">not in citation given</span></a> (<a href="/wiki/Talk:Lift_(force)#Suggested_revisions" title="Talk:Lift (force)">See discussion.</a>)</i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
<p>The lifting flow around a 2D airfoil is usually analyzed in a control volume that completely surrounds the airfoil, so that the inner boundary of the control volume is the airfoil surface, where the downward force per unit span <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

</math></span><img alt="-L'" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3931e98988127dcc8b46d2ff7772ddecd324a762" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:4.104ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span> is exerted on the fluid by the airfoil. The outer boundary is usually either a large circle or a large rectangle. At this outer boundary distant from the airfoil, the velocity and pressure are well represented by the velocity and pressure associated with a uniform flow plus a vortex, and viscous stress is negligible, so that the only force that must be integrated over the outer boundary is the pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lissaman_1996_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lissaman_1996-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Durand_1932_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-Durand_1932-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batchelor_1967_p._407_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-Batchelor_1967_p._407-91">[91]</a></sup> The free-stream velocity is usually assumed to be horizontal, with lift vertically upward, so that the vertical momentum is the component of interest.</p>
<p>For the free-air case (no ground plane), it is found that the force <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

</math></span><img alt="-L'" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3931e98988127dcc8b46d2ff7772ddecd324a762" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:4.104ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span> exerted by the airfoil on the fluid is manifested partly as momentum fluxes and partly as pressure differences at the outer boundary, in proportions that depend on the shape of the outer boundary, as shown in the diagram at right. For a flat horizontal rectangle that is much longer than it is tall, the fluxes of vertical momentum through the front and back are negligible, and the lift is accounted for entirely by the integrated pressure differences on the top and bottom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lissaman_1996_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lissaman_1996-89">[89]</a></sup> For a square or circle, the momentum fluxes and pressure differences account for half the lift each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lissaman_1996_89-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lissaman_1996-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Durand_1932_90-1"><a href="#cite_note-Durand_1932-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batchelor_1967_p._407_91-1"><a href="#cite_note-Batchelor_1967_p._407-91">[91]</a></sup> For a vertical rectangle that is much taller than it is wide, the unbalanced pressure forces on the top and bottom are negligible, and lift is accounted for entirely by momentum fluxes, with a flux of upward momentum that enters the control volume through the front accounting for half the lift, and a flux of downward momentum that exits the control volume through the back accounting for the other half.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lissaman_1996_89-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lissaman_1996-89">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>The results of all of the control-volume analyses described above are consistent with the Kutta-Joukowski theorem described in the previous subsection. Both the tall rectangle and circle control volumes have been used in derivations of the theorem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Durand_1932_90-2"><a href="#cite_note-Durand_1932-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batchelor_1967_p._407_91-2"><a href="#cite_note-Batchelor_1967_p._407-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p>When a ground plane is present, there is a pattern of higher-than-ambient pressure on the ground below an airplane in flight, as shown on the right<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> For steady, level flight, the integrated pressure force associated with this pattern is equal to the total aerodynamic lift of the airplane and to the airplane's weight. According to Newton's third law, this pressure force exerted on the ground by the air is matched by an equal-and-opposite upward force exerted on the air by the ground, which offsets all of the downward force exerted on the air by the airplane. The net force due to the lift, acting on the atmosphere as a whole, is therefore zero, and there is thus no integrated accumulation of vertical momentum in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lift_of_three-dimensional_wings">Lift of three-dimensional wings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Lift of three-dimensional wings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wing_isobars.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1150" data-file-width="3220" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Wing_isobars.jpg/300px-Wing_isobars.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Wing_isobars.jpg/450px-Wing_isobars.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Wing_isobars.jpg/600px-Wing_isobars.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>

</div>
</div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wing_velocity_vectors.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1182" data-file-width="3200" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Wing_velocity_vectors.jpg/300px-Wing_velocity_vectors.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Wing_velocity_vectors.jpg/450px-Wing_velocity_vectors.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Wing_velocity_vectors.jpg/600px-Wing_velocity_vectors.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wing_velocity_vectors.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>
</div>
<p>For wings of moderate-to-high <a href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio_(aeronautics)" title="Aspect ratio (aeronautics)">aspect ratio</a>, the flow at any station along the span except close to the tips behaves much like flow around a two-dimensional airfoil, and most explanations of lift, like those above, concentrate on two-dimensional flow. However, even for wings of high aspect ratio, the three-dimensional effects associated with finite span are significant across the whole span, not just close to the tips.</p>
<p>The lift tends to decrease in the spanwise direction from root to tip, and the pressure distributions around the airfoil sections change accordingly in the spanwise direction. Pressure distributions in planes perpendicular to the flight direction tend to look like the illustration at right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> This spanwise-varying pressure distribution is sustained by a mutual interaction with the velocity field. Flow below the wing is accelerated outboard, flow outboard of the tips is accelerated upward, and flow above the wing is accelerated inboard, which results in the flow pattern illustrated at right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is more downward turning of the flow than there would be in a two-dimensional flow with the same airfoil shape and sectional lift, and a higher sectional angle of attack is required to achieve the same lift compared to a two-dimensional flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> The wing is effectively flying in a downdraft of its own making, as if the freestream flow were tilted downward, with the result that the total aerodynamic force vector is tilted backward slightly compared to what it would be in two dimensions. The additional backward component of the force vector is called <a href="/wiki/Lift-induced_drag" title="Lift-induced drag">lift-induced drag</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tip_vortex_rollup.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="551" data-file-width="743" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Tip_vortex_rollup.png/300px-Tip_vortex_rollup.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Tip_vortex_rollup.png/450px-Tip_vortex_rollup.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Tip_vortex_rollup.png/600px-Tip_vortex_rollup.png 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tip_vortex_rollup.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Euler computation of a tip vortex rolling up from the trailed vorticity sheet.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The difference in the spanwise component of velocity above and below the wing (between being in the inboard direction above and in the outboard direction below) persists at the trailing edge and into the wake downstream. After the flow leaves the trailing edge, this difference in velocity takes place across a relatively thin shear layer called a vortex sheet. As the vortex sheet is convected downstream from the trailing edge, it rolls up at its outer edges, eventually forming distinct wingtip vortices. The combination of the wingtip vortices and the vortex sheets feeding them is called the vortex wake.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wing_horseshoe_vortex.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="3332" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Wing_horseshoe_vortex.jpg/300px-Wing_horseshoe_vortex.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Wing_horseshoe_vortex.jpg/450px-Wing_horseshoe_vortex.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Wing_horseshoe_vortex.jpg/600px-Wing_horseshoe_vortex.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wing_horseshoe_vortex.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Planview of a wing showing the horseshoe vortex system.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In addition to the vorticity in the trailing vortex wake there is vorticity in the wing's boundary layer, which is often called the bound vorticity and which connects the trailing sheets from the two sides of the wing into a vortex system in the general form of a horseshoe. The horseshoe form of the vortex system was recognized by the British aeronautical pioneer Lanchester in 1907.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></p>
<p>Given the distribution of bound vorticity and the vorticity in the wake, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biot-Savart_law" title="Biot-Savart law">Biot-Savart law</a> (a vector-calculus relation) can be used to calculate the velocity perturbation anywhere in the field, caused by the lift on the wing. Approximate theories for the lift distribution and lift-induced drag of three-dimensional wings are based on such analysis applied to the wing's horseshoe vortex system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> In these theories, the bound vorticity is usually idealized and assumed to reside at the camber surface inside the wing.</p>
<p>Because the velocity is deduced from the vorticity in such theories, there is a tendency for some authors to describe the situation in terms that imply that the vorticity is the cause of the velocity perturbations, using terms such as "the velocity induced by the vortex," for example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> But attributing causation to the vorticity in this way is not consistent with the physics. The real cause of the velocity perturbations is the pressure field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Viscous_effects:_Profile_drag_and_stalling">Viscous effects: Profile drag and stalling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Viscous effects: Profile drag and stalling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flow_separation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="189" data-file-width="320" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/67/Flow_separation.jpg" width="320"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">Airflow separating from a wing at a high <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_attack" title="Angle of attack">angle of attack</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>No matter how smooth the surface of an airfoil seems, any real surface is rough on the scale of air molecules. Air molecules flying into the surface bounce off the rough surface in random directions not related to their incoming directions. The result is that when the air is viewed as if it were a continuous material, it is seen to be unable to slide along the surface, and the air's tangential velocity at the surface goes to practically zero, something known as the <a href="/wiki/No-slip_condition" title="No-slip condition">no-slip condition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> Because the air at the surface has near-zero velocity, and air away from the surface is moving, there is a thin <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">boundary layer</a> in which the air close to the surface is subjected to a shearing motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_1991.2C_Chapter_17_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson_1991.2C_Chapter_17-106">[106]</a></sup> The air's <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> resists the shearing, giving rise to a shear stress at the airfoil's surface called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skin_friction#Skin_friction" title="Skin friction">skin-friction drag</a>. Over most of the surface of most airfoils, the boundary layer is naturally turbulent, which increases skin-friction drag.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_1991.2C_Chapter_17_106-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson_1991.2C_Chapter_17-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doenhoff_1958_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doenhoff_1958-107">[107]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under usual flight conditions, the boundary layer remains attached to both the upper and lower surfaces all the way to the trailing edge, and its effect on the rest of the flow is modest. Compared to the predictions of inviscid-flow theory, in which there is no boundary layer, the attached boundary layer reduces the lift by a modest amount and modifies the pressure distribution somewhat, which results in a viscosity-related pressure drag over and above the skin-friction drag. The total of the skin-friction drag and the viscosity-related pressure drag is usually called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Profile_drag#Form_drag" title="Profile drag">profile drag</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doenhoff_1958_107-1"><a href="#cite_note-Doenhoff_1958-107">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
<p>The maximum lift an airfoil can produce at a given airspeed is limited by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boundary_layer_separation" title="Boundary layer separation">boundary-layer separation</a>. As the angle of attack is increased, a point is reached where the boundary layer can no longer remain attached to the upper surface. When the boundary layer separates, it leaves a region of recirculating flow above the upper surface, as illustrated in the flow-visualization photo at right. This is known as the <i>stall</i>, or <i>stalling</i>. At angles of attack above the stall, lift is significantly reduced, though it is not zero. The maximum lift that can be achieved before stall, in terms of the <a href="/wiki/Lift_coefficient" title="Lift coefficient">lift coefficient</a>, is generally less than 2.0 for single-element airfoils and can be more than 3.0 for airfoils with high-lift slotted flaps deployed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lift_forces_on_bluff_bodies">Lift forces on bluff bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Lift forces on bluff bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Vortex_shedding" title="Vortex shedding">Vortex shedding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vortex-induced_vibration" title="Vortex-induced vibration">Vortex-induced vibration</a></div>
<p>The flow around <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bluff#Adjective" title="wikt:bluff">bluff</a> bodies – i.e. without a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/streamline" title="wikt:streamline">streamlined</a> shape, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stall_(flight)" title="Stall (flight)">stalling</a> airfoils – may also generate lift, besides a strong drag force. This lift may be steady, or it may <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillate</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Vortex_shedding" title="Vortex shedding">vortex shedding</a>. Interaction of the object's flexibility with the vortex shedding may enhance the effects of fluctuating lift and cause <a href="/wiki/Vortex-induced_vibration" title="Vortex-induced vibration">vortex-induced vibrations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamson_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williamson-110">[110]</a></sup> For instance, the flow around a circular cylinder generates a <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_vortex_street" title="Kármán vortex street">Kármán vortex street</a>: <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortices</a> being shed in an alternating fashion from each side of the cylinder. The oscillatory nature of the flow is reflected in the fluctuating lift force on the cylinder, whereas the mean lift force is negligible. The lift force <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> is characterised by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimensionless" title="Dimensionless">dimensionless</a> <a href="/wiki/Strouhal_number" title="Strouhal number">Strouhal number</a>, which depends (among others) on the <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">Reynolds number</a> of the flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup></p>
<p>For a flexible structure, this oscillatory lift force may induce <a href="/wiki/Vortex-induced_vibration" title="Vortex-induced vibration">vortex-induced vibrations</a>. Under certain conditions – for instance <a href="/wiki/Resonance" title="Resonance">resonance</a> or strong spanwise <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correlation" title="Correlation">correlation</a> of the lift force – the resulting motion of the structure due to the lift fluctuations may be strongly enhanced. Such vibrations may pose problems and threaten collapse in tall man-made structures like industrial <a href="/wiki/Chimney" title="Chimney">chimneys</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamson_110-1"><a href="#cite_note-Williamson-110">[110]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Magnus_effect" title="Magnus effect">Magnus effect</a>, a lift force is generated by a spinning cylinder in a freestream. Here the mechanical rotation acts on the boundary layer, causing it to separate at different locations on the two sides of the cylinder. The asymmetric separation changes the effective shape of the cylinder as far as the flow is concerned such that the cylinder acts like a lifting airfoil with circulation in the outer flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_explanations.2C_misconceptions.2C_and_controversies">Alternative explanations, misconceptions, and controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Alternative explanations, misconceptions, and controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many other alternative explanations for the generation of lift by an airfoil have been put forward, a few of which are presented here. Most of them are intended to explain the phenomenon of lift to a general audience. Although the explanations may share features in common with the explanations above, additional assumptions and simplifications may be introduced. This can reduce the validity of an alternative explanation to a limited sub-class of lift generating conditions, or might not allow a quantitative analysis. Several theories introduce assumptions which proved to be wrong, like the <i>equal transit-time</i> theory.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="False_explanation_based_on_equal_transit-time">False explanation based on equal transit-time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: False explanation based on equal transit-time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:588px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Equal_transit-time_NASA_wrong1.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="264" data-file-width="586" height="264" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2d/Equal_transit-time_NASA_wrong1.gif" width="586"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Equal_transit-time_NASA_wrong1.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An illustration of the incorrect equal transit-time explanation of airfoil lift.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Basic or popular sources often describe the "equal transit-time" theory of lift, which incorrectly assumes that the parcels of air that divide at the leading edge of an airfoil must rejoin at the trailing edge, forcing the air traveling along the longer upper surface to go faster. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernoulli%27s_Principle" title="Bernoulli's Principle">Bernoulli's Principle</a> is then cited to conclude that since the air moves slower along the bottom of the wing, the air pressure must be higher, pushing the wing up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, there is no physical principle that requires equal transit time and experimental results show that this assumption is false.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> In fact, the air moving over the top of an airfoil generating lift moves <i>much</i> <i>faster</i> than the equal transit theory predicts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_Incorrect_Theory2_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_Incorrect_Theory2-121">[121]</a></sup> Further, the theory violates <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion#Newton.27s_third_law" title="Newton's laws of motion">Newton's third law of motion</a>, since it describes a force on the wing with no opposite force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup></p>
<p>The assertion that the air must arrive simultaneously at the trailing edge is sometimes referred to as the "Equal Transit-Time Fallacy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy_regarding_the_Coand.C4.83_effect">Controversy regarding the Coandă effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Controversy regarding the Coandă effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coand%C4%83_effect" title="Coandă effect">Coandă effect</a></div>
<p>In its original sense, the <i>Coandă effect</i> refers to the tendency of a <a href="/wiki/Jet_(fluid)" title="Jet (fluid)">fluid jet</a> to stay attached to an adjacent surface that curves away from the flow, and the resultant <a href="/wiki/Entrainment_(hydrodynamics)" title="Entrainment (hydrodynamics)">entrainment</a> of ambient air into the flow. The effect is named for <a href="/wiki/Henri_Coand%C4%83" title="Henri Coandă">Henri Coandă</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romanian</a> aerodynamicist who exploited it in many of his patents.</p>
<p>More broadly, some consider the effect to include the tendency of any fluid <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">boundary layer</a> to adhere to a curved surface, not just the boundary layer accompanying a fluid jet. It is in this broader sense that the Coandă effect is used by some to explain why the air flow remains attached to the top side of an airfoil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scotteberhart_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-scotteberhart-128">[128]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jef_Raskin" title="Jef Raskin">Jef Raskin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raskin_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-raskin-129">[129]</a></sup> for example, describes a simple demonstration, using a straw to blow over the upper surface of a wing. The wing deflects upwards, thus demonstrating that the Coandă effect creates lift. This demonstration correctly demonstrates the Coandă effect as a fluid jet (the exhaust from a straw) adhering to a curved surface (the wing). However, the upper surface in this flow is a complicated, vortex-laden mixing layer, while on the lower surface the flow is <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quiescence" title="wikt:quiescence">quiescent</a>. The physics of this demonstration are very different from that of the general flow over the wing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auerbach_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-auerbach-130">[130]</a></sup> The usage in this sense is encountered in some popular references on aerodynamics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scotteberhart_128-1"><a href="#cite_note-scotteberhart-128">[128]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raskin_129-1"><a href="#cite_note-raskin-129">[129]</a></sup> This is a controversial use of the term "Coanda effect." The more established view in the aerodynamics field is that the Coandă effect is defined in the more limited sense above,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auerbach_130-1"><a href="#cite_note-auerbach-130">[130]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup> and the flow following the upper surface simply reflects an absence of boundary-layer separation and is not an example of the Coandă effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-133"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fmw_fluid_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-fmw_fluid-136">[136]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Misconception_regarding_the_role_of_viscosity">Misconception regarding the role of viscosity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Misconception regarding the role of viscosity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Explanations that use the term "Coandă effect" sometimes further assert that the viscosity of the flow in the boundary layer is responsible for the ability of the flow to follow the convex upper surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raskin_1994_137-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raskin_1994-137">[137]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup> However, the idea that viscosity plays a significant role in flow turning is not consistent with the physics of curved boundary-layer flows. Analysis of the momentum balance in the flow in the boundary layer shows that the flow curvature is caused almost exclusively by the pressure gradient and that viscosity plays practically no direct role in the ability of the flow to follow a curved surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-139"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Misconception_regarding_.22pulling_down.22_of_the_flow">Misconception regarding "pulling down" of the flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title='Edit section: Misconception regarding "pulling down" of the flow'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Explanations that refer to the Coandă effect sometimes also refer to the flow over the upper surface as "sticking" to the airfoil and being "pulled down" to follow the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raskin_1994_137-1"><a href="#cite_note-Raskin_1994-137">[137]</a></sup> Taken literally, this description is not consistent with the physics of gasses. For air to be pulled in the literal sense, it would have to be put in tension (negative pressure). The kinetic theory of gasses shows that in a gas at a positive absolute temperature the pressure cannot be negative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeans_1967.2C_Section_33_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jeans_1967.2C_Section_33-55">[55]</a></sup> Thus for the flow to curve downward over the upper surface, it must be pushed down by higher pressure above than below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup> The difference in pressure between the flow at the upper surface itself and the flow far above the airfoil is generally small compared with the background atmospheric pressure, so that the lowest pressure on the airfoil upper surface is still strongly positive in an absolute sense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup></p>

<li id="cite_note-115"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-115">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">"Unfortunately, this explanation falls to earth on three counts. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_reading">Further reading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lift_(force)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Further reading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="refbegin columns references-column-count references-column-count-2" style="-moz-column-count: 2; -webkit-column-count: 2; column-count: 2;">
<ul>
<li><i>Introduction to Flight</i>, John D. Anderson, Jr., McGraw-Hill, <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0072990716">ISBN 0-07-299071-6</a> – The author is the Curator of Aerodynamics at the Smithsonian Institution's National Air &amp; Space Museum and Professor Emeritus at the University of Maryland.</li>
<li><i>Understanding Flight</i>, by David Anderson and Scott Eberhardt, McGraw-Hill, <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0071363777">ISBN 0-07-136377-7</a> – The authors are a physicist and an aeronautical engineer. They explain flight in non-technical terms and specifically address the equal-transit-time myth. Turning of the flow around the wing is attributed to the Coanda effect, which is quite controversial.</li>
<li><i>Aerodynamics</i>, Clancy, L. J. (1975), Section 4.8, Pitman Publishing Limited, London <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0273011200">ISBN 0-273-01120-0</a>.</li>
<li><i>Aerodynamics, Aeronautics, and Flight Mechanics</i>, McCormick, Barnes W., (1979), Chapter 3, John Wiley &amp; Sons, Inc., New York <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0471030325">ISBN 0-471-03032-5</a>.</li>
<li><i>Fundamentals of Flight</i>, Richard S. Shevell, Prentice-Hall International Editions, <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0133329178">ISBN 0-13-332917-8</a> – This book is primarily intended as a text for a one semester undergraduate course in mechanical or aeronautical engineering, although its sections on theory of flight are understandable with a passing knowledge of calculus and physics.</li>
<li>"Observation of Perfect Potential Flow in Superfluid", Paul P. Craig and John R. Pellam (1957) <i>Physical Review</i> <b>108</b>(5), pp. 1109–1112, <a href="/wiki/Digital_object_identifier" title="Digital object identifier">doi</a>:<a class="external text" href="//dx.doi.org/10.1103%2FPhysRev.108.1109" rel="nofollow">10.1103/PhysRev.108.1109</a> – Experiments under <a href="/wiki/Superfluidity" title="Superfluidity">superfluidity</a> conditions, resulting in the vanishing of lift in inviscid flow since the <a href="/wiki/Kutta_condition" title="Kutta condition">Kutta condition</a> no longer is satisfied.</li>
<li>"Aerodynamics at the Particle Level", Charles A. Crummer (2005, revised 2012) – A treatment of aerodynamics emphasizing the particle nature of air, as opposed to the fluid approximation commonly used. <a class="external free" href="http://arxiv.org/pdf/nlin/0507032.pdf" rel="nofollow">http://arxiv.org/pdf/nlin/0507032.pdf</a></li>
<li>"Flight without Bernoulli" Chris Waltham Vol. 36, Nov. 1998 THE PHYSICS TEACHER – using a physical model relying only on Newton’s second law, the author presents a rigorous fluid dynamical treatment of flight. <a class="external free" href="http://www.df.uba.ar/users/sgil/physics_paper_doc/papers_phys/fluids/fly_no_bernoulli.pdf" rel="nofollow">http://www.df.uba.ar/users/sgil/physics_paper_doc/papers_phys/fluids/fly_no_bernoulli.pdf</a></li>
<li><i>Bernoulli, Newton, and Dynamic Lift</i> Norman F. Smith School Science and Mathematics vol 73 Part I: <a class="external free" href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1949-8594.1973.tb09040.x/pdf" rel="nofollow">http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1949-8594.1973.tb09040.x/pdf</a> Part II <a class="external free" href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1949-8594.1973.tb08998.x/pdf" rel="nofollow">http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1949-8594.1973.tb08998.x/pdf</a></li>
</ul>
