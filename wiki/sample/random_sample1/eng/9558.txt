<p><a href="/wiki/Don_(honorific)" title="Don (honorific)">Dona</a> <b>Teresa Cristina</b> (14 March 1822 – 28 December 1889), nicknamed "the Mother of the Brazilians",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> was the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Brazilian_consorts" title="List of Brazilian consorts">Empress consort</a> of Emperor Dom <a href="/wiki/Pedro_II_of_Brazil" title="Pedro II of Brazil">Pedro II of Brazil</a>, who reigned from 1831 to 1889. Born a Princess of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Two_Sicilies" title="Kingdom of the Two Sicilies">Kingdom of the Two Sicilies</a> in present-day southern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, she was the daughter of King Don <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_the_Two_Sicilies" title="Francis I of the Two Sicilies">Francesco I</a> (Francis I) of the Italian branch of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">House of Bourbon</a> and his wife <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maria_Isabella_of_Spain" title="Maria Isabella of Spain">Maria Isabel</a> (Maria Isabella). It was long believed by historians that the Princess was raised in an ultra-conservative, intolerant atmosphere which resulted in a timid and unassertive character in public and an ability to be contented with very little materially or emotionally. Recent studies revealed a more complex character, who despite having respected the social norms of the era, was able to assert a limited independence due to her strongly opinionated personality as well as her interest in learning, sciences and culture.</p>
<p>The Princess was married by proxy to Pedro II in 1843. Her spouse's expectations had been raised when a portrait was presented that depicted Teresa Cristina as an idealized beauty, but he was displeased by his bride's plain looks upon their first meeting later that year. Despite a cold beginning, the couple's relationship improved as time passed, due primarily to Teresa Cristina's patience, kindness, generosity and simplicity. These traits also helped her win the hearts of the Brazilian people, and her distance from political controversies shielded her from criticism. She also sponsored archaeological studies in Italy and Italian immigration to Brazil.</p>
<p>The marriage between Teresa Cristina and Pedro II never became passionately romantic, although a bond based upon family, mutual respect and fondness did develop. The Empress was a dutiful spouse and unfailingly supported the Emperor's positions and never interposed with her own views in public. She remained silent on the topic of his suspected extra-marital relationships—including a liaison with her daughters' governess. In turn, she was treated with unfailing respect and her position at Court and home was always secure. Of the four children Teresa Cristina bore him, two boys died in infancy and a daughter of <a href="/wiki/Typhoid_fever" title="Typhoid fever">typhoid fever</a> at the early age of 24.</p>
<p>She, along with the remaining members of the Imperial Family, was sent into exile after a coup d'état staged by a clique of army officers in 1889. Being cast from her beloved adopted land had a devastating effect on Teresa Cristina's spirit and health. Grieving and ill, she died of respiratory failure leading to cardiac arrest little more than a month after the monarchy's collapse. She was greatly loved by her subjects, both during her lifetime and afterwards. She was even respected by the Republicans who overthrew the Empire. Despite having had no direct impact on Brazil's political history, Teresa Cristina is well regarded by historians not only for her character and irreproachable behavior, but also for her sponsorship of Brazilian culture.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teresa_Cristina_of_the_Two_Sicilies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth">Birth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teresa_Cristina_of_the_Two_Sicilies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Birth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Teresa Cristina was the daughter of the then-<a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Calabria" title="Duke of Calabria">Duke of Calabria</a>, who later became King Don <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_the_Two_Sicilies" title="Francis I of the Two Sicilies">Francesco I</a> (Francis I) of the Two Sicilies. Through her father, she was a member of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon-Two_Sicilies" title="House of Bourbon-Two Sicilies">House of Bourbon-Two Sicilies</a>, also known as Bourbon-Naples, the Italian branch of the Spanish Bourbons. She was a descendant of France's "Sun King", <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a> in the male line through his grandson, Don <a href="/wiki/Philip_V_of_Spain" title="Philip V of Spain">Felipe V</a> (Philip V) of Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon1975210_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon1975210-3">[3]</a></sup> Teresa Cristina's mother was the <i>Infanta</i> <i>Doña</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maria_Isabella_of_Spain" title="Maria Isabella of Spain">Maria Isabel</a> (Maria Isabella), daughter of King Don <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV_of_Spain" title="Charles IV of Spain">Carlos IV</a> (Charles IV) of Spain, and a younger sister of <i>Doña</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carlota_of_Spain" title="Carlota of Spain">Carlota Joaquina</a>—who was the wife of King Dom <a href="/wiki/John_VI_of_Portugal" title="John VI of Portugal">João VI of Portugal</a> and the paternal grandmother of Teresa Cristina's future husband.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon1975210_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon1975210-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Born on 14 March 1822 in Naples,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZerbini200762_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZerbini200762-4">[4]</a></sup> Teresa Cristina became an orphan when her father died in 1830. Her mother is said to have neglected her after marrying a young officer in 1839.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon1975211_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon1975211-1">[1]</a></sup> Historiography has long asserted that she was raised in lonely isolation, in an environment of religious superstition, intolerance and conservatism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon1975211_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon1975211-1">[1]</a></sup> It has also described Teresa Cristina as a soft and timid character, unlike her ruthless father or her impulsive mother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon1975210_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon1975210-3">[3]</a></sup> She has even been depicted as unassertive, and accustomed to be satisfied in whatever circumstances she found herself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999365_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999365-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some historians have more recently held to a modified view of both the Neapolitan Bourbon court as a reactionary regime and of the extent of Teresa Cristina's passivity. Historian Aniello Angelo Avella states that the maligned interpretation of the Neapolitan Bourbons traces its origin to perspectives generated during the 19th century <i>il Risorgimento</i> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_unification" title="Italian unification">Italian unification</a>) following the 1861 conquest of the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies by the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sardinia" title="Kingdom of Sardinia">Kingdom of Sardinia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAvella20107_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAvella20107-6">[6]</a></sup> Teresa Cristina is revealed in her personal papers as a strong-headed character. She "was not a submissive woman but instead a person who respected the roles imposed by the ethics and values of her own times."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAvella20107_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAvella20107-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teresa_Cristina_of_the_Two_Sicilies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Imperatriz_D._Tereza_Cristina2.jpg"><img alt="A half-length painted portrait of a young woman with light brown hair, small mouth, petite nose, very small waist, and large, widely spaced eyes. In the background is a drawn drape revealing a bay with an erupting volcano behind." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1538" data-file-width="1220" height="252" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Imperatriz_D._Tereza_Cristina2.jpg/200px-Imperatriz_D._Tereza_Cristina2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Imperatriz_D._Tereza_Cristina2.jpg/300px-Imperatriz_D._Tereza_Cristina2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Imperatriz_D._Tereza_Cristina2.jpg/400px-Imperatriz_D._Tereza_Cristina2.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Imperatriz_D._Tereza_Cristina2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The portrait of Teresa Cristina that enticed Pedro II to accept the marriage proposal</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Upon learning that the young emperor of Brazil, <a href="/wiki/Dom_(title)" title="Dom (title)">Dom</a> <a href="/wiki/Pedro_II_of_Brazil" title="Pedro II of Brazil">Pedro II</a>, sought a wife, the government of the Two Sicilies offered the hand of Teresa Cristina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It also sent Pedro II a painting that greatly embellished the princess, which prompted him to accept the proposal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Proxy_marriage" title="Proxy marriage">proxy wedding</a> was held on 30 May 1843 in Naples, Pedro II being represented by his fiancée's brother <a href="/wiki/Prince_Leopold,_Count_of_Syracuse" title="Prince Leopold, Count of Syracuse">Prince Leopold, Count of Syracuse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarvalho200751_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarvalho200751-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELira_1977.2C_Vol_1122_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELira_1977.2C_Vol_1122-10">[10]</a></sup> A small Brazilian fleet composed of a frigate and two corvettes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon1975213_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon1975213-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELira_1977.2C_Vol_1120_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELira_1977.2C_Vol_1120-12">[12]</a></sup> departed for the Two Sicilies on 3 March 1843 to escort the new Empress of Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELira_1977.2C_Vol_1121_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELira_1977.2C_Vol_1121-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon1975214_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon1975214-14">[14]</a></sup> She arrived on Rio de Janeiro on 3 September 1843.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Pedro II immediately rushed to board the ship and greet his bride. Upon seeing this impetuous gesture, the crowds cheered and guns fired deafening salutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELongo200881_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELongo200881-16">[16]</a></sup> Teresa Cristina fell in love with her new husband at first sight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman199997_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman199997-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 17-year-old Pedro II was, for his part, clearly and greatly disappointed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> His first impressions were only of her physical flaws—and of how much her appearance differed from the portrait which had been sent to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman199997_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman199997-17">[17]</a></sup> Physically, she had dark brown hair<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOt.C3.A1vio_Filho1946121_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOt.C3.A1vio_Filho1946121-19">[19]</a></sup> and brown eyes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOt.C3.A1vio_Filho1946121_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOt.C3.A1vio_Filho1946121-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon1975212_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon1975212-20">[20]</a></sup> was short, slightly overweight, walked with a pronounced limp and, while not ugly, neither was she pretty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barman97_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-barman97-21">[21]</a></sup> According to historian Pedro Calmon, Teresa Cristina had no true limp, but her odd way of walking was instead the result of bowed legs causing her to lean alternately right and left as she walked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon1975213_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon1975213-11">[11]</a></sup> Pedro II's high expectations were crushed, and he allowed his feelings of revulsion and rejection to show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman199997_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman199997-17">[17]</a></sup> After a short interval, he left the ship. Perceiving his disillusionment, she burst into tears, lamenting that "the emperor did not like me!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Although a proxy marriage had already been performed, an extravagant state wedding was held on 4 September at the cathedral in Rio de Janeiro.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the marriage had been strained from the beginning, Teresa Cristina continued striving to be a good wife. Her constancy toward fulfilling her duty, along with the birth of children, softened Pedro II's attitude. The two discovered shared interests, and their concern for and delight in their children created a sense of family happiness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999126_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999126-24">[24]</a></sup> That they were sexually active and compatible is witnessed by the series of pregnancies which ensued. After the birth of their first son in February 1845, the Empress bore children in July 1846, July 1847, and July 1848—named <a href="/wiki/Afonso,_Prince_Imperial_of_Brazil" title="Afonso, Prince Imperial of Brazil">Afonso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isabel,_Princess_Imperial_of_Brazil" title="Isabel, Princess Imperial of Brazil">Isabel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Princess_Leopoldina_of_Brazil" title="Princess Leopoldina of Brazil">Leopoldina</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedro,_Prince_Imperial_of_Brazil" title="Pedro, Prince Imperial of Brazil">Pedro</a>, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999127_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999127-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Empress_consort_of_Brazil">Empress consort of Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teresa_Cristina_of_the_Two_Sicilies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Empress consort of Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_life">Domestic life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teresa_Cristina_of_the_Two_Sicilies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Domestic life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Teresa_cristina_of_brazil_circa_1851_frame_removed.png"><img alt="Photographic portrait of a woman seated and wearing a dark dress trimmed in dark lace, with her hair pulled back into a bun and no jewelry except for a simple ring on her left hand" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="996" data-file-width="748" height="266" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Teresa_cristina_of_brazil_circa_1851_frame_removed.png/200px-Teresa_cristina_of_brazil_circa_1851_frame_removed.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Teresa_cristina_of_brazil_circa_1851_frame_removed.png/300px-Teresa_cristina_of_brazil_circa_1851_frame_removed.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Teresa_cristina_of_brazil_circa_1851_frame_removed.png/400px-Teresa_cristina_of_brazil_circa_1851_frame_removed.png 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Teresa_cristina_of_brazil_circa_1851_frame_removed.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Teresa Cristina around age 29, c. 1851</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Teresa Cristina had grown to be a vital part of Pedro II's family life and routine. She never filled the roles of romantic lover or intellectual partner, however. Her devotion to the Emperor remained firm, though she feared being supplanted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999144_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999144-26">[26]</a></sup> She continued to appear with the Emperor in public, and he continued to treat her with respect and consideration. She was not rejected or slighted, but the relationship had changed. Pedro II treated her more as a close friend and companion than as a wife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999144_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999144-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>The long-held view is that the Empress accepted the circumscribed role in which she found herself, and that her life, duty and purpose were tied to her position as the Emperor's wife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999144_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999144-26">[26]</a></sup> However, her personal letters reveal that she could be strongheaded, sometimes at odds with her husband, and had a life of her own—albeit somewhat restricted. In a letter written on 2 May 1845 she stated: "I wait for the moment when we will meet, good Pedro, and seek forgiveness for all that I did to you during these days." In another letter of 24 January 1851, she acknowledged her difficult temperament: "I am not irritated at you [Pedro II] and you should forgive me because this is my character."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAvella20107_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAvella20107-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her friendships were limited to her ladies-in-waiting, and in particular Dona Josefina da Fonseca Costa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999144_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999144-26">[26]</a></sup> She was well liked by her attendants, a good judge of the character of visitors and courtiers, unpretentious, generous, kindly and an affectionate mother and grandmother. She dressed and acted modestly, only wearing jewelry for occasions of state, and gave the impression of being somewhat sad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999144_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999144-26">[26]</a></sup> She had no interest in politics and occupied her time writing letters, reading, doing needlework, and attending to religious obligations and charitable projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999144_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999144-26">[26]</a></sup> She possessed a beautiful voice, and often practiced her singing skills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZerbini200764_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZerbini200764-27">[27]</a></sup> Her appreciation for music also meant that she enjoyed opera and balls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon1975211.E2.80.93212_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon1975211.E2.80.93212-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Teresa Cristina did not lack intellectual interests, and she had developed passions for the arts, music and—in particular—archaeology. The Empress began assembling a collection of archaeological artifacts from her earliest days in Brazil, and she exchanged hundreds of others with her brother, King Don <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_the_Two_Sicilies" title="Ferdinand II of the Two Sicilies">Ferdinando II</a> (Ferdinand II).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZerbini200763.E2.80.9364_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZerbini200763.E2.80.9364-29">[29]</a></sup> She also sponsored archaeological studies in Italy and many of the artifacts—dated from the <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_civilization" title="Etruscan civilization">Etruscan civilization</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Roman period</a>—found were brought to Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAvella20104_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAvella20104-30">[30]</a></sup> The Empress also aided in recruiting Italian physicians, engineers, professors, pharmacists, nurses, artists, artisans and qualified workers with the goal of improving public education and public health in Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVanni200041.E2.80.9342_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVanni200041.E2.80.9342-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rivalry_with_the_Countess_of_Barral">Rivalry with the Countess of Barral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teresa_Cristina_of_the_Two_Sicilies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rivalry with the Countess of Barral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brazilian_Imperial_family_around_1864.jpg"><img alt="A photograph showing a bearded man seated at a table with an older woman with dark hair standing immediately to his left, and two younger women in long, mid-Victorian dresses standing one at the left side of the table and one at the right side of the table" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1364" data-file-width="860" height="317" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Brazilian_Imperial_family_around_1864.jpg/200px-Brazilian_Imperial_family_around_1864.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Brazilian_Imperial_family_around_1864.jpg/300px-Brazilian_Imperial_family_around_1864.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Brazilian_Imperial_family_around_1864.jpg/400px-Brazilian_Imperial_family_around_1864.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brazilian_Imperial_family_around_1864.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Princess_Leopoldina_of_Brazil" title="Princess Leopoldina of Brazil">Leopoldina</a>, Pedro II, Teresa Cristina (around age 41) and <a href="/wiki/Isabel,_Princess_Imperial_of_Brazil" title="Isabel, Princess Imperial of Brazil">Isabel</a>, c. 1863</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The relationship between Teresa Cristina and Pedro II never became passionately romantic. However, a bond based upon family, mutual respect and fondness did develop. The Empress was a dutiful spouse and unfailingly supported the Emperor's positions. She was silent on the subject of his relationships with other women, suspected or otherwise. In turn, she was treated with utmost respect and there was no question of her position ever being threatened or called into question.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELongo200883_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELongo200883-32">[32]</a></sup> No more children were born after July 1848, even after the death of her two sons in infancy. A likely reason for the halt to childbearing is that the Emperor became more attracted to other women who possessed beauty, wit and intelligence which the Empress could not provide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999129_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999129-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Teresa Cristina found ignoring her husband's secret infidelities—hidden from the public, though not always from the Empress—more difficult after Pedro II named an <i>aia</i> (governess) for their daughters on 9 November 1856.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman200238_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman200238-34">[34]</a></sup> The person chosen was <a href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADsa_de_Barros,_Countess_of_Barral" title="Luísa de Barros, Countess of Barral">Luísa de Barros, Countess of Barral</a>, the Brazilian-born wife of a French nobleman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman200236_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman200236-35">[35]</a></sup> Barral possessed all the traits that Pedro II most admired in a woman: she was charming, vivacious, elegant, sophisticated, educated and confident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman200239_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman200239-36">[36]</a></sup> Charged with the education and upbringing of the young princesses, Barral soon captured the hearts of both Pedro II and his eldest daughter, Isabel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman200239_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman200239-36">[36]</a></sup> Leopoldina was not won over and disliked the Countess.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman200242_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman200242-37">[37]</a></sup> Although Barral "may not have escaped Pedro II's embraces", she "certainly avoided his bed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999148_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999148-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Nonetheless, the Emperor's infatuation with the Countess sometimes put Teresa Cristina in an awkward position, as when her younger daughter Leopoldina naively asked her why Pedro II kept nudging Barral's foot during their class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarvalho200766_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarvalho200766-39">[39]</a></sup> The Countess's increasing intimacy with her husband and daughter was painful and vexing to Teresa Cristina. Although she feigned ignorance of the situation, it did not pass unnoticed. She wrote in her diary that Barral "wished to make me tell her that I did not like her, but I did not say either yes or no." Historian Tobias Monteiro wrote that the Empress "could not disguise that she detested Barral."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999148_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999148-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teresa_Cristina_of_the_Two_Sicilies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_the_Empire_and_banishment">End of the Empire and banishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teresa_Cristina_of_the_Two_Sicilies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: End of the Empire and banishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Teresa_cristina_1877.jpg"><img alt="A photographic portrait of a woman with graying hair dressed in an elaborate dark mid-Victorian period dress and leaning against the back of an upholstered chair" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="823" data-file-width="661" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Teresa_cristina_1877.jpg/220px-Teresa_cristina_1877.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Teresa_cristina_1877.jpg/330px-Teresa_cristina_1877.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Teresa_cristina_1877.jpg/440px-Teresa_cristina_1877.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Teresa_cristina_1877.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Teresa Cristina at age 55, 29 March 1877</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The death of her daughter Leopoldina of typhoid fever on 7 February 1871 devastated the small Imperial Family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999236_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999236-40">[40]</a></sup> Pedro II decided on a trip to Europe that same year to "cheer up" his wife among other reasons (as stated in his own words) and to pay a visit to Leopoldina's four small boys, who had lived in <a href="/wiki/Coburg" title="Coburg">Coburg</a> with their parents since the late 1860s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999236_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999236-40">[40]</a></sup> The Imperial couple would travel abroad again during 1876 and 1887.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999275.2C_333_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999275.2C_333-41">[41]</a></sup> Teresa Cristina preferred her ordinary life in Brazil, "dedicating herself to her family, religious devotions, and charitable works."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999365_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999365-5">[5]</a></sup> In fact, visiting her native land only served to resurrect painful memories. Her family had been dethroned in 1861 and the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies <a href="/wiki/Expedition_of_the_Thousand" title="Expedition of the Thousand">had been annexed</a> to what would later become the unified <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861%E2%80%931946)" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861–1946)">Kingdom of Italy</a>. Everyone she had known from her youth was gone. As she wrote in 1872: "I do not know how to tell what was the impression I had upon seeing again, after 28 years, my fatherland and not to find anyone for whom I cared."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZerbini200765_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZerbini200765-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Empress remained strong-willed even after years of marriage. Pedro II revealed in a letter written to the Countess of Barral in early 1881 that: "The [container] with the earrings which you mentioned, has been the cause for much recrimination on the part of someone [Teresa Cristina] who thinks that I have been to blame for their disappearance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999327_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999327-43">[43]</a></sup> Her son-in-law, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Gaston,_Count_of_Eu" title="Prince Gaston, Count of Eu">Prince Gaston, Count of Eu</a>, wrote a letter recounting how she had accidentally broken her arm in October 1885: "On Monday 26 when crossing the library on the way to dinner with the emperor who as usual preceded her by a few steps (and with whom, I infer from what she told us, she was arguing as she sometimes does), she caught her foot in a file under a table and fell down flat face forward."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999327_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999327-43">[43]</a></sup> Nonetheless, she continued to express undiminished love for her husband.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999327_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999327-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>The tranquil domestic routine ended when an Army faction rebelled and deposed Pedro II on 15 November 1889, ordering the entire Imperial Family to leave Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999357.2C_361_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999357.2C_361-44">[44]</a></sup> Upon hearing the order to depart, an officer told the Empress: "Resignation, my lady." She replied to him: "I have it always, but how not to weep having to leave this land forever!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELira_1977.2C_Vol_3114_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELira_1977.2C_Vol_3114-45">[45]</a></sup> According to historian Roderick J. Barman, the "events of November 15, 1889, broke her emotionally and physically." The Empress "loved Brazil and its inhabitants. She desired nothing more than to end her days there. Aged 66 and plagued by both cardiac asthma and arthritis, she now faced the prospect of accompanying her husband in unceasing movement across the face of Europe, spending her last years virtually alone in alien and uncomfortable lodgings."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999366_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999366-46">[46]</a></sup> Having been ill during almost the entire voyage across the Atlantic, Teresa Cristina and her family arrived in Lisbon, Portugal, on 7 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999369_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999369-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teresa_Cristina_of_the_Two_Sicilies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Teresa_cristina_circa_1887_01.jpg"><img alt="A photographic portrait of a seated woman with graying hair who is dressed in a dark and elaborate late-Victorian style dress and wearing a flowered bonnet" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1557" data-file-width="1087" height="315" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Teresa_cristina_circa_1887_01.jpg/220px-Teresa_cristina_circa_1887_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Teresa_cristina_circa_1887_01.jpg/330px-Teresa_cristina_circa_1887_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Teresa_cristina_circa_1887_01.jpg/440px-Teresa_cristina_circa_1887_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Teresa_cristina_circa_1887_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Teresa Cristina around age 65, c. 1887</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>From Lisbon the Imperial couple went on to <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999370_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999370-48">[48]</a></sup> Isabel and her family departed to Spain on a trip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999370_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999370-48">[48]</a></sup> On 24 December, the Imperial Family received official news that they had been banished forever from the country. Up until that point, they had only been requested to leave with no indication as to how long they were to stay away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999371_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999371-49">[49]</a></sup> The "news broke D. Teresa Cristina's will to live."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999371_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999371-49">[49]</a></sup> Pedro II wrote in his journal on 28 December 1889: "Hearing the Empress complain I went to see what it was. She is cold with a pain in her sides; but she does not have any fever."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999371_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999371-49">[49]</a></sup> As the day passed, Teresa Cristina's breathing became increasingly labored, and the failure of her respiratory system led to cardiac arrest and death at 2:00 pm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarman1999371_49-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarman1999371-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>As she lay dying, Teresa Cristina said to Maria Isabel de Andrade Pinto, Baroness of Japurá (sister-in-law of <a href="/wiki/Joaquim_Marques_Lisboa,_Marquis_of_Tamandar%C3%A9" title="Joaquim Marques Lisboa, Marquis of Tamandaré">Joaquim Marques Lisboa, Marquis of Tamandaré</a>): "Maria Isabel, I do not die of illness, I die of sorrow and of regret!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon19751734_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon19751734-50">[50]</a></sup> Her last words were: "I miss my daughter [Isabel] and my grandchildren. I can not embrace her for the last time. Brazil, beautiful land ... To there I can not return".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon19751735_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon19751735-51">[51]</a></sup> The streets of Porto were crowded with people gathered to witness her funeral procession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBesouchet1993565_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBesouchet1993565-52">[52]</a></sup> By request of her husband, Teresa Cristina's body was carried to the <a href="/wiki/Monastery_of_S%C3%A3o_Vicente_de_Fora" title="Monastery of São Vicente de Fora">Church of São Vicente de Fora</a> near <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, where it was interred in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Pantheon_of_the_House_of_Braganza" title="Royal Pantheon of the House of Braganza">Braganza Pantheon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon19751749.E2.80.931750_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon19751749.E2.80.931750-53">[53]</a></sup> Her remains, along with Pedro II's, were later repatriated to Brazil in 1921 with much fanfare and pomp. They were given a final resting place in the <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Petr%C3%B3polis" title="Cathedral of Petrópolis">Cathedral of Petrópolis</a> in 1939.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon19751914.E2.80.931916_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon19751914.E2.80.931916-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>The news of her death produced sincere mourning in Brazil. The Brazilian poet and journalist <a href="/wiki/Artur_Azevedo" title="Artur Azevedo">Artur Azevedo</a> wrote of the general view toward Teresa Cristina after her death: "I never spoke to her, but also never passed her without respectifuly removing my hat and bowing myself, not to the Empress, but to the sweet and honest figure of a poor, almost humble, bourgeoise. I saw many extremist republicans do the same." He continued: "They called her the mother of the Brazilians, and we all really attributed to her a kind of a filial veneration. That is the truth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalmon19751736_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalmon19751736-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>Newspapers in Brazil also reported her death. The <i>Gazeta de Notícias</i> (News Gazette) commented: "Who was this saintly lady, we do not need to repeat it. All Brazil knows that, in this blow which hurt the former Emperor profoundly, it is remembered that she was justly and universally proclaimed the mother of the Brazilians."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECenni200395_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECenni200395-56">[56]</a></sup> The <i>Jornal do Commercio</i> (Commercial Journal) wrote: "For forty and six years Dona Teresa Cristina lived in the Brazilian fatherland which she sincerely loved, and during that long time never, anywhere in this vast country, was her name pronounced except in praise and words of regard." It concluded: "Next to her husband, who was for a long time the head of the Brazilian nation, her influence was known to be felt only for the good."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECenni200394.E2.80.9395_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECenni200394.E2.80.9395-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Teresa_Cristina_of_the_Two_Sicilies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Petropolis-Cathedral1.jpg"><img alt="Inside a gothic chapel, a marble effigy of a bearded emperor in uniform and his wife lies atop an intricately carved stone sarcophagus" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Petropolis-Cathedral1.jpg/220px-Petropolis-Cathedral1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Petropolis-Cathedral1.jpg/330px-Petropolis-Cathedral1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Petropolis-Cathedral1.jpg/440px-Petropolis-Cathedral1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Petropolis-Cathedral1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tomb of Teresa Cristina and Pedro II within the <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Petr%C3%B3polis" title="Cathedral of Petrópolis">Cathedral of Petrópolis</a>, Brazil</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Teresa Cristina has been given a less than prominent place in Brazil's history. Historian Aniello Angelo Avella said that the Empress, nicknamed "by her contemporaries as 'Mother of the Brazilians'," is "completely unknown in Italy and little studied in Brazil".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAvella20101_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAvella20101-58">[58]</a></sup> According to his view, the few existing sources relegate her to having "lived in the shadow of her husband, dedicating herself to her daughters' education, to home affairs, to charity." The resulting image "is of a woman of limited culture, blank, silent, who compensated with kindness and virtues of the heart the lack of physical attributes." And this is the view that has come to be enshrined in history and the popular imagination, despite being not quite a true representation of Teresa Cristina, since she was a well learned and willful woman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAvella20101_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAvella20101-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to historian Eli Behar, Teresa Cristina became notable "for her discretion, which kept her far from being associated with any political movement; and for her tenderness and charity, which earned her the cognomen 'Mother of the Brazilians'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBehar198051_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBehar198051-59">[59]</a></sup> A similar opinion is voiced by Historian Benedito Antunes, who said that she "was beloved by Brazilians, who defined her, for her discretion, as the 'silent empress', and yet regarded her as 'the mother of the Brazilians'." He also praised the Empress for her sponsorship of cultural and scientific development: she "promoted culture in various ways, bringing from Italy artists, intellectuals, scientists, botanists, musicians, thus contributing to the progress and enrichment of the nation's cultural life."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAntunes2009183_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAntunes2009183-60">[60]</a></sup> This view is shared by historian Eugenia Zerbini, who argued that, thanks to her, Brazil now has the largest classical archaeological collection in Latin America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZerbini200763_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZerbini200763-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Just prior to his own death, Pedro II donated most of his possessions to the Brazilian government, which were later divided between the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_National_Archives" title="Brazilian National Archives">Brazilian National Archives</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Museum_of_Brazil" title="Imperial Museum of Brazil">Imperial Museum of Brazil</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_Brazil" title="National Library of Brazil">National Library of Brazil</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Historic_and_Geographic_Institute" title="Brazilian Historic and Geographic Institute">Brazilian Historic and Geographic Institute</a>. Pedro II imposed only one condition: that the gift was to be named in honor of his late wife, and so it is known as the "Teresa Cristina Maria Collection".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERodrigues2009_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERodrigues2009-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchwarcz199832_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchwarcz199832-63">[63]</a></sup> The collection is registered by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> as part of the heritage of humanity in its <a href="/wiki/Memory_of_the_World_Programme" title="Memory of the World Programme">Memory of the World Programme</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERio_.26_Cultura2009_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERio_.26_Cultura2009-64">[64]</a></sup> Finally, Teresa Cristina is remembered in the names of several Brazilian cities, including <a href="/wiki/Teres%C3%B3polis" title="Teresópolis">Teresópolis</a> (in Rio de Janeiro), <a href="/wiki/Teresina" title="Teresina">Teresina</a> (capital of <a href="/wiki/Piau%C3%AD" title="Piauí">Piauí</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cristina_(Brazil)" title="Cristina (Brazil)">Cristina</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Minas_Gerais" title="Minas Gerais">Minas Gerais</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Imperatriz" title="Imperatriz">Imperatriz</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Maranh%C3%A3o" title="Maranhão">Maranhão</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAntunes2009184_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAntunes2009184-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
