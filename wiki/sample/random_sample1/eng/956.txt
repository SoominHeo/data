<p>The <b>Grumman F9F Panther</b> was the manufacturer's first jet fighter and one of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>'s first successful <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">carrier-based</a> jet fighters. A single-engined, straight-winged day fighter, it was fitted with an armament of four 20 mm (0.79 in) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aircraft_cannon" title="Aircraft cannon">cannons</a> and could carry a wide assortment of air-to-ground munitions.</p>
<p>The Panther was used extensively by the U.S. Navy and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>. It was also the first jet aircraft used by the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Angels" title="Blue Angels">Blue Angels</a> <a href="/wiki/Aerobatics" title="Aerobatics">flight team</a>, being used by them from 1949 through to late 1954. The aircraft was exported to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> and was the first jet used by the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Naval_Aviation" title="Argentine Naval Aviation">Argentine Naval Aviation</a>.</p>
<p>Total F9F production was 1,382. The design evolved into the swept wing <a href="/wiki/Grumman_F-9_Cougar" title="Grumman F-9 Cougar">Grumman F-9 Cougar</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F9F_Panther&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:XF9F-3_XF9F-2_NAN12-48.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="389" data-file-width="802" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/XF9F-3_XF9F-2_NAN12-48.jpg/220px-XF9F-3_XF9F-2_NAN12-48.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/XF9F-3_XF9F-2_NAN12-48.jpg/330px-XF9F-3_XF9F-2_NAN12-48.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/XF9F-3_XF9F-2_NAN12-48.jpg/440px-XF9F-3_XF9F-2_NAN12-48.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:XF9F-3_XF9F-2_NAN12-48.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The XF9F-2 and XF9F-3 prototypes in 1948</div>

<p>Development studies at the <a href="/wiki/Grumman" title="Grumman">Grumman</a> company for jet-powered fighter aircraft began near the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> as the first jet engines emerged. In a competition for a jet-powered <a href="/wiki/Night_fighter" title="Night fighter">night fighter</a> for the United States navy, the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_F3D_Skyknight" title="Douglas F3D Skyknight">Douglas XF3D-1</a> was selected over <a href="/wiki/Grumman" title="Grumman">Grumman</a> Aircraft Engineering Corporation's G-75 two-seat, four-<a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_J30" title="Westinghouse J30">Westinghouse J30</a>-powered design, with Douglas being issued a contract on 3 April 1946. The U.S. Navy's <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Aeronautics" title="Bureau of Aeronautics">Bureau of Aeronautics</a> (BuAer) also issued a contract to Grumman for two Model G-75 experimental aircraft on 11 April 1946, being given the Navy designation <b>XF9F-1</b>, in case the Skyknight ran into problems.</p>
<p>Grumman soon realized the G-75 was a losing design but had been working on a completely different, single-engine day fighter known as the Grumman G-79. Due to some interesting bureaucracy, BuAer did not cancel the G-75 (XF9F-1) contract but changed the wording to include three entirely different G-79 prototypes. The G-79 became the Grumman F9F Panther.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The prototype Panther, piloted by test pilot Corky Meyer, first flew on 21 November 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> American engines available at the time included the <a href="/wiki/Allison_J33" title="Allison J33">Allison J33</a> and <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_J34" title="Westinghouse J34">Westinghouse J34</a>, but these were not considered sufficiently reliable,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> so the Navy specified the imported <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Nene" title="Rolls-Royce Nene">Rolls-Royce Nene</a> turbojet, which was also more powerful at 5,000 lb. of thrust. Production aircraft would have a Nene engine built under license by <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney">Pratt &amp; Whitney</a> as the J42. Since there was insufficient space within the wings and fuselage for fuel for the thirsty jet, permanently mounted wingtip fuel tanks were added, which incidentally improved the fighter's rate of roll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>It was cleared for flight from aircraft carriers in September 1949. During the development phase, Grumman decided to change the Panther's engine, selecting the <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_J48" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney J48">Pratt &amp; Whitney J48-P-2</a>, a license built version of the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_RB.44_Tay" title="Rolls-Royce RB.44 Tay">Rolls-Royce RB.44 Tay</a>. The other engine that had been tested was the <a href="/wiki/Allison_J33" title="Allison J33">Allison J33-A-16</a>. The armament was a quartet of 20 mm guns, the Navy having already switched to this caliber (as opposed to the USAAF/USAF which continued to use 12.7 mm M2/M3 guns). As well, the Panther soon was armed with underwing air-to-ground rockets and up to 2,000 lb (910 kg) of bombs.</p>
<p>From 1946, a swept-wing version was considered and after concerns about the Panther's inferiority to its MiG opponents in Korea, a conversion of the Panther (Design 93) resulted in a swept-wing derivative of the Panther, the <a href="/wiki/Grumman_F-9_Cougar" title="Grumman F-9 Cougar">F9F Cougar</a>, which retained the Panther's designation number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_p._506_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_p._506-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F9F_Panther&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Navy">U.S. Navy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F9F_Panther&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: U.S. Navy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:F9F-2_VF-111_NAN9-52.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="556" data-file-width="550" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/F9F-2_VF-111_NAN9-52.jpg/220px-F9F-2_VF-111_NAN9-52.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/F9F-2_VF-111_NAN9-52.jpg/330px-F9F-2_VF-111_NAN9-52.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/F9F-2_VF-111_NAN9-52.jpg/440px-F9F-2_VF-111_NAN9-52.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:F9F-2_VF-111_NAN9-52.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A VF-111 F9F-2 dropping bombs in Korea, 1951/52</div>

<p>The Grumman Panther was the primary U.S. Navy and USMC jet fighter and ground-attack aircraft in the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>. The Panther was the most widely used U.S. Navy jet fighter of the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, flying 78,000 sorties and scoring the first air-to-air kill by the U.S. Navy in the war, the downing of a <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korean</a> <a href="/wiki/Yakovlev_Yak-9" title="Yakovlev Yak-9">Yakovlev Yak-9</a> fighter. F9F-2s, F9F-3s and F9F-5s, as rugged attack aircraft, were able to sustain operations, even in the face of intense anti-aircraft fire. The pilots also appreciated the air conditioned cockpit, which was a welcome change from the humid environment of piston-powered aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite their relative slow speed, Panthers also managed to shoot down two Yak-9s and seven <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-15" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-15">Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-15s</a> for the loss of two F9Fs. On 3 July 1950, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant,_junior_grade" title="Lieutenant, junior grade">Lieutenant, junior grade</a> Leonard H. Plog of U.S. Navy's <a href="/wiki/VF-51" title="VF-51">VF-51</a> flying an F9F-3 scored the first U.S. Navy air victory of the war by shooting down a Yak-9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first MiG-15 was downed on 9 November 1950 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Commander" title="Lieutenant Commander">Lieutenant Commander</a> William (Bill) Amen of <a href="/wiki/VF-111" title="VF-111">VF-111 "Sundowners"</a> flying an F9F-2B. Two more MiG-15s were downed on 18 November 1950. The final four MiG-15s were downed on 18 November 1952 by Lt. Royce Williams of VF-781, flying off the <a href="/wiki/USS_Oriskany_(CV-34)" title="USS Oriskany (CV-34)">USS <i>Oriskany</i></a> during a series of air strikes against the North Korean port of Hoeryong, right across the mouth of the Tumen River from the major Soviet base at Vladivostok. Williams' victories were notable because all four were flown by <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Naval_Aviation" title="Soviet Naval Aviation">Soviet Naval Aviation</a> pilots.</p>

<p>In 1992, Russian authorities admitted that Captains Belyakov and Vandalov, and Lieutenants Pakhomkin and Tarshinov were lost on 18 November 1952. Information regarding this fight had been suppressed by the U.S. Navy at the time because personnel of the then-new <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">National Security Agency</a> had been involved in the intercept, and U.S. authorities were concerned that the Soviets might learn of this if the affair was publicized.</p>
<p>No other fighter pilot ever scored four MiG-15s in a single combat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Future astronaut <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a> flew the F9F extensively during the war, even ejecting from one of the aircraft when it was brought down by a wire strung across a valley, in 1951. Future astronaut <a href="/wiki/John_Glenn" title="John Glenn">John Glenn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> <a href="/wiki/All-star" title="All-star">all-star</a> baseball player <a href="/wiki/Ted_Williams" title="Ted Williams">Ted Williams</a> also flew the F9F as Marine Corps pilots.</p>
<p>Panthers were withdrawn from front-line service in 1956, but remained in training roles and with U.S. Naval Air Reserve and U.S. Marine Air Reserve units until 1958. The Navy's <a href="/wiki/Blue_Angels" title="Blue Angels">Blue Angels</a> flight demonstration team used the Panther for four years, beginning in 1951. The Panther was the Blue Angels' first jet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Some Panthers continued to serve in small numbers into the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> From September 1962 surviving operational Panthers were redesignated F-9 within the new combined US tri-service designation system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Argentine_Navy">Argentine Navy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grumman_F9F_Panther&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Argentine Navy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The only foreign buyer of the Panther was the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Naval_Aviation" title="Argentine Naval Aviation">Argentine Naval Aviation</a>, which bought 28 ex-USN F9F-2B aircraft in 1957; the first 10 arrived in 1958. Only 24 aircraft were put in service, the rest was used as spares. The first flight of an Argentine Panther was in December 1958, and the last aircraft was put in service in January 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InstAeroNav-F9F_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-InstAeroNav-F9F-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The catapults on the then only Argentine carrier, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Warrior_(R31)" title="HMS Warrior (R31)">ARA <i>Independencia</i></a>, were considered not powerful enough to launch the F9F, so the aircraft were land-based. However, in July 1963 a Panther (serial 0453/3-A-119) landed on the <i>Independencia</i> as part of trials; becoming the first jet to land on an Argentine aircraft carrier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InstAeroNav-F9F_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-InstAeroNav-F9F-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Argentine Navy F9F-2 Panthers saw combat in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1963_Argentine_Navy_Revolt" title="1963 Argentine Navy Revolt">1963 Argentine Navy Revolt</a>, bombing and strafing a column of the Army 8th Tank Regiment which was advancing on the rebelling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Base_Aeronaval_Punta_Indio" title="Base Aeronaval Punta Indio">Base Aeronaval Punta Indio</a> (English: <i>Punta Indio Naval Air Base</i>). The attack destroyed several <a href="/wiki/M4_Sherman" title="M4 Sherman">M4 Sherman</a> tanks, at the cost of one F9F Panther shot down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Argentine Panthers were involved in the general mobilization during the <a href="/wiki/Laguna_del_Desierto_incident" title="Laguna del Desierto incident">1965 border clash between Argentina and Chile</a> but no combat occurred. They were taken out of service in 1969 due to the lack of spare parts and replaced with <a href="/wiki/Douglas_A-4_Skyhawk" title="Douglas A-4 Skyhawk">Douglas A-4Q Skyhawks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Argentine Navy also operated the F-9 Cougar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InstAeroNav-F9F_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-InstAeroNav-F9F-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

<dt>F9F-2</dt>
<dd>First production version, powered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_J42" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney J42">Pratt &amp; Whitney J42</a> engine.</dd>
<dt>F9F-2B</dt>
<dd>Version fitted with underwing racks for bombs and rockets. All F9F-2s were eventually so modified, and the B designation was dropped.</dd>
<dt>F9F-2P</dt>
<dd>Unarmed photographic reconnaissance version used in Korea.</dd>
<dt>F9F-3</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Allison_J33" title="Allison J33">Allison J33</a> powered version produced as insurance against the failure of the J42, 54 built. All converted to J42 power later.</dd>
<dt>XF9F-4</dt>
<dd>Prototype used in the development of the F9F-4.</dd>
<dt>F9F-4</dt>
<dd>Version with longer fuselage with greater fuel load and powered by J33 engine. Most re-engined with J42s. F9F-4s were the first aircraft to successfully employ pressurized <a href="/wiki/Bleed_air" title="Bleed air">bleed air</a>, tapped from the engine's compressor stages, and <a href="/wiki/Blown_flap" title="Blown flap">blown across the surface</a> of the slot <a href="/wiki/Flap_(aeronautics)" title="Flap (aeronautics)">flaps</a>, simulating a higher airspeed across the control surface, and thus achieving a decrease in stalling speed of 9 kn for takeoff and 7 kn on power approach for landing.</dd>
<dt>F9F-5</dt>
<dd>Variant of F9F-4, but powered by <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_J48" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney J48">Pratt &amp; Whitney J48</a> engine, 616 built.</dd>
<dt>F9F-5P</dt>
<dd>Unarmed photo-<a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance" title="Reconnaissance">reconnaissance</a> version, 36 built, longer nose.</dd>
<dt>F9F-5K</dt>
<dd>After the F9F Panther was withdrawn operational service, a number of F9F-5s were converted into unmanned target drone aircraft.</dd>
<dt>F9F-5KD</dt>
<dd>As drone directors for the F9F-5K drones. Redesignated <b>DF-9E</b> in 1962.</dd>

<p style="font-size: 90%; margin:0; padding: .2em"><i>Data from</i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crew:</b> 1</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 37 ft 5 in (11.3 m)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">Wingspan</a>:</b> 38 ft 0 in (11.6 m)</li>
<li><b>Height:</b> 11 ft 4 in (3.8 m)</li>
<li><b>Wing area:</b> 250 ft² (23 m²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Manufacturer%27s_empty_weight" title="Manufacturer's empty weight">Empty weight</a>:</b> 9,303 lb (4,220 kg)</li>
<li><b>Loaded weight:</b> 14,235 lb (6,456 kg)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Maximum_takeoff_weight" title="Maximum takeoff weight">Max. takeoff weight</a>:</b> 16,450 lb (7,462 kg)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">Powerplant</a>:</b> 1 × <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_J42" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney J42">Pratt &amp; Whitney J42</a>-P-6/P-8 <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">turbojet</a>, 5,950 lbf (26.5 kN) with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_injection_(engines)" title="Water injection (engines)">water injection</a></li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Regulatory_V-speeds" title="V speeds">Maximum speed</a>:</b> 500 kn (575 mph, 925 km/h)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Range</a>:</b> 1,353 mi (1,175 nmi, 2,177 km)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(aeronautics)" title="Ceiling (aeronautics)">Service ceiling</a>:</b> 44,600 ft (13,600 m)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Rate_of_climb" title="Rate of climb">Rate of climb</a>:</b> 5,140 ft/min (26.1 m/s)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wing_loading" title="Wing loading">Wing loading</a>:</b> 71 lb/ft² (350 kg/m²)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Thrust-to-weight_ratio" title="Thrust-to-weight ratio">Thrust/weight</a>:</b> 0.42</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Armament</b></span><br/></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Guns:</b> 4 × 20 mm (0.79 in) <a href="/wiki/Hispano-Suiza_HS.404" title="Hispano-Suiza HS.404">AN/M3 cannon</a>, 190 rpg</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hardpoint" title="Hardpoint">Hardpoints</a>:</b> Underwing hardpoints  and provisions to carry combinations of:
<ul>
<li><b>Rockets:</b> 6 × 5 in (127 mm) rockets on underwing hardpoints</li>
<li><b>Bombs:</b> 2,000 lb (907 kg) of bombs</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
