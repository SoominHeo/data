<p>In chemistry, a <b>zwitterion</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ər/ 'er' in 'finger'">ər</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/./ syllable break">.</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">TSVIT</span></span></b>-ər-eye-ən</i></a></span>; from <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>zwitter</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ˈtsvɪtɐ]</a></span>, meaning "<a href="/wiki/Hermaphrodite" title="Hermaphrodite">hermaphrodite</a>"), formerly called a <b>dipolar ion</b>, is a neutral <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a> with both positive and negative <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_charge" title="Electrical charge">electrical charges</a>. (In some cases multiple positive and negative charges may be present.) Zwitterions are distinct from molecules that have <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">dipoles</a> at different locations within the molecule. Zwitterions are sometimes called <b>inner salts</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zwitter_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-zwitter-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike simple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a> compounds that may only form <i>either</i> a cationic or anionic species, a zwitterion simultaneously has both ionic states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zwitterion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">Amino acids</a> are the best-known examples of zwitterions. These compounds contain an <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> and a carboxylate group, and can be viewed as arising via a kind of intramolecular <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction" title="Acid–base reaction">acid–base reaction</a>: The <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a> group deprotonates the <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a>.</p>

<p>The zwitterionic structure of <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a> in the solid state has been confirmed by <a href="/wiki/Neutron_diffraction" title="Neutron diffraction">neutron diffraction</a> measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> At least in some cases, the zwitterionic form of amino acids also persists in the gas phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to the amino acids, many other compounds that contain both acidic and basic centres <a href="/wiki/Tautomer" title="Tautomer">tautomerize</a> to the zwitterionic form. Examples, such as <a href="/wiki/Bicine" title="Bicine">bicine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tricine" title="Tricine">tricine</a>, contain a basic secondary or tertiary amine fragment together with a carboxylic acid fragment. <a href="/wiki/Neutron_diffraction" title="Neutron diffraction">Neutron diffraction</a> measurements show that solid <a href="/wiki/Sulfamic_acid" title="Sulfamic acid">sulfamic acid</a> exists as a zwitterion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sass_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sass-5">[5]</a></sup> Many <a href="/wiki/Alkaloid" title="Alkaloid">alkaloids</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LSD" title="LSD">LSD</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psilocybin" title="Psilocybin">psilocybin</a>, exist as zwitterions because they contain carboxylates and ammonium centres.</p>
<p>Many zwitterions contain <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_ammonium_cation" title="Quaternary ammonium cation">quaternary ammonium cations</a>. Since it lacks N–H bonds, the ammonium center cannot participate in <a href="/wiki/Tautomer" title="Tautomer">tautomerization</a>. Zwitterions containing quaternary-ammonium centers are common in biology, a common example are the <a href="/wiki/Betaine" title="Betaine">betaines</a>, which serve as <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a> in fish. The membrane-forming phospholipids are also commonly zwitterions. The polar head groups in these compounds are zwitterions, resulting from the presence of the anionic phosphate and cationic quaternary ammonium centres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_compounds">Related compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zwitterion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Related compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dipolar_compound" title="Dipolar compound">Dipolar compounds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amine_oxide" title="Amine oxide">amine oxides</a> are not classified as zwitterions;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dipolar_compounds_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-dipolar_compounds-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="source explains what dipolar compounds are, but doesn't state that zwitterions are not included (December 2016)">not in citation given</span></a> (<a href="/wiki/Talk:Zwitterion#Problem_with_.22Related_Compounds.22" title="Talk:Zwitterion">See discussion.</a>)</i>]</sup> zwitterionic compounds are neutral compounds having <i>unit</i> electrical charges on atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zwitter_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-zwitter-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
