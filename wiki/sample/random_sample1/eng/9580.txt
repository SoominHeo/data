<p><b>Ernest Borgnine</b> (born <b>Ermes Effron Borgnino</b>; <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɔːr/ 'or' in 'abhor'">ɔːr</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/aɪ/ long 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>; January 24, 1917 – July 8, 2012) was an American film, television, character and voice actor whose career spanned more than six decades. He was noted for his gruff, yet calm voice, Machiavellian eyebrows and gap-toothed Cheshire cat grin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A popular performer, he had also appeared as a guest on numerous talk shows and as a panelist on several game shows.</p>
<p>Borgnine's 61-year career began in 1951, and included playing opposite <a href="/wiki/Jon_Hall_(actor)" title="Jon Hall (actor)">Jon Hall</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/China_Corsair" title="China Corsair">China Corsair</a></i> (1951), <a href="/wiki/Burt_Lancaster" title="Burt Lancaster">Burt Lancaster</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/From_Here_to_Eternity" title="From Here to Eternity">From Here to Eternity</a></i> (1953), <a href="/wiki/Gary_Cooper" title="Gary Cooper">Gary Cooper</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Vera_Cruz_(film)" title="Vera Cruz (film)">Vera Cruz</a></i> (1954) and <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Tracy" title="Spencer Tracy">Spencer Tracy</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Bad_Day_at_Black_Rock" title="Bad Day at Black Rock">Bad Day at Black Rock</a></i> (1955). He also played the unconventional lead in many films, winning the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Actor">Academy Award for Best Actor</a> in 1955 for <i><a href="/wiki/Marty_(film)" title="Marty (film)">Marty</a></i>. He achieved continuing success in the 1960s sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/McHale%27s_Navy" title="McHale's Navy">McHale's Navy</a></i> (1962–1966), in which Borgnine played the starring role of the title character, and co-starred opposite <a href="/wiki/Jan-Michael_Vincent" title="Jan-Michael Vincent">Jan-Michael Vincent</a> in the mid-1980s action series <i><a href="/wiki/Airwolf" title="Airwolf">Airwolf</a></i> (1984–1986) as Dominic Santini, in addition to a wide variety of other roles. Borgnine earned an <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a> nomination at age 92, for his work on the series <i><a href="/wiki/ER_(TV_series)" title="ER (TV series)">ER</a></i>. He was also known for being the original voice of <a href="/wiki/Mermaid_Man_and_Barnacle_Boy#Mermaid_Man" title="Mermaid Man and Barnacle Boy">Mermaid Man</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/SpongeBob_SquarePants" title="SpongeBob SquarePants">SpongeBob SquarePants</a></i>, a role he played from 1999 until his death in 2012.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ernest Borgnine was born Ermes Effron Borgnino (<small>Italian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[borˈɲiːno]</a></span>) on January 24, 1917, in <a href="/wiki/Hamden,_Connecticut" title="Hamden, Connecticut">Hamden, Connecticut</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictionary_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dictionary-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clooney_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-clooney-3">[3]</a></sup> He was the son of Anna (née Boselli; 1894–1949),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> who emigrated from <a href="/wiki/Carpi,_Emilia-Romagna" title="Carpi, Emilia-Romagna">Carpi</a> (<a href="/wiki/Province_of_Modena" title="Province of Modena">Modena</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861%E2%80%931946)" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861–1946)">Italy</a>) to the United States, and Camillo Borgnino (1891–1975),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> who emigrated from <a href="/wiki/Ottiglio" title="Ottiglio">Ottiglio</a> (<a href="/wiki/Province_of_Alessandria" title="Province of Alessandria">Alessandria</a>, Italy).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filmref_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-filmref-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Borgnine's parents separated when he was two years old, and his mother and he lived in Italy for about <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap">4<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> years. By 1923, his parents had reconciled, the family name was changed from Borgnino to Borgnine, and his father changed his first name to Charles. Ernest had a sister, Evelyn Borgnine Velardi (1925–2013).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The family settled in <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven, Connecticut</a>, where he graduated from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Hillhouse_High_School" title="James Hillhouse High School">James Hillhouse High School</a>. Borgnine took to sports while growing up, but showed no interest in acting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fantle_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-fantle-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naval_service">Naval service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naval service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ernest-Borgnine_2004.JPEG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1862" data-file-width="1312" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Ernest-Borgnine_2004.JPEG/170px-Ernest-Borgnine_2004.JPEG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Ernest-Borgnine_2004.JPEG/255px-Ernest-Borgnine_2004.JPEG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Ernest-Borgnine_2004.JPEG/340px-Ernest-Borgnine_2004.JPEG 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ernest-Borgnine_2004.JPEG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Borgnine wearing a chief petty officer's cap in October 2004.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Borgnine joined the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> in October 1935, after graduation from high school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctmag_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ctmag-9">[9]</a></sup> He served aboard the destroyer/destroyer <a href="/wiki/Minesweeper" title="Minesweeper">minesweeper</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USS_Lamberton_(DMS-2)" title="USS Lamberton (DMS-2)">USS <i>Lamberton</i></a> (DD-119; AG-21 and DMS-2)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and was honorably discharged from the Navy in October 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-navy.mil_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-navy.mil-11">[11]</a></sup> In January 1942, he reenlisted in the Navy after the <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">attack on Pearl Harbor</a>. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, he patrolled the Atlantic Coast on an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antisubmarine_warfare" title="Antisubmarine warfare">antisubmarine warfare</a> ship, the <a href="/wiki/USS_Sylph_(PY-12)" title="USS Sylph (PY-12)">USS <i>Sylph</i> (PY-12)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In September 1945, he was honorably discharged from the Navy. He served a total of almost ten years in the Navy and obtained the grade of <a href="/wiki/Gunner%27s_mate" title="Gunner's mate">gunner's mate</a> 1st class. His military awards include the <a href="/wiki/Good_Conduct_Medal_(United_States)" title="Good Conduct Medal (United States)">Navy Good Conduct Medal</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Defense_Service_Medal" title="American Defense Service Medal">American Defense Service Medal</a> with Fleet Clasp, <a href="/wiki/American_Campaign_Medal" title="American Campaign Medal">American Campaign Medal</a> with <span class="nowrap"> </span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>16</sub></span>" bronze star, and the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II_Victory_Medal_(United_States)" title="World War II Victory Medal (United States)">World War II Victory Medal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-navy.mil_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-navy.mil-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1997, Borgnine received the United States Navy Memorial, Lone Sailor Award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 7, 2000, Borgnine was named the Veterans Foundation's Veteran of the Year.</p>
<p>In October 2004, Borgnine received the honorary title of <a href="/wiki/Chief_petty_officer" title="Chief petty officer">chief petty officer</a> from <a href="/wiki/Master_Chief_Petty_Officer_of_the_Navy" title="Master Chief Petty Officer of the Navy">Master Chief Petty Officer of the Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Terry_D._Scott" title="Terry D. Scott">Terry D. Scott</a>. The ceremony for Borgnine's naval advancement was held at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_Memorial" title="United States Navy Memorial">U.S. Navy Memorial</a> in Washington, D.C. He received the special honor for his naval service and support of the Navy and navy families worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chief_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-chief-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 5, 2007, he received the California Commendation Medal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting_career">Acting career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Acting career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Borgnine returned to his parents' house in Connecticut after his Navy discharge without a job to go back to and no direction. In a British Film Institute interview about his life and career, he said:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>After World War II, we wanted no more part in war. I didn't even want to be a boy-scout. I went home and said that I was through with the Navy and so now, what do we do? So I went home to mother, and after a few weeks of patting me on the back and, "You did good," and everything else, one day she said, "Well?" like mothers do. Which meant, "All right, you gonna get a job or what?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bfi.org.uk_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-bfi.org.uk-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>He took a local factory job, but was unwilling to settle down to that kind of work. His mother encouraged him to pursue a more glamorous profession and suggested to him that his personality would be well suited for the stage. He surprised his mother by taking the suggestion to heart, although his father was far from enthusiastic. In 2011, Borgnine remembered,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>She said, "You always like getting in front of people and making a fool of yourself, why don't you give it a try?" I was sitting at the kitchen table and I saw this light. No kidding. It sounds crazy. And 10 years later, I had <a href="/wiki/Grace_Kelly" title="Grace Kelly">Grace Kelly</a> handing me an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a>.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage">Stage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Stage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He studied acting and graduated, auditioned, and was accepted as an intern to the <a href="/wiki/Barter_Theatre" title="Barter Theatre">Barter Theatre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Abingdon,_Virginia" title="Abingdon, Virginia">Abingdon, Virginia</a>. It had been named for the director's allowing audiences to barter produce for admission during the cash-lean years of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>. In 1947, Borgnine landed his first stage role in <i><a href="/wiki/State_of_the_Union_(play)" title="State of the Union (play)">State of the Union</a></i>. Although it was a short role, he won over the audience. His next role was as the Gentleman Caller in <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Williams" title="Tennessee Williams">Tennessee Williams</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/The_Glass_Menagerie" title="The Glass Menagerie">The Glass Menagerie</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1949, Borgnine went to New York, where he had his <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> debut in the role of a nurse in the play <i><a href="/wiki/Harvey_(play)" title="Harvey (play)">Harvey</a></i>. More roles on stage led him to being cast for decades as a <a href="/wiki/Character_actor" title="Character actor">character actor</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Films">Films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>An appearance as the villain on TV's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_Video" title="Captain Video">Captain Video</a></i> led to Borgnine's casting in the motion picture <i><a href="/wiki/The_Whistle_at_Eaton_Falls" title="The Whistle at Eaton Falls">The Whistle at Eaton Falls</a></i> (1951) for Columbia Pictures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> That year, Borgnine moved to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, where he eventually received his big break in Columbia's <i><a href="/wiki/From_Here_to_Eternity" title="From Here to Eternity">From Here to Eternity</a></i> (1953), playing the sadistic Sergeant "Fatso" Judson, who beats a stockade prisoner in his charge, Angelo Maggio (played by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">Frank Sinatra</a>). Borgnine built a reputation as a dependable character actor and played villains in early films, including movies such as <i><a href="/wiki/Johnny_Guitar" title="Johnny Guitar">Johnny Guitar</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Vera_Cruz_(film)" title="Vera Cruz (film)">Vera Cruz</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Bad_Day_at_Black_Rock" title="Bad Day at Black Rock">Bad Day at Black Rock</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1955, the actor starred as a warmhearted butcher in <i><a href="/wiki/Marty_(film)" title="Marty (film)">Marty</a></i>, the film version of <a href="/wiki/Marty_(teleplay)" title="Marty (teleplay)">the television play of the same name</a>. He gained an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Actor">Academy Award for Best Actor</a> over Frank Sinatra, <a href="/wiki/James_Dean" title="James Dean">James Dean</a> (who had died by the time of the ceremony), and former Best Actor winners <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Tracy" title="Spencer Tracy">Spencer Tracy</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Cagney" title="James Cagney">James Cagney</a>.</p>
<p>Borgnine's film career flourished for the next three decades, including roles in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Flight_of_the_Phoenix_(1965_film)" title="The Flight of the Phoenix (1965 film)">The Flight of the Phoenix</a></i> (1965), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dirty_Dozen" title="The Dirty Dozen">The Dirty Dozen</a></i> (1967), <i><a href="/wiki/Ice_Station_Zebra" title="Ice Station Zebra">Ice Station Zebra</a></i> (1968), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Poseidon_Adventure_(1972_film)" title="The Poseidon Adventure (1972 film)">The Poseidon Adventure</a></i> (1972), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_of_the_North" title="Emperor of the North">Emperor of the North</a></i> (1973), <i><a href="/wiki/Convoy_(1978_film)" title="Convoy (1978 film)">Convoy</a></i> (1978), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Black_Hole" title="The Black Hole">The Black Hole</a></i> (1979), <i><a href="/wiki/All_Quiet_on_the_Western_Front" title="All Quiet on the Western Front">All Quiet on the Western Front</a></i> (1979), and <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_from_New_York" title="Escape from New York">Escape from New York</a></i> (1981).</p>
<p>One of his most famous roles was that of Dutch, a member of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wild_Bunch" title="The Wild Bunch">The Wild Bunch</a></i> in the 1969 Western classic from director <a href="/wiki/Sam_Peckinpah" title="Sam Peckinpah">Sam Peckinpah</a>. Of his role in <i>The Wild Bunch</i>, Borgnine later said,</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I did [think it was a moral film]. Because to me, every picture should have some kind of a moral to it. I feel that when we used to watch old pictures, as we still do I'm sure, the bad guys always got it in the end and the good guys always won out. Today it's a little different. Today it seems that the bad guys are getting the good end of it. There was always a moral in our story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bfi.org.uk_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-bfi.org.uk-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Borgnine made his TV debut as a character actor in <i><a href="/wiki/Captain_Video_and_His_Video_Rangers" title="Captain Video and His Video Rangers">Captain Video and His Video Rangers</a></i>, beginning in 1951. These two episodes led to countless other television roles that Borgnine would gain in <i><a href="/wiki/Goodyear_Television_Playhouse" title="Goodyear Television Playhouse">Goodyear Television Playhouse</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Ford_Television_Theatre" title="The Ford Television Theatre">The Ford Television Theatre</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Fireside_Theatre" title="Fireside Theatre">Fireside Theatre</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Frontier_Justice_(TV_series)" title="Frontier Justice (TV series)">Frontier Justice</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Laramie_(TV_series)" title="Laramie (TV series)">Laramie</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bob_Hope_Presents_the_Chrysler_Theatre" title="Bob Hope Presents the Chrysler Theatre">Bob Hope Presents the Chrysler Theatre</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Run_for_Your_Life_(TV_series)" title="Run for Your Life (TV series)">Run for Your Life</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Little_House_on_the_Prairie_(TV_series)" title="Little House on the Prairie (TV series)">Little House on the Prairie</a></i> (a two-part episode entitled "The Lord is My Shepherd"), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Love_Boat" title="The Love Boat">The Love Boat</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Magnum,_P.I." title="Magnum, P.I.">Magnum, P.I.</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Highway_to_Heaven" title="Highway to Heaven">Highway to Heaven</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Murder,_She_Wrote" title="Murder, She Wrote">Murder, She Wrote</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Walker,_Texas_Ranger" title="Walker, Texas Ranger">Walker, Texas Ranger</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Home_Improvement_(TV_series)" title="Home Improvement (TV series)">Home Improvement</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Touched_by_an_Angel" title="Touched by an Angel">Touched by an Angel</a></i>, and the final episodes of <i><a href="/wiki/ER_(TV_series)" title="ER (TV series)">ER</a></i>, the first episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Wagon_Train" title="Wagon Train">Wagon Train</a></i>, and many others.</p>
<p>In 2009, at the age of 92, Borgnine earned an Emmy nomination for his performances in the final two episodes of <i><a href="/wiki/ER_(TV_series)" title="ER (TV series)">ER</a></i>.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="McHale.27s_Navy"><i>McHale's Navy</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: McHale's Navy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ernest_Borgnine_McHale_McHale%27s_Navy_1963.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="994" data-file-width="793" height="276" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Ernest_Borgnine_McHale_McHale%27s_Navy_1963.JPG/220px-Ernest_Borgnine_McHale_McHale%27s_Navy_1963.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Ernest_Borgnine_McHale_McHale%27s_Navy_1963.JPG/330px-Ernest_Borgnine_McHale_McHale%27s_Navy_1963.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Ernest_Borgnine_McHale_McHale%27s_Navy_1963.JPG/440px-Ernest_Borgnine_McHale_McHale%27s_Navy_1963.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ernest_Borgnine_McHale_McHale%27s_Navy_1963.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Borgnine as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Commander" title="Lieutenant Commander">Lieutenant Commander</a> McHale in <i>McHale's Navy</i> in 1963</div>

<p>In 1962, Borgnine signed a contract with <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a> for the lead role as the gruff but lovable skipper, Quinton McHale, in what began as a serious one-hour 1962 episode called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seven_Against_the_Sea" title="Seven Against the Sea">Seven Against the Sea</a></i> for <i>Alcoa Premiere</i>, and later reworked to a comedy called <i><a href="/wiki/McHale%27s_Navy" title="McHale's Navy">McHale's Navy</a></i>, a World War II sitcom, which also co-starred unfamiliar comedians <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joe_Flynn_(US_actor)" title="Joe Flynn (US actor)">Joe Flynn</a> as Capt. Wally Binghamton and <a href="/wiki/Tim_Conway" title="Tim Conway">Tim Conway</a> as Ens. Charles Parker. The insubordinate crew of PT-73 helped the show become an overnight success during its first season, landing in the Top 30 in 1963.</p>
<p>Like the McHale character, Borgnine was a longtime navy man in real life. He thrived on the adulation from fans for their favorite navy man, and in 1963 received an Emmy nomination for Outstanding Lead Actor in a Comedy Series. At the end of the fourth season, in 1966 low ratings and repetitive storylines brought <i>McHale's Navy</i> to an end.</p>
<p>Tim Conway said about the sitcom: "You know, we were all guys, it was about the war, and about men, so, there weren't many women working on the show, so we can spit, talk, swear, and everything—smoke? Gosh. So, it was male oriented." Conway once referred to Ernest Borgnine making new friends off of the Universal set, "It was the beginning of the trams, going through Universal. Ernie was probably one of the few people at Universal, who would stop the trams and say, 'Hello, how are you?' He would talk to everybody at the tram." While the show <i>McHale's Navy</i> was going strong, Tim had also said of Borgnine's short-lived marriage to <a href="/wiki/Ethel_Merman" title="Ethel Merman">Ethel Merman</a>, "Ernie is volatile. I mean, there's no question about that; and Ethel was a very strong lady. So, you put 2 bombs in a room, something is going to explode, and I guess it probably did." He also said about the cancellation of <i>McHale's Navy</i> was, "We had gone from the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">South Pacific</a> to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, and then, once in a while, we got to New York or something. The storylines were beginning to duplicate themselves. So, they actually said, 'Maybe, they had its run!'". Conway kept in touch with Borgnine for more than 40 years, while living not too far from one another. In 1999, the duo reunited to guest-voice in several episodes of the popular 2000s animated comedy, <i><a href="/wiki/SpongeBob_SquarePants" title="SpongeBob SquarePants">SpongeBob SquarePants</a></i>. Katy Jurado's death in 2002 drew Borgnine and Conway much closer, as Tim had heard so much of the actress's death. He said he heard his resisting friend once referred to one of his ex-wives, "Beautiful, but a tiger."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> After Conway lost his TV captain, he later said, if Borgnine was more than likely to died an Italian count, had it not been for Mussolini, "I can't envision him as a count," Tim had also said about <i>McHale's Navy</i> debuted, a half a century ago, boosting both ABC and the Navy fortunes: "But maybe as a king---certainly not a count." The last thing he said about his acting mentor's long career: "There were no limits to Ernie," said Conway, "When you look at his career---<i>Fatso Judson</i> to <i>Marty</i>, that's about as varied as you get in characters and he handled both of them with equal delicacy and got the most out of those characters."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Airwolf"><i>Airwolf</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Airwolf">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Borgnine returned to a new contract with <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a> in 1983, for a co-starring role opposite <a href="/wiki/Jan-Michael_Vincent" title="Jan-Michael Vincent">Jan-Michael Vincent</a>, on <i><a href="/wiki/Airwolf" title="Airwolf">Airwolf</a></i>. After he was approached by producer <a href="/wiki/Donald_P._Bellisario" title="Donald P. Bellisario">Donald P. Bellisario</a>, who had been impressed by Borgnine's guest role as a wrestler in a 1982 episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Magnum,_P.I." title="Magnum, P.I.">Magnum, P.I.</a></i>, he immediately agreed. He played Dominic Santini, a helicopter pilot, in the series, which became an immediate hit. Borgnine's strong performances belied his exhaustion due to the grueling production schedule, and the challenges of working with his younger, troubled series lead. The show was cancelled by <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> in 1986.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Single_Guy"><i>The Single Guy</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Single Guy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>He auditioned a third time for a co-starring role opposite <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Silverman" title="Jonathan Silverman">Jonathan Silverman</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Single_Guy" title="The Single Guy">The Single Guy</a></i> as doorman Manny Cordoba, which lasted two seasons. According to Silverman, Borgnine came to work with more energy and passion than all other stars combined. He was the first person to arrive on the set every day and the last to leave.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Merlin.27s_Shop_of_Mystical_Wonders"><i>Merlin's Shop of Mystical Wonders</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Merlin's Shop of Mystical Wonders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1996, Borgnine starred in the televised fantasy/thriller film <i><a href="/wiki/Merlin%27s_Shop_of_Mystical_Wonders" title="Merlin's Shop of Mystical Wonders">Merlin's Shop of Mystical Wonders</a></i> (partially adapted from the 1984 horror film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Devil%27s_Gift" title="The Devil's Gift">The Devil's Gift</a></i>). As narrator and storyteller, Borgnine recounts a string of related supernatural tales, his modern-day fables notably centering on an enchanted and malicious <a href="/wiki/Cymbal-banging_monkey_toy" title="Cymbal-banging monkey toy">cymbal-banging monkey toy</a> stolen from the wizard <a href="/wiki/Merlin" title="Merlin">Merlin</a>. The film was later featured in the parodical television series <i><a href="/wiki/Mystery_Science_Theater_3000" title="Mystery Science Theater 3000">Mystery Science Theater 3000</a></i>, and has since gained a prominent cult following.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mstk.info_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-mstk.info-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_activities">Other activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Also in 1996, Borgnine toured the United States on a bus to meet his fans and see the country. The trip was the subject of a 1997 documentary, <i>Ernest Borgnine on the Bus</i>. He also served one year as the chairman of the National Salute to Hospitalized Veterans, visiting patients in many <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs" title="United States Department of Veterans Affairs">Department of Veterans Affairs</a> medical centers.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_after_1999">Work after 1999</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Work after 1999">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Starting in 1999, Borgnine provided his voice talent to the <a href="/wiki/Animated_sitcom" title="Animated sitcom">animated sitcom</a> <i><a href="/wiki/SpongeBob_SquarePants" title="SpongeBob SquarePants">SpongeBob SquarePants</a></i> as the elderly superhero <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mermaid_Man" title="Mermaid Man">Mermaid Man</a> (where he was paired up with his <i><a href="/wiki/McHale%27s_Navy" title="McHale's Navy">McHale's Navy</a></i> co-star <a href="/wiki/Tim_Conway" title="Tim Conway">Tim Conway</a> as the voice of Mermaid Man's sidekick <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barnacle_Boy" title="Barnacle Boy">Barnacle Boy</a>). He expressed affection for this role, in no small part for its popularity among children. After his death Nickelodeon re-aired all of the episodes in which Mermaid Man appeared in memoriam. Borgnine also appeared as himself in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/Boy-Scoutz_%27n_the_Hood" title="Boy-Scoutz 'n the Hood">Boy-Scoutz 'n the Hood</a>", in addition to a number of television commercials. In 2000, he was the executive producer of <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hoover_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hoover (film) (page does not exist)">Hoover</a></i>, in which he was the only credited actor.</p>
<p>In 2007, Borgnine starred in the <a href="/wiki/Hallmark_Cards" title="Hallmark Cards">Hallmark</a> original film <i><a href="/wiki/A_Grandpa_for_Christmas" title="A Grandpa for Christmas">A Grandpa for Christmas</a></i>. He played a man who, after his estranged daughter ends up in the hospital because of a car accident, discovers that he has a granddaughter he never knew about. She is taken into his care, and they soon become great friends. Borgnine received a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golden_Globe" title="Golden Globe">Golden Globe</a> nomination for Best Actor in a Mini-Series or Motion Picture made for Television for his performance. At 90, he was the oldest Golden Globe nominee ever.</p>
<p>Borgnine's autobiography <i>Ernie</i> was published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citadel_Press" title="Citadel Press">Citadel Press</a> in July 2008. <i>Ernie</i> is a loose, conversational recollection of highlights from his acting career and notable events from his personal life.</p>
<p>On April 2, 2009, he appeared in the last episode of the long-running medical series <i><a href="/wiki/ER_(TV_series)" title="ER (TV series)">ER</a></i>. His role was that of a husband whose long marriage ended with his wife's death. In his final scene, his character is in a hospital bed lying beside his just-deceased wife. His performance garnered an Emmy nomination for Outstanding Guest Actor in a Drama Series, his third nomination and his first in 29 years (since being nominated for Outstanding Supporting Actor in a Limited Series or a Special in 1980 for <i><a href="/wiki/All_Quiet_on_the_Western_Front_(1979_film)" title="All Quiet on the Western Front (1979 film)">All Quiet on the Western Front</a></i>).</p>
<p>In 2009, at age 92, he starred as Frank, the main character of <i><a href="/wiki/Another_Harvest_Moon" title="Another Harvest Moon">Another Harvest Moon</a></i>, directed by Greg Swartz and also starring <a href="/wiki/Piper_Laurie" title="Piper Laurie">Piper Laurie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anne_Meara" title="Anne Meara">Anne Meara</a>. On October 2, 2010, Borgnine appeared as himself in a sketch on <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i>. On October 15, 2010, he appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/Red_(2010_film)" title="Red (2010 film)">Red</a></i>, which was filmed earlier that year. In late 2011, Borgnine completed what would be his last film, playing Rex Page in <i>The Man Who Shook The Hand of Vicente Fernandez</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life_and_death">Personal life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Personal life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Borgnine married five times. His first wife was Rhoda Kemins (1949–1958), whom he met while serving in the Navy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schmidt_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-schmidt-21">[21]</a></sup> They had one daughter, Nancee (born May 28, 1952). He then married actress <a href="/wiki/Katy_Jurado" title="Katy Jurado">Katy Jurado</a> (1959–1963).</p>
<p>Borgnine's marriage to singer <a href="/wiki/Ethel_Merman" title="Ethel Merman">Ethel Merman</a> (1964) lasted thirty-two days. Their time together was mostly spent hurling profane insults at each other, and both would later admit that the marriage was a colossal mistake (Merman's description of the marriage in her autobiography was a solitary blank page). Their divorce was finalized on May 25, 1965.</p>
<p>Borgnine married Donna Rancourt (1965–1972), with whom he had a son, Cristopher (born August 9, 1969) and two daughters, Sharon (born August 5, 1965) and Diana (born December 29, 1970). His fifth and last marriage was to <a href="/wiki/Tova_Traesnaes" title="Tova Traesnaes">Tova Traesnaes</a>, which lasted from February 24, 1973 until his death.</p>
<p>In 2000, Borgnine received his 50-year pin as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freemason" title="Freemason">Freemason</a> in Abingdon Lodge No. 48, Abingdon, Virginia. He joined the Scottish Rite Valley of Los Angeles in 1964, received the KCCH in 1979, was crowned a 33° Inspector General Honorary in 1983, and received the Grand Cross of the Court of Honour in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Borgnine was a heavy smoker until 1962, after which he became a militant anti-smoker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Borgnine died of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_failure" title="Renal failure">kidney failure</a> on July 8, 2012 at <a href="/wiki/Cedars-Sinai_Medical_Center" title="Cedars-Sinai Medical Center">Cedars-Sinai Medical Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> with his family at his side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAtimes_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAtimes-24">[24]</a></sup> He was 95 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTobit_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-25">[25]</a></sup> Upon his death, he was cremated, his ashes were given to his family.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_SpongeBob_Movie:_Sponge_Out_of_Water" title="The SpongeBob Movie: Sponge Out of Water">The SpongeBob Movie: Sponge Out of Water</a></i> was dedicated to Borgnine.</p>

<p>Borgnine's hometown of <a href="/wiki/Hamden,_Connecticut" title="Hamden, Connecticut">Hamden, Connecticut</a>, where he enjoyed a large and vocal following, named a street in his honor. For 30 years (1972–2002), Borgnine marched in <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a>'s annual <a href="/wiki/Great_Circus_Parade" title="Great Circus Parade">Great Circus Parade</a> as the "Grand Clown".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mjs_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-mjs-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1994, Borgnine received the <a href="/wiki/Ellis_Island_Medal_of_Honor" title="Ellis Island Medal of Honor">Ellis Island Medal of Honor</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Ethnic_Coalition" title="National Ethnic Coalition">National Ethnic Coalition</a> of Organizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1997, Borgnine was the commencement speaker at <a href="/wiki/Lakeland_University" title="Lakeland University">Lakeland College</a>, and received an honorary doctorate in humane letters in recognition of his distinguished acting career.</p>
<p>In 1998, the <a href="/wiki/Palm_Springs,_California" title="Palm Springs, California">Palm Springs, California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palm_Springs_Walk_of_Stars" title="Palm Springs Walk of Stars">Walk of Stars</a> dedicated a Golden Palm Star to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PS_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-PS-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_awards_and_nominations">Film awards and nominations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Film awards and nominations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Borgnine won the 1955 <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Actor">Academy Award for Best Actor</a> for his portrayal of Marty Piletti in the film <i>Marty</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTobit_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-25">[25]</a></sup> At the time of his death, he was the oldest living recipient of the Best Actor Oscar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbs_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbs-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>For his contribution to the motion picture industry, Ernest Borgnine received a star on the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Walk_of_Fame" title="Hollywood Walk of Fame">Hollywood Walk of Fame</a> at 6324 Hollywood Blvd. In 1996, he was inducted into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Performers_Hall_of_Fame" title="Western Performers Hall of Fame">Western Performers Hall of Fame</a> at the <a href="/wiki/National_Cowboy_%26_Western_Heritage_Museum" title="National Cowboy &amp; Western Heritage Museum">National Cowboy &amp; Western Heritage Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City" title="Oklahoma City">Oklahoma City</a>.</p>
<p>He was honored with the <a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild_Life_Achievement_Award" title="Screen Actors Guild Life Achievement Award">Screen Actors Guild Life Achievement Award</a> at the <a href="/wiki/17th_Screen_Actors_Guild_Awards" title="17th Screen Actors Guild Awards">17th Screen Actors Guild Awards</a>, held January 30, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_from_fraternal_groups">Awards from fraternal groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Awards from fraternal groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2000, Borgnine received his 50-year pin as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freemason" title="Freemason">Freemason</a> in Abingdon Lodge No. 48, Abingdon, Virginia. He joined the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Rite" title="Scottish Rite">Scottish Rite</a> Valley of Los Angeles (in the Southern Jurisdiction of the U.S.A) in 1964, received the KCCH in 1979, was crowned a 33° Inspector General Honorary in 1983, and received the Grand Cross of the Court of Honour in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masons_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-masons-31">[31]</a></sup> He was also a member of the Loyal Order of Moose at that organization's Lodge in <a href="/wiki/Junction_City,_Oregon" title="Junction City, Oregon">Junction City, Oregon</a>. He volunteered to be <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stories_of_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stories of Service (page does not exist)">Stories of Service</a> National spokesman, urging his fellow World War II vets to come forward and share their stories.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quotes">Quotes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernest_Borgnine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Quotes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-move" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Edit-copy_purple-wikiq.svg"><img alt="Edit-copy purple-wikiq.svg" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Edit-copy_purple-wikiq.svg/40px-Edit-copy_purple-wikiq.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Edit-copy_purple-wikiq.svg/60px-Edit-copy_purple-wikiq.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Edit-copy_purple-wikiq.svg/80px-Edit-copy_purple-wikiq.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></a></div>
</td>
<td class="mbox-text"><span class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>is a candidate to be <a class="extiw" href="https://meta.wikimedia.org/wiki/Help:Transwiki" title="m:Help:Transwiki">copied</a> to <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Main_Page" title="q:Main Page">Wikiquote</a> using the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Transwiki_log" title="Wikipedia:Transwiki log">Transwiki</a> process</b>.</span></td>

</table>
<ul>
<li>Ernest: "<a href="/wiki/Spencer_Tracy" title="Spencer Tracy">Spencer Tracy</a> was the first actor I've seen who could just look down into the dirt and command a scene. He played a set-up with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ryan" title="Robert Ryan">Robert Ryan</a> that way. He's looking down at the road and then he looks at Ryan at just the precise, right minute. I tell you, Rob could've stood on his head and zipped open his fly and the scene would've still been Mr. Tracy's."</li>
<li>Ernest: "The trick is not to become somebody else. You become somebody else when you're in front of a camera or when you're on stage. There are some people who carry it all the time. That, to me, is not acting. What you've gotta do is find out what the writer wrote about and put it into your mind. This is acting. Not going out and researching what the writer has already written. This is crazy!"</li>
<li>Ernest: "Everything I do has a moral to it. Yes, I've been in films that have had shootings. I made <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wild_Bunch" title="The Wild Bunch">The Wild Bunch</a></i> (1969), which was the beginning of the splattering of blood and everything else. But there was a moral behind it. The moral was that, by golly, bad guys got it. That was it. Yeah."</li>
<li>Ernest: "Ever since they opened the floodgates with <a href="/wiki/Clark_Gable" title="Clark Gable">Clark Gable</a> saying, '<a href="/wiki/Frankly,_my_dear,_I_don%27t_give_a_damn" title="Frankly, my dear, I don't give a damn">Frankly, my dear, I don't give a damn</a>,' somebody's ears pricked up and said, 'Oh boy, here we go!'. Writers used to make such wonderful pictures without all that swearing, all that cursing. And now it seems that you can't say three words without cursing. And I don't think that's right."</li>
<li>Ernest on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drugs" title="Drugs">drugs</a>: "No, I've never done anything. At least, not to my knowledge. I once took a bunch of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goofballs" title="Goofballs">goofballs</a> by accident. They looked like candy. They were in a little bowl at a party. I grabbed a handful and went to town. That was some New Year's Eve. I didn't have a coherent thought till February."</li>
<li>Ernest on his marriage to <a href="/wiki/Ethel_Merman" title="Ethel Merman">Ethel Merman</a>: "Biggest mistake of my life. I thought I was marrying <a href="/wiki/Rosemary_Clooney" title="Rosemary Clooney">Rosemary Clooney</a>."</li>
<li>Ernest on his $5,000 salary for playing the eponymous lead in <i><a href="/wiki/Marty_(film)" title="Marty (film)">Marty</a></i> (1955), which won him a Best Actor Oscar: "...I would have done it for nothing."</li>
<li>Ernest on Women's Rights: "They tried it the wrong way. You can't expect anyone to take you seriously if you burn your undies and tell me I'm a pig. That's why it failed. Too many ugly broads telling me that they don't want to sleep with me. Who wanted you anyway?"</li>
<li>Ernest: "I'm 81 years old and I like to speak my mind. As a legacy, on the day I die, I'd like to have a newspaper publish all the things that I find wrong in the United States today. And my first would be to get rid of the politicians."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fantle_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-fantle-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
