<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fish scale</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a> of most fishes are covered with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scale_(zoology)" title="Scale (zoology)">scales</a>. Scales vary enormously in size, shape, structure, and extent, ranging from strong and rigid armour plates in fishes such as <a href="/wiki/Shrimpfish" title="Shrimpfish">shrimpfishes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boxfish" title="Boxfish">boxfishes</a>, to microscopic or absent in fishes such as <a href="/wiki/Eel" title="Eel">eels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anglerfish" title="Anglerfish">anglerfishes</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a> of a scale can be used to identify the species of fish it came from.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartilaginous_fish" title="Cartilaginous fish">Cartilaginous fishes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Batoidea" title="Batoidea">rays</a>) are covered with placoid scales. Most <a href="/wiki/Osteichthyes" title="Osteichthyes">bony fishes</a> are covered with the cycloid scales of <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a>, or the ctenoid scales of <a href="/wiki/Perch" title="Perch">perch</a>, or the ganoid scales of <a href="/wiki/Sturgeon" title="Sturgeon">sturgeons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gar" title="Gar">gars</a>. Some species are covered instead by <a href="/wiki/Scute" title="Scute">scutes</a>, and others have no outer covering on the skin.</p>
<p>Fish scales are part of the fish's <a href="/wiki/Integumentary_system" title="Integumentary system">integumentary system</a>, and are produced from the <a href="/wiki/Mesoderm" title="Mesoderm">mesoderm</a> layer of the <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a>, which distinguishes them from <a href="/wiki/Reptile_scale" title="Reptile scale">reptile scales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The same <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> involved in tooth and hair development in <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> are also involved in scale development. The placoid scales of cartilaginous fishes are also called dermal denticles and are structurally homologous with vertebrate teeth. It has been suggested that the scales of bony fishes are similar in structure to teeth, but they probably originate from different tissue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Most fish are also covered in a protective layer of <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a> (slime).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Placoid_scales">Placoid scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fish_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Placoid scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Placoid scales are found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartilaginous_fish" title="Cartilaginous fish">cartilaginous fishes</a>: <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Batoidea" title="Batoidea">rays</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chimaera" title="Chimaera">chimaeras</a>. They are also called <i>dermal denticles</i>. Placoid scales are structurally <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homologous</a> with <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tooth_(animal)" title="Tooth (animal)">teeth</a> ("denticle" translates to "small tooth"), having a central <a href="/wiki/Pulp_(tooth)" title="Pulp (tooth)">pulp cavity</a> supplied with <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a>, surrounded by a conical layer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dentine" title="Dentine">dentine</a>, all of which sits on top of a rectangular basal plate that rests on the <a href="/wiki/Dermis" title="Dermis">dermis</a>. The outermost layer is composed of <a href="/wiki/Vitrodentine" title="Vitrodentine">vitrodentine</a>, a largely inorganic <a href="/wiki/Tooth_enamel" title="Tooth enamel">enamel</a>-like substance. Placoid scales cannot grow in size, but rather more scales are added as the fish increases in size.</p>
<p>Similar scales can also be found under the head of the <a href="/wiki/Denticle_herring" title="Denticle herring">denticle herring</a>. The amount of scale coverage is much less in rays and chimaeras.</p>
<p>The skin of sharks is entirely covered by placoid scales. The scales are supported by spines, which feel rough when stroked in a backward direction, but when flattened by the forward movement of water, create tiny <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortices</a> that reduce hydrodynamic drag, making swimming both more efficient as well as quieter compared to that of bony fishes. The rough, <a href="/wiki/Sandpaper" title="Sandpaper">sandpaper</a>-like texture of shark and ray skin, coupled with its toughness, has led it to be valued as a source of rawhide <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a>, called <a href="/wiki/Shagreen" title="Shagreen">shagreen</a>. One of the many historical applications of shark shagreen was in making hand-grips for <a href="/wiki/Sword" title="Sword">swords</a>.</p>
<p>Unlike bony fish, sharks have a complicated dermal <a href="/wiki/Corset" title="Corset">corset</a> made of flexible <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagenous</a> <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fibers</a> arranged as a <a href="/wiki/Helix" title="Helix">helical</a> network surrounding their body. The corset works as an outer skeleton, providing attachment for their swimming muscles and thus saving energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cartilagious_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cartilagious-3">[3]</a></sup> Their dermal teeth give them <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrodynamic" title="Hydrodynamic">hydrodynamic</a> advantages, as the scales reduce the <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a> of swimming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SkinTeeth_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-SkinTeeth-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leptoid_scales">Leptoid scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fish_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Leptoid scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Leptoid scales are found on higher-order bony fish, the <a href="/wiki/Teleost" title="Teleost">teleosts</a> (the more <a href="/wiki/Synapomorphy" title="Synapomorphy">derived</a> <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> of ray-finned fishes). As the fish grow, scales are added in concentric layers. The scales are arranged so as to overlap in a head-to-tail configuration, like roof tiles, allowing a smoother flow of water over the body and thereby reducing <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Leptoid scales come in two forms: cycloid and ctenoid.</p>

<p>Cycloid (circular) scales have a smooth texture and are uniform, with a smooth outer edge or margin. They are most common on fish with soft fin rays, such as <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ctenoid_scales">Ctenoid scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fish_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ctenoid scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tmulti tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:298px;max-width:298px">

<p>Ctenoid (toothed) scales are like cycloid scales, with small teeth along their outer edges. They are usually found on fishes with spiny fin rays, such as the <a href="/wiki/Perciformes" title="Perciformes">perch-like</a> fishes. The scales have a rough texture with a toothed outer or posterior edge featuring tiny teeth called <b>ctenii</b>. These scales contain almost no bone, being composed of a surface layer containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyapatite" title="Hydroxyapatite">hydroxyapatite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> and a deeper layer composed mostly of <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a>. The enamel of the other scale types is reduced to superficial ridges and ctenii.</p>

<p>Ctenoid scales can be further subdivided into three types:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crenate</b> scales, where the margin of the scale bears indentations and projections.</li>
<li><b>Spinoid</b> scales, where the scale bears spines that are continuous with the scale itself.</li>
<li><b>True ctenoid</b> scales, where the spines on the scale are distinct structures.</li>
</ul>
<p>Both cycloid and ctenoid scales are overlapping, making them more flexible than cosmoid and ganoid scales. Unlike ganoid scales, they grow in size through additions to the margin. The scales of some species exhibit bands of uneven seasonal growth called <b>annuli</b> (singular <b>annulus</b>). These bands can be used to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Identification_of_aging_in_fish" title="Identification of aging in fish">age the fish</a>. Most ray-finned fishes have ctenoid scales. Some species of <a href="/wiki/Flatfish" title="Flatfish">flatfishes</a> have ctenoid scales on the eyed side and cycloid scales on the blind side, while other species have ctenoid scales in males and cycloid scales in females.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ganoid_scales">Ganoid scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fish_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ganoid scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spotted_Gar_(Lepisosteus_oculatus)_(3149758934).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="853" data-file-width="1280" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Spotted_Gar_%28Lepisosteus_oculatus%29_%283149758934%29.jpg/220px-Spotted_Gar_%28Lepisosteus_oculatus%29_%283149758934%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Spotted_Gar_%28Lepisosteus_oculatus%29_%283149758934%29.jpg/330px-Spotted_Gar_%28Lepisosteus_oculatus%29_%283149758934%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Spotted_Gar_%28Lepisosteus_oculatus%29_%283149758934%29.jpg/440px-Spotted_Gar_%28Lepisosteus_oculatus%29_%283149758934%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spotted_Gar_(Lepisosteus_oculatus)_(3149758934).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<div class="center" style="width:auto; margin-left:auto; margin-right:auto;">The <a href="/wiki/Longnose_gar" title="Longnose gar">longnose gar</a> has diamond-shape ganoid scales</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Ganoid scales are found in the <a href="/wiki/Sturgeon" title="Sturgeon">sturgeons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paddlefish" title="Paddlefish">paddlefishes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gar" title="Gar">gars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bowfin" title="Bowfin">bowfin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bichir" title="Bichir">bichirs</a>. They are derived from cosmoid scales, with a layer of dentine in the place of cosmine, and a layer of inorganic bone salt called <a href="/wiki/Ganoine" title="Ganoine">ganoine</a> in place of vitrodentine. Most are diamond-shaped and connected by peg-and-socket joints. They are usually thick and have a minimal amount of overlap as compared to other scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In sturgeons, the scales are greatly enlarged into armour plates along the sides and back, while in the bowfin the scales are greatly reduced in thickness to resemble cycloid scales (see above).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elasmoid_scales">Elasmoid scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fish_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Elasmoid scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coelacanth-PaleozoologicalMuseumOfChina-May23-08.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2349" data-file-width="3642" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Coelacanth-PaleozoologicalMuseumOfChina-May23-08.jpg/220px-Coelacanth-PaleozoologicalMuseumOfChina-May23-08.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Coelacanth-PaleozoologicalMuseumOfChina-May23-08.jpg/330px-Coelacanth-PaleozoologicalMuseumOfChina-May23-08.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Coelacanth-PaleozoologicalMuseumOfChina-May23-08.jpg/440px-Coelacanth-PaleozoologicalMuseumOfChina-May23-08.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coelacanth-PaleozoologicalMuseumOfChina-May23-08.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<div class="center" style="width:auto; margin-left:auto; margin-right:auto;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lobe-finned_fish" title="Lobe-finned fish">Lobe-finned fishes</a>, like this preserved <a href="/wiki/Coelacanth" title="Coelacanth">coelacanth</a>, have elasmoid scales</div>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Elasmoid scales are thin, imbricated scales composed of a layer of dense, lamellar bone called isopedine, above which is a layer of tubercles usually composed of bone, as in <i><a href="/wiki/Eusthenopteron" title="Eusthenopteron">Eusthenopteron</a></i>. The layer of dentine that was present in the first sarcopterygians is usually reduced, as in the extant <a href="/wiki/Coelacanth" title="Coelacanth">coelacanth</a>, or entirely absent, as in extant <a href="/wiki/Lungfish" title="Lungfish">lungfish</a> and in the Devonian <i><a href="/wiki/Eusthenopteron" title="Eusthenopteron">Eusthenopteron</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Elasmoid scales have appeared several times over the course of fish evolution. They are present in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lobe-finned_fish" title="Lobe-finned fish">lobe-finned fishes</a>: <a href="/wiki/Coelacanth" title="Coelacanth">coelacanths</a>, all extant and some extinct <a href="/wiki/Lungfish" title="Lungfish">lungfishes</a>, some tetrapodomorphs like <i><a href="/wiki/Eusthenopteron" title="Eusthenopteron">Eusthenopteron</a></i>, amiids, and teleosts, whose cycloid and ctenoid scales represent the least mineralized elasmoid scales.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmoid_scales">Cosmoid scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fish_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cosmoid scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cosmoid scales are found in several ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lobe-finned_fish" title="Lobe-finned fish">lobe-finned fishes</a>, including some of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Lungfish" title="Lungfish">lungfishes</a>, and were probably derived from a fusion of placoid scales. They are composed of a layer of dense, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lamellar" title="Lamellar">lamellar</a> bone called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Isopedine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Isopedine (page does not exist)">isopedine</a>, above which is a layer of spongy bone supplied with blood vessels. The bone layers are covered by a complex dentine layer called <a href="/wiki/Cosmine" title="Cosmine">cosmine</a> and a superficial outer coating of vitrodentine. Cosmoid scales increase in size through the growth of the lamellar bone layer.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scutes">Scutes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fish_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scutes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Scute" title="Scute">scute</a> is another, less common, type of scale. Scute comes from Latin for <i>shield</i>, and can take the form of:</p>
<ul>
<li>an external shield-like bony plate, or</li>
<li>a modified, thickened scale that often is keeled or spiny, or</li>
<li>a projecting, modified (rough and strongly ridged) scale, usually associated with the lateral line, or on the caudal peduncle forming caudal keels, or along the ventral profile.</li>
</ul>
<p>Some fish, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pineconefish" title="Pineconefish">pineconefish</a>, are completely or partially covered in scutes. <a href="/wiki/Alosa" title="Alosa">River herrings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Threadfin" title="Threadfin">threadfins</a> have an abdominal row of scutes, which are scales with raised, sharp points that are used for protection. Some <a href="/wiki/Carangidae" title="Carangidae">jacks</a> have a row of scutes following the <a href="/wiki/Lateral_line" title="Lateral line">lateral line</a> on either side.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thelodont_scales">Thelodont scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fish_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Thelodont scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thelodont_denticles.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2122" data-file-width="2414" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Thelodont_denticles.png/220px-Thelodont_denticles.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Thelodont_denticles.png/330px-Thelodont_denticles.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Thelodont_denticles.png/440px-Thelodont_denticles.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thelodont_denticles.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Left to right: denticles of <i><a href="/wiki/Paralogania" title="Paralogania">Paralogania</a></i> (?), <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shielia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shielia (page does not exist)">Shielia</a> taiti</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lanarkia" title="Lanarkia">Lanarkia</a> horrida</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The bony scales of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thelodont" title="Thelodont">thelodonts</a>, the most abundant form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fish" title="Fossil fish">fossil fish</a>, are well understood. The scales were formed and shed throughout the organisms' lifetimes, and quickly separated after their death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner1982_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turner1982-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Bone, a tissue that is both resistant to mechanical damage and relatively prone to fossilization, often preserves internal detail, which allows the <a href="/wiki/Histology" title="Histology">histology</a> and growth of the scales to be studied in detail. The scales comprise a non-growing "crown" composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dentine" title="Dentine">dentine</a>, with a sometimes-ornamented <a href="/wiki/Tooth_enamel" title="Tooth enamel">enameloid</a> upper surface and an aspidine base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C3.A4rss2006b_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.A4rss2006b-9">[9]</a></sup> Its growing base is made of cell-free bone, which sometimes developed anchorage structures to fix it in the side of the fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janvier1998_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janvier1998-10">[10]</a></sup> Beyond that, there appear to be five types of bone-growth, which may represent five natural groupings within the thelodontsâ€”or a spectrum ranging between the end members meta- (or ortho-) dentine and mesodentine tissues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner1991_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turner1991-11">[11]</a></sup> Interestingly, each of the five scale morphs appears to resemble the scales of more derived groupings of fish, suggesting that thelodont groups may have been stem groups to succeeding clades of fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janvier1998_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janvier1998-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, using scale morphology alone to distinguish species has some pitfalls. Within each organism, scale shape varies hugely according to body area,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M.C3.A4rss1986_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-M.C3.A4rss1986-12">[12]</a></sup> with intermediate forms appearing between different areasâ€”and to make matters worse, scale morphology may not even be constant within one area. To confuse things further, scale morphologies are not unique to taxa, and may be indistinguishable on the same area of two different species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Botella2006_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Botella2006-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The morphology and histology of thelodonts provides the main tool for quantifying their diversity and distinguishing between species, although ultimately using such <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent</a> traits is prone to errors. Nonetheless, a framework comprising three groups has been proposed based upon scale morphology and histology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner1991_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Turner1991-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modifications">Modifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fish_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Modifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scale_Common_Roach.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4272" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Scale_Common_Roach.JPG/220px-Scale_Common_Roach.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Scale_Common_Roach.JPG/330px-Scale_Common_Roach.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Scale_Common_Roach.JPG/440px-Scale_Common_Roach.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scale_Common_Roach.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The cycloid scales of a <a href="/wiki/Common_roach" title="Common roach">common roach</a>. The series of lateral line scales is visible in the lower half of the image.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Different groups of fish have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evolved" title="Evolved">evolved</a> a number of modified scales to serve various functions.</p>
<ul>
<li>Almost all fishes have a <a href="/wiki/Lateral_line" title="Lateral line">lateral line</a>, a system of <a href="/wiki/Mechanoreceptor" title="Mechanoreceptor">mechanoreceptors</a> that detect water movements. In bony fishes, the scales along the lateral line have central pores that allow water to contact the sensory cells.</li>
<li>The dorsal fin spines of <a href="/wiki/Squaliformes" title="Squaliformes">dogfish sharks</a> and chimaeras, the stinging tail spines of <a href="/wiki/Myliobatiformes" title="Myliobatiformes">stingrays</a>, and the "saw" teeth of <a href="/wiki/Sawfish" title="Sawfish">sawfishes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sawshark" title="Sawshark">sawsharks</a> are fused and modified placoid scales.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Porcupine_fish" title="Porcupine fish">Porcupine fishes</a> have scales modified into <a href="/wiki/Spine_(zoology)" title="Spine (zoology)">spines</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surgeonfish" title="Surgeonfish">Surgeonfishes</a> have a sharp, blade-like spines on either side of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caudal_peduncle" title="Caudal peduncle">caudal peduncle</a>.</li>
<li>Some <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herrings</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anchovies" title="Anchovies">anchovies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Halfbeak" title="Halfbeak">halfbeaks</a> have <b>deciduous scales</b>, which are easily shed and aid in escaping predators.</li>
<li>Male <i><a href="/wiki/Percina" title="Percina">Percina</a></i> darters have a row of enlarged <b>caducous scales</b> between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelvic_fin" title="Pelvic fin">pelvic fins</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Many groups of bony fishes, including <a href="/wiki/Pipefish" title="Pipefish">pipefishes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seahorse" title="Seahorse">seahorses</a>, several families of <a href="/wiki/Catfish" title="Catfish">catfishes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stickleback" title="Stickleback">sticklebacks</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poacher" title="Poacher">poachers</a>, have developed external bony plates, structurally resembling placoid scales, as protective armour. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boxfish" title="Boxfish">boxfishes</a>, the plates are all fused together to form a rigid shell enclosing the entire body. Yet these bony plates are not modified scales, but skin that has been ossified.</p>
