<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Children's Corner</i></h1>

<p><i><b>Children's Corner</b></i> (L. 113) is a six-movement suite for solo piano by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Debussy" title="Claude Debussy">Claude Debussy</a>. It was published by Durand in 1908, and was given its world première in Paris by <a href="/wiki/Harold_Bauer" title="Harold Bauer">Harold Bauer</a> on 18 December that year. In 1911, an orchestration of the work by Debussy's friend <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Caplet" title="André Caplet">André Caplet</a> received its première and was subsequently published. A typical performance of the suite lasts roughly 15 minutes.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dedication">Dedication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Children%27s_Corner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Dedication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is dedicated to Debussy's daughter, Claude-Emma (known as "Chou-Chou"), who was three years old at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The pieces are not intended to be played by children; rather they are meant to be evocative of childhood and some of the toys in Claude-Emma's toy collection.</p>
<p>Claude-Emma was born on 30 October 1905 in Paris, and is described as a lively and friendly child who was adored by her father. She died of <a href="/wiki/Diphtheria" title="Diphtheria">diphtheria</a> on 14 July 1919, scarcely a year after her father's death.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Children%27s_Corner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are six pieces in the suite, each with an English-language title. This choice of language is most likely Debussy's nod towards Chou-Chou's English governess. The pieces are:</p>
<ol>
<li><i>Doctor Gradus ad Parnassum</i></li>
<li><i>Jimbo's Lullaby</i></li>
<li><i>Serenade for the Doll</i></li>
<li><i>The Snow is Dancing</i></li>
<li><i>The Little Shepherd</i></li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golliwogg" title="Golliwogg">Golliwogg</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Cakewalk" title="Cakewalk">Cakewalk</a></i></li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_pieces">The pieces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Children%27s_Corner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The pieces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Doctor_Gradus_ad_Parnassum"><i>Doctor Gradus ad Parnassum</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Children%27s_Corner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Doctor Gradus ad Parnassum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The title of the first alludes to <a href="/wiki/Johann_Joseph_Fux" title="Johann Joseph Fux">Johann Joseph Fux</a>'s (1660-1741), <i><a href="/wiki/Gradus_ad_Parnassum" title="Gradus ad Parnassum">Gradus ad Parnassum</a></i> ("Steps to <a href="/wiki/Mount_Parnassus" title="Mount Parnassus">Parnassus</a>"). <i>Gradus ad Parnassum</i> "became the first 'counterpoint text' in the modern sense and the greatest schoolbook in the history of European music."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Although it is unlikely that this textbook would be found on the book shelf of the average French pianist, the "Gradus Ad Parnassum" by <a href="/wiki/Muzio_Clementi" title="Muzio Clementi">Muzio Clementi</a> was - and still is in some quarters. The exercises resemble <a href="/wiki/Carl_Czerny" title="Carl Czerny">Czerny</a>'s in many respects and are often boring to practice or listen to for many people. This piece is actually a rather ingenious study in finger independence with a Twentieth Century vocabulary. In the middle, the pianist slows down and tries his material in other keys for exercise. Debussy's <i>Doctor Gradus Ad Parnassum</i> is of intermediate difficulty and requires experienced fingers. The pianist gets wilder toward the end and finishes the piece with a bang.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jimbo.27s_Lullaby"><i>Jimbo's Lullaby</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Children%27s_Corner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Jimbo's Lullaby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This work describes an elephant, <a href="/wiki/Jumbo" title="Jumbo">Jumbo</a>, who came from the French Sudan and lived briefly in the Jardin des Plantes in Paris around the time of Debussy's birth. The misspelling "Jimbo" betrays the Parisian accent which often confuses the pronunciation of "um" and "un" with "im" and "in". It is a beautiful lullaby with some dark moments and whole-tone passages in the middle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Serenade_of_the_Doll"><i>Serenade of the Doll</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Children%27s_Corner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Serenade of the Doll">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This piece, in triple meter, is marked <i>Allegretto ma non troppo</i> (moderately fast, but not too fast). It is a description of an Oriental porcelain doll and features the Chinese pentatonic scale throughout. Debussy notes that the entire piece should be played with the soft pedal. Some pianists contend that Debussy really meant "Serenade For the Doll".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Snow_is_Dancing"><i>The Snow is Dancing</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Children%27s_Corner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Snow is Dancing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Technically, this piece is quite difficult as it requires precise semi-detached playing in both hands with the melody between them. Again, there are darker moments in the bass near the middle. It portrays snow and muted objects seen through it.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="subjective language and content. please substantiate (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Little_Shepherd"><i>The Little Shepherd</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Children%27s_Corner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Little Shepherd">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Little Shepherd depicts a shepherd with his flute. There are three solos and three commentaries following them. The first solo actually has a breath mark at the end. This piece has different modes in it and uses dissonances which resolve into tonality.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Golliwogg.27s_Cakewalk"><i>Golliwogg's Cakewalk</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Children%27s_Corner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Golliwogg's Cakewalk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tristan_quote_in_Children%27s_Corner.png"><img alt="Tristan quote in Children's Corner.png" data-file-height="190" data-file-width="601" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/09/Tristan_quote_in_Children%27s_Corner.png" width="601"/></a></div>
<p>At the time of its composition, <a href="/wiki/Golliwog" title="Golliwog">Golliwoggs</a> were in fashion, due partly to the popularity at that time of the novels of <a href="/wiki/Florence_Kate_Upton" title="Florence Kate Upton">Florence Kate Upton</a> ("golliwog" is a later usage). They were stuffed black dolls with red pants, red bow ties and wild hair, somewhat reminiscent of the black-face minstrels of the time. This is a ragtime piece with its syncopations and banjo-like effects. The dynamic range is quite large and very effective. The B section of this dance is interrupted on several occasions by the love-death leitmotif of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>'s opera <i><a href="/wiki/Tristan_und_Isolde" title="Tristan und Isolde">Tristan und Isolde</a></i>, marked <i>avec une grande émotion</i> (with great feeling). Each quotation is followed with banjo imitations. The <a href="/wiki/Cakewalk" title="Cakewalk">cakewalk</a> was a dance or a strut and the dancer with the most elaborate steps won a cake ("took the cake").</p>
