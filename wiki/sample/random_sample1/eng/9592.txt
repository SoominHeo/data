<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Jester</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<p>A <b>jester</b>, <b>court jester</b>, or <b>fool</b> was historically an entertainer during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> eras who was a member of the household of a nobleman or a monarch employed to entertain him and his guests. A jester was also an itinerant performer who entertained common folk at fairs and markets. Jesters are also modern-day entertainers who resemble their historical counterparts. Jesters in medieval times are often thought to have worn brightly coloured clothes and eccentric hats in a <a href="/wiki/Motley" title="Motley">motley</a> pattern and their modern counterparts usually mimic this costume. In medieval times jesters entertained with a wide variety of skills: principal ones included songs, music, and <a href="/wiki/Storytelling" title="Storytelling">storytelling</a>; additional ones included <a href="/wiki/Acrobatics" title="Acrobatics">acrobatics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juggling" title="Juggling">juggling</a>, telling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jokes" title="Jokes">jokes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Magic_(illusion)" title="Magic (illusion)">magic</a>. Much of the entertainment was performed in a comic style and many jesters made contemporary jokes in word or song about people or events well known to their audiences.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The modern use of the English word <i>jester</i> did not come into use until the mid-16th century during Tudor times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FJ_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FJ-1">[1]</a></sup> This modern term derives from the older form <i>gestour</i>, or <i>jestour</i>, originally from Anglo-Norman (French) meaning <i>story-teller</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Minstrel" title="Minstrel">minstrel</a></i>. Other earlier terms included <i>fol</i>, <i>disour</i>, and <i>bourder</i>. These terms described entertainers who differed in their skills and performances but who all shared many similarities in their role as comedic performers for their audiences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FJ_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FJ-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_significance">Political significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Laughing_Fool.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4880" data-file-width="3126" height="265" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Laughing_Fool.jpg/170px-Laughing_Fool.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Laughing_Fool.jpg/255px-Laughing_Fool.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Laughing_Fool.jpg/340px-Laughing_Fool.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Laughing_Fool.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Laughing jester, circa 1500</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Scholar David Carlyon has cast doubt on the "daring political jester", calling historical tales "apocryphal", and concluding that "popular culture embraces a sentimental image of the clown; writers reproduce that sentimentality in the jester, and academics in the Trickster", but it "falters as analysis".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jesters could also give bad news to the King that no one else would dare deliver. The best example of this is in 1340, when the French fleet was destroyed at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Sluys" title="Battle of Sluys">Battle of Sluys</a> by the English. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phillippe_VI" title="Phillippe VI">Phillippe VI</a>'s jester told him the English sailors "don't even have the guts to jump into the water like our brave French".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early jesters were popular in Ancient Egypt, and entertained Egyptian pharaohs. Jesters were popular with the Aztec people in the 14th to 16th centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_royal_court_jesters">English royal court jesters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: English royal court jesters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WillSommers_engraving_300dpi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1773" data-file-width="1041" height="290" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/WillSommers_engraving_300dpi.jpg/170px-WillSommers_engraving_300dpi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/WillSommers_engraving_300dpi.jpg/255px-WillSommers_engraving_300dpi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/WillSommers_engraving_300dpi.jpg/340px-WillSommers_engraving_300dpi.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WillSommers_engraving_300dpi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
17th-century engraving of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Sommers" title="William Sommers">Will Sommers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>'s jester</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many courts throughout English royal history employed entertainers and most had professional fools, sometimes called <i>licensed fools</i>. Entertainment included <a href="/wiki/Early_music" title="Early music">music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Storytelling" title="Storytelling">storytelling</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Physical_comedy" title="Physical comedy">physical comedy</a>. It has also been suggested they performed <a href="/wiki/Acrobatics" title="Acrobatics">acrobatics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juggling" title="Juggling">juggling</a></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> employed a jester named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Sommers" title="William Sommers">Will Sommers</a>.</p>
<p>During the reigns of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" title="James I of England">James I of England</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> wrote his plays and performed with his theatre company the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chamberlain%27s_Men" title="Lord Chamberlain's Men">Lord Chamberlain's Men</a> (later called the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Men_(playing_company)" title="King's Men (playing company)">King's Men</a>). Clowns and jesters were featured in Shakespeare's plays, and the company's expert on jesting was <a href="/wiki/Robert_Armin" title="Robert Armin">Robert Armin</a>, author of the book <i>Fooled upon Foole</i>. In Shakespeare's <i><a href="/wiki/Twelfth_Night" title="Twelfth Night">Twelfth Night</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Feste" title="Feste">Feste</a> the jester is described as "wise enough to play the fool".</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%22The_Court_Jester%22_(4540421737).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="436" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/%22The_Court_Jester%22_%284540421737%29.jpg/170px-%22The_Court_Jester%22_%284540421737%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/%22The_Court_Jester%22_%284540421737%29.jpg/255px-%22The_Court_Jester%22_%284540421737%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/%22The_Court_Jester%22_%284540421737%29.jpg/340px-%22The_Court_Jester%22_%284540421737%29.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%22The_Court_Jester%22_(4540421737).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A court jester, carrying a <a href="/wiki/Marotte" title="Marotte">marotte</a>, selected for the <a href="/wiki/Pageant_of_Empire" title="Pageant of Empire">Pageant of Empire</a> in 1909</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_VI_of_Scotland" title="James VI of Scotland">James VI of Scotland</a> also employed a jester called <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Armstrong" title="Archibald Armstrong">Archibald Armstrong</a>. During his lifetime Armstrong was given great honours at court. He was eventually thrown out of the King's employment when he over-reached himself and insulted too many influential people. Even after his disgrace, books telling of his jests were sold in London streets. He held some influence at court still in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> and estates of land in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. Charles later employed a jester called <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Hudson" title="Jeffrey Hudson">Jeffrey Hudson</a> who was very popular and loyal. Jeffrey Hudson had the title of <i>Royal <a href="/wiki/Dwarfism" title="Dwarfism">Dwarf</a></i> because he was short of stature. One of his jests was to be presented hidden in a giant pie from which he would leap out. Hudson fought on the <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">Royalist</a> side in the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>. A third jester associated with Charles I was called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Muckle_John&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Muckle John (page does not exist)">Muckle John</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_tradition">End of tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: End of tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Restoration" title="English Restoration">Restoration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> did not reinstate the tradition of the court jester, but he did greatly patronize the theatre and proto-<a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> entertainments, especially favouring the work of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Killigrew" title="Thomas Killigrew">Thomas Killigrew</a>. Though Killigrew was not officially a jester, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pepys" title="Samuel Pepys">Samuel Pepys</a> in his famous diary does call Killigrew "The King's fool and jester, with the power to mock and revile even the most prominent without penalty" (12 February 1668). The last British nobles to keep jesters were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Bowes-Lyon" title="Elizabeth Bowes-Lyon">Queen Mother</a>'s family, the Bowes-Lyons.</p>
<p>In the 18th century, jesters had died out except in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, travelling groups of jesters performed plays featuring stylized characters in a form of theatre called the <i><a href="/wiki/Commedia_dell%27arte" title="Commedia dell'arte">commedia dell'arte</a></i>. A version of this passed into <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folk_tradition" title="Folk tradition">folk tradition</a> in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Puppet" title="Puppet">puppet</a> show, <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_and_Judy" title="Punch and Judy">Punch and Judy</a></i>. In France the tradition of the court jester ended with the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>.</p>
<p>In 1968, the <a href="/wiki/Canada_Council" title="Canada Council">Canada Council</a> awarded a $3,500 grant to Joachim Foikis of <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a> "to revive the ancient and time-honoured tradition of town fool".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In the 21st century, the jester is still seen at medieval-style <a href="/wiki/Fair" title="Fair">fairs</a> and pageants.</p>
<p>In 2015, the town of <a href="/wiki/Conwy" title="Conwy">Conwy</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Wales" title="North Wales">North Wales</a> appointed <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Russel_Erwood&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Russel Erwood (page does not exist)">Russel Erwood</a> (aka <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Erwyd_le_Fol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Erwyd le Fol (page does not exist)">Erwyd le Fol</a>) as the official resident jester of the town and its people, a post that had been vacant since 1295.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>'s most famous court jester was <a href="/wiki/Sta%C5%84czyk" title="Stańczyk">Stańczyk</a>, whose jokes were usually related to political matters, and who later became a historical symbol for Poles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kochanowski_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kochanowski-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jakubowski_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jakubowski-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004 <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> appointed <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Roder" title="Nigel Roder">Nigel Roder</a> ("Kester the Jester") as the State Jester for England, the first since Muckle John 355 years previously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However following an objection by the National Guild of Jesters, English Heritage accepted they were not authorised to grant such a title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Roder was succeeded as "Heritage Jester" by Pete Cooper ("Peterkin the Fool").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Till_Eulenspiegel" title="Till Eulenspiegel">Till Eulenspiegel</a> is a folkloric hero dating back to medieval times and ruling each year over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fasching" title="Fasching">Fasching</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">Carnival</a> time, mocking politicians and public figures of power and authority with <a href="/wiki/Political_satire" title="Political satire">political satire</a> like a modern-day court jester. He holds a mirror to make us aware of our times (<a href="/wiki/Zeitgeist" title="Zeitgeist">Zeitgeist</a>), and his <a href="/wiki/Sceptre" title="Sceptre">sceptre</a>, his "bauble" or <a href="/wiki/Marotte" title="Marotte">marotte</a>, is the symbol of his power.</p>

<p>In 17th century <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dwarfism" title="Dwarfism">little people</a>, often with deformities, were employed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffoon" title="Buffoon">buffoons</a> to entertain the king and his family, especially the children. In <a href="/wiki/Diego_Vel%C3%A1zquez" title="Diego Velázquez">Velázquez</a>'s painting <a href="/wiki/Las_Meninas" title="Las Meninas">Las Meninas</a> two dwarves are included: Mari Bárbola a female dwarf from <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrocephalus" title="Hydrocephalus">hydrocephalus</a>, and <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolasito_Pertusato" title="es:Nicolasito Pertusato">Nicolasito Portusato</a> from <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. Mari Bárbola can also be seen in a later portrait of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Margarita_Teresa_of_Spain" title="Margarita Teresa of Spain">princess Margarita Teresa</a> in mourning by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juan_Bautista_Martinez_del_Mazo" title="Juan Bautista Martinez del Mazo">Juan Bautista Martinez del Mazo</a>. There are other paintings by Velázquez that include court dwarves such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Balthasar_Charles_With_a_Dwarf" title="Prince Balthasar Charles With a Dwarf">Prince Balthasar Charles With a Dwarf</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> from the 13th to 18th centuries, the <i><a href="/wiki/Taikomochi" title="Taikomochi">taikomochi</a></i>, a kind of male <a href="/wiki/Geisha" title="Geisha">geisha</a>, attended the feudal lords (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daimy%C5%8Ds" title="Daimyōs">daimyōs</a></i>). They entertained mostly through dancing and storytelling, and were at times counted on for strategic advice. By the 16th century they would fight alongside their lord in battle in addition to their other duties.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a> was the first royal court to appoint a court jester in modern times; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taufa%27ahau_Tupou_IV" title="Taufa'ahau Tupou IV">Taufa'ahau Tupou IV</a>, the King of Tonga, appointed <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Bogdonoff" title="Jesse Bogdonoff">JD Bogdanoff</a> to that role in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Bogdanoff was later embroiled in a financial scandal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_jester_as_a_symbol">The jester as a symbol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The jester as a symbol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The root of the word "fool" is from the Latin <i>follis</i>, which means "bag of wind" or that which contains air or breath.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fool_in_Tarot">Fool in Tarot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fool in Tarot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarot_cards" title="Tarot cards">Tarot</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Fool_(Tarot_card)" title="The Fool (Tarot card)">"The Fool"</a> is a card of the <a href="/wiki/Major_Arcana" title="Major Arcana">Major Arcana</a>. The tarot depiction of the Fool includes a man (or less often, a woman) <a href="/wiki/Juggling" title="Juggling">juggling</a> unconcernedly or otherwise distracted, often with a dog or cat at his heels. The fool is in the act of unknowingly walking off the edge of a cliff, precipice or other high place. Another Tarot character is <a href="/wiki/Death_(Tarot_card)" title="Death (Tarot card)">Death</a>. In the Middle Ages, Death is often shown in Jester's garb because "The last laugh is reserved for death."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Also, Death humbles everyone just as jesters make fun of everyone regardless of standing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fool_in_literature">Fool in literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fool in literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In literature, the jester is symbolic of common sense and of honesty, notably in <i><a href="/wiki/King_Lear" title="King Lear">King Lear</a></i>, where the court jester is a character used for insight and advice on the part of the monarch, taking advantage of his license to mock and speak freely to dispense frank observations and highlight the folly of his monarch. This presents a clashing irony as a "greater" man could dispense the same advice and find himself being detained in the dungeons or even executed. Only as the lowliest member of the court can the jester be the monarch's most useful adviser.</p>
<p>Author <a href="/wiki/Alan_Gordon_(author)" title="Alan Gordon (author)">Alan Gordon</a> also writes about jesters as advisers to the king, who actually make up a super-secret spy ring that try to keep peace and control the leaders of different countries. The Fool's Guild of these novels is portrayed as a mockery to the church, and they refer to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a> as "Their Savior, The First Fool."</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buffoon">Buffoon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jester&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Buffoon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In similar vein, <b>buffoon</b> is a term for someone who provides amusement through inappropriate appearance or behavior. In Australian colloquial slang, <i>buffoon</i> can be used affectionately like the term <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dags_(subculture)" title="Dags (subculture)">dag</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Originally the term was used to describe a ridiculous but amusing person. The term is now frequently used in a derogatory sense to describe someone considered foolish, or someone displaying inappropriately vulgar, bumbling or ridiculous behavior that is a source of general amusement.</p>
<p>The term originates from the old Italian "buffare", meaning to puff out one's cheeks<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> that also applies to <a href="/wiki/Bouffon" title="Bouffon">bouffon</a>.</p>

<p>Today, the jester is portrayed in different formats of <a href="/wiki/Medieval_reenactment" title="Medieval reenactment">medieval reenactment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Entertainment" title="Entertainment">entertainment</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stage_(theatre)" title="Stage (theatre)">stage</a> performance, and <a href="/wiki/Carnival" title="Carnival">carnival</a>. During the Burgundian and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhenish" title="Rhenish">Rhenish</a> <a href="/wiki/Carnival_in_the_Netherlands" title="Carnival in the Netherlands">carnival</a>, cabaret performances in local dialect are held. In <a href="/wiki/North_Brabant" title="North Brabant">Brabant</a> this person is called a "tonpraoter" or "sauwelaar", and is actually in or on a barrel. In <a href="/wiki/Limburg_(Netherlands)" title="Limburg (Netherlands)">Limburg</a> they are named "buuttereedner" or "buutteredner" and in <a href="/wiki/Zeeland" title="Zeeland">Zeeland</a> they are called an "ouwoer". They all perform a cabaret speech in dialect, during which many current issues are reviewed. Often there are local situations and celebrities from local and regional politics who are mocked, ridiculed and insulted. The "Tonpraoter" or "Buuttereedner" may be considered successors of the jesters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
