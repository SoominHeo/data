<p>A <b>fisherman</b> or <b>fisher</b> is someone who captures <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and other animals from a body of water, or gathers <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Worldwide, there are about 38 million <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">commercial</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" title="Artisan fishing">subsistence</a> fishermen and <a href="/wiki/Fish_farming" title="Fish farming">fish farmers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-2">[2]</a></sup> The term can also be applied to <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" title="Recreational fishing">recreational fishermen</a> and may be used to describe both men and women. Fishing has existed as a means of obtaining food since the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Early_humans_followed_the_coast_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Early_humans_followed_the_coast-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisherman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Egyptian_fishery3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="133" data-file-width="400" height="73" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Egyptian_fishery3.jpg/220px-Egyptian_fishery3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Egyptian_fishery3.jpg/330px-Egyptian_fishery3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9a/Egyptian_fishery3.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Egyptian_fishery3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_fishing" title="History of fishing">History of fishing</a></div>
<p>Fishing has existed as a means of obtaining food since the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> period. During the time of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egyptians</a>, fishermen provided the majority of food for <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a>. Fishing had become a major means of survival as well as a business venture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Fishing and the fisherman have also influenced <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion" title="Ancient Egyptian religion">Ancient Egyptian religion</a>; <a href="/wiki/Mullet_(fish)" title="Mullet (fish)">mullets</a> were worshiped as a sign of the arriving <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood season</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bastet" title="Bastet">Bastet</a> was often manifested in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Catfish" title="Catfish">catfish</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion" title="Ancient Egyptian religion">ancient Egyptian literature</a>, the method that <a href="/wiki/Amun" title="Amun">Amun</a> used to create the world is associated with the <a href="/wiki/Tilapia" title="Tilapia">tilapia</a>'s method of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mouth-brooding" title="Mouth-brooding">mouth-brooding</a>.</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commerce">Commerce</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisherman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Commerce">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Charles_Napier_Hemy_-_The_Fisherman_1888.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="535" data-file-width="795" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Charles_Napier_Hemy_-_The_Fisherman_1888.jpg/220px-Charles_Napier_Hemy_-_The_Fisherman_1888.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Charles_Napier_Hemy_-_The_Fisherman_1888.jpg/330px-Charles_Napier_Hemy_-_The_Fisherman_1888.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Charles_Napier_Hemy_-_The_Fisherman_1888.jpg/440px-Charles_Napier_Hemy_-_The_Fisherman_1888.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Charles_Napier_Hemy_-_The_Fisherman_1888.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<div class="center" style="width:auto; margin-left:auto; margin-right:auto;"><i>The fisherman</i> – by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Napier_Hemy" title="Charles Napier Hemy">Charles Napier Hemy</a>, 1888</div>
</div>
</div>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Commercial_fishing" title="Commercial fishing">Commercial fishing</a></div>
<p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAO" title="FAO">FAO</a>, there were about 39 million fishers in countries producing more than 200,000 tonnes in 2012, which is nearly 140% the number in 1995. The total <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fishery</a> production of 66 million tonnes equated to an average productivity of 3.5 tonnes per person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of this growth took place in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> countries, where four-fifths of world fishers and fish farmers dwell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most fishermen are men involved in offshore and deep-sea fisheries. Women fish in some regions inshore from small boats or collect <a href="/wiki/Shellfish" title="Shellfish">shellfish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a>. In many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" title="Artisan fishing">artisanal fishing</a> communities, women are responsible for making and repairing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_nets" title="Fishing nets">nets</a>, post-harvest <a href="/wiki/Fish_processing" title="Fish processing">processing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_marketing" title="Fish marketing">marketing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreation">Recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisherman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" title="Recreational fishing">Recreational fishing</a></div>
<p>Recreational fishing is fishing for pleasure or competition. It can be contrasted with commercial fishing, which is fishing for <a href="/wiki/Profit_(economics)" title="Profit (economics)">economic profit</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" title="Artisan fishing">subsistence fishing</a>, which is fishing for survival.</p>
<p>The most common form of recreational fishing is done with a <a href="/wiki/Fishing_rod" title="Fishing rod">rod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_reel" title="Fishing reel">reel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing_line" title="Fishing line">line</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish_hook" title="Fish hook">hooks</a> and any one of a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Fishing_bait" title="Fishing bait">baits</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fishing_lure" title="Fishing lure">Lures</a> are frequently used in place of bait. Some people make handmade lures, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_plastic" title="Soft plastic">plastic lures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Artificial_fly" title="Artificial fly">artificial flies</a>.</p>
<p>The practice of catching or attempting to catch fish with a hook is called <a href="/wiki/Angling" title="Angling">angling</a>. When angling, it is sometimes expected or required that the fish be <a href="/wiki/Catch_and_release" title="Catch and release">caught and released</a>. <a href="/wiki/Big-game_fishing" title="Big-game fishing">Big-game fishing</a> is fishing from boats to catch large open-water species such as <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shark" title="Shark">sharks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marlin" title="Marlin">marlin</a>. <a href="/wiki/Noodling" title="Noodling">Noodling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trout_tickling" title="Trout tickling">trout tickling</a> are also recreational activities.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Communities">Communities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisherman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Communities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Fishing_village" title="Fishing village">Fishing village</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artisan_fishing" title="Artisan fishing">Artisan fishing</a></div>
<p>For some <a href="/wiki/Fishing_village" title="Fishing village">communities</a>, fishing provides not only a source of food and work but also <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a> and <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultural</a> identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_issues">Safety issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisherman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Safety issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Work_in_Fishing_Convention_2007" title="Work in Fishing Convention 2007">Work in Fishing Convention 2007</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fishing_industry" title="Fishing industry">fishing industry</a> is hazardous for fishermen. Between 1992 and 1999, US commercial <a href="/wiki/Fishing_vessel" title="Fishing vessel">fishing vessels</a> averaged 78 deaths per year. The main contributors to fatalities are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_Profile_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO_Profile-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>inadequate preparation for emergencies</li>
<li>poor vessel maintenance and inadequate safety equipment</li>
<li>lack of awareness of or ignoring stability issues.</li>
</ul>
<p>Many fishermen, while accepting that fishing is dangerous, staunchly defend their independence. Many proposed laws and additional regulation to increase safety have been defeated because fishermen oppose them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_Profile_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAO_Profile-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alaska's commercial fishermen work in one of the world's harshest environments. Many of the hardships they endure include isolated fishing grounds, high winds, seasonal darkness, very cold water, icing, and short fishing seasons, where very long work days are the norm. Fatigue, physical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(medicine)" title="Stress (medicine)">stress</a>, and financial pressures face most Alaska fishermen through their careers. The hazardous work conditions faced by fishermen have a strong impact on their safety. Out of 948 work-related deaths that took place in Alaska during 1990-2006, one-third (311) occurred to fishermen. This is equivalent to an estimated annual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatality_rate" title="Fatality rate">fatality rate</a> of 128/100,000 workers/year. This fatality rate is 26 times that of the overall U.S. work-related fatality rate of approximately 5/100,000 workers/year for the same time period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIOSH_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIOSH-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the work-related fatality rate for commercial fishermen in Alaska is still very high, it does appear to be decreasing: since 1990, there has been a 51 percent decline in the annual fatality rate. The successes in commercial fishing are due in part to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Coast_Guard" title="U.S. Coast Guard">U.S. Coast Guard</a> implementing new safety requirements in the early 1990s. These safety requirements contributed to 96 percent of the commercial fishermen surviving vessel sinkings/capsizings in 2004, whereas in 1991, only 73 percent survived. While the number of occupational deaths in commercial fishermen in Alaska has been reduced, there is a continuing pattern of losing 20 to 40 vessels every year. There are still about 100 fishermen who must be rescued each year from cold Alaska waters. Successful rescue is still dependent on the expertly trained personnel of the US Coast Guard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Search_and_Rescue" title="Search and Rescue">Search and Rescue</a> operations, and such efforts can be hindered by the harshness of seas and the weather. Furthermore, the people involved in Search and Rescue operations are themselves at considerable risk for injury or death during these rescue attempts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIOSH_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIOSH-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
