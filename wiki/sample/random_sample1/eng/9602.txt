<p><b>Irvine Welsh</b> (born 27 September 1957) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_novelist" title="Scottish novelist">Scottish novelist</a>, playwright and short story writer. He is recognised for his novel <i><a href="/wiki/Trainspotting_(novel)" title="Trainspotting (novel)">Trainspotting</a></i>, which was later made into a <a href="/wiki/Trainspotting_(film)" title="Trainspotting (film)">film of the same name</a>. His work is characterised by a raw <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots dialect</a> and brutal depiction of <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> life. He has also written plays and screenplays, and directed several short films.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irvine_Welsh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Irvine Welsh was born in <a href="/wiki/Leith" title="Leith">Leith</a>, the port area of the Scottish capital <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>. His birth record states that he was born in 1957, though it has been widely reported that it is actually 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> When he was four, his family moved to <a href="/wiki/Muirhouse" title="Muirhouse">Muirhouse</a>, in Edinburgh, where they stayed in local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Housing_scheme" title="Housing scheme">housing schemes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ir_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ir-2">[2]</a></sup> His mother worked as a waitress. His father was a dock worker in Leith until bad health forced him to quit, after which he became a carpet salesman; he died when Welsh was 25. Welsh left <a href="/wiki/Ainslie_Park_High_School" title="Ainslie Park High School">Ainslie Park High School</a> when he was 16 and then completed a City and Guilds course in electrical engineering. He became an apprentice TV repairman until an <a href="/wiki/Electric_shock" title="Electric shock">electric shock</a> persuaded him to move on to a series of other jobs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ir_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ir-2">[2]</a></sup> He left Edinburgh for the London <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk</a> scene in 1978, where he played <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a> and sang in The Pubic Lice and Stairway 13,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ir_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-ir-2">[2]</a></sup> the latter a reference to the <a href="/wiki/1971_Ibrox_disaster" title="1971 Ibrox disaster">Ibrox disaster</a>. A series of arrests for petty crimes and finally a suspended sentence for trashing a North London community centre inspired Welsh to correct his ways. He worked for <a href="/wiki/Hackney_London_Borough_Council" title="Hackney London Borough Council">Hackney London Borough Council</a> in London and studied <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a> with the support of the <a href="/wiki/Manpower_Services_Commission" title="Manpower Services Commission">Manpower Services Commission</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ir_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-ir-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Welsh returned to Edinburgh in the late 1980s, where he worked for the city council in the housing department. He then studied for an MBA at Heriot Watt University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Welsh has made several reading tours around the world and has been involved with <a href="/wiki/House_music" title="House music">house music</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DJ" title="DJ">DJ</a>, promoter and producer. Welsh has been an official ambassador of the <a href="/wiki/Homeless_World_Cup" title="Homeless World Cup">Homeless World Cup</a> movement since 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an interview with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Daily_Mail" title="The Daily Mail">The Daily Mail</a></i> on 7 August 2006, he described himself as "not so much middle-class as upper-class. I'm very much a gentleman of leisure. I write. I sit and look out of my window into the garden. I enjoy books. I love the density and complexity of <a href="/wiki/Jane_Austen" title="Jane Austen">Jane Austen</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Eliot" title="George Eliot">George Eliot</a>. I listen to music; I travel. I can go off to a film festival whenever I like." He also describes himself as monogamous: "it sounds boring but it's the way I am".</p>
<p>On 20 April 2012, on BBC Breakfast, he stated that he lives in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> with his wife, Elizabeth. Previously he lived in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, Ireland, and regularly attended <a href="/wiki/Bohemian_F.C." title="Bohemian F.C.">Bohemian Football Club</a> games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiction">Fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irvine_Welsh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>To date, Welsh has published eleven novels and four collections of short stories. His <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_novel" title="First novel">first novel</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Trainspotting_(novel)" title="Trainspotting (novel)">Trainspotting</a></i>, was published in 1993. Set in the mid-1980s, it uses a series of loosely connected short stories to tell the story of a group of characters tied together by decaying friendships, <a href="/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a> <a href="/wiki/Substance_use_disorder" title="Substance use disorder">addiction</a> and stabs at escape from the oppressive boredom and brutality of their lives in the housing schemes. It was released to shock and outrage in some circles and great acclaim in others; <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Out_(company)" title="Time Out (company)">Time Out</a></i> called it "funny, unflinchingly abrasive, authentic and inventive", and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times_(UK)" title="The Sunday Times (UK)">The Sunday Times</a></i> called Welsh "the best thing that has happened to British writing for decades". It was adapted as a play, and a <a href="/wiki/Trainspotting_(film)" title="Trainspotting (film)">film adaptation</a>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Danny_Boyle" title="Danny Boyle">Danny Boyle</a> and written by <a href="/wiki/John_Hodge_(screenwriter)" title="John Hodge (screenwriter)">John Hodge</a>, was released in 1996. Welsh appeared in the film as Mikey Forrester, a minor character. The film was a worldwide success. U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dole" title="Bob Dole">Bob Dole</a> decried its supposed moral depravity and glorification of drug use during the 1996 presidential <a href="/wiki/Political_campaign" title="Political campaign">campaign</a>, although he admitted that he had not seen the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The novel has since achieved a cult status, aided by the global success of the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Next, Welsh released <i><a href="/wiki/The_Acid_House" title="The Acid House">The Acid House</a></i>, a collection of short stories from <i>Rebel Inc.</i>, <i>New Writing Scotland</i> and other sources. Many of the stories take place in and around the housing schemes from <i>Trainspotting</i>, and employ many of the same themes; a touch of fantasy is apparent in stories such as <i>The Acid House</i>, where the minds of a baby and a drug user swap bodies, or <i>The Granton Star Cause</i>, where <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> transforms a man into a fly<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-3">[3]</a></sup> as punishment for wasting his life. Welsh adapted three of the stories for a later <a href="/wiki/The_Acid_House_(film)" title="The Acid House (film)">film of the same name</a>, in which he also appeared.</p>
<p>Welsh's third book (and second novel), <i><a href="/wiki/Marabou_Stork_Nightmares" title="Marabou Stork Nightmares">Marabou Stork Nightmares</a></i>, alternates between a typically grim tale of thugs and schemes in sub-working class Scotland and a hallucinatory adventure tale set in South Africa. Gradually, common themes begin to emerge between the two stories, culminating in a shocking ending.</p>
<p>His next book, <i><a href="/wiki/Ecstasy:_Three_Tales_of_Chemical_Romance" title="Ecstasy: Three Tales of Chemical Romance">Ecstasy: Three Tales of Chemical Romance</a></i> (1996), became his most high-profile work since <i>Trainspotting</i>, released in the wave of publicity surrounding the film. It consists of three unconnected <a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">novellas</a>: the first, <i>Lorraine Goes To Livingston</i>, is a bawdy satire of classic British <a href="/wiki/Romance_novel" title="Romance novel">romance novels</a>, the second, <i>Fortune's Always Hiding</i>, is a revenge story involving <a href="/wiki/Thalidomide" title="Thalidomide">thalidomide</a> and the third, <i>The Undefeated</i>, is a sly, subtle romance between a young woman dissatisfied with the confines of her suburban life and an aging clubgoer.</p>
<p>A corrupt police officer and his <a href="/wiki/Cestoda" title="Cestoda">tapeworm</a> served as the narrators for his third novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Filth_(novel)" title="Filth (novel)">Filth</a></i> (1998). The main character of <i>Filth</i> was a vicious <a href="/wiki/Antisocial_personality_disorder" title="Antisocial personality disorder">sociopathic</a> policeman. The novel was adapted to a <a href="/wiki/Filth_(film)" title="Filth (film)">film with the same name</a> in 2013.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Glue_(novel)" title="Glue (novel)">Glue</a></i> (2001) was a return to the locations, themes and episodic form of <i>Trainspotting</i>, telling the stories of four characters spanning several decades in their lives and the bonds that held them together.</p>
<p>Having revisited some of them in passing in <i>Glue</i>, Welsh brought most of the <i>Trainspotting</i> characters back for a sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Porno_(novel)" title="Porno (novel)">Porno</a></i>, in 2002. In this book Welsh explores the impact of pornography on the individuals involved in producing it, as well as society as a whole, and the impact of ageing and maturity in individuals against their will. The book is set just after the opening of the new Scottish Parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc.co.uk_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.co.uk-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Bedroom_Secrets_of_the_Master_Chefs" title="The Bedroom Secrets of the Master Chefs">The Bedroom Secrets of the Master Chefs</a></i> (2006), deals with a young, alcoholic civil servant who finds himself inadvertently putting a curse on his nemesis, a nerdy co-worker. In 2007, Welsh published <i><a href="/wiki/If_You_Liked_School_You%27ll_Love_Work" title="If You Liked School You'll Love Work">If You Liked School You'll Love Work</a></i>, his first collection of short stories in over a decade.</p>
<p>Welsh contributed a novella called <i>Contamination</i> to <i>The Weekenders: Travels in the Heart of Africa</i>. Welsh, <a href="/wiki/Ian_Rankin" title="Ian Rankin">Ian Rankin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_McCall_Smith" title="Alexander McCall Smith">Alexander McCall Smith</a> each contributed a short story for the <i>One City</i> compilation published in 2005 in benefit of the One City Trust for social inclusion in Edinburgh.</p>
<p>In <i>Crime</i>, Ray Lennox (from Welsh's previous work, <i>Filth</i>) is recovering from a mental breakdown induced by occupational stress and cocaine abuse, and a particularly horrifying child sex murder case back in Edinburgh. The story takes place in Florida.</p>
<p>In 2012 Welsh published a prequel to <i>Trainspotting</i>, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Skagboys" title="Skagboys">Skagboys</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timesonline_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-timesonline-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian.co.uk_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian.co.uk-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Set in Leith in the early 1980s, it introduces the <i>Trainspotting</i> characters and follows them as they fall into heroin addiction. Given as a series of linked short stories, the book is also interspersed with brief commentaries on contemporary British politics. In particular, the consequences of the destruction of industry in the northern cities are drawn for the young working class.</p>
<p>His eighth novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sex_Lives_of_Siamese_Twins" title="The Sex Lives of Siamese Twins">The Sex Lives of Siamese Twins</a></i>, was published in May 2014.</p>
<p>In April 2015 his ninth novel was published via <a href="/wiki/Vintage_Books" title="Vintage Books">Vintage Books</a>, titled <i>A Decent Ride</i> and featuring the returning character ‘Juice’ Terry Lawson (previously from <i>Glue</i>). Despite this, it is a standalone story and not a sequel.</p>
<p>Welsh's tenth novel, released in April 2016, is titled <i>The Blade Artist</i>, and centers around a seemingly rehabilitated Francis Begbie now living in California with a wife and children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It was shortlisted for the Fiction Book of the Year at Saltire Literary Awards 2016. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_and_stage">Film and stage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irvine_Welsh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Film and stage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As well as fiction, Irvine Welsh has written several stage plays, including <i>Headstate</i>, <i>You'll Have Had Your Hole</i>, and the musical <i>Blackpool</i>, which featured original songs by <a href="/wiki/Vic_Godard" title="Vic Godard">Vic Godard</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Subway_Sect" title="Subway Sect">Subway Sect</a>.</p>
<p>More recently he co-authored <i>Babylon Heights</i> with his screen writing partner <a href="/wiki/Dean_Cavanagh" title="Dean Cavanagh">Dean Cavanagh</a>. The play premiered in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> at the Exit Theatre and made its European première in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Mill_Theatre_Dundrum" title="The Mill Theatre Dundrum">The Mill Theatre Dundrum</a>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Graham_Cantwell" title="Graham Cantwell">Graham Cantwell</a>. The plot revolves around the behind-the-scenes antics of a group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Munchkins" title="Munchkins">Munchkins</a> on the set of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">The Wizard of Oz</a></i>. The production included the use of oversized sets with actors of regular stature.</p>
<p>Cavanagh and Welsh have also collaborated on screenplays. <i>The Meat Trade</i> is based on the 19th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Port_murders" title="West Port murders">West Port murders</a>. Despite the historical source material, Welsh has set the story in the familiar confines of present-day Edinburgh, with <a href="/wiki/Burke_and_Hare_murders" title="Burke and Hare murders">Burke</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Hare_(murderer)" title="William Hare (murderer)">Hare</a> depicted as brothers who <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body-snatching" title="Body-snatching">steal human organs</a> to meet the demands of the global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_transplant" title="Organ transplant">transplant</a> market.</p>
<p><i>Wedding Belles</i>, a film made for Channel 4 that was written by Welsh and Cavanagh, aired at the end of March 2007. The film centres around the lives of four young women, who are played by <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Gomez" title="Michelle Gomez">Michelle Gomez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Henderson" title="Shirley Henderson">Shirley Henderson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shauna_MacDonald" title="Shauna MacDonald">Shauna MacDonald</a>, and Kathleen McDermot. <i>Wedding Belles</i> was nominated for a Scottish BAFTA and was subsequently sold to TV channels in Canada and Europe.</p>
<p>Welsh has directed several short films for bands. In 2001 he directed a 15-minute film for <a href="/wiki/Gene_(band)" title="Gene (band)">Gene</a>'s song "Is It Over" which is taken from the album <i>Libertine</i>. In 2006 he directed a short film to accompany the track "<a href="/wiki/Atlantic_(song)" title="Atlantic (song)">Atlantic</a>" from <a href="/wiki/Keane_(band)" title="Keane (band)">Keane</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Under_the_Iron_Sea" title="Under the Iron Sea">Under the Iron Sea</a></i>.</p>
<p>Welsh directed his first short dramatic film, <i>NUTS</i>, which he co-wrote with Cavanagh. The film features <a href="/wiki/Joe_McKinney" title="Joe McKinney">Joe McKinney</a> as a man dealing with <a href="/wiki/Testicular_cancer" title="Testicular cancer">testicular cancer</a> in post <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celtic_tiger" title="Celtic tiger">Celtic tiger</a> Ireland. It was released in 2007.</p>
<p>Welsh co-directed "The Right to liberty", a chapter of the documentary film <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Ten_Commandments" title="The New Ten Commandments">The New Ten Commandments</a></i>, in 2008.</p>
<p>In 2009 Welsh directed the film <i>Good Arrows</i> (co-directed by Helen Grace). It was written by Welsh and Cavanagh. The film is about a darts player who suffers from depression which causes him to lose his skill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irvine_Welsh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As well as <a href="/wiki/Recreational_drug_use" title="Recreational drug use">recreational drug use</a>, Welsh's <a href="/wiki/Fiction" title="Fiction">fiction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-fiction" title="Non-fiction">non-fiction</a> is dominated by the question of <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working class</a> and Scottish identity in the period spanning the 1960s to the present day. Within this, he explores the rise and fall of the council housing scheme, denial of opportunity, <a href="/wiki/Sectarianism" title="Sectarianism">sectarianism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" title="Football (soccer)">football</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hooliganism" title="Hooliganism">hooliganism</a>, sex, suppressed <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuality</a>, dance clubs, low-paid work, <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">freemasonry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_republicanism" title="Irish republicanism">Irish republicanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodomy" title="Sodomy">sodomy</a>, class divisions, <a href="/wiki/Emigration" title="Emigration">emigration</a> and, perhaps most of all, the humour, <a href="/wiki/Prejudice" title="Prejudice">prejudices</a> and axioms of the Scots.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sam_Leith" title="Sam Leith">Sam Leith</a>, writing in the <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a></i>, argues that: "Welsh’s concerns are with sin and salvation, with the exercise of free will and with the individual soul. He's much more interested in <a href="/wiki/Teleology" title="Teleology">teleology</a> than sociology."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irvine_Welsh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Welsh's novels share characters, giving the feel of a "shared universe" within his writing. For example, characters from <i>Trainspotting</i> make cameo appearances in <i>The Acid House</i>, <i>Marabou Stork Nightmares</i>, <i>Ecstasy</i>, <i>Filth</i>, and slightly larger appearances in <i>Glue</i>, whose characters then appear in <i>Porno</i>.</p>
<p>Welsh is known for writing in his native <a href="/wiki/Central_Scots" title="Central Scots">Edinburgh Scots dialect</a>. He generally ignores the traditional conventions of literary Scots, used for example by <a href="/wiki/Allan_Ramsay_(poet)" title="Allan Ramsay (poet)">Allan Ramsay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fergusson" title="Robert Fergusson">Robert Fergusson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Louis_Stevenson" title="Robert Louis Stevenson">Robert Louis Stevenson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/James_Orr_(poet)" title="James Orr (poet)">James Orr</a>. Instead, he transcribes dialects phonetically.</p>
<p>Like <a href="/wiki/Alasdair_Gray" title="Alasdair Gray">Alasdair Gray</a> before him, Welsh also experiments with <a href="/wiki/Typography" title="Typography">typography</a>. In the book <i>Filth</i>, the tapeworm's <a href="/wiki/Internal_monologue" title="Internal monologue">internal monologue</a> is imposed over the top of the protagonist's own internal monologue (the worm's host), visibly depicting the tapeworm's voracious appetite, much like the "Climax of Voices" in Gray's novel <i><a href="/wiki/1982,_Janine" title="1982, Janine">1982, Janine</a></i>.</p>
