<p><b>Eou yadam</b> (<small>Korean pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Korean" title="Help:IPA for Korean">[ʌ.u jadam]</a></span>, "Eou's Unofficial Histories") is a collection of stories by Yu Mong-in (유몽인, 1559–1623), a scholar, official and writer of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseon_Dynasty" title="Joseon Dynasty">Joseon Dynasty</a> of <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> (1392-1910).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The title is composed of his pen name, <i>"Eou"</i>, and <i>"yadam"</i>, which can be roughly translated as "unofficial histories" or "miscellaneous talks" in English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Eou yadam was written in classical Chinese, the written lingua franca of the time. It remains five volumes in one book although <i>Eou yadam</i> originally consisted of 10 volumes. It is regarded the progeniter of <i>yadam,</i> a body of collected stories that flourished in the late Joseon period. While some of his contemporaries praised Eou Yadam as written in a lucid, and succinct literary style, Eu Yadam was never published until the late 19th century because of Yu Mong-in's unfortunate political career, which bought the capital punishment for him and his son for the rumour that they plotted against the then-reigning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Injo" title="King Injo">King Injo</a>. Eu yadam is cited in a number of Joseon literary works by scholars such as Jang Yu (1587–1638), Yi Ik (1681–1763), Jong Yak-yong (1762–1836), etc. At least thirty different editions of Eou yadam are extant. These were used by Yu Mong-in's descendants to reconstruct Eou yadam. This version is called the Manjong-jae version. A number of manuscripts are found at libraries such as <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_Korea" title="National Library of Korea">National Library of Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kyujanggak" title="Kyujanggak">Kyujanggak</a>, Seoul, South Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EncyKorea_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EncyKorea-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
