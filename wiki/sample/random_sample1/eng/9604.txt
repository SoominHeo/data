<p>An <b>adjustable wrench</b> (US) or <b>adjustable spanner</b> (UK) is a <a href="/wiki/Wrench" title="Wrench">wrench</a> with a "jaw" of adjustable width, allowing it to be used with different sizes of <a href="/wiki/Fastener" title="Fastener">fastener</a> head (<a href="/wiki/Nut_(hardware)" title="Nut (hardware)">nut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Screw" title="Screw">bolt</a>, etc.) rather than just one fastener, as with a conventional fixed spanner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms_and_names">Forms and names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjustable_spanner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Forms and names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In many European as well as Middle Eastern countries (e.g. France, Germany, Portugal, Spain, Italy, Syria, Lebanon, Turkey, etc.) the adjustable wrench is called an "English key" as it was first invented in 1842 by the English engineer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Clyburn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Richard Clyburn (page does not exist)">Richard Clyburn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murray1845_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-murray1845-2">[2]</a></sup> Another English engineer, <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Beard_Budding" title="Edwin Beard Budding">Edwin Beard Budding</a>, is also credited with the invention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LloydMitchinson2012_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-LloydMitchinson2012-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DayMcNeil2002_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DayMcNeil2002-4">[4]</a></sup> Improvements followed: on 22 September 1885 Enoch Harris received US patent 326868<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> for his spanner that permitted both the jaw width and the angle of the handles to be adjusted and locked. Other countries, like Denmark, Poland and Israel, refer to it as a "Swedish key" as its invention has been attributed to the Swedish inventor <a href="/wiki/Johan_Petter_Johansson" title="Johan Petter Johansson">Johan Petter Johansson</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> who in 1892 received a <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bergh2014_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bergh2014-7">[7]</a></sup> for an improved design of the adjustable spanner that is still used today. Johansson's spanner was a further development of Clyburn's original "screw spanner".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In some countries (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>) it is called "French key" (in Poland, "Swedish" or "French" key depending on type). In Canada and the United States, the tool is known as a Crescent wrench or an adjustable wrench.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corporation1999_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Corporation1999-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are many forms of adjustable spanners, from the taper locking spanners which needed a hammer to set the movable jaw to the size of the nut, to the modern screw adjusted spanner. Some adjustable spanners automatically adjust to the size of the nut. Simpler models use a serrated edge to lock the movable jaw to size, while more sophisticated versions are digital types that use sheets or feelers to set the size.</p>
<p>The fixed jaw can withstand bending stress far better than can the movable jaw, because the latter is supported only by the flat surfaces on either side of the guide slot, not the full thickness of the tool. The tool is therefore usually angled so that the movable jaw's area of contact is closer to the body of the tool, which means less bending stress.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Monkey_wrench" title="Monkey wrench">Monkey wrenches</a> are another type of adjustable spanner with a long history; the origin of the name is unclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The type of straight adjustable spanner with jaws at right angles to the handle shown here as an "English Key" is mainly called a "King Dick" spanner in the United Kingdom because of a popular British brand of small, handy and reliable adjustable spanner used throughout the 1900s and used in great numbers during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proper_use">Proper use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjustable_spanner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proper use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The movable jaw should be snugly adjusted to the nut or bolt head in order to prevent damage to the fastener's head, or <i>rounding</i>. This type of spanner should not be used on a rounded off nut, as this can overload the movable jaw. Nor should such a wrench be used "end on" in cramped quarters (except perhaps when the nut is barely more than finger-tight), where a <a href="/wiki/Socket_wrench" title="Socket wrench">socket wrench</a> is more appropriate.</p>
<p>Some cheaper brands' jaws move when twisting on tight nuts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Famous_brands">Famous brands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjustable_spanner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Famous brands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United States and Canada, the adjustable spanner (adjustable wrench) is colloquially referred to as a "crescent wrench" due to the widespread <a href="/wiki/Crescent_(brand)" title="Crescent (brand)">Crescent brand</a> of adjustable wrenches. The Crescent brand of hand tools is owned and marketed by <a href="/wiki/Apex_Tool_Group" title="Apex Tool Group">Apex Tool Group, LLC</a>. In some parts of Europe, adjustable spanners are often called a <a href="/wiki/Bahco" title="Bahco">Bahco</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bahco_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-bahco-11">[11]</a></sup> This term refers to the company of the Swedish inventor <a href="/wiki/Johan_Petter_Johansson" title="Johan Petter Johansson">Johan Petter Johansson</a>, which was originally called B.A. (<b>B</b>ernt <b>A</b>ugust) <b>H</b>jort &amp; <b>Co</b>mpany. The Swedes themselves call the key "skiftnyckel" which is translated into adjustable key (shifting key).</p>
