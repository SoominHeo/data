<p><i><b>Unbreakable Kimmy Schmidt</b></i> is an American <a href="/wiki/Web_television" title="Web television">web television</a> <a href="/wiki/Sitcom" title="Sitcom">sitcom</a> created by <a href="/wiki/Tina_Fey" title="Tina Fey">Tina Fey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Carlock" title="Robert Carlock">Robert Carlock</a>, starring <a href="/wiki/Ellie_Kemper" title="Ellie Kemper">Ellie Kemper</a> in the title role, that has streamed on <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> since March 6, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-netflixpickup_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-netflixpickup-1">[1]</a></sup> Originally set for a 13-episode first season on <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> for spring 2015, the show was sold to <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> and given a two-season order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The series follows 29-year-old Kimmy Schmidt (Kemper) as she adjusts to life in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> after her rescue from a doomsday cult in <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a> where she and three other women were held by Reverend Richard Wayne Gary Wayne (<a href="/wiki/Jon_Hamm" title="Jon Hamm">Jon Hamm</a>) for 15 years. Determined to be seen as something other than a victim and armed only with a positive attitude, Kimmy decides to restart her life by moving to New York City, where she quickly befriends her street-wise landlady Lillian Kaushtupper (<a href="/wiki/Carol_Kane" title="Carol Kane">Carol Kane</a>), finds a roommate in struggling actor Titus Andromedon (<a href="/wiki/Tituss_Burgess" title="Tituss Burgess">Tituss Burgess</a>), and gains a job as a nanny for the melancholy and out-of-touch socialite Jacqueline Voorhees (<a href="/wiki/Jane_Krakowski" title="Jane Krakowski">Jane Krakowski</a>). With their help, Kimmy struggles to adapt to an unfamiliar world and jump-start the adult life that had been taken away from her.</p>
<p>Since its premiere, the show has received critical acclaim,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indian_Country_Today_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indian_Country_Today-3">[3]</a></sup> with critic Scott Meslow calling it "the first great sitcom of the streaming era".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Week_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Week-4">[4]</a></sup> As of July 14, 2016, the series has been nominated for eleven <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award" title="Primetime Emmy Award">Primetime Emmy Awards</a>, including two nominations for <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Comedy_Series" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Comedy Series">Outstanding Comedy Series</a>.</p>
<p>On January 17, 2016, the series was renewed for a third season on Netflix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_and_development">Production and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unbreakable_Kimmy_Schmidt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The show was created by <a href="/wiki/Tina_Fey" title="Tina Fey">Tina Fey</a> and Robert Carlock when NBC executives asked them to develop a show for Ellie Kemper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modernprincess_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Modernprincess-6">[6]</a></sup> Fey stated that they found an "innocence" about Kemper's face, but also noted there was a "strength" to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modernprincess_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Modernprincess-6">[6]</a></sup> One idea was for the show to center on Kemper's character waking up from a coma, but this idea was abandoned in favor of the cult-survivor storyline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modernprincess_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Modernprincess-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The show was initially under development for NBC under the title <i>Tooken</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbcpickup_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbcpickup-7">[7]</a></sup> However, this was later changed to the current title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Eventually NBC sold the series to Netflix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-netflixpickup_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-netflixpickup-1">[1]</a></sup> Fey has stated that this was in part due to NBC "not feeling confident about watching comedies". Prior to the network switch, NBC planned to air the series as either a mid-season replacement or as a summer series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modernprincess_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Modernprincess-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting">Casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unbreakable_Kimmy_Schmidt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Casting announcements for the remaining roles were held on March 2014, with <a href="/wiki/Tituss_Burgess" title="Tituss Burgess">Tituss Burgess</a> cast as the role of Kimmy's roommate, Titus Andromedon. Titus is a talented singer who works odd entertainment jobs such as dressing up like a robot in Times Square or as a singing waiter at a theme restaurant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cast_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-cast-9">[9]</a></sup> Also cast was <a href="/wiki/Carol_Kane" title="Carol Kane">Carol Kane</a> as Kimmy and Titus' landlord Lillian Kaushtupper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly afterwards, <a href="/wiki/Sara_Chase" title="Sara Chase">Sara Chase</a> and Lauren Adams joined the series. Chase signed on for the role of Cyndee, Kimmy's closest friend during their years in the cult; Adams was cast in the role of Gretchen, a 10-year member of the cult who believes everything that she is told.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cast2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-cast2-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jane_Krakowski" title="Jane Krakowski">Jane Krakowski</a> was later cast in the role of Jacqueline Voorhees, a wealthy Manhattanite who hires Kimmy as a nanny. <a href="/wiki/Megan_Dodds" title="Megan Dodds">Megan Dodds</a> was originally cast before she was replaced with Krakowski.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cast3_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cast3-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unbreakable_Kimmy_Schmidt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The show's theme song, "Unbreakable", was produced by <a href="/wiki/The_Gregory_Brothers" title="The Gregory Brothers">The Gregory Brothers</a> and written by Jeff Richmond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It is a tribute to The Gregory Brothers’ YouTube show <a href="/wiki/Songify_the_News" title="Songify the News">Songify the News</a> – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auto_Tune" title="Auto Tune">auto-tuned</a> news interviews that became <a href="/wiki/Viral_video" title="Viral video">viral videos</a>. Richmond also wrote "Peeno Noir", a song performed by character Titus Andromedon during season 1, episode 6 of the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">Synopsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unbreakable_Kimmy_Schmidt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Synopsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Unbreakable_Kimmy_Schmidt_episodes#Season_1_.282015.29" title="List of Unbreakable Kimmy Schmidt episodes">first season</a>, Kimmy Schmidt (<a href="/wiki/Ellie_Kemper" title="Ellie Kemper">Ellie Kemper</a>) was in 8th grade when she was kidnapped for fifteen years by Reverend Richard Wayne Gary Wayne (<a href="/wiki/Jon_Hamm" title="Jon Hamm">Jon Hamm</a>). The Reverend held Kimmy and three other women hostage in an underground bunker, and convinced them that doomsday had come, gone, and had left them the sole survivors of humanity. Once the women are rescued, they go on to appear on the <i><a href="/wiki/Today_(U.S._TV_program)" title="Today (U.S. TV program)">Today Show</a></i> in New York City. After the show, Kimmy finds herself not wanting to return to Indiana, but instead to start a new life in New York City. Roaming around the city, Kimmy comes across landlady Lillian Kaushtupper (<a href="/wiki/Carol_Kane" title="Carol Kane">Carol Kane</a>). Kaushtupper offers Kimmy a chance to room with Titus Andromedon (<a href="/wiki/Tituss_Burgess" title="Tituss Burgess">Tituss Burgess</a>) in her downstairs apartment. However, Kimmy has to seek a job in order to get the apartment. Returning a child who has tried to steal candy, Kimmy encounters his mother, Jacqueline Voorhees (<a href="/wiki/Jane_Krakowski" title="Jane Krakowski">Jane Krakowski</a>) a Manhattan trophy wife, who mistakes her for a nanny, and who Kimmy mistakes as someone trapped in a cult. Soon after, Jacqueline hires Kimmy to be a nanny for her 10-year-old son.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> As Season 1 continues, Kimmy falls in love with a Vietnamese man, named Dong (<a href="/wiki/Ki_Hong_Lee" title="Ki Hong Lee">Ki Hong Lee</a>) from her G.E.D. class, goes to court to testify against the Reverend, and discovers how the world has changed in the 15 years she was locked up.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Unbreakable_Kimmy_Schmidt_episodes#Season_2_.282016.29" title="List of Unbreakable Kimmy Schmidt episodes">second season</a>, Kimmy is sick of working for Jacqueline and acquires a job at a year round Christmas store and then as an <a href="/wiki/Uber_(company)" title="Uber (company)">Uber</a> driver. She tries to get over Dong who enters a green card marriage with another G.E.D. student and eventually is deported. As Kimmy tries to move on, so do Titus and Jacqueline, who both find boyfriends. Titus begins dating a construction worker (<a class="external text" href="http://www.imdb.com/name/nm1713738/?ref_=ttfc_fc_cl_t9" rel="nofollow">Mike Carlsen</a>) and Jaqueline begins dating a lawyer (<a href="/wiki/David_Cross" title="David Cross">David Cross</a>). Jacqueline also goes back to her Native American heritage and decides to take down the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Redskins" title="Washington Redskins">Redskins</a>, who her boyfriend's father happens to own. When Season 2 ends, Titus leaves to be a performer on a cruise and Lillian protests the invasion of hipsters on her neighborhood, while Kimmy makes amends with her mother (<a href="/wiki/Lisa_Kudrow" title="Lisa Kudrow">Lisa Kudrow</a>) after advice from her therapist (<a href="/wiki/Tina_Fey" title="Tina Fey">Tina Fey</a>) before receiving a phone call from The Reverend in prison, telling her that they need to get a divorce.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cast_and_characters">Cast and characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unbreakable_Kimmy_Schmidt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cast and characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_cast">Main cast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unbreakable_Kimmy_Schmidt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Main cast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ellie_Kemper" title="Ellie Kemper">Ellie Kemper</a> as <a href="/wiki/Kimmy_Schmidt_(character)" title="Kimmy Schmidt (character)">Kimberly "Kimmy" Cougar Schmidt</a>, the titular character. Armed with only unflagging optimism, a childlike sense of wonder and an 8th-grade education, she attempts to regain the life that was taken from her and navigate her way through the unfamiliar struggles of New York life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cast_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-cast-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tituss_Burgess" title="Tituss Burgess">Tituss Burgess</a> as <a href="/wiki/Titus_Andromedon" title="Titus Andromedon">Titus Andromedon</a> (born Ronald Wilkerson), Kimmy's flamboyantly gay roommate and an aspiring actor and singer. Melodramatic and self-centered, Titus nevertheless cares deeply for, and is very protective of Kimmy. Despite his talent and ego, he is plagued by self-doubt after years of rejection in the business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cast_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-cast-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carol_Kane" title="Carol Kane">Carol Kane</a> as Lillian Kaushtupper, Kimmy and Titus' odd landlady. A proud born-and-bred New Yorker with a long, complex criminal history, she fights tooth and nail against the possible <a href="/wiki/Gentrification" title="Gentrification">gentrification</a> of her neighborhood. Despite her willingness to do anything to make a buck, she has a very big heart and will go out of her way to help her tenants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cast5_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-cast5-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jane_Krakowski" title="Jane Krakowski">Jane Krakowski</a> as <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_White_(character)" title="Jacqueline White (character)">Jacqueline White</a> (formerly Voorhees; née Jackie Lynn White), a wealthy and insecure socialite who hires Kimmy as a nanny. Despite coming across as arrogant, condescending and out of touch, she is very fond of (and heavily reliant on) her new employee, who helps her gain perspective on her unhappy marriage. She is secretly of <a href="/wiki/Lakota_people" title="Lakota people">Lakota</a> <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> descent and is <a href="/wiki/Passing_(racial_identity)" title="Passing (racial identity)">passing for white</a>. As the series progresses, she reconnects with her family and her heritage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cast3_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-cast3-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unbreakable_Kimmy_Schmidt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The show has been widely acclaimed by television critics with significant praise going into the writing &amp; the acting abilities of the cast, with many calling it the best sitcom of the streaming era.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_1">Season 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unbreakable_Kimmy_Schmidt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Season 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first season of <i>Unbreakable Kimmy Schmidt</i> received critical acclaim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indian_Country_Today_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Indian_Country_Today-3">[3]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, the first season has a rating of 95%, based on 55 reviews, with an average rating of 7.5/10. The site's critical consensus reads, "Blessed with originality and a spot-on performance from Ellie Kemper, <i>The Unbreakable Kimmy Schmidt</i> is as odd as it is hilarious."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the first season has a score of 78 out of 100, based on 29 critics, indicating "generally favorable reviews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Scott Meslow of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Week" title="The Week">The Week</a></i> called the series "the first great sitcom of the streaming era", praising its wit, edge, and feminist tone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Week_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Week-4">[4]</a></sup> Brian Moylan of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> noted that it is "the sort of show that could benefit from multiple viewings, because the jokes are so packed in you’re sure to miss something while laughing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/TV_Guide" title="TV Guide">TV Guide</a></i> named it the "best new comedy of 2015".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a></i> reviewer Max Nicholson gave the first season an 8.3 out of 10 'Great' rating, saying "Tina Fey and Robert Carlock's <i>Unbreakable Kimmy Schmidt</i> is another winner in Netflix's original series catalog. Not only is it charming and funny, but it's unabashedly kooky, and Ellie Kemper nails the lead role."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The series' first season was nominated for seven <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award" title="Primetime Emmy Award">Primetime Emmy Awards</a>, including one for <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Comedy_Series" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Comedy Series">Outstanding Comedy Series</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some criticized the use of Native Americans on the show, feeling that the depictions and language used on the show were racist and offensive. Vulture referred to the Native American plot as “offensive”,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> while Buzzfeed wrote that the show had a “major race problem”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Part of Buzzfeed's criticism also addressed that this was one of the few depictions of Native Americans in recent years, with Anne Helen Petersen saying: “the way Native Americans are represented on this show matters. It’s not one representation among a cornucopia of representations; it’s the single mainstream representation in years.” The Daily Beast addressed that when it comes to race, “especially in its portrayal of a key Vietnamese character, the show leaves much to be desired.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the wake of the controversy, Tina Fey retorted: “I feel like we put so much effort into writing and crafting everything, they need to speak for themselves. There’s a real culture of demanding apologies, and I’m opting out of that.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_2">Season 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unbreakable_Kimmy_Schmidt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Season 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The second season of <i>Unbreakable Kimmy Schmidt</i> has received further critical acclaim. On <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, the second season holds a 100% approval rating, based on 24 reviews, with an average rating of 7.6/10. The site's critical consensus reads, "Not letting up in season two, <i>Unbreakable Kimmy Schmidt</i> is still odd in the best of ways, wonderfully building on its unique comedy stylings and brilliantly funny cast."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, the second season has a score of 82 out of 100, based on 16 reviews, indicating "universal acclaim".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The third episode from this season, “Kimmy Goes to a Play!,” involved the use of <a href="/wiki/Examples_of_yellowface" title="Examples of yellowface">yellowface</a> and a group of Asian-Americans critical of yellowface. The group is referred to as "Respectful Asian Portrayals in Entertainment" ("R.A.P.E" for short) and consists of perpetually outraged Asian-American protesters with no humanizing or vindicating traits. Claire Fallon wrote, "It’s bizarre, too, that in the same season in which a major character’s arc is built on her embrace of her Lakota identity and her growing determination to destroy the racist mascot of the Washington football team, “Kimmy Schmidt” portrays Asian-American activists’ concerns with yellowface and other misrepresentation as unworthy of consideration." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>Anna Akana criticized the use of yellowface and the R.A.P.E. acronym in "Kimmy Goes to a Play!" during her 2016 Asians in Entertainment Key Note speech. Alex Abad-Santos wrote, “The odd thing about this episode is that it's another Tina Fey project that paints Asian people, specifically Asian women, as crappy characters. [...] The plot feels like a pointed, ironic response to anyone who has criticized Fey's past projects for being lazy and racist.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
