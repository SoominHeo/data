<p>The <b>Urnfield culture</b> (c. 1300 BCE – 750 BCE) was a late <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> culture of central <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. The name comes from the custom of <a href="/wiki/Cremation" title="Cremation">cremating</a> the dead and placing their ashes in <a href="/wiki/Urn" title="Urn">urns</a> which were then buried in fields. The Urnfield culture followed the <a href="/wiki/Tumulus_culture" title="Tumulus culture">Tumulus culture</a> and was succeeded by the <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chadcorc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-chadcorc-1">[1]</a></sup> Linguistic evidence and continuity with the following Hallstatt culture suggests that the people of this area spoke an early form of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a>, perhaps originally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic" title="Proto-Celtic">proto-Celtic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chadcorc_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-chadcorc-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kruta_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kruta-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chronology">Chronology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chronology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is believed that in some areas, such as in southwestern Germany, it was in existence around 1200 BCE (beginning of Ha A), but the Bronze D <a href="/wiki/Riegsee" title="Riegsee">Riegsee</a>-phase already contains cremations. As the transition from the middle <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> to the Urnfield culture was gradual, there are questions regarding how to define it. The Urnfield culture covers the phases Hallstatt A and B (Ha A and B) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Reinecke" title="Paul Reinecke">Paul Reinecke</a>'s chronological system, not to be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt culture</a> (Ha C and D) of the following <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>. This corresponds to the Phases Montelius III-IV of the Northern Bronze Age. Whether Reinecke's Bronze D is included varies according to author and region. The Urnfield culture is divided into the following sub-phases (based on Müller-Karpe sen.):</p>

<p>The existence of the Ha B3-phase is contested, as the material consists of female burials only. As can be seen by the arbitrary 100-year ranges, the dating of the phases is highly schematic. The phases are based on typological changes, which means that they do not have to be strictly contemporaneous across the whole distribution. All in all, more radiocarbon- and dendro-dates would be highly desirable.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Urnfield culture grew from the preceding <a href="/wiki/Tumulus_culture" title="Tumulus culture">tumulus culture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chadcorc_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-chadcorc-1">[1]</a></sup> The transition is gradual, in the <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> as well as the burial rites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chadcorc_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-chadcorc-1">[1]</a></sup> In some parts of Germany, cremation and inhumation existed simultaneously (facies Wölfersheim). Some graves contain a combination of tumulus-culture pottery and Urnfield <a href="/wiki/Sword" title="Sword">swords</a> (Kressborn, Bodenseekreis) or tumulus culture incised pottery together with early Urnfield types (Mengen). In the North, the Urnfield culture was only adopted in the HaA2 period. 16 pins deposited in a swamp in Ellmoosen (Kr. Bad Aibling, Germany) cover the whole chronological range from Bronze B to the early Urnfield period (Ha A). This demonstrates a considerable ritual continuity. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loire_(river)" title="Loire (river)">Loire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seine" title="Seine">Seine</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne_River" title="Rhône River">Rhône</a>, certain fords contain deposits from the late Neolithic onwards up to the Urnfield period.</p>
<p>The origins of the cremation rite are commonly believed to be in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, where it was widespread since the first half of the second millennium BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gimbutas_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gimbutas-3">[3]</a></sup> The neolithic <a href="/wiki/Cucuteni-Trypillian_culture" title="Cucuteni-Trypillian culture">Cucuteni-Trypillian culture</a> of modern-day northeastern <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> were also practicing cremation rituals as early as approximately 5,500 BCE. Some cremations begin to be found in the <a href="/wiki/Lusatian_culture" title="Lusatian culture">Proto-Lusatian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trzciniec_culture" title="Trzciniec culture">Trzciniec culture</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_local_groups">Distribution and local groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and local groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Urnfield culture was located in an area stretching from western Hungary to eastern France, from the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> to near the North Sea. Local groups, mainly differentiated by pottery, include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Knov%C3%ADz_culture" title="Knovíz culture">Knovíz culture</a> in western and Northern <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, southern Thuringia and North-eastern Bavaria</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Milavce_culture" title="Milavce culture">Milavce culture</a> in southeastern Bohemia</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Unstrut_culture" title="Unstrut culture">Unstrut culture</a> in <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a>, a mixture between Knovíz-culture and the South-German Urnfield culture</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lusatian_culture" title="Lusatian culture">Lusatian culture</a> in northern Bohemia, Lusatia and <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a></li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/South-German_Urnfield_culture" title="South-German Urnfield culture">South-German Urnfield culture</a></p>
<ul>
<li>Northeast-Bavarian Group, divided into a lower <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavarian</a> and an upper <a href="/wiki/Palatinate_(region)" title="Palatinate (region)">Palatinate</a> group</li>
<li>Lower-Main-Swabian group in southern Hesse and Baden-Württemberg, including the <a href="/wiki/Marburg" title="Marburg">Marburger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hanau" title="Hanau">Hanauer</a>, lower <a href="/wiki/Main_(river)" title="Main (river)">Main</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedberg,_Hesse" title="Friedberg, Hesse">Friedberger</a> facies</li>
<li>Rhenish-Swiss group in Rhineland-Palatinate, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, (abbreviated RSFO in French)</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lower-Rhine_Urnfield_culture" title="Lower-Rhine Urnfield culture">Lower-Rhine Urnfield culture</a></p>
<ul>
<li>Lower Hessian Group</li>
<li>North-Netherlands-Westphalian group</li>
<li>Northwest-Group in the Dutch Delta region</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Middle-Danube_Urnfield_culture" title="Middle-Danube Urnfield culture">Middle-Danube Urnfield culture</a></p>
<ul>
<li>Velatice-Baierdorf in <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a></li>
<li>Čaka in western <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a></li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/G%C3%A1va_culture" title="Gáva culture">Gáva culture</a></p>
<p>Sometimes the distribution of artifacts belonging to these groups shows sharp and consistent borders, which might indicate some political structures, like tribes. Metalwork is commonly of a much more widespread distribution than pottery and does not conform to these borders. It may have been produced at specialised workshops catering for the elite of a large area.</p>
<p>Important French cemeteries include Châtenay and <a href="/wiki/Lingolsheim" title="Lingolsheim">Lingolsheim</a> (Alsace). An unusual earthwork was constructed at <a href="/wiki/Goloring" title="Goloring">Goloring</a> near <a href="/wiki/Koblenz" title="Koblenz">Koblenz</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_cultures">Related cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Related cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Urna_cineraria_biconica_da_chiusi_IX.VII_sec._ac._01.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Urna_cineraria_biconica_da_chiusi_IX.VII_sec._ac._01.JPG/220px-Urna_cineraria_biconica_da_chiusi_IX.VII_sec._ac._01.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Urna_cineraria_biconica_da_chiusi_IX.VII_sec._ac._01.JPG/330px-Urna_cineraria_biconica_da_chiusi_IX.VII_sec._ac._01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Urna_cineraria_biconica_da_chiusi_IX.VII_sec._ac._01.JPG/440px-Urna_cineraria_biconica_da_chiusi_IX.VII_sec._ac._01.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Urna_cineraria_biconica_da_chiusi_IX.VII_sec._ac._01.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Villanovan cinerary urn from <a href="/wiki/Chiusi" title="Chiusi">Chiusi</a> 9th-7th century <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">b.c.</span></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The central European <a href="/wiki/Lusatian_culture" title="Lusatian culture">Lusatian culture</a> forms part of the Urnfield tradition, but continues into the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> without a notable break.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Piliny_culture" title="Piliny culture">Piliny culture</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> grew from the <a href="/wiki/Tumulus_culture" title="Tumulus culture">Tumulus culture</a>, but used urn burials as well. The pottery shows strong links to the <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A1va_culture" title="Gáva culture">Gáva culture</a>, but in the later phases, a strong influence of the Lusatian culture is found. In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> the late Bronze Age <a href="/wiki/Canegrate_culture" title="Canegrate culture">Canegrate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proto-Villanovan_culture" title="Proto-Villanovan culture">Proto-Villanovan</a> cultures and the early Iron Age <a href="/wiki/Villanovan_culture" title="Villanovan culture">Villanovan culture</a> show similarities with the urnfields of central Europe. Urnfields are found in the French <a href="/wiki/Languedoc" title="Languedoc">Languedoc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a> from the 9th to 8th centuries. The change in burial custom was most probably influenced by developments further east.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Golasecca_culture" title="Golasecca culture">Golasecca culture</a> in northern Italy developed with continuity from the Canegrate culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kruta_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kruta-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stifter_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stifter-4">[4]</a></sup> Canegrate represented a completely new cultural dynamic to the area expressed in pottery and bronzework, making it a typical western example of the Urnfield culture, in particular the Rhine-Switzerland-Eastern France (RSFO) Urnfield culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kruta_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kruta-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stifter_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stifter-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Lepontic_language" title="Lepontic language">Lepontic Celtic language</a> inscriptions of the area show the language of the Golasecca culture was clearly <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> making it probable that the 13th-century <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">b.c.</span> language of at least the RSEF area of the western Urnfields was also Celtic or a precursor to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kruta_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kruta-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stifter_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stifter-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Placename evidence has also been used to point to an association of the Urnfield materials with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic" title="Proto-Celtic">Proto-Celtic</a> language group in central Europe, and it has been argued that it was the ancestral culture of the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChadCorc_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChadCorc-5">[5]</a></sup> The Urnfield layers of the <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt culture</a>, Ha A and Ha B, are succeeded by the Iron Age "Hallstatt period" proper (Ha C and Ha D, 8th-6th centuries <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">b.c.</span>), associated with the early Celts; Ha D is in turn succeeded by the <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La Tène culture">La Tène culture</a>, the archaeological culture associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_Celts" title="Continental Celts">Continental Celts</a> of antiquity.</p>
<p>The influence of the Urnfield culture spread widely and found its way to the northeastern <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian</a> coast, where the nearby <a href="/wiki/Celtiberians" title="Celtiberians">Celtiberians</a> of the interior adapted it for use in their cemeteries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cremin_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cremin-6">[6]</a></sup> Evidence for east-to-west early Urnfield (Bronze D-Hallstatt A) elite contacts such as rilled-ware, swords and crested helmets has been found in the southwest of the Iberian peninsula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koch2013_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-koch2013-7">[7]</a></sup> The appearance of such elite status markers provides the simplest explanation for the spread of Celtic languages in this area from prestigious, proto-Celtic, early-Urnfield metalworkers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koch2013_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-koch2013-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Migrations">Migrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Migrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The numerous hoards of the Urnfield culture and the existence of fortified settlements (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hill_fort" title="Hill fort">hill forts</a>) were taken as evidence for widespread warfare and upheaval by some scholars. Written sources describe several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_collapse" title="Bronze Age collapse">collapses and upheavals in the Eastern Mediterranean, Anatolia and the Levant around the time</a> of the Urnfield origins:</p>
<ul>
<li>End of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mycenaean_period" title="Mycenaean period">Mycenean</a> culture with a conventional date of ca. 1200 BCE</li>
<li>Destruction of <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Troy</a> VI ca. 1200 BCE</li>
<li>Battles of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramses_III" title="Ramses III">Ramses III</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Sea_Peoples" title="Sea Peoples">Sea Peoples</a>, 1195–1190 BCE</li>
<li>End of the <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittite</a> empire 1180 BCE</li>
<li>Settlement of the <a href="/wiki/Philistines" title="Philistines">Philistines</a> in Palestine ca. 1170 BCE</li>
</ul>
<p>Some scholars, among them Wolfgang Kimmig and P. Bosch-Gimpera have postulated a Europe-wide wave of migrations. The so-called <a href="/wiki/Dorian_invasion" title="Dorian invasion">Dorian invasion</a> of Greece was placed in this context as well (although more recent evidence suggests that the Dorians moved in 1100 BCE into a post Mycenaean vacuum, rather than precipitating the collapse). Better methods of dating have shown that these events are not as closely connected as once thought.</p>
<p>More recently <a href="/wiki/Robert_Drews" title="Robert Drews">Robert Drews</a>, after having reviewed and dismissed the migration hypothesis, has suggested that the observed cultural associations may be in fact partly explained as the result of a new kind of warfare based upon the slashing <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_sword#Europe" title="Bronze Age sword">Naue II sword</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and with bands of infantry replacing chariots in warfare. Drews suggests that the political instability that this brought to centralised states based upon <a href="/wiki/Maryannu" title="Maryannu">maryannu</a> chariotry caused the breakdown of these polities.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Settlements">Settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The number of settlements increased sharply in comparison with the preceding tumulus culture. Unfortunately, few have been comprehensively excavated. Fortified settlements, often on hilltops or in river-bends, are typical for the urnfield culture. They are heavily fortified with dry-stone or wooden ramparts. Excavations of open settlements are rare, but they show that large 3-4 aisled houses built with wooden posts and wall of <a href="/wiki/Wattle_and_daub" title="Wattle and daub">wattle and daub</a> were common. Pit dwellings are known as well; they might have served as cellars.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_settlements">Open settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Open settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The houses were one or two-aisled. Some were quite small, 4.5 m × 5 m at the <a href="/wiki/Runder_Berg" title="Runder Berg">Runder Berg</a> (Urach, Germany), 5-8m long in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=K%C3%BCnzig&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Künzig (page does not exist)">Künzig</a> (Bavaria, Germany), others up to 20 m long. They were built with wooden posts and walls of wattle and daub. At the Velatice-settlement of <a href="/wiki/Lov%C4%8Di%C4%8Dky" title="Lovčičky">Lovčičky</a> (<a href="/wiki/South_Moravian_Region" title="South Moravian Region">Moravia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>) 44 houses have been excavated. Large bell shaped <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Storage_pit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Storage pit (page does not exist)">storage pits</a> are known from the Knovíz-culture. The settlement of <a href="/wiki/Radonice_(Chomutov_District)" title="Radonice (Chomutov District)">Radonice</a> (Louny) contained over 100 pits. They were most probably used to store grain and demonstrate a considerable surplus-production.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pile_dwellings">Pile dwellings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pile dwellings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Burgstallkogel_Sulm_valley.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="751" data-file-width="1024" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Burgstallkogel_Sulm_valley.jpg/220px-Burgstallkogel_Sulm_valley.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Burgstallkogel_Sulm_valley.jpg/330px-Burgstallkogel_Sulm_valley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Burgstallkogel_Sulm_valley.jpg/440px-Burgstallkogel_Sulm_valley.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Burgstallkogel_Sulm_valley.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Site of a hill fort, Urnfield Culture, <a href="/wiki/Burgstallkogel_(Sulm_valley)" title="Burgstallkogel (Sulm valley)">Burgstallkogel (Sulm valley)</a></div>

</div>
<p>On lakes of southern Germany and Switzerland, numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pile_dwellings" title="Pile dwellings">pile dwellings</a> were constructed. They consist either of simple one-room houses made of wattle and daub, or <a href="/wiki/Log_cabin" title="Log cabin">log-built</a>. The settlement at <a href="/wiki/Zug" title="Zug">Zug</a>, Switzerland, was destroyed by fire and gives important insights into the material culture and the settlement organisation of this period. It has yielded a number of <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">dendro-dates</a> as well.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fortified_settlements">Fortified settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fortified settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fortified hilltop settlements become common in the Urnfield period. Often a steep spur was used, where only part of the circumference had to be fortified. Depending on the locally available materials, dry-stone walls, gridded timbers filled with stones or soil or plank and palisade type <i><a href="/wiki/Pfostenschlitzmauer" title="Pfostenschlitzmauer">pfostenschlitzmauer</a></i> fortifications were used. Other fortified settlements used rivers-bends and swampy areas.</p>
<p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hill_fort" title="Hill fort">hill fort</a> of Hořovice near Beroun (CR), 50 ha were surrounded by a stone wall. Most settlements are much smaller. Metal working is concentrated in the fortified settlements. On the Runder Berg near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urach" title="Urach">Urach</a>, Germany, 25 stone <a href="/wiki/Molding_(process)" title="Molding (process)">moulds</a> have been found.</p>
<p>Hillforts are interpreted as central places. Some scholars see the emergence of hill forts as a sign of increased warfare. Most hillforts were abandoned at the end of the Bronze Age.</p>
<p>As far as we know, there are no special dwellings for an upper class, but few settlements have been excavated to any extent. In the <a href="/wiki/Franche-Comt%C3%A9" title="Franche-Comté">Franche-Comté</a>, caves were used as dwellings, perhaps in times of trouble.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pottery">Pottery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pottery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The pottery is normally well made, with a smooth surface and a normally sharply carinated profile. Some forms are thought to imitate metal prototypes. Biconical pots with cylindrical necks are especially characteristic. There is some incised decoration, but a large part of the surface was normally left plain. Fluted decoration is common. In the Swiss pile dwellings, the incised decoration was sometimes inlaid with <a href="/wiki/Tin_foil" title="Tin foil">tin foil</a>. <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">Pottery kilns</a> were already known (Elchinger Kreuz, Bavaria), as is indicated by the homogeneous surface of the vessels as well. Other vessels include cups of beaten sheet-<a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> with riveted handles (type Jenišovice) and large <a href="/wiki/Cauldron" title="Cauldron">cauldrons</a> with cross attachments. Wooden vessels have only been preserved in waterlogged contexts, for example from Auvernier (Neuchâtel), but may have been quite widespread.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tools_and_weapons">Tools and weapons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tools and weapons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Frankleben_hoard" title="Frankleben hoard">Frankleben hoard</a></div>
<p>The early urnfield period (1300 BCE) was a time when the warriors of central Europe could be heavily armored with body armor, helmets and shields all made of bronze, most likely borrowing the idea from <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greece" title="Mycenaean Greece">Mycenaean</a> <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The leaf-shaped Urnfield <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_sword" title="Bronze Age sword">sword</a> could be used for slashing, in contrast to the stabbing-swords of the preceding tumulus culture. It commonly possessed a <a href="/wiki/Ricasso" title="Ricasso">ricasso</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Hilt" title="Hilt">hilt</a> was normally made from bronze as well. It was cast separately and consisted of a different alloy. These solid hilted swords were known since Bronze D (Rixheim swords). Other swords have tanged blades and probably had a wood, bone, or antler hilt. Flange-hilted swords had organic inlays in the hilt. Swords include Auvernier, Kressborn-Hemigkofen, Erbenheim, Möhringen, Weltenburg, Hemigkofen and Tachlovice-types.</p>
<p>Protective gear like <a href="/wiki/Shield" title="Shield">shields</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuirass" title="Cuirass">cuirasses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greave" title="Greave">greaves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helmet" title="Helmet">helmets</a> are extremely rare and almost never found in burials. The best-known example of a bronze shield comes from <a href="/wiki/Plze%C5%88" title="Plzeň">Plzeň</a> in Bohemia and has a riveted handhold. Comparable pieces have been found in Germany, Western Poland, Denmark, Great Britain and Ireland. They are supposed to have been made in upper Italy or the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Alps" title="Eastern Alps">Eastern Alps</a> and imitate wooden shields. Irish bogs have yielded examples of leather shields (Clonbrinn, Co. Wexford). Bronze cuirasses are known since Bronze D (<a href="/wiki/%C4%8Caka" title="Čaka">Čaka</a>, grave II, Slovakia). Complete bronze cuirasses have been found in Saint Germain du Plain, nine examples, one inside the other, in Marmesse, Haute Marne (France), fragments in Albstadt-Pfeffingen (Germany). Bronze dishes (phalerae) may have been sewn on a leather armour. Greaves of richly decorated sheet-bronze are known from Kloštar Ivanić (Croatia) and the Paulus cave near <a href="/wiki/Beuron" title="Beuron">Beuron</a> (Germany).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chariots">Chariots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Chariots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DBP_-_Bronzekultwagen_-_30_Pfennig_-_1976.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="670" data-file-width="1070" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/DBP_-_Bronzekultwagen_-_30_Pfennig_-_1976.jpg/220px-DBP_-_Bronzekultwagen_-_30_Pfennig_-_1976.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/DBP_-_Bronzekultwagen_-_30_Pfennig_-_1976.jpg/330px-DBP_-_Bronzekultwagen_-_30_Pfennig_-_1976.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/DBP_-_Bronzekultwagen_-_30_Pfennig_-_1976.jpg/440px-DBP_-_Bronzekultwagen_-_30_Pfennig_-_1976.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DBP_-_Bronzekultwagen_-_30_Pfennig_-_1976.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Miniature bronze wagon.</div>

<p>About a dozen <a href="/wiki/Wagon" title="Wagon">wagon</a>-burials of four wheeled wagons with bronze fittings are known from the early Urnfield period. They include Hart an der Altz (Kr. Altötting), Mengen (Kr. Sigmaringen), Poing (Kr. Ebersberg), Königsbronn (Kr. Heidenheim) from Germany and St. Sulpice (<a href="/wiki/Vaud" title="Vaud">Vaud</a>), Switzerland. In Alz, the chariot had been placed on the pyre, pieces of bone are attached to the partially melted metal of the axles. Bronze (one-part) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horse_bit" title="Horse bit">bits</a> appear at the same time. Two-part horse bits are only known from late Urnfield contexts and may be due to eastern influence. Wood- and bronze spoked wheels are known from <a href="/wiki/Stade" title="Stade">Stade</a> (Germany), a wooden spoked wheel from Mercurago, Italy. Wooden dish-wheels have been excavated at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Corcelettes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Corcelettes (page does not exist)">Corcelettes</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grandson%E2%80%93Corcelettes_Les_Violes" title="fr:Grandson–Corcelettes Les Violes">fr</a>)</span>, Switzerland and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wasserburg_Buchau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wasserburg Buchau (page does not exist)">Wasserburg Buchau</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wasserburg_Buchau" title="de:Wasserburg Buchau">de</a>)</span>, Germany (diameter 80 cm).</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Milav%C4%8De" title="Milavče">Milavče</a> near <a href="/wiki/Doma%C5%BElice" title="Domažlice">Domažlice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, a four-wheeled miniature bronze wagon bearing a large <a href="/wiki/Cauldron" title="Cauldron">cauldron</a> (diameter 30 cm) contained a cremation. This exceptionally rich burial was covered by a <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">barrow</a>. The wagon from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Acholshausen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Acholshausen (page does not exist)">Acholshausen</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acholshausen" title="de:Acholshausen">de</a>)</span> (Bavaria) comes from a male burial.</p>
<p>Such wagons are known from the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_Bronze_Age" title="Nordic Bronze Age">Nordic Bronze Age</a> as well. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Skallerup_wagon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Skallerup wagon (page does not exist)">Skallerup wagon</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://da.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skallerupvognen" title="da:Skallerupvognen">da</a>)</span>, Denmark, contained a cremation as well. At Pekatel (Kr. Schwerin) in <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg" title="Mecklenburg">Mecklenburg</a> a cauldron-wagon and other rich grave goods accompanied an inhumation under a barrow (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montelius" title="Montelius">Montelius</a> III/IV). Another example comes from <a href="/wiki/Ystad" title="Ystad">Ystad</a> in Sweden. South-eastern European examples include Kanya in Hungary and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Or%C4%83%C5%9Ftie" title="Orăştie">Orăştie</a> in <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>. Clay miniature wagons, sometimes with waterfowl were known there since the middle Bronze Age (Dupljaja, Vojvodina, Serbia).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lusatian_culture" title="Lusatian culture">Lusatian</a> chariot from Burg (<a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>, Germany) has three <a href="/wiki/Wheel" title="Wheel">wheels</a> on a single <a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axle</a>, on which waterfowl perch. The grave of <a href="/wiki/Gammertingen" title="Gammertingen">Gammertingen</a> (Kr. Sigmaringen, Germany) contained two socketed horned applications that probably belonged to a miniature wagon comparable to the Burg example, together with six miniature spoked wheels.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hoards">Hoards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hoards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Radanhaenger-edited.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="416" data-file-width="629" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Radanhaenger-edited.jpg/220px-Radanhaenger-edited.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Radanhaenger-edited.jpg/330px-Radanhaenger-edited.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Radanhaenger-edited.jpg/440px-Radanhaenger-edited.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Radanhaenger-edited.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bronze wheel pendants, <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a>, c. 1550 – 1000 BCE.</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Hoard" title="Hoard">Hoards</a> are very common in the Urnfield culture. The custom is abandoned at the end of the Bronze Age. They were often deposited in rivers and wet places like swamps. As these spots were often quite inaccessible, they most probably represent gifts to the gods. Other hoards contain either broken or miscast objects that were probably intended for reuse by bronze smiths. As Late Urnfield hoards often contain the same range of objects as earlier graves, some scholars interpret hoarding as a way to supply personal equipment for the hereafter. In the river Trieux, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B4tes_du_Nord" title="Côtes du Nord">Côtes du Nord</a>, complete swords were found together with numerous antlers of red deer that may have had a religious significance as well.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron">Iron</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Iron">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:UKRazor.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="232" data-file-width="150" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/ce/UKRazor.jpg" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:UKRazor.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Crescent shaped Urnfield razor<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An iron ring from Vorwohlde (Kr. Grafschaft Diepholz, Germany) dating to the 15th century BCE is the earliest evidence of iron in Central Europe. During the late Bronze Age, Iron was used to decorate the hilts of swords (Schwäbisch-Hall-Gailenkirchen, Unterkrumbach, Kr. Hersbruck) and knives (Dotternhausen, <a href="/wiki/Plettenberg_(mountain)" title="Plettenberg (mountain)">Plettenberg</a>, Germany) and pins. The use of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> for weapons and domestic items in Europe only started in the following <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt culture</a>. The widespread use of iron for tools only occurred in the late <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La Tène culture">La Tène culture</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cattle, pigs, sheep and goats were kept, as well as horses and dogs, and maybe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geese" title="Geese">geese</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> were rather small, with a height of 1.20 m at the <a href="/wiki/Withers" title="Withers">withers</a>. Horses were not much bigger with a mean of 1.25 m.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_clearance" title="Forest clearance">Forest clearance</a> was intensive in the Urnfield period. Probably open meadows were created for the first time, as shown by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pollen_analysis" title="Pollen analysis">pollen analysis</a>. This led to increased <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> and sediment-load of the rivers.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">Wheat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a> were cultivated, together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulses" title="Pulses">pulses</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horse_bean" title="Horse bean">horse bean</a>. <a href="/wiki/Poppy_seed" title="Poppy seed">Poppy seeds</a> were used for oil or as a <a href="/wiki/Recreational_drug_use" title="Recreational drug use">drug</a>. <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">Millet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oat" title="Oat">oats</a> were cultivated for the first time in Hungary and Bohemia, <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a> was already cultivated, further west it was only a noxious weed. <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">Flax</a> seems to have been of reduced importance, maybe because mainly <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a> was used for clothes. Hazel nuts, apples, pears, sloes and acorns were collected. Some rich graves contain bronze <a href="/wiki/Sieve" title="Sieve">sieves</a> that have been interpreted as <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>-sieves (Hart an der Alz). This beverage would have been imported from the South, but supporting evidence is lacking. In the <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lacustrine</a> settlement of Zug, remains of a broth made of <a href="/wiki/Spelt" title="Spelt">spelt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">millet</a> have been found. In the lower-Rhine urnfields, leavened <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a> was often placed on the pyre and burnt fragments have thus been preserved.</p>
<p>Wool was spun (finds of <a href="/wiki/Spindle_(textiles)" title="Spindle (textiles)">spindle whorls</a> are common) and woven on the <a href="/wiki/Warp-weighted_loom" title="Warp-weighted loom">warp-weighted loom</a>; bronze needles (<i>Unteruhldingen</i>) were used for <a href="/wiki/Sewing" title="Sewing">sewing</a>.</p>
<p>There is some suggestion that the Urnfield culture is associated with a wetter climatic period than the earlier Tumulus cultures. This may be associated with the diversion of the mid-latitude winter storms north of the Pyrenees and the Alps, possibly associated with drier conditions in the Mediterranean basin.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Graves">Graves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Graves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the tumulus-period, multiple inhumations under barrows were common, at least for the upper levels of society. In the Urnfield period, inhumation and burial in single <a href="/wiki/Flat_grave" title="Flat grave">flat graves</a> prevails, though some barrows exist.</p>
<p>In the earliest phases of the Urnfield period, man-shaped graves were dug, sometimes provided with a stone lined floor, in which the cremated remains of the deceased were spread. Only later, burial in urns became prevalent. Some scholars speculate that this may have marked a fundamental shift in people's beliefs or myths about life and the afterlife.</p>
<p>The size of the urnfields is variable. In Bavaria, they can contain hundreds of burials, while the largest cemetery in <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dautmergen" title="Dautmergen">Dautmergen</a> has only 30 graves. The dead were placed on <a href="/wiki/Pyre" title="Pyre">pyres</a>, covered in their personal jewellery, which often shows traces of the fire and sometimes food-offerings. The cremated bone-remains are much larger than in the Roman period, which indicates that less wood was used. Often, the bones have been incompletely collected. Most urnfields are abandoned with the end of the Bronze Age, only the Lower Rhine urnfields continue in use in the early Iron Age (Ha C, sometimes even D).</p>
<p>The cremated bones could be placed in simple pits. Sometimes the dense concentration of the bones indicates a container of organic material, sometimes the bones were simply shattered.</p>
<p>If the bones were placed in urns, these were often covered by a shallow bowl or a stone. In a special type of burial (bell-graves) the urns are completely covered by an inverted larger vessel. As graves rarely overlap, they may have been marked by wooden posts or stones. Stone-pacing graves are typical of the Unstrut group.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grave_gifts">Grave gifts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Grave gifts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The urn containing the cremated bones is often accompanied by other, smaller ceramic vessels, like bowls and cups. They may have contained food. The urn is often placed in the centre of the assemblage. Often, these vessels have not been placed on the pyre. Metal grave gifts include razors, weapons that often have been deliberately destroyed (bent or broken), bracelets, pendants and pins. Metal grave gifts become rarer towards the end of the Urnfield culture, while the number of hoards increase. Burnt animal bones are often found, they may have been placed on the pyre as food. The marten bones in the grave of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seddin" title="Seddin">Seddin</a> may have belonged to a garment (pelt). <a href="/wiki/Amber" title="Amber">Amber</a> or <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> <a href="/wiki/Bead" title="Bead">beads</a> (Pfahlbautönnchen) are luxury items.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upper-class_graves">Upper-class graves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Upper-class graves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upper-class burials were placed in wooden chambers, rarely stone <a href="/wiki/Cist" title="Cist">cists</a> or chambers with a stone-paved floor and covered with a <a href="/wiki/Tumulus" title="Tumulus">barrow</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cairn" title="Cairn">cairn</a>. The graves contain especially finely made pottery, animal bones, usually pork, sometimes gold rings or sheets, in exceptional cases miniature wagons. Some of these rich burials contain the remains of more than one person. In this case, women and children are normally seen as sacrifices. Until more is known about the status distribution and the social structure of the late Bronze Age, this interpretation should be viewed with caution, however. Towards the end of the Urnfield period, some bodies were burnt in situ and then covered by a barrow, reminiscent of the burial of <a href="/wiki/Patroclus" title="Patroclus">Patroclus</a> as described by <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> and the burial of <a href="/wiki/Beowulf" title="Beowulf">Beowulf</a> (with the additional <a href="/wiki/Ship_burial" title="Ship burial">ship burial</a> element). In the early Iron Age, inhumation became the rule again.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cult">Cult</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urnfield_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Cult">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kyffh%C3%A4user" title="Kyffhäuser">Kyffhäuser</a> caves in <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a> contain headless skeletons and split human and animal bones that have been interpreted as sacrifices. Other deposits include grain, knotted vegetable fibres and hair and bronze objects (axes, pendants and pins). The <a href="/wiki/Ith" title="Ith">Ith</a>-caves (<a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a>) have yielded comparative material.</p>
<p>In the Knovíz-culture, human bones with cut-marks and traces of burning have been found in settlement pits. They have been interpreted as evidence for <a href="/wiki/Cannibalism" title="Cannibalism">cannibalism</a>. As these bones form a large part of the burials known this may have been a quite regular treatment including the ritual manipulation and dismemberment of human corpses. Moon-shaped clay <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_dog" title="Fire dog">fire dogs</a> are thought to have a religious significance, as well as crescent shaped razors.</p>
<p>An obsession with waterbirds is indicated by numerous pictures and three-dimensional representations. Combined with the hoards deposited in rivers and swamps, it indicates religious beliefs connected with water. This has led some scholars to believe in serious droughts during the late Bronze Age. Sometimes the water-birds are combined with circles, the so-called sun-barque-motif.</p>
