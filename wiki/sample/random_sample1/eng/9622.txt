<p><b>Zebras</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">ZEB</span></span></b>-rə</i></a></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i><b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">ZEE</span></span></b>-brə</i></a></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> are several species of <a href="/wiki/Fauna_of_Africa" title="Fauna of Africa">African</a> <a href="/wiki/Equidae" title="Equidae">equids</a> (horse family) united by their distinctive black and white striped coats. Their stripes come in different patterns, unique to each individual. They are generally social animals that live in small <a href="/wiki/Harem_(zoology)" title="Harem (zoology)">harems</a> to large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herds" title="Herds">herds</a>. Unlike their closest relatives, <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Donkey" title="Donkey">donkeys</a>, zebras have never been truly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated" title="Domesticated">domesticated</a>.</p>
<p>There are three species of zebras: the <a href="/wiki/Plains_zebra" title="Plains zebra">plains zebra</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%A9vy%27s_zebra" title="Grévy's zebra">Grévy's zebra</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_zebra" title="Mountain zebra">mountain zebra</a>. The plains zebra and the mountain zebra belong to the subgenus <i>Hippotigris</i>, but Grévy's zebra is the sole species of subgenus <i>Dolichohippus</i>. The latter resembles an <a href="/wiki/Asinus" title="Asinus">ass</a>, to which it is closely related, while the former two are more horse-like. All three belong to the genus <i>Equus</i>, along with other living equids.</p>
<p>The unique stripes of zebras make them one of the animals most familiar to people. They occur in a variety of habitats, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grasslands" title="Grasslands">grasslands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savannas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodlands</a>, thorny <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scrubland" title="Scrubland">scrublands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a>, and coastal <a href="/wiki/Hill" title="Hill">hills</a>. However, various anthropogenic factors have had a severe impact on zebra populations, in particular hunting for skins and habitat destruction. Grévy's zebra and the mountain zebra are endangered. While plains zebras are much more plentiful, one subspecies, the <a href="/wiki/Quagga" title="Quagga">quagga</a>, became extinct in the late 19th century – though there is currently a plan, called the <a href="/wiki/Quagga_Project" title="Quagga Project">Quagga Project</a>, that aims to breed zebras that are phenotypically similar to the quagga in a process called <a href="/wiki/Breeding_back" title="Breeding back">breeding back</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<p>The name "zebra" in English dates back to c. 1600, from Italian <i>zebra</i>, perhaps from <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alves2014_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alves2014-2">[2]</a></sup> which in turn is said to be <a href="/wiki/Kongo_language" title="Kongo language">Congolese</a> (as stated in the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i>). The <i>Encarta Dictionary</i> says its ultimate origin is uncertain, but perhaps it may come from Latin <i>equiferus</i> meaning "wild horse"; from <i>equus</i> ("horse") and <i>ferus</i> ("wild, untamed"). The word was traditionally pronounced with a long initial vowel, but over the course of the 20th century, the pronunciation with the short initial vowel became the usual one in the UK and Commonwealth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The pronunciation with a long initial vowel remains standard in the United States.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_evolution">Taxonomy and evolution</span></h2>

<p>Zebras evolved among the Old World horses within the last 4 million years. It has been suggested that zebras are <a href="/wiki/Polyphyly" title="Polyphyly">polyphyletic</a> and that striped equids evolved more than once. Extensive stripes are posited to have been of little use to equids that live in low densities in deserts (like asses and some horses) or ones that live in colder climates with shaggy coats and annual shading (like some horses).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoofed_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoofed-4">[4]</a></sup> However, molecular evidence supports zebras as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophyletic" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a> lineage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vilstrup_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vilstrup-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forst.C3.A9n_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forst.C3.A9n-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<p>There are three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" title="Extant taxon">extant</a> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>. Collectively, two of the species have eight <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> (seven extant). Zebra populations are diverse, and the relationships between, and the <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomic</a> status of, several of the subspecies are not well known.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Genus: <i><a href="/wiki/Equus_(genus)" title="Equus (genus)">Equus</a></i></b>
<ul>
<li><b>Subgenus: <i>Hippotigris</i></b>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Plains_zebra" title="Plains zebra">Plains zebra</a>, <i>Equus quagga</i>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Plains_zebra" title="Plains zebra">plains zebra</a> (<i>Equus quagga</i>, formerly <i>Equus burchelli</i>) is the most common, and has or had about six subspecies distributed across much of southern and eastern Africa. It, or particular subspecies of it, have also been known as the common zebra, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dauw" title="Dauw">dauw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burchell%27s_zebra" title="Burchell's zebra">Burchell's zebra</a> (actually the subspecies <i>Equus quagga burchellii</i>), Chapman's zebra, <a href="/wiki/Johan_August_Wahlberg" title="Johan August Wahlberg">Wahlberg</a>'s zebra, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Selous" title="Frederick Selous">Selous</a>' zebra, <a href="/wiki/Grant%27s_zebra" title="Grant's zebra">Grant's zebra</a>, Boehm's zebra and the <a href="/wiki/Quagga" title="Quagga">quagga</a> (another <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinct</a> subspecies, <i>Equus quagga quagga</i>).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mountain_zebra" title="Mountain zebra">mountain zebra</a> (<i>Equus zebra</i>) of southwest Africa tends to have a sleek coat with a white belly and narrower stripes than the plains zebra. It has two subspecies and is classified as <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%A9vy%27s_zebra" title="Grévy's zebra">Grévy's zebra</a> (<i>Equus grevyi</i>) is the largest type, with a long, narrow head, making it appear rather <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">mule</a>-like. It is an inhabitant of the semi-arid <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>. Grévy's zebra is the rarest species, and is classified as <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a>.</p>
<p>Although zebra species may have overlapping ranges, they do not interbreed. In captivity, plains zebras have been crossed with mountain zebras. The hybrid foals lacked a <a href="/wiki/Dewlap" title="Dewlap">dewlap</a> and resembled the plains zebra apart from their larger ears and their hindquarters pattern. Attempts to breed a Grévy's zebra stallion to mountain zebra mares resulted in a high rate of miscarriage. In captivity, crosses between zebras and other (non-zebra) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equine" title="Equine">equines</a> have produced several distinct <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Zebroid" title="Zebroid">zebroid</a>, zeedonk, zony, and zorse. In certain regions of Kenya, plains zebras and Grévy's zebra coexist, and fertile hybrids occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_attributes">Physical attributes</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size_and_weight">Size and weight</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grantszebra-skull-tobuzoo-oct16-2012.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2203" data-file-width="2706" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Grantszebra-skull-tobuzoo-oct16-2012.jpg/220px-Grantszebra-skull-tobuzoo-oct16-2012.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Grantszebra-skull-tobuzoo-oct16-2012.jpg/330px-Grantszebra-skull-tobuzoo-oct16-2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Grantszebra-skull-tobuzoo-oct16-2012.jpg/440px-Grantszebra-skull-tobuzoo-oct16-2012.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grantszebra-skull-tobuzoo-oct16-2012.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The skull of a <a href="/wiki/Grant%27s_zebra" title="Grant's zebra">Grant's zebra</a>.</div>

<p>The common plains zebra is about 1.2–1.3 m (47–51 in) at the shoulder with a body ranging from 2–2.6 m (6.6–8.5 ft) long with a 0.5 m (20 in) tail. It can weigh up to 350 kg (770 lb), males being slightly bigger than females. <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%A9vy%27s_zebra" title="Grévy's zebra">Grévy's zebra</a> is considerably larger, while the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_zebra" title="Mountain zebra">mountain zebra</a> is somewhat smaller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stripes">Stripes</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zebra_running_Ngorongoro.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1328" data-file-width="1992" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Zebra_running_Ngorongoro.jpg/220px-Zebra_running_Ngorongoro.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Zebra_running_Ngorongoro.jpg/330px-Zebra_running_Ngorongoro.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Zebra_running_Ngorongoro.jpg/440px-Zebra_running_Ngorongoro.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zebra_running_Ngorongoro.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The black and white stripes may have one or several functions.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It was previously believed that zebras were white animals with black stripes, since some zebras have white underbellies. Embryological evidence, however, shows that the animal's background color is black and the white stripes and bellies are additions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoofed_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hoofed-4">[4]</a></sup> It is likely that the stripes are caused by a combination of factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCGill_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCGill-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post_2015_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Post_2015-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The stripes are typically vertical on the head, neck, forequarters, and main body, with horizontal stripes at the rear and on the legs of the animal.</p>
<p>A wide variety of hypotheses have been proposed to account for the evolution of the striking stripes of zebras. The more traditional of these (1 and 2, below) relate to <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflage</a>.</p>
<p>1. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_direction" title="Vertical direction">vertical</a> striping may help the zebra hide in the grass by <a href="/wiki/Disruptive_coloration" title="Disruptive coloration">disrupting its outline</a>. In addition, even at moderate distances, the striking striping merges to an apparent grey. However, the camouflage has been contested with arguments that most of a zebra's predators (such as <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hyena" title="Hyena">hyenas</a>) cannot see well at a distance, and are more likely to have smelled or heard a zebra before seeing it from a distance, especially at night.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>2. The stripes may help to confuse predators by <a href="/wiki/Camouflage#Motion_dazzle" title="Camouflage">motion dazzle</a>—a group of zebras standing or moving close together may appear as one large mass of flickering stripes, making it more difficult for the lion to pick out a target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It has been suggested that when moving, the stripes may confuse observers, such as mammalian predators and biting insects, by two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_illusion" title="Visual illusion">visual illusions</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Wagon-wheel_effect" title="Wagon-wheel effect">wagon-wheel effect</a>, where the perceived motion is inverted, and the <a href="/wiki/Barberpole_illusion" title="Barberpole illusion">barberpole illusion</a>, where the perceived motion is in a wrong direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>3. The stripes may serve as visual cues and identification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoofed_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hoofed-4">[4]</a></sup> Although the striping pattern is unique to each individual, it is not known whether zebras can recognize one another by their stripes.</p>
<p>4. Experiments by different researchers indicate that the stripes are effective in attracting fewer flies, including blood-sucking <a href="/wiki/Tsetse_fly" title="Tsetse fly">tsetse flies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Horse-fly" title="Horse-fly">tabanid horseflies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCGill_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBCGill-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> A 2012 experiment in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> showed that zebra-striped models were nearly minimally attractive to tabanid horseflies. These flies are attracted to linearly polarized light, and the study showed that black and white stripes disrupt the attractive pattern. Further, attractiveness increases with stripe width, so the relatively narrow stripes of the three living species of zebras should be unattractive to horseflies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>5. Stripes may be used to cool the zebra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post_2015_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Post_2015-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Air may move more quickly over black light-absorbing stripes while moving more slowly over white stripes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post_2015_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Post_2015-11">[11]</a></sup> This would create convection currents around the zebra that would cool it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post_2015_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Post_2015-11">[11]</a></sup> One study analyzes that zebras have more stripes in hotter habitats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post_2015_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Post_2015-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gaits">Gaits</span></h3>
<p>Zebras have four <a href="/wiki/Horse_gait" title="Horse gait">gaits</a>: walk, <a href="/wiki/Trot" title="Trot">trot</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canter" title="Canter">canter</a> and gallop. They are generally slower than horses, but their great <a href="/wiki/Endurance" title="Endurance">stamina</a> helps them outrun predators. When chased, a zebra will zig-zag from side to side, making it more difficult for the predator to attack. When cornered, the zebra will rear up and kick or bite its attacker.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Senses">Senses</span></h3>
<p>Zebras have excellent eyesight. It is believed that they can see in color.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Need ref for what colour vision they have. Presumably like horses, which can distinguish colours but not red from green. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Like most <a href="/wiki/Ungulate" title="Ungulate">ungulates</a>, the zebra's eyes are on the sides of its head, giving it a wide field of view. Zebras also have <a href="/wiki/Night_vision" title="Night vision">night vision</a>, although not as advanced as that of most of their predators.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Zebras have excellent hearing and have larger, rounder ears than horses; like other ungulates, zebras can turn their ears in almost any direction. In addition to superb eyesight and hearing, zebras also have acute <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">senses</a> of smell and taste.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harems">Harems</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tanzanian_Animals.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1073" data-file-width="2133" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Tanzanian_Animals.jpg/220px-Tanzanian_Animals.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Tanzanian_Animals.jpg/330px-Tanzanian_Animals.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Tanzanian_Animals.jpg/440px-Tanzanian_Animals.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tanzanian_Animals.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Zebras</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Like most members of the horse family, zebras are highly social. Their social structure, however, depends on the species. <a href="/wiki/Mountain_zebra" title="Mountain zebra">Mountain zebras</a> and plains zebras live in groups, known as 'harems', consisting of one stallion with up to six mares and their foals. Bachelor males either live alone or with groups of other bachelors until they are old enough to challenge a breeding stallion. When attacked by packs of <a href="/wiki/Hyena" title="Hyena">hyenas</a> or <a href="/wiki/African_wild_dog" title="African wild dog">wild dogs</a> a zebra group will huddle together with the foals in the middle while the stallion tries to ward them off.</p>
<p>Unlike the other zebra species, Grévy's zebras do not have permanent social bonds. A group of these zebras rarely stays together for more than a few months. The foals stay with their mothers, while adult males live alone. Like the other two zebra species, bachelor male zebras will organize in groups.</p>
<p>Like horses, zebras sleep standing up, and only sleep when neighbors are around to warn them of predators.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communication">Communication</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Equus_quagga_boehmi_(grazing).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="3798" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Equus_quagga_boehmi_%28grazing%29.jpg/220px-Equus_quagga_boehmi_%28grazing%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Equus_quagga_boehmi_%28grazing%29.jpg/330px-Equus_quagga_boehmi_%28grazing%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Equus_quagga_boehmi_%28grazing%29.jpg/440px-Equus_quagga_boehmi_%28grazing%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Equus_quagga_boehmi_(grazing).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Zebra feeding on grass</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Zebras communicate with each other with high-pitched barks and whinnying. Grévy's zebras make mulelike brays. A zebra's ears signify its mood. When a zebra is in a calm, tense or friendly mood, its ears stand erect. When it is frightened, its ears are pushed forward. When angry, the ears are pulled backward. When surveying an area for predators, zebras will stand in an alert posture with ears erect, head held high, and staring. When tense, they will also snort. When a predator is spotted or sensed, a zebra will bark (or bray) loudly.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_foraging">Food and foraging</span></h3>
<p>Zebras feed almost entirely on grasses, but may occasionally eat shrubs, herbs, twigs, leaves and bark. Their digestive systems allow them to subsist on diets of lower nutritional quality than that necessary for other herbivores.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Plains_zebra#Reproduction" title="Plains zebra">Plains zebra § Reproduction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%A9vy%27s_zebra#Reproduction" title="Grévy's zebra">Grévy's zebra § Reproduction</a></div>
<p>Female zebras mature earlier than the males, and a mare may have her first foal by the age of three. Males are not able to breed until the age of five or six. Mares may give birth to one foal every twelve months. She nurses the foal for up to a year. Like horses, zebras are able to stand, walk and suckle shortly after they are born. A zebra foal is brown and white instead of black and white at birth.</p>
<p>Plains and mountain zebra foals are protected by their mothers, as well as the head stallion and the other mares in their group. Grévy's zebra foals have only their mother as a regular protector, since, as noted above, Grévy's zebra groups often disband after a few months.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interaction_with_humans">Interaction with humans</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestication">Domestication</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WalterRothschildWithZebras.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="426" data-file-width="800" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/WalterRothschildWithZebras.jpg/220px-WalterRothschildWithZebras.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/WalterRothschildWithZebras.jpg/330px-WalterRothschildWithZebras.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/WalterRothschildWithZebras.jpg/440px-WalterRothschildWithZebras.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Attempts have been made to train zebras for riding, since they have better resistance than horses to African diseases. Most of these attempts failed, as would have initial attempts to tame wild horses, due to the zebra's more unpredictable nature and tendency to panic under stress. For this reason, zebra-mules or <a href="/wiki/Zebroid" title="Zebroid">zebroids</a> (crosses between any species of zebra and a horse, pony, donkey or ass) are preferred over purebred zebras.</p>
<p>In England, the zoological collector <a href="/wiki/Walter_Rothschild,_2nd_Baron_Rothschild" title="Walter Rothschild, 2nd Baron Rothschild">Walter Rothschild</a> frequently used zebras to draw a carriage. In 1907, <a href="/wiki/Rosendo_Ribeiro" title="Rosendo Ribeiro">Rosendo Ribeiro</a>, the first doctor in Nairobi, Kenya, used a riding zebra for house calls. In the mid-19th century, Governor <a href="/wiki/George_Grey" title="George Grey">George Grey</a> imported zebras to <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> from his previous posting in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, and used them to pull his carriage on his privately owned <a href="/wiki/Kawau_Island" title="Kawau Island">Kawau Island</a>.</p>

<p>Captain Horace Hayes, in "Points of the Horse" (circa 1893), compared the usefulness of different zebra species. In 1891, Hayes broke a mature, intact mountain zebra stallion to ride in two days time, and the animal was quiet enough for his wife to ride and be photographed upon. He found the <a href="/wiki/Burchell%27s_zebra" title="Burchell's zebra">Burchell's zebra</a> easy to break, and considered it ideal for domestication, as it was immune to the bite of the <a href="/wiki/Tsetse_fly" title="Tsetse fly">tsetse fly</a>. He considered the <a href="/wiki/Quagga" title="Quagga">quagga</a> (now extinct) well-suited to domestication due to being easy to train to saddle and harness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span></h3>
<p>Modern man has had great impact on the zebra population. Zebras were, and still are, hunted for their skins, and for meat. They also compete with livestock for forage<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Competition_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Competition-22">[22]</a></sup> and are sometimes culled.</p>
<p>The Cape mountain zebra was hunted to near extinction, with less than 100 individuals by the 1930s. The population has since increased to about 700 due to conservation efforts. Both mountain zebra subspecies are currently protected in national parks, but are still endangered.</p>

<p>The Grévy's zebra is also endangered. Hunting and competition from livestock have greatly decreased their population. Because of the population's small size, environmental hazards, such as drought, are capable of affecting the entire species. Plains zebras are much more numerous and have a healthy population. Nevertheless, they too have been reduced by hunting and loss of habitat to farming. One subspecies, the quagga, is now extinct.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_depictions">Cultural depictions</span></h3>
<p>Zebras have been the subject of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folk tales</a> which tell how they got their stripes. According to a <a href="/wiki/San_people" title="San people">San</a> folk tale of <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a>, the zebra was once all white, but acquired its black stripes after a fight with a <a href="/wiki/Baboon" title="Baboon">baboon</a> over a waterhole. After kicking the baboon so hard, the zebra lost his balance and tripped over a fire, and the fire sticks left scorch marks all over his white coat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In the film <i><a href="/wiki/Fantasia_(1940_film)" title="Fantasia (1940 film)">Fantasia</a></i>, two <a href="/wiki/Centaur" title="Centaur">centaurs</a> are depicted being half human and half zebra, instead of the typical half human and half horse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

<p>Zebras are a popular subject in art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The fourth <a href="/wiki/Mughal_emperors" title="Mughal emperors">Mughal emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Jahangir" title="Jahangir">Jahangir</a> (r.1605–24), commissioned a painting of the zebra, which was completed by <a href="/wiki/Ustad_Mansur" title="Ustad Mansur">Ustad Mansur</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Zebra stripes are also a popular style for furniture, carpets and fashion.</p>
<p>When depicted in movies and cartoons, zebras are most often miscellaneous characters, but have had some starring roles, notably in <i><a href="/wiki/Madagascar_(2005_film)" title="Madagascar (2005 film)">Madagascar</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Racing_Stripes" title="Racing Stripes">Racing Stripes</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Khumba" title="Khumba">Khumba</a></i>. One of the recurring characters in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/My_Little_Pony:_Friendship_is_Magic" title="My Little Pony: Friendship is Magic">My Little Pony: Friendship is Magic</a></i> is a zebra named Zecora. Zebras also serve as mascots and symbols for products and corporations, notably <a href="/wiki/Zebra_Technologies" title="Zebra Technologies">Zebra Technologies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fruit_Stripe" title="Fruit Stripe">Fruit Stripe</a> gum as well as Investec. Zebras are featured on the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Botswana" title="Coat of arms of Botswana">coat of arms of Botswana</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biofuel">Biofuel</span></h3>
<p>Recent research has shown that TU-103, a strain of <i><a href="/wiki/Clostridium" title="Clostridium">Clostridium</a></i> bacteria found in zebra feces, can convert nearly any form of cellulose into <a href="/wiki/Butanol_fuel" title="Butanol fuel">butanol fuel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
