<p>The <b>Ulster Defence Association</b> (<b>UDA</b>) is the largest<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ulster_loyalism" title="Ulster loyalism">Ulster loyalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Ulster_loyalism#Paramilitary_and_vigilante_groups" title="Ulster loyalism">paramilitary and terrorist</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> group in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. It was formed in September 1971 and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Ulster_Defence_Association_actions" title="Timeline of Ulster Defence Association actions">undertook a campaign</a> of almost twenty-four years during <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a>. For most of this time it was a legal organisation. Its declared goal was to defend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulster_Protestant" title="Ulster Protestant">Ulster Protestant</a> loyalist areas<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and to combat <a href="/wiki/Irish_republicanism" title="Irish republicanism">Irish republicanism</a>, particularly the Provisional <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">Irish Republican Army</a> (IRA). In the 1970s, uniformed UDA members openly patrolled these areas armed with batons and held large marches and rallies. Within the UDA was a group tasked with launching paramilitary attacks; it used the covername <b>Ulster Freedom Fighters</b> (<b>UFF</b>) so that the UDA would not be outlawed. The British government outlawed the "UFF" in November 1973, but the UDA itself was not <a href="/wiki/Proscription" title="Proscription">proscribed</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrorist_group" title="Terrorist group">terrorist group</a> until August 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The UDA/UFF terrorists were responsible for more than 400 deaths. The vast majority of its victims were <a href="/wiki/Irish_Catholic" title="Irish Catholic">Irish Catholic</a> civilians,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cain.ulst.ac.uk_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-cain.ulst.ac.uk-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> killed at random, in what the group called retaliation for IRA actions or attacks on Protestants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> High-profile attacks carried out by the group include the <a href="/wiki/Milltown_Cemetery_attack" title="Milltown Cemetery attack">Milltown massacre</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sean_Graham_bookmakers%27_shooting" title="Sean Graham bookmakers' shooting">Sean Graham bookmakers' shooting</a>, the <a href="/wiki/1993_Castlerock_killings" title="1993 Castlerock killings">Castlerock killings</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Greysteel_massacre" title="Greysteel massacre">Greysteel massacre</a>. Most of its attacks were in Northern Ireland, but from 1972 onward it also carried out bombings in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>. The UDA/UFF declared a <a href="/wiki/Ceasefire" title="Ceasefire">ceasefire</a> in 1994 and ended its campaign in 2007, but some of its members have continued to engage in violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The other main loyalist terrorist group during the conflict was the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Volunteer_Force" title="Ulster Volunteer Force">Ulster Volunteer Force</a> (UVF). All three groups are Proscribed Organisations in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Terrorism_Act_2000" title="Terrorism Act 2000">Terrorism Act 2000</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulster_Defence_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginning">Beginning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulster_Defence_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beginning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Ulster Defence Association emerged from a series of meetings during the summer of 1971 of <a href="/wiki/Ulster_loyalism" title="Ulster loyalism">loyalist</a> "<a href="/wiki/Vigilante" title="Vigilante">vigilante</a>" groups called "defence associations".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cain_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-cain-17">[17]</a></sup> The largest of these were the <a href="/wiki/Shankill_Defence_Association" title="Shankill Defence Association">Shankill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodvale_Defence_Association" title="Woodvale Defence Association">Woodvale Defence Associations</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> with other groups based in East Belfast, the Hammer and Roden Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The first meeting was chaired by <a href="/wiki/Billy_Hull" title="Billy Hull">Billy Hull</a>, with Alan Moon as its vice-chair. Moon was quickly replaced by <a href="/wiki/Jim_Anderson_(loyalist)" title="Jim Anderson (loyalist)">Jim Anderson</a> and had left the organisation by the time of its formal launch in September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>By this point, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Harding_Smith" title="Charles Harding Smith">Charles Harding Smith</a> had become the group's leader, with former British soldier <a href="/wiki/Davy_Fogel" title="Davy Fogel">Davy Fogel</a> as his second-in-command, who trained the new recruits in military tactics, the use of guns, and unarmed combat. Its most prominent early spokesperson was <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Herron" title="Tommy Herron">Tommy Herron</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cain_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-cain-17">[17]</a></sup> however, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Tyrie" title="Andy Tyrie">Andy Tyrie</a> would emerge as leader soon after.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Its original motto was <i>Cedenta Arma Togae</i> ("Law before violence") and it was a legal organisation until it was banned by the British Government on 10 August 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cain_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-cain-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>At its peak of strength it held around forty thousand members, mostly part-time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> During this period of legality, the UDA committed a large number of attacks using the name Ulster Freedom Fighters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Paddy_Wilson_and_Irene_Andrews_killings" title="Paddy Wilson and Irene Andrews killings">the assassination of</a> <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_and_Labour_Party" title="Social Democratic and Labour Party">Social Democratic and Labour Party</a> (SDLP) politician <a href="/wiki/Paddy_Wilson" title="Paddy Wilson">Paddy Wilson</a> in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The UDA was involved in the successful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulster_Workers_Council_Strike" title="Ulster Workers Council Strike">Ulster Workers Council Strike</a> in 1974, which brought down the <a href="/wiki/Sunningdale_Agreement" title="Sunningdale Agreement">Sunningdale Agreement</a>—an agreement which some <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">unionists</a> thought conceded too much to <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalism" title="Irish nationalism">nationalist</a> demands. The UDA enforced this <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">general strike</a> through widespread intimidation across Northern Ireland. The strike was led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VUPP" title="VUPP">VUPP</a> Assemblyman and UDA member, <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Barr" title="Glenn Barr">Glenn Barr</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The UDA were often referred to as "Wombles" by their rivals, mainly the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Volunteer_Force" title="Ulster Volunteer Force">Ulster Volunteer Force</a> (UVF). The name is derived from the furry fictional creatures <a href="/wiki/The_Wombles" title="The Wombles">The Wombles</a>, and was given to the UDA because many of its members wore fur-trimmed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snorkel_parka" title="Snorkel parka">parkas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nelson179_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-nelson179-28">[28]</a></sup> Its headquarters is in Gawn Street, off the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newtownards_Road" title="Newtownards Road">Newtownards Road</a> in east Belfast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and its current motto is <i>Quis Separabit</i>, which is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "Who will separate [us]?"</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Women.27s_units">Women's units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulster_Defence_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Women's units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The UDA had several women's units, which acted independent of each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taylor_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-taylor-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcevoy12_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcevoy12-31">[31]</a></sup> Although they occasionally helped man roadblocks, the women's units were typically involved in local community work and responsible for the assembly and delivery of food parcels to UDA prisoners. This was a source of pride for the UDA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcevoy16_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcevoy16-32">[32]</a></sup> The first women's unit was founded on the <a href="/wiki/Shankill_Road" title="Shankill Road">Shankill Road</a> by <a href="/wiki/Wendy_Millar" title="Wendy Millar">Wendy "Bucket" Millar</a>, whose sons Herbie and James "Sham" Millar would later become prominent UDA members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heraldwilson_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-heraldwilson-33">[33]</a></sup> The UDA women's department was headed by Jean Moore, who also came from the Shankill Road. She had also served as the president of the women's auxiliary of the <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_Association_of_Workers" title="Loyalist Association of Workers">Loyalist Association of Workers</a>. Her brother Ingram "Jock" Beckett, one of the UDA's founding members, had been killed in March 1972 by a rival UDA faction in an internal dispute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dillon232_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-dillon232-34">[34]</a></sup> Moore was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Hester_Dunn" title="Hester Dunn">Hester Dunn</a> of east Belfast, who also ran the public relations and administration section at the UDA headquarters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wood94_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-wood94-35">[35]</a></sup> Wendy Millar's Shankill Road group was a particularly active women's unit, and another was based in <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Row" title="Sandy Row">Sandy Row</a>, south Belfast, a traditional UDA stronghold. The latter was commanded by Elizabeth "Lily" Douglas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kiely108_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-kiely108-36">[36]</a></sup> Her teenaged daughter, Elizabeth was one of the members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-life_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-life-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Sandy Row women's UDA unit was disbanded after it carried out a vicious "romper room" punishment beating on 24 July 1974 which left 32-year-old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anne_Ogilby_killing" title="Anne Ogilby killing">Ann Ogilby</a> dead. The body of Ogilby, a Protestant single mother who had an affair with the husband of one of the unit's members, was found in a ditch five days later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simpson32_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-simpson32-38">[38]</a></sup> The day of the fatal beating Ogilby was abducted and forced upstairs to the first floor of a disused bakery in Sandy Row that had been converted into a UDA club. Two teenage girls, Henrietta Cowan and Christine Smith,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-simpson38_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-simpson38-39">[39]</a></sup> acting under Elizabeth Douglas' orders to give Ogilby a "good rompering",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kiely111_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-kiely111-40">[40]</a></sup> punched, kicked, then battered her to death with bricks and sticks; the autopsy later revealed that Ogilby had suffered 24 blows to the head and body. The killing, which was carried out within earshot of Ogilby's six-year-old daughter, caused widespread revulsion throughout Northern Ireland and was condemned by the UDA prisoners serving inside the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maze_Prison" title="Maze Prison">Maze Prison</a>. None of the other UDA women's units had consented to or been aware of the fatal punishment beating until it was reported in the news.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcevoy12_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-mcevoy12-31">[31]</a></sup> Douglas, Cowan, and Smith were convicted of the murder and sentenced to imprisonment at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Armagh_Women%27s_Jail&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Armagh Women's Jail (page does not exist)">Armagh Women's Jail</a>. Seven other members of the women's unit and a UDA man were also convicted for their part in the murder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-life_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-life-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kiely111_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-kiely111-40">[40]</a></sup> The UDA "romper rooms", named after <a href="/wiki/Romper_Room" title="Romper Room">the children's television programme</a>, were places where victims were beaten and tortured prior to being killed. This was known as a "rompering". The "romper rooms" were normally located in disused buildings, lock-up garages, warehouses, and rooms above pubs and drinking clubs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nelson126_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-nelson126-41">[41]</a></sup> The use of the "romper rooms" was a more common practise among male members of the UDA than their female counterparts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcevoy12_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-mcevoy12-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrorist_campaign">Terrorist campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulster_Defence_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Terrorist campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Throughout the majority of its period of legality, the UDA's attacks were carried out under the name "Ulster Freedom Fighters" (UFF). The UDA's campaign of violence began in 1972. In May of that year, the UDA's pressured leader Tommy Herron decided that responsibility for acts of violence committed by the UDA would be claimed by the "UFF". Its first public statements came one month later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UFF_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-UFF-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>The UDA's official position during the Troubles was that if the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">Provisional Irish Republican Army</a> (Provisional IRA) called off its campaign of violence, then it would do the same. However, if the British government announced that it was withdrawing from Northern Ireland, then the UDA would act as "the IRA in reverse."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OBrien_91_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-OBrien_91-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Active throughout the Troubles, its armed campaign gained prominence in the early 1990s through <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Adair" title="Johnny Adair">Johnny Adair</a>'s ruthless leadership of the Lower <a href="/wiki/Shankill_Road" title="Shankill Road">Shankill</a> 2nd Battalion, C. Company, which resulted in a greater degree of tactical independence for individual brigades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> C. Company's hit squad, led by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_McKeag" title="Stephen McKeag">Stephen McKeag</a>, became notorious for a campaign of random murders of Catholic civilians in the first half of the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>They benefited, along with the Ulster Volunteer Force, and a group called <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Resistance" title="Ulster Resistance">Ulster Resistance</a> (set up by the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Unionist_Party" title="Democratic Unionist Party">Democratic Unionist Party</a>), from a shipment of arms imported from <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a> in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OBrien_92_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-OBrien_92-46">[46]</a></sup> The weapons landed included rocket launchers, 200 rifles, 90 pistols and over 400 grenades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OBrien_92_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-OBrien_92-46">[46]</a></sup> Although almost two–thirds of these weapons were later recovered by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">Royal Ulster Constabulary</a> (RUC), they enabled the UDA to launch an assassination campaign against their perceived enemies.</p>

<p>North Belfast UDA brigadier <a href="/wiki/Davy_Payne" title="Davy Payne">Davy Payne</a> was arrested after his "scout" car had been stopped at a RUC checkpoint and large caches of the weaponry were discovered in the boots of his associates' cars. He was sentenced to 19 years in prison.</p>
<p>In 1992 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brian_Nelson_(double_agent)" title="Brian Nelson (double agent)">Brian Nelson</a>, a prominent UDA member convicted of sectarian killings, revealed that he was also a <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> agent. This led to allegations that the British Army and RUC were helping the UDA to target Irish republican activists. UDA members have since confirmed that they received intelligence files on republicans from British Army and RUC intelligence sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the most high-profile UDA attacks came in October 1993, when three masked men attacked a restaurant called the Rising Sun in the predominantly Catholic village of <a href="/wiki/Greysteel" title="Greysteel">Greysteel</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Londonderry" title="County Londonderry">County Londonderry</a>, where two hundred people were celebrating Halloween. The two men entered and opened fire. Eight people, including six Catholics and two Protestants were killed and nineteen wounded in what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Greysteel_massacre" title="Greysteel massacre">Greysteel massacre</a>. The "UFF" claimed the attack was in retaliation to the IRA's <a href="/wiki/Shankill_Road_bombing" title="Shankill Road bombing">Shankill Road bombing</a>, which killed nine people seven days earlier.</p>
<p>According to the Sutton database of deaths at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Ulster" title="University of Ulster">University of Ulster</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Conflict_Archive_on_the_Internet" title="Conflict Archive on the Internet">CAIN project</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> the UDA was responsible for 259 killings during the Troubles. 208 of its victims were civilians (predominantly Catholics), 12 were civilian political activists (mainly members of <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a>), 37 were other loyalist paramilitaries (including 30 of its own members), three were members of the security forces and 11 were republican paramilitaries. A number of these attacks were carried out with the assistance or complicity of the British Army, the RUC, or both, according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stevens_Report" title="Stevens Report">Stevens Enquiry</a>, although the exact number of people killed as a result of collusion has not been revealed. The preferred modus operandi of the UDA was individual killings of civilian targets in nationalist areas, rather than large-scale bomb or mortar attacks.</p>
<p>The UDA employed various codewords whenever they claimed their attacks. These included: "The Crucible", "Titanic", "Ulster Troubles" and "Captain Black".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-ceasefire_activities">Post-ceasefire activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulster_Defence_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-ceasefire activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Its ceasefire was welcomed by the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> Secretary of State, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Murphy_(UK_politician)" title="Paul Murphy (UK politician)">Paul Murphy</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Constable" title="Chief Constable">Chief Constable</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Police_Service_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Police Service of Northern Ireland">Police Service of Northern Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Orde" title="Hugh Orde">Hugh Orde</a>.</p>

<p>Since the ceasefire, the UDA has been accused of taking <a href="/wiki/Vigilante" title="Vigilante">vigilante</a> action against alleged drug dealers, including <a href="/wiki/Tarring_and_feathering" title="Tarring and feathering">tarring and feathering</a> a man on the Taughmonagh estate in south Belfast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> It has also been involved in several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loyalist_Feud" title="Loyalist Feud">feuds</a> with the UVF, which led to many killings. The UDA has also been riddled by its own internecine warfare, with self-styled "brigadiers" and former figures of power and influence, such as <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Adair" title="Johnny Adair">Johnny Adair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Gray_(UDA_member)" title="Jim Gray (UDA member)">Jim Gray</a> (themselves bitter rivals), falling rapidly in and out of favour with the rest of the leadership. Gray and <a href="/wiki/John_Gregg_(UDA)" title="John Gregg (UDA)">John Gregg</a> are amongst those to have been killed during the internal strife. On 22 February 2003, the UDA announced a "12-month period of military inactivity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> It said it would review its ceasefire every three months. The UPRG's <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Gallagher" title="Frankie Gallagher">Frankie Gallagher</a> has since taken a leading role in ending the association between the UDA and drug dealing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following an August 2005 <i><a href="/wiki/Sunday_World" title="Sunday World">Sunday World</a></i> article that poked fun at the gambling losses of one of its leaders, the UDA banned the sale of the newspaper from shops in areas it controls. Shops that defy the ban have suffered arson attacks, and at least one newsagent was threatened with death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Police_Service_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Police Service of Northern Ireland">Police Service of Northern Ireland</a> began accompanying the paper's delivery vans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The UDA was also considered to have played an instrumental role in loyalist riots in Belfast in September 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 13 November 2005 the UDA announced that it would "consider its future", in the wake of the standing down of the Provisional IRA and <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_Volunteer_Force" title="Loyalist Volunteer Force">Loyalist Volunteer Force</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2006, the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Monitoring_Commission" title="Independent Monitoring Commission">Independent Monitoring Commission</a> (IMC) reported UDA involvement in organised crime, drug trafficking, counterfeiting, extortion, money laundering and robbery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>

<p>On 20 June 2006, the UDA expelled <a href="/wiki/Shoukri_brothers" title="Shoukri brothers">Andre Shoukri and his brother Ihab</a>, two of its senior members who were heavily involved in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organised_crime" title="Organised crime">organised crime</a>. Some saw this as a sign that the UDA was slowly coming away from crime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The move did see the southeast <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">Antrim</a> brigade of the UDA, which had been at loggerheads with the leadership for some time, support Shoukri and break away under former UPRG spokesman <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Kirkham" title="Tommy Kirkham">Tommy Kirkham</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Other senior members met with <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a> <a href="/wiki/Bertie_Ahern" title="Bertie Ahern">Bertie Ahern</a> for talks on 13 July in the same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 11 November 2007 the UDA announced that the Ulster Freedom Fighters would be stood down from midnight of the same day,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> with its weapons "being put beyond use" although it stressed that these would not be decommissioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the group expressed a willingness to move from criminal activity to "community development," the IMC said it saw little evidence of this move because of the views of its members and the lack of coherence in the group's leadership as a result of its decentralised structure. While the report indicated the leadership intends to move towards its stated goals, factionalism hindered this change and was the strongest hindrance to progress. Although most loyalist actions were curtailed since the IMC's previous report, most of loyalist paramilitary activity was coming from the UDA.</p>
<p>The IMC report concluded that the leadership's willingness to change has resulted in community tension and the group would continue to be monitored, although "the mainstream UDA still has some way to go." Furthermore, the IMC warned the group to "recognise that the organisation's time as a paramilitary group has passed and that decommissioning is inevitable." Decommissioning was said to be the "biggest outstanding issue for loyalist leaders, although not the only one."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMC_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMC-64">[64]</a></sup></p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ballyduff_UFF.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A UDA/UFF South-East Antrim Brigade mural in Newtownabbey</div>

<p>On 6 January 2010, the UDA announced that it had put its weapons "verifiably beyond use".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decommissioned_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-decommissioned-65">[65]</a></sup> The decommissioning was completed five weeks before a government amnesty deadline beyond which any weapons found could have been used as evidence for a prosecution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decommissioned_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-decommissioned-65">[65]</a></sup> The decommissioning was confirmed by Canadian General <a href="/wiki/John_de_Chastelain" title="John de Chastelain">John de Chastelain</a>, chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Independent_International_Commission_on_Decommissioning" title="Independent International Commission on Decommissioning">Independent International Commission on Decommissioning</a>, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Eames" title="Lord Eames">Lord Eames</a>, former <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Armagh_(Church_of_Ireland)" title="Archbishop of Armagh (Church of Ireland)">Archbishop of Armagh</a> and Sir <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_Quigley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George Quigley (page does not exist)">George Quigley</a>, former top civil servant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decommissioned2_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-decommissioned2-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>Chastelain stated that the decommissioning included arms, ammunition, explosives and explosive devices and the UDA stated that the arms "constitute the totality of those under their control".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decommissioned_65-2"><a href="#cite_note-decommissioned-65">[65]</a></sup> Following the decommissioning the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Political_Research_Group" title="Ulster Political Research Group">Ulster Political Research Group</a>, the UDA's political representatives, stated that the "Ulster Defence Association was formed to defend our communities; we state quite clearly and categorically that this responsibility now rests with the Government and its institutions where legitimacy resides".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decommissioned2_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-decommissioned2-66">[66]</a></sup> UDA representative Frankie Gallagher also stated that the group now regretted being responsible for the killing of more than 400 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Shaun_Woodward" title="Shaun Woodward">Shaun Woodward</a>, the British <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Northern_Ireland" title="Secretary of State for Northern Ireland">Secretary of State for Northern Ireland</a>, stated that this "is a major act of leadership by the UDA and further comprehensive evidence of the success of politics over violence in Northern Ireland" and the act was also welcomed by Sinn Féin and DUP politicians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The President of the Republic of Ireland, <a href="/wiki/Mary_McAleese" title="Mary McAleese">Mary McAleese</a>, described the decommissioning as "a very positive milestone on the journey of peace".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> US Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a> also welcomed the move as a step towards lasting peace in Northern Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_East_Antrim_breakaway_group">South East Antrim breakaway group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulster_Defence_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: South East Antrim breakaway group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/UDA_South_East_Antrim_Brigade" title="UDA South East Antrim Brigade">UDA South East Antrim Brigade</a></div>
<p>The breakaway faction continues to use the "UDA" title in its name, although it too expressed willingness to move towards "community development." Although serious crime is not prevalent among its members, some who were arrested for drug peddling and extortion were exiled by the Brigade. A clear distinction between the factions was not available in the 20th IMC report, as this was the first report to differentiate between the two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMC_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-IMC-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulster_Defence_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_the_Ulster_Nation.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="397" data-file-width="600" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Flag_of_the_Ulster_Nation.svg/220px-Flag_of_the_Ulster_Nation.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Flag_of_the_Ulster_Nation.svg/330px-Flag_of_the_Ulster_Nation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Flag_of_the_Ulster_Nation.svg/440px-Flag_of_the_Ulster_Nation.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_the_Ulster_Nation.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Some UDA leaders supported an independent Northern Ireland in the mid–late 1970s</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the 1970s the group favoured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_independence" title="Northern Ireland independence">Northern Ireland independence</a>, but they have retreated from this position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Ulster_Political_Research_Group" title="New Ulster Political Research Group">New Ulster Political Research Group</a> (NUPRG) was initially the political wing of the UDA, founded in 1978, which then evolved into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulster_Loyalist_Democratic_Party" title="Ulster Loyalist Democratic Party">Ulster Loyalist Democratic Party</a> in 1981 under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/John_McMichael" title="John McMichael">John McMichael</a>, a prominent UDA member killed by the IRA in 1987, amid suspicion that he was set up to be killed by some of his UDA colleagues.</p>
<p>In 1987, the UDA's deputy commander John McMichael (who was then the leader of the UFF) promoted a document entitled <i>Common Sense</i>, which promoted a consensual end to the conflict in Northern Ireland, while maintaining the Union. The document advocated a power-sharing assembly involving both nationalists and unionists, an agreed constitution and new Bill of Rights. It is not clear, however, whether this programme was adopted by the UDA as their official policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OBrien_91_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-OBrien_91-43">[43]</a></sup> However, the killing of McMichael that same year and the subsequent removal of Tyrie from the leadership and his replacement with an Inner Council saw the UDA concentrate on stockpiling weapons rather than political ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1989, the ULDP changed its name to the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Democratic_Party" title="Ulster Democratic Party">Ulster Democratic Party</a> (UDP). It finally dissolved itself in 2001 following very limited electoral success and internal difficulties. <a href="/wiki/Gary_McMichael" title="Gary McMichael">Gary McMichael</a>, son of John McMichael, was the last leader of the UDP, which supported the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Political_Research_Group" title="Ulster Political Research Group">Ulster Political Research Group</a> (UPRG) was subsequently formed to give political analysis to the UDA and act as community workers in loyalist areas. It is currently represented on the <a href="/wiki/Belfast_City_Council" title="Belfast City Council">Belfast City Council</a>.</p>
<p>In early January 1994, the UDA released a document calling for <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_cleansing" title="Ethnic cleansing">ethnic cleansing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Repartition_of_Ireland" title="Repartition of Ireland">repartition</a>, with the goal of making Northern Ireland wholly Protestant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood_184-185_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wood_184-185-73">[73]</a></sup> The plan was to be implemented should the British Army withdraw from Northern Ireland. Areas in the south and west with strong Catholic/nationalist majorities would be handed over to the Republic, and those Catholics left stranded in the "Protestant state" would be "expelled, nullified, or interned".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood_184-185_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wood_184-185-73">[73]</a></sup> The story was printed in <i><a href="/wiki/Sunday_Independent_(Ireland)" title="Sunday Independent (Ireland)">The Sunday Independent</a></i> newspaper on 16 January.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> The "doomsday plan" was based on the work of Dr <a href="/wiki/Liam_Kennedy_(historian)" title="Liam Kennedy (historian)">Liam Kennedy</a>, a lecturer at <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_University_Belfast" title="Queen's University Belfast">Queen's University Belfast</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood_184-185_73-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wood_184-185-73">[73]</a></sup> who in 1986 had published a book called <i>Two Ulsters: A Case for Repartition</i> although it did not call for ethnic cleansing. The UDP's Raymond Smallwoods said "I wasn't consulted but the scenario set out is a perfectly plausible one".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood_184-185_73-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wood_184-185-73">[73]</a></sup> The DUP's <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Wilson" title="Sammy Wilson">Sammy Wilson</a> stated that the plan "shows that some loyalist paramilitaries are looking ahead and contemplating what needs to be done to maintain our separate Ulster identity"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wood_184-185_73-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wood_184-185-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Links_with_other_groups">Links with other groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulster_Defence_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Links with other groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his book <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Sun_(Goodrick-Clarke_book)" title="Black Sun (Goodrick-Clarke book)">Black Sun</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Goodrick-Clarke" title="Nicholas Goodrick-Clarke">Nicholas Goodrick-Clarke</a> claimed that the UDA had links with <a href="/wiki/Neo-Nazism" title="Neo-Nazism">Neo-Nazi</a> groups in Britain—specifically <a href="/wiki/Combat_18" title="Combat 18">Combat 18</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> (formed in 1991) and the <a href="/wiki/British_Movement" title="British Movement">British National Socialist Movement</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> (formed in 1985). He claims that members of these groups helped to smuggle weapons for the UDA. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_S_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ian S Wood (page does not exist)">Ian S Wood</a>'s book <i>Crimes of Loyalty: A History of the UDA</i> claims that the UDA has received backing from Combat 18, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_National_Front" title="British National Front">British National Front</a> and the <a href="/wiki/British_National_Party" title="British National Party">British National Party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> In 2006, the BBC also reported that the group has links with Combat 18.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> It is unknown whether these links still exist. The links may not have been politically motivated, but for mutually beneficial arms deals. On one occasion the UDA sent Louis Scott, one of a few black members of the UDA, to make the transaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Adair" title="Johnny Adair">Johnny Adair</a>, who had been in Combat 18 before the UDA, established stronger links once he became a brigadier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Red_Hand_Defenders" title="Red Hand Defenders">Red Hand Defenders</a> is a cover name used by breakaway factions of the UDA and the LVF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> The term was originally coined in 1997 when members of the LVF carried out attacks on behalf of Johnny Adair's "UFF 2nd Battalion, 'C' Company (Shankill Road)" and vice versa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> The relationship between the UDA (specifically Adair's West Belfast Brigade, not the wider leadership of the UDA) was initially formed after the death of <a href="/wiki/Billy_Wright_(loyalist)" title="Billy Wright (loyalist)">Billy Wright</a>, the previous leader of the LVF, and grew from Adair's personal friendship with <a href="/wiki/Mark_Fulton_(loyalist)" title="Mark Fulton (loyalist)">Mark 'Swinger' Fulton</a>, the organisation's new chief.</p>
<p>The necessity for a cover name resulted from the need to avoid tensions between the UDA and the UVF, the organisation from which the LVF had broken away. It was perceived that any open co-operation between the UDA and the LVF would anger the UVF, something which proved to be the case in following years and resulted in a <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_feud" title="Loyalist feud">loyalist feud</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> There has been debate as to whether or not the Red Hand Defenders have become an entity in their own right<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> made up of dissident factions from both the UDA and the LVF (both of which have now declared ceasefires whilst the RHD has not), although much intelligence has been based on the claims of responsibility which, as has been suggested,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> are frequently misleading.</p>
<p>A 1985 <a href="/wiki/MI5" title="MI5">MI5</a> assessment reported that 85% of the UDA's "targeting material" came from the security forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_leadership">Structure and leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulster_Defence_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Structure and leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The UDA is made up of:</p>
<ul>
<li>the Inner Council</li>
<li>the Ulster Freedom Fighters (UFF)—whose role was to carry out attacks on republican and nationalist targets. However, many regard the UFF as merely a covername used when the UDA wished to claim responsibility for attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></li>
<li>the Ulster Defence Force (UDF)—whose role was to give "specialist military training" to a select group of UDA members. The UDF was initiated by <a href="/wiki/John_McMichael" title="John McMichael">John McMichael</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup> (the then UDA/UFF commander) in 1985 as a response to the Anglo-Irish Agreement. The UDF operated training camps in rural parts of Northern Ireland that young loyalists such as <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Adair" title="Johnny Adair">Johnny Adair</a> claim to have attended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup> One reported 'survival' training technique was to leave trainees stranded in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> with only £1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup> Some of the training was given by former British Army soldiers and officers. It was described by the UDA as "the nucleus of a new loyalist army at the ready".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Young_Militants" title="Ulster Young Militants">Ulster Young Militants</a> (UYM)—the "youth wing" of the group. Formed in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Political_Research_Group" title="Ulster Political Research Group">Ulster Political Research Group</a> (UPRG)—the UDA's "political advisory body". Formed in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The UDA operated a devolved structure of leadership, each with a brigadier representing one of its six "brigade areas".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup> Currently, it is not entirely clear whether or not this structure has been maintained in the UDA's post cease-fire state. The UDA's six "brigade areas" were:</p>
<ul>
<li>North Belfast</li>
<li>East Belfast</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/UDA_South_Belfast_Brigade" title="UDA South Belfast Brigade">South Belfast</a>, the UDA's largest brigade area, covering all of South Belfast down to Lisburn and operating as far away as South County Down, Lurgan, Portadown and Counties Tyrone and Fermanagh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDonaldCusack186187_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-McDonaldCusack186187-89">[89]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/UDA_West_Belfast_Brigade" title="UDA West Belfast Brigade">West Belfast</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/UDA_South_East_Antrim_Brigade" title="UDA South East Antrim Brigade">Southeast [County] Antrim</a></li>
<li>North County Antrim &amp; County Londonderry</li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dervock_UDA_wall_sign.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Dervock_UDA_wall_sign.JPG/220px-Dervock_UDA_wall_sign.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Dervock_UDA_wall_sign.JPG/330px-Dervock_UDA_wall_sign.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Dervock_UDA_wall_sign.JPG/440px-Dervock_UDA_wall_sign.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

A wall sign in <a href="/wiki/Dervock" title="Dervock">Dervock</a> showing support for the North Antrim and Londonderry brigade.</div>

<p>In addition to these six core brigades two others may have existed. A seventh Mid-Ulster Brigade is mentioned by Steve Bruce as having existed for part of the UDA's history<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> although Henry McDonald and Jim Cusack characterise this as a "battalion" rather than a brigade and suggest that its rural location prevented it from fully developing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> In the late 1970s a Scottish Brigade was established under the command of Roddy McDonald but this proved short-lived. The security forces infiltrated this brigade almost immediately and in 1979 arrested almost its entire membership, ninety people in all. Six members received particularly lengthy prison sentences for their involvement in UDA activities in <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a> and the Scottish Brigade quietly disappeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some of the notable brigadiers include:</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jackie_McDonald" title="Jackie McDonald">Jackie McDonald</a>—South Belfast (~1980s-present)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_280.E2.80.93283_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004_280.E2.80.93283-93">[93]</a></sup> Resident of the Taughmonagh estate in South Belfast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_280.E2.80.93283_93-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004_280.E2.80.93283-93">[93]</a></sup> McDonald was a cautious supporter of the UDA's ceasefire and a harsh critic of Johnny 'Mad Dog' Adair during his final years of membership of the organisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_280.E2.80.93283_93-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004_280.E2.80.93283-93">[93]</a></sup> McDonald remains the only brigadier who did not have a commonly used nickname.</p>
<p>Johnny 'Mad Dog' Adair—West Belfast (1990–2002)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup> An active figure in the UDA/UFF, Adair rose to notoriety in the early 1990s when he led the <i>2nd Battalion, C Company</i> unit in West Belfast which was responsible for one of the bloodiest killing sprees of <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-5"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jim_Gray_(UDA_member)" title="Jim Gray (UDA member)">Jim 'Doris Day' Gray</a>—East Belfast (1992–2005)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-6"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> An unlikely figure in Northern Ireland loyalism, the openly bi-sexual<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-7"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup> Gray was a controversial figure in the organisation until his death on 4 October 2005. Always flamboyantly dressed, Gray was a key figure in the UDA's negotiations with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Secretary" title="Northern Ireland Secretary">Northern Ireland Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Reid,_Baron_Reid_of_Cardowan" title="John Reid, Baron Reid of Cardowan">John Reid</a>. It is widely believed that Gray received his nickname from the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">Royal Ulster Constabulary</a> (RUC) <a href="/wiki/Special_Branch" title="Special Branch">Special Branch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-8"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jimbo_Simpson" title="Jimbo Simpson">Jimbo 'Bacardi Brigadier' Simpson</a>—North Belfast (Unknown–2002)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-9"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup> Simpson is believed to have been an alcoholic, hence his nickname. He was leader of the UDA in the volatile North Belfast area, an interface between Catholics and Protestants in the <a href="/wiki/New_Lodge,_Belfast" title="New Lodge, Belfast">New Lodge</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiger%27s_Bay" title="Tiger's Bay">Tiger's Bay</a> neighbourhoods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-10"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Billy_McFarland" title="Billy McFarland">Billy 'The Mexican' McFarland</a>—North Antrim and Londonderry (Unknown–2013)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-11"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup> He earned his nickname because of his moustache and swarthy appearance, and had overall command of the UDA's North Antrim and Derry brigade at the time of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belfast_Agreement" title="Belfast Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a>. He supported the leadership against <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Adair" title="Johnny Adair">Johnny Adair</a> and has been associated with the magazine 'Warrior', which makes the case for <a href="/wiki/Ulster_nationalism" title="Ulster nationalism">Ulster Independence</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Shoukri_brothers" title="Shoukri brothers">Andre 'The Egyptian' Shoukri</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-12"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup>—North Belfast (2002–2005)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lister_2004_85-13"><a href="#cite_note-Lister_2004-85">[85]</a></sup> Initially a close ally of Johnny Adair, Shoukri and his brother Ihab became involved with the UDA in his native North Belfast. The son of an Egyptian father and a Northern Irish mother, he was expelled from the UDA in 2005 following allegations of criminality.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Gregg_(UDA)" title="John Gregg (UDA)">John 'Grug' Gregg</a>—South East Antrim (c.1993<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>–2003) John 'Grug' Gregg was a man with a fearsome reputation within the loyalist movement, known as a "Hawk" in loyalist circles, and controlled the streets of south east Antrim. On 14 March 1984, he severely wounded Sinn Féin president Gerry Adams in an assassination attempt for which he was jailed. When asked by the BBC in prison if he regretted anything about the shooting, his reply was "only that I didn't succeed." He was killed on Belfast's Nelson Street, along with another UDA member (Rab Carson), while travelling in a taxi from the docks in 2003, and the murder was blamed on supporters of Johnny Adair, who had recently been expelled from the UDA in 2002.</p>

<p>Malcolm Sutton's <i>Index of Deaths from the Conflict in Ireland</i>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Conflict_Archive_on_the_Internet" title="Conflict Archive on the Internet">Conflict Archive on the Internet</a> (CAIN), states that the UDA/UFF was responsible for at least 260 killings, and lists a further 256 loyalist killings that have not yet been attributed to a particular group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> According to the book <i>Lost Lives</i> (2006 edition), it was responsible for 431 killings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lostlives-stats_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-lostlives-stats-97">[97]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of those killed by the UDA/UFF:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cain.ulst.ac.uk_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-cain.ulst.ac.uk-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>209 (~80%) were civilians, 12 of whom were civilian political activists</li>
<li>11 (~4%) were members or former members of republican paramilitary groups</li>
<li>37 (~14%) were members or former members of loyalist paramilitary groups</li>
<li>3 (~1%) were members of the British security forces</li>
</ul>
<p>The CAIN database says there were 91 UDA members and four former members killed in the conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup></p>
