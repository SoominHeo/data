<p><b>Alternative hip hop</b> (also known as <b>alternative rap</b>) is a subgenre of <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop music</a> that encompasses the wide range of styles of hip hop that have not become identified as mainstream. <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> defines it as follows: "Alternative rap refers to <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a> groups that tend not to conform to any of the traditional forms of rap, such as <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miami_bass" title="Miami bass">bass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_hip_hop" title="Hardcore hip hop">hardcore</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pop_rap" title="Pop rap">pop</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Party_rap" title="Party rap">party rap</a>. Instead, they blur genres – drawing equally from <a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alternative hip hop developed in the late 1980s. Its commercial momentum was impeded by the then also newly emerging, significantly harder-edged West Coast <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a>. A resurgence came about in the late 1990s and early 2000s with the rejuvenated interest in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indie_music" title="Indie music">indie music</a> by the general public. In the 2000s alternative hip hop reattained its place within the mainstream, due in part to the declining commercial viability of gangsta rap as well as the crossover success of artists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OutKast" title="OutKast">OutKast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>. The alternative hip hop movement expanded beyond the US to include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somali_people" title="Somali people">Somali</a>-Canadian poet <a href="/wiki/K%27naan" title="K'naan">K'naan</a>, Japanese rapper <a href="/wiki/Shing02" title="Shing02">Shing02</a>, and British artist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M.I.A._(artist)" title="M.I.A. (artist)">MIA</a>. Alternative hip hop acts have attained much critical acclaim, but receive relatively little exposure through radio and other media outlets.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_hip_hop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_hip_hop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Originating in the late-80s, in midst of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_age_hip_hop" title="Golden age hip hop">golden age of hip hop</a>, alternative hip hop was headed primarily by <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_hip_hop" title="East Coast hip hop">East Coast</a> rappers such as <a href="/wiki/De_La_Soul" title="De La Soul">De La Soul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beastie_Boys" title="Beastie Boys">Beastie Boys</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pete_Rock_%26_C.L._Smooth" title="Pete Rock &amp; C.L. Smooth">Pete Rock &amp; C.L. Smooth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jungle_Brothers" title="Jungle Brothers">Jungle Brothers</a>, <a href="/wiki/A_Tribe_Called_Quest" title="A Tribe Called Quest">A Tribe Called Quest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brand_Nubian" title="Brand Nubian">Brand Nubian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Digable_Planets" title="Digable Planets">Digable Planets</a> in subsidiary conjunction by <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_hip_hop" title="West Coast hip hop">West Coast</a> acts such as <a href="/wiki/The_Pharcyde" title="The Pharcyde">The Pharcyde</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Del_Tha_Funkee_Homosapien" title="Del Tha Funkee Homosapien">Del Tha Funkee Homosapien</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digital_Underground" title="Digital Underground">Digital Underground</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_Fellowship" title="Freestyle Fellowship">Freestyle Fellowship</a> as well as certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_rap" title="Southern rap">Southern</a> acts such as <a href="/wiki/Arrested_Development_(group)" title="Arrested Development (group)">Arrested Development</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goodie_Mob" title="Goodie Mob">Goodie Mob</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OutKast" title="OutKast">OutKast</a>. Similar to the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> movement, alternative hip hop segued into the mainstream at the dawn of the 1990s. Arrested Development along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Fugees" title="The Fugees">The Fugees</a>, stand as some of the first few alternative rap to be recognized by mainstream audiences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup> The classic debut albums <i><a href="/wiki/3_Feet_High_and_Rising" title="3 Feet High and Rising">3 Feet High and Rising</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/People%27s_Instinctive_Travels_and_the_Paths_of_Rhythm" title="People's Instinctive Travels and the Paths of Rhythm">People's Instinctive Travels and the Paths of Rhythm</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Bizarre_Ride_II_the_Pharcyde" title="Bizarre Ride II the Pharcyde">Bizarre Ride II the Pharcyde</a></i> achieved minor commercial success as they garnered immense acclaim from music critics, who described the records as managing to be both ambitiously innovative but playful masterpieces, hailing the artists as the future of hip hop music as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erlewine_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Erlewine-2">[2]</a></sup> Christened as "The <i><a href="/wiki/Sgt._Pepper%27s_Lonely_Hearts_Club_Band" title="Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band">Sgt. Pepper</a></i> of hip hop", De La Soul's debut album <i>3 Feet High and Rising</i> was considered the forefront of the subgenre. As music critic Jon Bush wrote in retrospect:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The most inventive, assured, and playful debut in hip-hop history, <i>3 Feet High and Rising</i> not only proved that rappers didn't have to talk about the streets to succeed, but also expanded the palette of sampling material with a kaleidoscope of sounds and references culled from <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">disco</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country music</a>. Weaving clever wordplay and deft rhymes across two dozen tracks loosely organized around a game-show theme, De La Soul broke down boundaries all over the LP, moving easily from the groovy my-philosophy intro "The Magic Number" to an intelligent, caring inner-city vignette named "Ghetto Thang" to the freewheeling end-of-innocence tale "Jenifa Taught Me (Derwin's Revenge)." Rappers Posdnuos and Trugoy the Dove talked about anything they wanted (up to and including <a href="/wiki/Body_odor" title="Body odor">body odor</a>), playing fast and loose on the mic like Biz Markie. Thinly disguised under a layer of humor, their lyrical themes ranged from true love ("<a href="/wiki/Eye_Know" title="Eye Know">Eye Know</a>") to the destructive power of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drugs" title="Drugs">drugs</a> ("Say No Go") to Daisy Age philosophy ("Tread Water") to <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sex</a> ("Buddy"). <a href="/wiki/Prince_Paul_(producer)" title="Prince Paul (producer)">Prince Paul</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Stetsasonic" title="Stetsasonic">Stetsasonic</a>) and DJ Pasemaster Mase led the way on the production end, with dozens of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Music_sampling" title="Music sampling">samples</a> from all sorts of left-field artists—including <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Cash" title="Johnny Cash">Johnny Cash</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Mad_Lads" title="The Mad Lads">The Mad Lads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steely_Dan" title="Steely Dan">Steely Dan</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(group)" title="Public Enemy (group)">Public Enemy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hall_%26_Oates" title="Hall &amp; Oates">Hall &amp; Oates</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Turtles" title="The Turtles">the Turtles</a>. The pair didn't just use those samples as <a href="/wiki/Hook_(music)" title="Hook (music)">hooks</a> or drumbreaks—like most hip-hop producers had in the past—but as split-second fills and in-jokes that made some tracks sound more like DJ records. Even "Potholes on My Lawn," which samples a mouth harp and yodeling (for the chorus, no less), became a big R&amp;B hit. If it was easy to believe the revolution was here from listening to the rapping and production on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(group)" title="Public Enemy (group)">Public Enemy</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/It_Takes_a_Nation_of_Millions_to_Hold_Us_Back" title="It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back">It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back</a></i>, with De La Soul the Daisy Age seemed to promise a new era of positivity in hip-hop.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— Jon Bush</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mainstream_decline">Mainstream decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_hip_hop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mainstream decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Contrary to <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a>, which went on to become a mainstay in mainstream music and replaced the <a href="/wiki/Glam_metal" title="Glam metal">glam metal</a> of the previous generation as the most popular form of rock music, alternative hip hop's commercial momentum was impeded by the then also newly emerging, significantly harder-edged West Coast <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erlewine_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Erlewine-2">[2]</a></sup> With its aggressive tone, nihilistic tendencies, and violent imagery, gangsta rap was considered to be the more entertaining, more lucrative subgenre as signified by the high chart placings, radio success and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-platinum" title="Multi-platinum">multiplatinum</a>-selling records of gangsta rappers such as <a href="/wiki/Snoop_Dogg" title="Snoop Dogg">Snoop Dogg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warren_G" title="Warren G">Warren G</a> and <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>, who were widely embraced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_record_label" title="Major record label">major record labels</a> and produced a legion of imitators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erlewine_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Erlewine-2">[2]</a></sup> Albums such as <i><a href="/wiki/Straight_Outta_Compton" title="Straight Outta Compton">Straight Outta Compton</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronic" title="The Chronic">The Chronic</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Doggystyle" title="Doggystyle">Doggystyle</a></i> redefined the direction of hip hop, which resulted in lyricism concerning the gangsta lifestyle becoming the driving force of sales figures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caramanica_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Caramanica-3">[3]</a></sup> The situation <a href="/wiki/Illmatic#Decline_of_alternative_hip_hop" title="Illmatic">broke way</a> around the mid-90s with the emergence and mainstream popularity of East Coast <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hardcore_rap" title="Hardcore rap">hardcore rap</a> artists such as <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nas" title="Nas">Nas</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_B.I.G." title="The Notorious B.I.G.">The Notorious B.I.G.</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mobb_Deep" title="Mobb Deep">Mobb Deep</a>. Both West Coast gangsta rap and East Coast hardcore and their many derivatives subsequently became more prominent in <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">popular music</a>, whereas alt-rap became largely relegated to the <a href="/wiki/Underground_hip_hop" title="Underground hip hop">underground scene</a>. Following this development, many alternative rap acts eventually either disbanded or faded into obscurity.</p>
<p>In his 1995 book on the current state of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hip_hop_culture" title="Hip hop culture">hip hop culture</a>, music critic Stephen Rodrick wrote that, at that time, alternative hip-hop had "drawn little more than barely concealed yawns from other rappers and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_music" title="Urban music">urban</a> audiences" and came to the conclusion that the subgenre was a complete failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodrick_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rodrick-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revival">Revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_hip_hop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>However, a resurgence came about in the late 1990s and early 2000s with the rejuvenated interest in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indie_music" title="Indie music">indie music</a> by the general public. Since the mid-90's, <a href="/wiki/Independent_record_label" title="Independent record label">independent record labels</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Rawkus_Records" title="Rawkus Records">Rawkus Records</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhymesayers" title="Rhymesayers">Rhymesayers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anticon" title="Anticon">Anticon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stones_Throw_Records" title="Stones Throw Records">Stones Throw</a> and <a href="/wiki/Definitive_Jux" title="Definitive Jux">Definitive Jux</a> have experienced lesser mainstream success with alternative rap acts such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daniel_Dumile" title="Daniel Dumile">MF DOOM</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(hip_hop_group)" title="Atmosphere (hip hop group)">Atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antipop_Consortium" title="Antipop Consortium">Antipop Consortium</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Star_(hip_hop_group)" title="Black Star (hip hop group)">Black Star</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gorillaz" title="Gorillaz">Gorillaz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pharoahe_Monch" title="Pharoahe Monch">Pharoahe Monch</a>, <a href="/wiki/El-P" title="El-P">El-P</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aesop_Rock" title="Aesop Rock">Aesop Rock</a>. It was in the 2000s that alternative hip hop reattained its place within the mainstream, due in part to the declining commercial viability of gangsta rap as well as the crossover success of artists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OutKast" title="OutKast">OutKast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gnarls_Barkley" title="Gnarls Barkley">Gnarls Barkley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Not only did OutKast's fifth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Speakerboxxx/The_Love_Below" title="Speakerboxxx/The Love Below">Speakerboxxx/The Love Below</a></i> receive universal acclaim from music critics and manage to appeal listeners of all ages spanning numerous musical genres but also spawned two number-one hit singles. The album eventually went on to win a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Album_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Album of the Year">Album of the Year</a>—making it only the second hip hop album to win the award—and has been <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">certified</a> <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">diamond</a> by selling 11 times <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">RIAA</a> for shipping more than 11 million units, becoming one of the best selling albums of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gnarls Barkley experienced a surprise hit with their debut single "<a href="/wiki/Crazy_(Gnarls_Barkley_song)" title="Crazy (Gnarls Barkley song)">Crazy</a>". Due to high download sales, it reached number-one of the single charts in several countries, including the United Kingdom, where it became the best selling single of 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The song was named the best song of 2006 by both <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Village_Voice" title="Village Voice">Village Voice</a>'</i>s annual <a href="/wiki/Pazz_%26_Jop" title="Pazz &amp; Jop">Pazz &amp; Jop</a> critics poll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pazz_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pazz-9">[9]</a></sup> <i>Rolling Stone</i> later ranked "Crazy" as the number-one song of the entire decade. The song has since sold over two million copies in the United States alone and has been certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">double platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The duo were the recipient of multiple accolades, winning at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/49th_Grammy_Awards" title="49th Grammy Awards">49th Grammy Awards</a> a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Urban/Alternative_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Urban/Alternative Performance">Grammy Award for Best Urban/Alternative Performance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Alternative_Music_Album" title="Grammy Award for Best Alternative Music Album">Best Alternative Music Album</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Industry observers view the 2007 <a href="/wiki/Graduation_(album)#Release_and_promotion" title="Graduation (album)">sales competition</a> between Kanye West's <i><a href="/wiki/Graduation_(album)" title="Graduation (album)">Graduation</a></i> and 50 Cent's <i><a href="/wiki/Curtis_(50_Cent_album)" title="Curtis (50 Cent album)">Curtis</a></i> as a turning point for hip hop. West emerged the victor, selling nearly a million copies in the first week alone. Ben Detrick of <i><a href="/wiki/XXL_(magazine)" title="XXL (magazine)">XXL</a></i> cited the outcome of the sales competition as being responsible for altering the direction of hip hop and paving the way for new rappers who didn't follow the <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_hip_hop" title="Hardcore hip hop">hardcore</a>-<a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangster</a> mold, writing, "If there was ever a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/watershed" title="wiktionary:watershed">watershed</a> moment to indicate hip hop's changing direction, it may have come when 50 Cent competed with Kanye in 2007 to see whose album would claim superior sales. 50 lost handily, and it was made clear that excellent songcrafting trumped a street-life experience. Kanye led a wave of new artists—<a href="/wiki/Kid_Cudi" title="Kid Cudi">Kid Cudi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wale_(rapper)" title="Wale (rapper)">Wale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lupe_Fiasco" title="Lupe Fiasco">Lupe Fiasco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kidz_in_the_Hall" title="Kidz in the Hall">Kidz in the Hall</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drake_(entertainer)" title="Drake (entertainer)">Drake</a>—who lacked the interest or ability to create narratives about any past gunplay or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drug-dealing" title="Drug-dealing">drug-dealing</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-XXL_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-XXL-12">[12]</a></sup> Similarly, in a retrospective article, Rosie Swash of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> viewed the album's sales competition with 50 Cent's <i>Curtis</i> as a historical moment in hip hop, writing that it "highlighted the diverging facets of hip-hop in the last decade; the former was gangsta rap for the noughties, while West was the thinking man's alternative."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The alternative hip hop movement is not limited solely to the United States, as genre-defying rappers such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somali_people" title="Somali people">Somali</a>-Canadian poet <a href="/wiki/K%27naan" title="K'naan">K'naan</a>, Japanese rapper <a href="/wiki/Shing02" title="Shing02">Shing02</a>, and especially British artist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M.I.A._(artist)" title="M.I.A. (artist)">MIA</a> have achieved considerable worldwide recognition. K'naan's 2009 single <a href="/wiki/Wavin%27_Flag" title="Wavin' Flag">Wavin' Flag</a> reached number two on the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Hot_100" title="Canadian Hot 100">Canadian Hot 100</a> while its various remixes topped the charts in several countries. Shing02 was chosen for rapping "Battlecry", the theme song of the hit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hip-hop_culture" title="Hip-hop culture">hip-hop</a>-influenced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chanbara" title="Chanbara">chanbara</a> anime <i><a href="/wiki/Samurai_Champloo" title="Samurai Champloo">Samurai Champloo</a></i>, which was produced by Japanese <a href="/wiki/Jazz_rap" title="Jazz rap">jazz rap</a> DJ <a href="/wiki/Nujabes" title="Nujabes">Nujabes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine placed M.I.A in the <a href="/wiki/Time_100" title="Time 100">Time 100</a> list of "World's Most Influential people" for having "global influence across many genres."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-influential_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-influential-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Groups like the British animated band <a href="/wiki/Gorillaz" title="Gorillaz">Gorillaz</a> also experienced mainstream popularization during this period of time, selling over 20 million albums total between the albums <i><a href="/wiki/Gorillaz_(album)" title="Gorillaz (album)">Gorillaz</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Demon_Days" title="Demon Days">Demon Days</a>.</i> Today, due in part to the increasing use of <a href="/wiki/Music_download" title="Music download">music distribution</a> through the internet, many alternative rap artists are able to find acceptance by far-reaching audiences. Several burgeoning artists and groups such as <a href="/wiki/Lupe_Fiasco" title="Lupe Fiasco">Lupe Fiasco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kid_Cudi" title="Kid Cudi">Kid Cudi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wale_(rapper)" title="Wale (rapper)">Wale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chiddy_Bang" title="Chiddy Bang">Chiddy Bang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hoodie_Allen" title="Hoodie Allen">Hoodie Allen</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Cool_Kids" title="The Cool Kids">The Cool Kids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hamilton_(rapper)" title="Charles Hamilton (rapper)">Charles Hamilton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asher_Roth" title="Asher Roth">Asher Roth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chance_the_Rapper" title="Chance the Rapper">Chance the Rapper</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Childish_Gambino" title="Childish Gambino">Childish Gambino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danny!" title="Danny!">Danny!</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dels_(musician)" title="Dels (musician)">DELS</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N.E.R.D." title="N.E.R.D.">N.E.R.D.</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OFWGKTA" title="OFWGKTA">OFWGKTA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yelawolf" title="Yelawolf">Yelawolf</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._Cole" title="J. Cole">J. Cole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghostpoet" title="Ghostpoet">Ghostpoet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danny_Brown" title="Danny Brown">Danny Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blu_(rapper)" title="Blu (rapper)">Blu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rockie_Fresh" title="Rockie Fresh">Rockie Fresh</a>, <a href="/wiki/SpaceGhostPurrp" title="SpaceGhostPurrp">SpaceGhostPurrp</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Swank&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Swank (page does not exist)">The Swank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flatbush_Zombies" title="Flatbush Zombies">Flatbush Zombies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Death_Grips" title="Death Grips">Death Grips</a> openly acknowledge being directly influenced by their '90s alt-rap predecessors in addition to alt-rock groups while their music has been noted by critics as expressing eclectic sounds, life experiences and emotions rarely seen in mainstream hip hop. Also <a href="/wiki/Ratking_(group)" title="Ratking (group)">Ratking</a> recently signed to <a href="/wiki/XL_Recordings" title="XL Recordings">XL Recordings</a> style is infused with 90s hip hop and punk from that era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soul_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Soul-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_and_cultural_reactions">Critical and cultural reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_hip_hop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critical and cultural reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to its emphasis on abstracted artistry, <a href="/wiki/Experimental_music" title="Experimental music">experimental</a> sonancy, and subversive lyricism, alternative hip hop is frequently the recipient of critical acclaim but is generally shunned by media outlets and viewed as a financial liability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Erlewine_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Erlewine-2">[2]</a></sup> Rapper-singer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Q-Tip_(rapper)" title="Q-Tip (rapper)">Q-Tip</a>, frontman of the highly influential alternative rap group A Tribe Called Quest, had his sophomore solo effort <i><a href="/wiki/Kamaal/The_Abstract" title="Kamaal/The Abstract">Kamaal/The Abstract</a></i> shelved for nearly a decade after his record label deemed the genre-bending album as sounding uncommercial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Q-Tip was quoted as saying:</p>
<table class="cquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">I am really disappointed that <i>Kamaal</i> wasn't released. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LA_Reid" title="LA Reid">LA Reid</a> didn't know what to do with it; then, three years later, they release <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OutKast" title="OutKast">OutKast</a>. What OutKast is doing now, those are the kinds of sounds that are on <i>Kamaal the Abstract</i>. Maybe even a little more out. <i>Kamaal</i> was just me, guerrilla.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<p>Similarly, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Entertainment_Television" title="Black Entertainment Television">BET</a> infamously refused to play "Lovin' It", the <a href="/wiki/Lead_single" title="Lead single">lead single</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>-based alt-rap duo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Little_Brother_(band)" title="Little Brother (band)">Little Brother</a>'s socio-politically charged <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Minstrel_Show" title="The Minstrel Show">The Minstrel Show</a></i>, which provided a tongue-in-cheek critique of African-American pop culture, on the grounds that the group's music was "too intelligent" for their target audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOHH-Intelligent_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-SOHH-Intelligent-21">[21]</a></sup> The network was subsequently satirized by the animated series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boondocks_(TV_series)" title="The Boondocks (TV series)">The Boondocks</a></i> – which regularly features underground/alternative rap as <a href="/wiki/Background_music" title="Background music">background music</a> – in the banned episode <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hunger_Strike" title="The Hunger Strike">The Hunger Strike</a></i>. The episode, which portrayed BET as an evil organization dedicated to the self-genocidal mission of eradicating black people through violent, overtly sexual programming, was banned by <a href="/wiki/Cartoon_Network" title="Cartoon Network">Cartoon Network</a> and has yet to be aired in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Some alternative rap groups tend to be embraced primarily by alternative rock and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indie_music" title="Indie music">indie music</a> fans, rather than hip hop or pop audiences. Alternative hip hop has gained more fans and recognition following the internet age. Online magazines like <a href="/wiki/The_Fader" title="The Fader">The FADER</a> tend to embrace alternative hip hop more than mainstream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allmusic_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
