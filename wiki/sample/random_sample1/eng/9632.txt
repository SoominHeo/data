<p><b>Business continuity planning</b> (or <b>business continuity and resiliency planning</b>) is the process of creating systems of prevention and recovery to deal with potential threats to a company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Any event that could negatively impact operations is included in the plan, such as <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain" title="Supply chain">supply chain</a> interruption, loss of or damage to critical infrastructure (major machinery or computing /network resource). As such, BCP is a subset of <a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">risk management</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>In the US, government entities refer to the process as <i>continuity of operations planning</i> (COOP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_Standards">Current Standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Current Standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In December 2006, the <a href="/wiki/BSI_Group" title="BSI Group">British Standards Institution</a> (BSI) released an independent standard for BCP — BS 25999-1. Prior to the introduction of <a href="/wiki/BS_25999" title="BS 25999">BS 25999</a>, BCP professionals relied on information security standard <a href="/wiki/BS_7799" title="BS 7799">BS 7799</a>, which only peripherally addressed BCP to improve an organization's information security procedures. BS 25999's applicability extends to all organizations. In 2007, the BSI published BS 25999-2 "Specification for Business Continuity Management", which specifies requirements for implementing, operating and improving a documented business continuity management system (BCMS).</p>
<p>Business continuity management is standardized across the UK by British Standards (BS) through BS 25999-2:2007 and BS 25999-1:2006. BS 25999-2:2007 business continuity management is the British Standard for business continuity management across all organizations. This includes industry and its sectors. The standard provides a best practice framework to minimize disruption during unexpected events that could bring business to a standstill. The document gives a practical plan to deal with most eventualities – from extreme weather conditions to terrorism, IT system failure, and staff sickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>This document was superseded in November 2012 by the British standard BS ISO22301:2012, the current standard for business continuity planning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_Contingencies_Act">Civil Contingencies Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Civil Contingencies Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, following crises in the preceding years, the UK government passed the Civil Contingencies Act 2004 (The Act). This provides the legislation for civil protection in the UK: Businesses need to have continuity planning measures in place in order to survive and continue to thrive whilst working towards keeping the incident as minimal as possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Act was separated into two parts: Part 1 focuses on local arrangements for civil protection, establishing a statutory framework of roles and responsibilities for local responders. Part 2 focused on emergency powers, establishing a modern framework for the use of special legislative measures that might be necessary to deal with the effects of the most serious emergency.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis">Analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The analysis phase consists of impact analysis, threat analysis and impact scenarios.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_impact_analysis_.28BIA.29">Business impact analysis (BIA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Business impact analysis (BIA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A Business impact analysis (BIA) differentiates critical (urgent) and non-critical (non-urgent) organization functions/activities. Critical functions are those whose disruption is regarded as unacceptable. Perceptions of acceptability are affected by the cost of recovery solutions. A function may also be considered critical if dictated by law. For each critical (in scope) function, two values are then assigned:</p>
<ul>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recovery_Point_Objective" title="Recovery Point Objective">Recovery Point Objective</a> (RPO) – the acceptable latency of data that will not be recovered. For example is it acceptable for the company to lose 2 days of data?<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recovery_Time_Objective" title="Recovery Time Objective">Recovery Time Objective</a> (RTO)  – the acceptable amount of time to restore the function.</li>
</ul>
<p>The recovery point objective must ensure that the maximum tolerable data loss for each activity is not exceeded. The recovery time objective must ensure that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_Tolerable_Period_of_Disruption" title="Maximum Tolerable Period of Disruption">Maximum Tolerable Period of Disruption</a> (MTPoD) for each activity is not exceeded.</p>
<p>Next, the impact analysis results in the recovery requirements for each critical function. Recovery requirements consist of the following information:</p>
<ul>
<li>The business requirements for recovery of the critical function, and/or</li>
<li>The technical requirements for recovery of the critical function</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threat_and_risk_analysis_.28TRA.29">Threat and risk analysis (TRA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Threat and risk analysis (TRA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After defining recovery requirements, each potential threat may require unique recovery steps. Common threats include:</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-count column-count-3" style="-moz-column-count: 3; -webkit-column-count: 3; column-count: 3;">
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Epidemic" title="Epidemic">Epidemic</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">Earthquake</a></li>
<li>Fire</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">Flood</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hacker_(computer_security)" title="Hacker (computer security)">Cyber attack</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sabotage" title="Sabotage">Sabotage</a> (insider or external threat)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">Hurricane</a> or other major storm</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">Power outage</a></li>
<li>Water outage (supply interruption, contamination)</li>
<li>Telecomms outage</li>
<li>IT outage</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">Terrorism</a>/<a href="/wiki/Piracy" title="Piracy">Piracy</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/War" title="War">War</a>/civil disorder</li>
<li>Theft (insider or external threat, vital information or material)</li>
<li>Random failure of mission-critical systems</li>
</ul>
</div>
<p>The impact of an epidemic can be regarded as purely human, and may be alleviated with technical and business solutions. However, if people behind these plans are affected by the disease, then the process can stumble.</p>
<p>During the 2002–2003 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SARS" title="SARS">SARS</a> outbreak, some organizations grouped staff into separate teams, and rotated the teams between primary and secondary work sites, with a rotation frequency equal to the <a href="/wiki/Incubation_period" title="Incubation period">incubation period</a> of the disease. The organizations also banned face-to-face intergroup contact during business and non-business hours. The split increased <a href="/wiki/Resilience_(organizational)" title="Resilience (organizational)">resiliency</a> against the threat of <a href="/wiki/Quarantine" title="Quarantine">quarantine</a> measures if one person in a team was exposed to the disease.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_scenarios">Impact scenarios</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Impact scenarios">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After identifying the applicable threats, impact scenarios are considered to support the development of a business recovery plan. Business continuity testing plans may document scenarios for each identified threats and impact scenarios. More localized impact scenarios – for example loss of a specific floor in a building – may also be documented. The BC plans should reflect the requirements to recover the business in the widest possible damage. The risk assessment should cater to developing impact scenarios that are applicable to the business or the premises it operates. For example, it might not be logical to consider tsunami in the region of Mideast since the likelihood of such a threat is negligible.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recovery_requirement">Recovery requirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recovery requirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the analysis phase, business and technical recovery requirements precede the solutions phase. Asset inventories allow for quick identification of deployable resources. For an office-based, IT-intensive business, the plan requirements may cover desks, human resources, applications, data, manual workarounds, computers and peripherals. Other business environments, such as production, distribution, warehousing etc. will need to cover these elements, but likely have additional issues.</p>
<p>The robustness of an <a href="/wiki/Emergency_management" title="Emergency management">emergency management</a> plan is dependent on how much money an organization or business can place into the plan. The organization must balance realistic feasibility with the need to properly prepare. In general, every $1 put into an emergency management plan will prevent $7 of loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_design">Solution design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Solution design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The solution design phase identifies the most cost-effective <a href="/wiki/Disaster_recovery" title="Disaster recovery">disaster recovery</a> solution that meets two main requirements from the impact analysis stage.</p>
<p>For IT purposes, this is commonly expressed as the minimum application and data requirements and the time in which the minimum application and application data must be available.</p>
<p>Outside the IT domain, preservation of hard copy information, such as contracts, skilled staff or restoration of embedded technology in a process plant must be considered. This phase overlaps with <a href="/wiki/Disaster_recovery" title="Disaster recovery">disaster recovery planning</a> methodology. The solution phase determines:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crisis_management" title="Crisis management">crisis management</a> command structure</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Backup_site" title="Backup site">secondary work sites</a></li>
<li>telecommunication architecture between primary and secondary work sites</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Data_replication" title="Data replication">data replication</a> methodology between primary and secondary work sites</li>
<li>applications and data required at the secondary work site</li>
<li>physical data requirements at the secondary work site.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation">Implementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Implementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The implementation phase involves policy changes, material acquisitions, staffing and testing.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing_and_organizational_acceptance">Testing and organizational acceptance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Testing and organizational acceptance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The purpose of testing is to achieve organizational acceptance that the solution satisfies the recovery requirements. Plans may fail to meet expectations due to insufficient or inaccurate recovery requirements, solution design flaws or solution implementation errors. Testing may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Crisis command team call-out testing</li>
<li>Technical swing test from primary to secondary work locations</li>
<li>Technical swing test from secondary to primary work locations</li>
<li>Application test</li>
<li>Business process test</li>
</ul>
<p>At minimum, testing is conducted on a biannual schedule.</p>
<p>The 2008 book <i>Exercising for Excellence</i>, published by The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Standards_Institution" title="British Standards Institution">British Standards Institution</a> identified three types of exercises that can be employed when testing business continuity plans.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tabletop_exercises">Tabletop exercises</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tabletop exercises">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tabletop exercises typically involve a small number of people and concentrates on a specific aspect of a BCP. They can easily accommodate complete teams from a specific area of a business.</p>
<p>Another form involves a single representative from each of several teams. Typically, participants work through simple scenario and then discuss specific aspects of the plan. For example, a fire is discovered out of working hours.</p>
<p>The exercise consumes only a few hours and is often split into two or three sessions, each concentrating on a different theme.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medium_exercises">Medium exercises</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Medium exercises">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A medium exercise is conducted within a "Virtual World" and brings together several departments, teams or disciplines. It typically concentrates on multiple BCP aspects, prompting interaction between teams. The scope of a medium exercise can range from a few teams from one organisation co-located in one building to multiple teams operating across dispersed locations. The environment needs to be as realistic as practicable and team sizes should reflect a realistic situation. Realism may extend to simulated news broadcasts and websites.</p>
<p>A medium exercise typically lasts a few hours, though they can extend over several days. They typically involve a "Scenario Cell" that adds pre-scripted "surprises" throughout the exercise.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Complex_exercises">Complex exercises</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Complex exercises">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A complex exercise aims to have as few boundaries as possible. It incorporates all the aspects of a medium exercise. The exercise remains within a virtual world, but maximum realism is essential. This might include no-notice activation, actual evacuation and actual invocation of a disaster recovery site.</p>
<p>While start and stop times are pre-agreed, the actual duration might be unknown if events are allowed to run their course.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance">Maintenance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Maintenance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Biannual or annual maintenance cycle maintenance of a BCP manual is broken down into three periodic activities.</p>
<ul>
<li>Confirmation of information in the manual, roll out to staff for awareness and specific training for critical individuals.</li>
<li>Testing and verification of technical solutions established for recovery operations.</li>
<li>Testing and verification of organization recovery procedures.</li>
</ul>
<p>Issues found during the testing phase often must be reintroduced to the analysis phase.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Information.2Ftargets">Information/targets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Information/targets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The BCP manual must evolve with the organization. Activating the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Call_tree&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Call tree (page does not exist)">call tree</a> verifies the notification plan's efficiency as well as contact data accuracy. Like most business procedures, business continuity planning has its own jargon. Organisation-wide understanding of business continuity jargon is vital and glossaries are available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Types of organisational changes that should be identified and updated in the manual include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Staffing</li>
<li>Important clients</li>
<li>Vendors/suppliers</li>
<li>Organization structure changes</li>
<li>Company investment portfolio and mission statement</li>
<li>Communication and transportation infrastructure such as roads and bridges</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical">Technical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Technical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Specialized technical resources must be maintained. Checks include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Computer_virus" title="Computer virus">Virus</a> definition distribution</li>
<li>Application security and service patch distribution</li>
<li>Hardware operability</li>
<li>Application operability</li>
<li>Data verification</li>
<li>Data application</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing_and_verification_of_recovery_procedures">Testing and verification of recovery procedures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Business_continuity_planning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Testing and verification of recovery procedures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As work processes change, previous recovery procedures may no longer be suitable. Checks include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Are all work processes for critical functions documented?</li>
<li>Have the systems used for critical functions changed?</li>
<li>Are the documented work checklists meaningful and accurate?</li>
<li>Do the documented work process recovery tasks and supporting disaster recovery infrastructure allow staff to recover within the predetermined recovery time objective?</li>
</ul>
