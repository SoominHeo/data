<p><b>Krisztina Egerszegi</b> (<small>Hungarian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Hungarian" title="Help:IPA for Hungarian">[ˈkristinɒ ˈɛɡɛrsɛɡi]</a></span>; born 16 August 1974) is a <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungarian</a> former world record holding swimmer and one of the greatest Hungarian Olympic champions of the modern era. She is a three-time Olympian (<a href="/wiki/Hungary_at_the_1988_Summer_Olympics" title="Hungary at the 1988 Summer Olympics">1988</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary_at_the_1992_Summer_Olympics" title="Hungary at the 1992 Summer Olympics">1992</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hungary_at_the_1996_Summer_Olympics" title="Hungary at the 1996 Summer Olympics">1996</a>) and five time Olympic champion; and one of three individuals (<a href="/wiki/Dawn_Fraser" title="Dawn Fraser">Dawn Fraser</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Phelps" title="Michael Phelps">Michael Phelps</a> being the other two) to have ever won the same swimming event at three consecutive Summer Olympics. She is the most successful and greatest individual female swimmer ever, having won five individual Olympic gold medals, the only female swimmer to do so. Katie Ledecky of the United States matched her total of 5 Olympic gold medals, winning 1 individual gold in London 2012, 3 individual ones and one team gold in Rio in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sr_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-sr-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olympic1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-olympic1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Egerszegi held the <a href="/wiki/List_of_world_records_in_swimming" title="List of world records in swimming">world record</a> in the long course 200 m backstroke for almost 17 years.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Krisztina_Egerszegi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Egerszegi made her international debut at the <a href="/wiki/1987_European_Aquatics_Championships" title="1987 European Aquatics Championships">1987 European Aquatics Championships</a> at the age of 13, coming fourth in the 200 m backstroke and fifth in the 100 m backstroke. At the <a href="/wiki/1988_Summer_Olympics" title="1988 Summer Olympics">1988 Summer Olympics</a> she won silver medal in the 100 m backstroke and became Olympic champion in the 200 m backstroke. At the age of 14 years and 41 days she became the youngest-ever female Olympic champion in swimming. This youth record was broken in 1992 by <a href="/wiki/Kyoko_Iwasaki" title="Kyoko Iwasaki">Kyoko Iwasaki</a> of Japan, who won a gold medal in the 200 meter breaststroke at the 1992 Barcelona Olympic Games at the age of 14 years and six days. At the end of 1988, she was the top-ranked swimmer in 200 m backstroke in the world ranking as well as she became the Swimmer of the Year and the Best Female Athlete of the Year in Hungary.</p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/1989_European_Aquatics_Championships" title="1989 European Aquatics Championships">1989 European Aquatics Championships</a> she competed in three events adding the 400 m medley to the 100 m and 200 m backstroke, winning silver medal in all three events despite she struggled with a strong cold during the entire Championships. At the end of that year, she topped again the world ranking in 200 m backstroke and she was selected again the Best Female Athlete of the Year in Hungary.</p>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/1990_Goodwill_Games" title="1990 Goodwill Games">1990 Goodwill Games</a> in Seattle, she won the Hungarian team's only gold and silver medal (first in 200 m backstroke, second in 100 m backstroke), with which Hungary finished 16th in the overall standings. At the Hungarian Championships, which was held in December due to the <a href="/wiki/1991_World_Aquatics_Championships" title="1991 World Aquatics Championships">World Championships</a> in Australia she won 12 gold medals. Egerszegi finished the year of 1990 again as the Best Swimmer and as the Best Female Athlete of Hungary.</p>
<p>In 1991 she competed at the <a href="/wiki/1991_World_Aquatics_Championships" title="1991 World Aquatics Championships">World Championships</a> in Perth, Australia winning both backstroke events (100 and 200 metres) and becoming the first Hungarian female swimmer of all-time who won gold medals at the World Championships. A few months later at the <a href="/wiki/1991_European_Aquatics_Championships" title="1991 European Aquatics Championships">European Championships</a> in Athens, Greece, she won three gold medals and set world records in the 100 m (1:00.31 min) and 200 m (2:06.62 min) backstroke events. Eleven years after <a href="/wiki/Rica_Reinisch" title="Rica Reinisch">Rica Reinisch</a>, Egerszegi was the first female swimmer since 1980, who set world records on both 100 m and 200 m at the same event. Since 1983 (when <a href="/wiki/Rick_Carey" title="Rick Carey">Rick Carey</a> completed this feat in Clovis, USA), Egerszegi was the first swimmer who broke both world records of the backstroke at the same event. At the Hungarian championships, she added 10 gold medals to her unique collection. In December, she won her 4 consecutive awards as the Best Swimmer and the Best Athlete of the Year in Hungary. The Italian Gazzetta dello Sport has been voted her as the world's second best athlete.</p>
<p>In April 1992, along with the 2 time Olympic champion sprint canoer <a href="/wiki/Zsolt_Gyulay" title="Zsolt Gyulay">Zsolt Gyulay</a> has administered the official Olympic oath on behalf of the Hungarian Olympic Team and athletes. At the Hungarian national championships, she collected 8 more gold medals. At the <a href="/wiki/1992_Summer_Olympics" title="1992 Summer Olympics">1992 Summer Olympics</a> in Barcelona, Spain, she won three individual gold medals, becoming the only female athlete at the Games to do so. End of 1992, she got the most votes again at the Best Swimmer and Best Athlete of the Year selection. The Gazzetta dello Sport has been top-ranked her as the Best Female Athlete of the Year.</p>
<p>In 1993, she claimed 10 first places at the Hungarian National Championships then at the <a href="/wiki/1993_European_Aquatics_Championships" title="1993 European Aquatics Championships">European Championships</a> in Sheffield, England, she competed in the 200 m butterfly for the first time and went on to win four gold medals also winning the 400 m medley and the 100 m and 200 m backstroke. In December, she was selected the Best Swimmer and Best Athlete of the Year in Hungary, while the <i>Gazzetta dello Sport</i> and the <i>L'Equipe</i> voted her as the best female athlete of the year.</p>
<p>In 1994, she won 9 gold medals at the Hungarian Championship and then she announced that she would retire after the <a href="/wiki/1994_World_Aquatics_Championships" title="1994 World Aquatics Championships">World Championships</a> in Rome which was overshadowed by the suspicious Chinese swimmers' performances. China won 12 of the 16 women's titles, but these achievements were sullied less than a month later when seven Chinese swimmers tested positive for banned drugs at the Asian Games in Hiroshima.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In Rome, Egerszegi lost the 100 m and 200 m backstroke, both events were won by <a href="/wiki/He_Cihong" title="He Cihong">He Cihong</a>, who was only 13th (1:03.50) on 100 m backstroke two years earlier in Barcelona, where Egerszegi won in an Olympic record time of 1:00.68. In 1996, at the Olympic Games in Atlanta, He Cihong was 25th on the 100 m backstroke. On the 200 m backstroke, He Cihong qualified neither for Barcelona, nor Atlanta; both Olympic golds were won by Egerszegi. After getting fifth place in the 100 m backstroke and coming second in the 200 m backstroke, Egerszegi decided to compete for two more years, citing the two defeats as the main reason.</p>
<p>At the Hungarian Championship in 1995, she claimed 9 gold medals. She competed at the <a href="/wiki/1995_European_Aquatics_Championships" title="1995 European Aquatics Championships">1995 European Aquatics Championships</a> where she won the last two of her nine European titles in the 400 m medley and the 200 m backstroke event. For the first time, she competed in the 4×100 m medley relay where one of her teammates was a young <a href="/wiki/%C3%81gnes_Kov%C3%A1cs" title="Ágnes Kovács">Ágnes Kovács</a>, a future Olympic champion. They came second and Egerszegi has named this silver medal as 'the one that made her the happiest'. She decided not to compete in the 100 m backstroke even though her time of 1:00.93 clocked during the 4×100 m relay final was better than <a href="/wiki/Mette_Jacobsen" title="Mette Jacobsen">Mette Jacobsen</a>'s winning time of 1:02.46 by almost two seconds.</p>
<p>In 1996, she closed her final appearance at the Hungarian National Championships with collecting 9 gold medals. In Atlanta, at the Centennial Olympic Games, although Egerszegi advanced into the final with the best time on 400 individual medley, she finished in third. This was her first and only bronze medal during her Olympic career. She competed in the 4×100 m medley relay as member of the Hungarian team, who finished in 11th. Interestingly, her time in this event at the qualification round, would have won the 100 m backstroke event. Her final career appearance was one of the finest farewell of all-time in swimming sport: she won her beloved 200 m backstroke event with the greatest margin in any short distance events of the swimming sport, collecting her fifth individual gold medals and defending her titles for three consecutive Olympic Games.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_greatest_margin">The greatest margin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Krisztina_Egerszegi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The greatest margin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/1996_Summer_Olympics" title="1996 Summer Olympics">1996 Summer Olympics</a> she won her first and only Olympic bronze medal in the 400 m medley and went on to win the 200 m backstroke becoming the second of only three swimmers in Olympic history (Dawn Fraser and Michael Phelps being the other two) to win gold for the same event at three successive Olympics (200 m backstroke: 1988, 1992, 1996). Between the winning time of Egerszegi (2:07.83) and time of the runner-up <a href="/wiki/Whitney_Hedgepeth" title="Whitney Hedgepeth">Whitney Hedgepeth</a> (2:11.98), the margin of victory was 4.15 seconds, which is the greatest in any women's 200 m event in the swimming history. In 1996, Egerszegi did not enter the 100 m backstroke, yet her leadoff backstroke time in the medley relay, 1:01.15, was faster than the winning time in the 100 m backstroke final.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-olympic1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-olympic1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Between 1988 and 1996 she won 5 individual Olympic <a href="/wiki/Gold_medal" title="Gold medal">gold medals</a>, which was a record for a swimmer for individual gold medal wins. This record has since been broken by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Phelps" title="Michael Phelps">Michael Phelps</a>, who has won 13 individual gold medals. Egerszegi still holds the record with 5 individual Olympic <a href="/wiki/Gold_medal" title="Gold medal">gold medals</a> as the most of any female swimmer in the history of the sport.</p>
<p>Egerszegi announced her retirement from swimming soon after the Olympic games, at the age of only 22. In the same year, she became the board member of the Hungarian Swimming Association and in 2007 the member of the Hungarian Olympic Committee as well as she was named for 9th times as the Best Swimmer of the Year and for 7th times as the Best Female Athlete of the Year.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Records">Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Krisztina_Egerszegi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Egerszegi won the 100 m backstroke at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_LC_Championships_1991" title="European LC Championships 1991">1991 European Championships</a> in Athens setting her first world record with 1:00:31. She broke <a href="/wiki/Ina_Kleber" title="Ina Kleber">Ina Kleber</a>'s time (1:00:59) from 1984. Three days later, Egerszegi broke Betsy Mitchell's five-years-old record (2:08.60) in the 200 m backstroke, setting her second world record at 2:06.62 min. She held this <a href="/wiki/List_of_world_records_in_swimming" title="List of world records in swimming">world record</a> in the long course 200 m backstroke for 16 and half years (25 August 1991 – 16 February 2008). Her record in 200 m backstroke remained the oldest <a href="/wiki/List_of_European_records_in_swimming" title="List of European records in swimming">European</a> record until 1 August 2009, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anastasia_Valeryevna_Zuyeva" title="Anastasia Valeryevna Zuyeva">Anastasia Zuyeva</a> (RUS) broke it at <a href="/wiki/2009_World_Aquatics_Championships" title="2009 World Aquatics Championships">2009 World Aquatics Championships</a> in Rome. Egerszegi set her first Olympic record on 200 m backstroke with 2:09.29 in 1988, which she improved in 1992 (2:07.06). This record was broken in 2008 at the <a href="/wiki/Swimming_at_the_2008_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Women%27s_200_metre_backstroke" title="Swimming at the 2008 Summer Olympics – Women's 200 metre backstroke">Beijing Olympics</a> by <a href="/wiki/Kirsty_Coventry" title="Kirsty Coventry">Kirsty Coventry</a>. Egerszegi set the Olympic record on 100 backstroke with 1:00.68 in 1992, which was broken by <a href="/wiki/Diana_Mocanu" title="Diana Mocanu">Diana Mocanu</a> (1:00.21) in 2000 in Sydney. Egerszegi's Hungarian (and world) record times on 100 m (1:00.31) and on 200 m backstroke (2:06.62) were the oldest ones on the list of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Hungarian_records_in_swimming" title="List of Hungarian records in swimming">Hungarian records</a>. The Hungarian record on 100 m backstroke was broken in 22 years later (April 2013) and the record on 200 m backstroke was broken at <a href="/wiki/2015_World_Aquatics_Championships" title="2015 World Aquatics Championships">2015 World Aquatics Championships</a> in Kazan, Russia. Both Hungarian records were set by <a href="/wiki/Katinka_Hossz%C3%BA" title="Katinka Hosszú">Katinka Hosszú</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_Hungarian_culture">Impact on Hungarian culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Krisztina_Egerszegi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Impact on Hungarian culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Her 1988 winning in Seoul became one of the biggest TV-moments in Hungary. The famous phrase "Come on Little Mouse! Come on little girl!" ("Gyere Egérke! Gyere kicsi lány!") by Tamás Vitray, who was the speaker on the air, is part of the popular culture. Egerszegi is still regarded as the role model of the "champion" in the country.</p>
<p>Her nickname was "Egérke" ("Little Mouse") or "Egér" ("Mouse"), a play on her surname, because of her youthfulness and size. After the <a href="/wiki/1992_Summer_Olympics" title="1992 Summer Olympics">1992 Summer Olympics</a>, where she became the most successful swimmer with three individual gold medals, she was called Krisztina Királynő ("Queen Kristina") both in the Hungarian and in some international media.</p>
<p>Her career was described in a 1993 book <i>Egerszegi</i> by László Ládonyi and György Volly and in a 2000 documentary film of the same title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honors">Awards and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Krisztina_Egerszegi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Awards and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/International_Swimming_Hall_of_Fame" title="International Swimming Hall of Fame">International Swimming Hall of Fame</a> in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ishof_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ishof-8">[8]</a></sup> She was named Hungarian Sportswoman of the Year on a record-breaking seven occasions (1988, 1989, 1990, 1991, 1992, 1993 and 1996) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimming_World_Swimmers_of_the_Year" title="Swimming World Swimmers of the Year">Female World Swimmer of the Year</a> three times.</p>
<p>Egerszegi, one of the most prominent FINA athletes of all times, was awarded the Olympic Order on 23 June 2001, during the celebration of the Olympic Day by the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> (IOC) in Lausanne (SUI). On 20 August 2013, she was awarded with the highest State Order of Hungary, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Order_of_Saint_Stephen_Hungary&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Order of Saint Stephen Hungary (page does not exist)">Order of Saint Stephen Hungary</a> Medal by the Hungarian President <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A1nos_%C3%81der" title="János Áder">János Áder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
