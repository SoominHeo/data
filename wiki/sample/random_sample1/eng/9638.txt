<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Energy-Efficient Ethernet</h1>

<p><b>Energy-Efficient Ethernet</b> (<b>EEE</b>) is a set of enhancements to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twisted-pair" title="Twisted-pair">twisted-pair</a> and backplane <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> family of <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">computer networking</a> standards that allow for less power consumption during periods of low data activity. The intention is to reduce power consumption by 50% or more, while retaining full compatibility with existing equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Electrical_and_Electronics_Engineers" title="Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers">Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers</a> (IEEE), through the <b>IEEE 802.3az</b> task force developed the standard. The first study group had its call for interest in November 2006, and the official standards task force was authorized in May 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The IEEE ratified the final standard in September 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Some companies introduced technology to reduce the power required for Ethernet before the standard was ratified, using the name <b>Green Ethernet</b>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_savings">Potential savings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy-Efficient_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Potential savings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/IT_energy_management" title="IT energy management">IT energy management</a></div>
<p>In 2005, all the <a href="/wiki/Network_interface_controller" title="Network interface controller">network interface controllers</a> in the United States (in computers, switches, and routers) used an estimated 5.3 terawatt-hours of electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEEESpectrum_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-IEEESpectrum-4">[4]</a></sup> According to a researcher at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lawrence_Berkeley_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Berkeley Laboratory">Lawrence Berkeley Laboratory</a>, Energy-Efficient Ethernet can potentially save an estimated <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>450 million</span> a year in energy costs in the U.S. Most of the savings from homes (<span class="nowrap">$200 million</span>), and offices (<span class="nowrap">$170 million</span>), and the remaining <span class="nowrap">$80 million</span> from data centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EETimes_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EETimes-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concepts">Concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy-Efficient_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The power reduction is accomplished in a few ways. In <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Fast_Ethernet" title="Fast Ethernet">100 Mbit/s</a></span>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigabit_ethernet" title="Gigabit ethernet">1 gigabit</a> and <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/10_Gigabit_Ethernet" title="10 Gigabit Ethernet">10 Gbit/s</a></span> speed data links, energy is used to keep the <a href="/wiki/Physical_layer" title="Physical layer">physical layer</a> transmitters on all the time. If they could be put into "sleep" mode when no data is being sent, that energy could be saved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EETimes_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-EETimes-5">[5]</a></sup> When the controlling software or firmware decides that no data needs to be sent, it can issue a low-power idle (LPI) request to the Ethernet controller physical layer <a href="/wiki/PHY_(chip)" title="PHY (chip)">PHY</a>. The PHY will then send LPI symbols for a specified time onto the link, and then disable its transmitter. Refresh signals are sent periodically to maintain link signaling integrity. When there is data to transmit, a normal IDLE signal is sent for a predetermined period of time. The data link is considered to be always operational, as the receive signal circuit remains active even when the transmit path is in sleep mode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ethernet:_The_Definitive_Guide_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ethernet:_The_Definitive_Guide-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_Green_Ethernet">Comparison to Green Ethernet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy-Efficient_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Comparison to Green Ethernet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some energy-efficient switch-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_circuits" title="Integrated circuits">integrated circuits</a> were developed before the IEEE 802.3az standard was finalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brcm-een_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-brcm-een-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Green Ethernet technology was a superset of the 802.3az standard. In addition to the link load power savings of Energy-Efficient Ethernet, Green Ethernet works in one of two ways. First, it detects link status, allowing each port on the switch to power down into a standby or ‘sleep’ mode when a connected device, such as a computer, is not active. Second, it detects cable length and adjusts the power accordingly. Previous standard switches provide enough power to send a signal up to 100 meters (330 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, this is often unnecessary, especially in the home, where 5 to 10 meters (16 to 33 ft) of cabling are typical between rooms. In addition to the pure power saving benefits of Green Ethernet, backing off the transmit power on shorter cable runs reduces alien crosstalk, and improves the overall performance of the cabling system.</p>
<p>Green Ethernet also encompasses the use of more efficient circuitry in Ethernet chips, and the use of "off-load engines" on Ethernet interface cards intended for network servers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brcm-een_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-brcm-een-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Routers">Routers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy-Efficient_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Routers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2008, the term was used for switches, and, in July 2008, used with wireless routers which featured user-selectable off periods for <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> to further reduce energy consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_savings">Power savings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy-Efficient_Ethernet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Power savings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Green Ethernet was first employed on home products. However, low port counts mean that significant cost savings are not going to be made using this technology only in the home. Turning off existing devices when they are idle is likely to provide a more immediate saving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smallnetDGStest_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-smallnetDGStest-11">[11]</a></sup> Projected power savings of up to 80 percent were estimated using Green Ethernet switches,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dlink2007_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-dlink2007-12">[12]</a></sup> translating into a longer product life due to reduced heat dissipation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
