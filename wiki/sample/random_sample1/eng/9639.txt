<p><b>Energia</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru"><b>Энергия</b></span>, <span lang="ru-Latn" xml:lang="ru-Latn"><i>Energiya</i></span>, "Energy") was a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> rocket that was designed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NPO_Energia" title="NPO Energia">NPO Energia</a> to serve as a heavy-lift <a href="/wiki/Expendable_launch_system" title="Expendable launch system">expendable launch system</a> as well as a booster for the <a href="/wiki/Buran_(spacecraft)" title="Buran (spacecraft)">Buran spacecraft</a>. Control system main developer enterprise was the <a href="/wiki/Khartron" title="Khartron">NPO "Electropribor"</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The Energia used four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strap-on_booster" title="Strap-on booster">strap-on</a> boosters each powered by a four-chamber <a href="/wiki/RD-170" title="RD-170">RD-170</a> engine burning <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a>/<a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">LOX</a>, and a central core stage with 4 one-chamber <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RD-0120_(rocket_engine)" title="RD-0120 (rocket engine)">RD-0120</a> (11D122) engines fueled by <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a>/LOX.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The launch system had two functionally different operational variants: <i>Energia-Polyus</i>, the initial test configuration, in which the <a href="/wiki/Polyus_(spacecraft)" title="Polyus (spacecraft)">Polyus</a> system was used as a final stage to put the payload into orbit, and <i>Energia-Buran</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hendrickx_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hendrickx-5">[5]</a></sup> in which the <a href="/wiki/Buran_(spacecraft)" title="Buran (spacecraft)">Buran</a> spacecraft was the payload and the source of the orbit insertion impulse.</p>
<p>The rocket had the capacity to place about 100 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">Low Earth orbit</a>, up to 20 tonnes to <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geostationary orbit</a> and up to 32 tonnes to a <a href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit" title="Lunar orbit">translunar trajectory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EnergiaOffSite_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-EnergiaOffSite-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Work on the Energia/Buran system began in 1976 after the decision was made to cancel the unsuccessful <a href="/wiki/N1_(rocket)" title="N1 (rocket)">N1 (rocket)</a>. The cancelled N1 rocket-based Manned Lunar Launch Facilities and Infrastructure were used for Energia (notably the huge horizontal assembly building), just as NASA reused infrastructure designed for the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> in the Space Shuttle program. Energia also replaced the "Vulkan" concept, which was a design based on the <a href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket_family)" title="Proton (rocket family)">Proton (rocket family)</a> and using the same <a href="/wiki/Hypergolic_propellant" title="Hypergolic propellant">hypergolic propellants</a>, but much larger and more powerful. The "Vulkan" designation was later given to a variation of the Energia which has eight boosters and multiple stages.</p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Energia-Polyus_drawing.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Polyus satellite on Energia launch vehicle</div>

<p>The Energia was designed to launch the Russian "<a href="/wiki/Buran_(spacecraft)" title="Buran (spacecraft)">Buran</a>" reusable shuttle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hendrickx_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hendrickx-5">[5]</a></sup> and for that reason was designed to carry its payload mounted on the side of the stack, rather than on the top, as is done with other launch vehicles. After design of the Energia-Buran system, it was also proposed that the booster could be used without the Buran as a heavy-lift cargo launch vehicle; this configuration was originally given the name "Buran-T".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JBIS-2002_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-JBIS-2002-6">[6]</a></sup> This configuration required the addition of an upper stage to perform the final orbital insertion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JBIS-2002_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-JBIS-2002-6">[6]</a></sup> The first launch of the Energia was in the configuration of a heavy launch vehicle, with the large <a href="/wiki/Polyus_(spacecraft)" title="Polyus (spacecraft)">Polyus</a> military satellite as a payload, however Polyus failed to correctly perform the orbital insertion.</p>
<p>Due to the termination of the <a href="/wiki/Buran_programme" title="Buran programme">Buran programme</a> the Energia program was concluded after only two launches, and further the payload on the first launch didn't perform the final boost properly. The legacy of Energia/Buran project manifests itself most visibly in form of the <a href="/wiki/RD-170" title="RD-170">RD-170</a> family of rocket engines, and the <a href="/wiki/Zenit_(rocket_family)" title="Zenit (rocket family)">Zenit</a> launcher, with the first stage roughly the same as one of the Energia first-stage boosters.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_launch_.28Energia-Polyus.29">First launch (Energia-Polyus)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First launch (Energia-Polyus)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Energia was first test-launched on 15 May 1987, with the <a href="/wiki/Polyus_(spacecraft)" title="Polyus (spacecraft)">Polyus spacecraft</a> as the payload. A <a href="/wiki/Zarya" title="Zarya">FGB</a> ("Functional Cargo Block") engine section originally built as a cancelled <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">Mir</a> module was incorporated into the upper stage used to inject the payload into orbit, similarly to Buran and the US Space Shuttle performing the final orbital insertion, since the planned "Buran-T" upper stage had not yet progressed beyond the planning stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JBIS-2002_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-JBIS-2002-6">[6]</a></sup> The intended orbit was altitude 280 km (170 mi), inclination 64.6°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Soviets had originally announced that the launch was a successful sub-orbital test of the new Energia booster with a dummy payload, but some time later it was revealed that the flight had, in fact, been intended to orbit the Polyus, a <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome_Site_250" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome Site 250">UKSS</a> (Russian: <i>Универсальный Комплекс Стенд-Старт</i>, Universal Complex Stand-Start) military payload. The two stages of the Energia launcher functioned as designed, but due to a software error in its attitude control system, Polyus' orbital insertion motor failed to inject the payload into orbit. Instead, the Polyus reentered the atmosphere over the Pacific ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_launch_.28Energia-Buran.29">Second launch (Energia-Buran)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Second launch (Energia-Buran)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The second flight, and the first one where payload successfully reached orbit, was launched on 15 November 1988. This mission launched the unmanned Soviet Shuttle vehicle, <a href="/wiki/Buran_(spacecraft)" title="Buran (spacecraft)">Buran</a>. At apogee, the Buran spacecraft made a 66.7 m/s burn to reach a final orbit of 251 km x 263 km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hendrickx_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hendrickx-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discontinuation">Discontinuation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Discontinuation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Production of Energia rockets ended with the fall of the Soviet Union and the end of the Buran shuttle project. Ever since, there have been persistent rumors of the renewal of production, but given the current political realities, that is highly unlikely. While the Energia is no longer in production, the Zenit boosters are still in production and in use. The four strap-on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_Rocket_Booster" title="Liquid Rocket Booster">liquid-fuel boosters</a>, which burned kerosene and liquid oxygen, were the basis of the Zenit rocket which used the same engines. The engine is the four combustion chamber <a href="/wiki/RD-170" title="RD-170">RD-170</a>. Its derivative, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RD-171" title="RD-171">RD-171</a>, is still used on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zenit_rocket" title="Zenit rocket">Zenit rocket</a>. A half-sized derivative of the engine, the two-chamber <a href="/wiki/RD-180" title="RD-180">RD-180</a>, powers <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_V_rocket" title="Atlas V rocket">Atlas V</a> rocket, while the single-chamber derivative, the <a href="/wiki/RD-191" title="RD-191">RD-191</a>, has been used to launch the Korean <a href="/wiki/Naro-1" title="Naro-1">Naro-1</a> (as a reduced-thrust variant named the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RD-151" title="RD-151">RD-151</a>) and the Russian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angara_(rocket)" title="Angara (rocket)">Angara</a> rocket.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revival">Revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2016, plans were announced to develop a <a href="/wiki/Super_heavy-lift_launch_vehicle" title="Super heavy-lift launch vehicle">super heavy-lift launch vehicle</a> from existing Energia components instead of pushing the less-powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angara_A5" title="Angara A5">Angara A5</a>V project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rt-20160822_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-rt-20160822-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This would allow Russia to launch missions towards establishing a <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_the_Moon" title="Colonization of the Moon">permanent Moon base</a> with simpler logistics, launching just one or two 80–160-ton super-heavy rockets instead of four 40-ton Angara A5Vs implying quick-sequence launches and multiple in-orbit rendez-vous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rt-20160822_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-rt-20160822-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>Three major variants were conceptualized after the original configuration, each with vastly different payloads.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energia_M">Energia M</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Energia M">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Energia M was the smallest design configuration. The number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zenit_rocket" title="Zenit rocket">Zenit boosters</a> was reduced from four to two, and instead of four <a href="/wiki/RD-0120" title="RD-0120">RD-0120</a> engines in the core, it had only one. It was designed to replace the Proton rocket, but lost the 1993 competition to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angara_rocket" title="Angara rocket">Angara rocket</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energia_II_.28Uragan.29">Energia II (<i>Uragan</i>)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Energia II (Uragan)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Energia II, named <b>Uragan</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru"><b>Ураган</b></span>, <i>Hurricane</i>), was a rocket proposed to be fully reusable with the design feature to land on a conventional airfield. Unlike the Energia, which was planned to be semi-reusable (like that of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>), the Uragan concept was to have allowed the complete recovery of all Buran/Energia elements, like that of the original totally reusable Orbiter/Booster concept of the U.S. Shuttle.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Energia II core as proposed would be capable of re-entering and gliding to a landing, presumably using technology developed for the Buran.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vulkan-Hercules">Vulkan-Hercules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Vulkan-Hercules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The final unflown configuration was also the largest. With eight Zenit booster rockets and an Energia-M core as the upper stage, the "Vulkan" (which was the same name of another Soviet heavy lift rocket that was cancelled years earlier) or "Hercules" (which is the same name designated to the N-1 rockets<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>) configuration could have launched up to 175 tonnes into orbit.</p>
<p>The development of rocket-carrier "Vulcan" and the refurbishment of the "Energia" launch pad for its launches was in progress in 1990-1993. But later on the work on this project was cancelled due to lack of funds and the collapse of the Soviet Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
