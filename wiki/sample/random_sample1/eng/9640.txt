<p>The <i><b><span lang="akk" xml:lang="akk">En√ªma Eli≈°</span></b></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">Akkadian Cuneiform</a>: <span lang="und-Xsux" style="font-size:125%;font-family:'Segoe UI Historic','Akkadian','Noto Sans Cuneiform','Noto Sans Sumero-Akkadian Cuneiform';" title="cuneiform text" xml:lang="und-Xsux"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%F0%92%82%8A%F0%92%89%A1%F0%92%88%A0%F0%92%82%8A%F0%92%87%BA#Akkadian" title="wikt:íÇäíâ°íà†íÇäíá∫">íÇäíâ°íà†íÇäíá∫</a></span>, also spelled "Enuma Elish"), is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonian_mythology" title="Babylonian mythology">Babylonian</a> <a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">creation myth</a> (named after its opening words). It was recovered by <a href="/wiki/Austen_Henry_Layard" title="Austen Henry Layard">Austen Henry Layard</a> in 1849 (in fragmentary form) in the ruined <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Ashurbanipal" title="Library of Ashurbanipal">Library of Ashurbanipal</a> at <a href="/wiki/Nineveh" title="Nineveh">Nineveh</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mosul" title="Mosul">Mosul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>), and published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Smith_(assyriologist)" title="George Smith (assyriologist)">George Smith</a> in 1876.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <i>En√ªma Eli≈°</i> has about a thousand lines and is recorded in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Babylonian_language" title="Old Babylonian language">Old Babylonian</a> on seven <a href="/wiki/Clay_tablet" title="Clay tablet">clay tablets</a>, each holding between 115 and 170 lines of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">Sumero-Akkadian cuneiform script</a>. Most of Tablet V has never been recovered but, aside from this <a href="/wiki/Lacuna_(manuscripts)" title="Lacuna (manuscripts)">lacuna</a>, the text is almost complete.</p>
<p>This epic is one of the most important sources for understanding the <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonian</a> worldview, centered on the supremacy of <a href="/wiki/Marduk" title="Marduk">Marduk</a> and the creation of humankind for the service of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesopotamian_deities" title="Mesopotamian deities">gods</a>. Its primary original purpose, however, is not an exposition of <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Theogony" title="Theogony">theogony</a> but the elevation of Marduk, the chief god of <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a>, above other Mesopotamian gods.</p>
<p>The <i>En√ªma Eli≈°</i> exists in various copies from Babylon and Assyria. The version from Ashurbanipal's library dates to the 7th century BCE. The composition of the text probably dates to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronze_age" title="Bronze age">bronze age</a>, to the time of <a href="/wiki/Hammurabi" title="Hammurabi">Hammurabi</a> or perhaps the early <a href="/wiki/Kassites" title="Kassites">Kassite</a> era (roughly 18th to 16th centuries BCE), although some scholars favor a later date of <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">‚Äâ1100 BCE</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">Summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=En%C3%BBma_Eli%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the seven tablets that contain this were first discovered, evidence indicated that it was used as a "ritual", meaning it was recited during a ceremony or celebration. That celebration is now thought to be the <a href="/wiki/Akitu" title="Akitu">Akitu</a> festival, or Babylonian new year. This tells of the creation of the world, and of Marduk's victory over <a href="/wiki/Tiamat" title="Tiamat">Tiamat</a>, and how it relates to him becoming king of the gods. This is then followed by an invocation to Marduk by his fifty names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<p>The epic names two primeval gods: <a href="/wiki/Abzu" title="Abzu">Aps√ª</a> (or Abzu) who represents fresh water and <a href="/wiki/Tiamat" title="Tiamat">Tiamat</a> representing oceanic waters. Several other gods are created - <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ea_(Babylonian_god)" title="Ea (Babylonian god)">Ea</a> and his brothers - who reside in Tiamat's vast body. They make so much noise that the babel or noise annoys Tiamat and Aps√ª greatly. Aps√ª wishes to kill the young gods, but Tiamat disagrees. The vizier, <a href="/wiki/Mummu" title="Mummu">Mummu</a>, agrees with Aps√ª's plan to destroy them. Tiamat, in order to stop this from occurring, warns Ea (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nudimmud" title="Nudimmud">Nudimmud</a>), the most powerful of the gods. Ea uses magic to put Aps√ª into a coma, then kills him, and shuts Mummu out. Ea then becomes the chief god, marries <a href="/wiki/Damgalnuna" title="Damgalnuna">Damkina</a>, and has a son, <a href="/wiki/Marduk" title="Marduk">Marduk</a>, greater still than Ea himself. Marduk is given wind to play with and he uses the wind to make dust storms and tornadoes. This disrupts Tiamat's great body and causes the gods still residing inside her to be unable to sleep.</p>
<p>The gods persuade Tiamat to take revenge for the death of her husband, Aps√ª. Her power grows, and some of the gods join her. She creates 11 monsters (<a href="/wiki/Ba%C5%A1mu" title="Ba≈°mu">Ba≈°mu</a>, ‚ÄúVenomous Snake‚Äù; <a href="/wiki/U%C5%A1umgallu" title="U≈°umgallu">U≈°umgallu</a>, ‚ÄúGreat Dragon‚Äù; <a href="/wiki/Mu%C5%A1ma%E1%B8%AB%E1%B8%AB%C5%AB" title="Mu≈°ma·∏´·∏´≈´">Mu≈°ma·∏´·∏´≈´</a>, ‚ÄúExalted Serpent‚Äù; <a href="/wiki/Mu%C5%A1%E1%B8%ABu%C5%A1%C5%A1u" title="Mu≈°·∏´u≈°≈°u">Mu≈°·∏´u≈°≈°u</a>, ‚ÄúFurious Snake‚Äù; <a href="/wiki/Lahmu" title="Lahmu">La·∏´mu</a>, the ‚ÄúHairy One‚Äù; <a href="/wiki/Ugallu" title="Ugallu">Ugallu</a>, the ‚ÄúBig Weather-Beast‚Äù; <a href="/wiki/Uridimmu" title="Uridimmu">Uridimmu</a>, ‚ÄúMad Lion‚Äù; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Girtablilu" title="Girtablilu">Girtablull√ª</a>, ‚ÄúScorpion-Man‚Äù; <a href="/wiki/Um%C5%AB_dabr%C5%ABtu" title="Um≈´ dabr≈´tu">Um≈´ dabr≈´tu</a>, ‚ÄúViolent Storms‚Äù; <a href="/wiki/Kulull%C3%BB" title="Kulull√ª">Kulull√ª</a>, ‚ÄúFish-Man‚Äù; and <a href="/wiki/Kusarikku" title="Kusarikku">Kusarikku</a>, ‚ÄúBull-Man‚Äù) to help her win the battle and elevates <a href="/wiki/Kingu" title="Kingu">Kingu</a>, her new husband, to "supreme dominion." A lengthy description of the other gods' inability to deal with the threat follows. Marduk offers to save the gods if he is appointed as their leader and allowed to remain so even after the threat passes. When the gods agree to Marduk's conditions he is selected as their champion against Tiamat, and becomes very powerful. Marduk challenges Tiamat to combat and destroys her. He then rips her corpse into two halves with which he fashions the earth and the skies. Marduk then creates the calendar, organizes the planets and stars, and regulates the moon, the sun, and weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABJ_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABJ-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The gods who have pledged their allegiance to Tiamat are initially forced into labor in the service of the gods who sided with Marduk. But they are freed from these labors when Marduk then destroys Tiamat's husband, <a href="/wiki/Kingu" title="Kingu">Kingu</a>, and uses his blood to create humankind to do the work for the gods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABJ_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ABJ-4">[4]</a></sup> Most noteworthy is Marduk's symbolic elevation over <a href="/wiki/Enlil" title="Enlil">Enlil</a>, who was seen by earlier Mesopotamian civilizations as the king of the gods.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_the_Bible">Relation to the Bible</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=En%C3%BBma_Eli%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relation to the Bible">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Panbabylonism" title="Panbabylonism">Panbabylonism</a></div>
<p>The <i>En√ªma Eli≈°</i> was recognized as being related to the Hebrew <a href="/wiki/Genesis_creation_narrative" title="Genesis creation narrative">Genesis creation account</a> from its first publication (Smith 1876).</p>
<p>In one interpretation, Genesis 1:1-3 can be taken as describing the state of <a href="/wiki/Chaos_(cosmogony)" title="Chaos (cosmogony)">chaos</a> immediately before God's act of creation:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>"In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth. The earth was without form and void, and darkness was over the face of the deep. And the spirit of God was hovering over the face of the waters. And God said, ‚ÄòLet there be light,‚Äô and there was light."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Though most scholarly work on the En√ªma Eli≈° regard its primary original purpose to be the elevation of Marduk, the chief god of Babylon, above other Mesopotamian gods, researchers seeking to remove Babylonian influence onto Judaism assert counter arguments. To address the similarities between the accounts, Christian apologist <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Hyers" title="Conrad Hyers">Conrad Hyers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Princeton_Theological_Seminary" title="Princeton Theological Seminary">Princeton Theological Seminary</a>, stated that the Genesis creation myth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polemics" title="Polemics">polemically</a> addressed earlier Babylonian and other <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagan</a> creation myths<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hyers_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hyers-7">[7]</a></sup> to "repudiate the divinization of nature and the attendant myths of divine origins, divine conflict, and divine ascent," thus rejecting the idea that Genesis borrowed from or appropriated the form of the En√ªma Eli≈°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hyers_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hyers-7">[7]</a></sup> According to this theory, the En√ªma Eli≈° was comfortable using connections between the divine and inert matter, while the aim of Genesis was to state the superiority of the Israelite god Elohim over all creation (and subsequent deities).</p>
<p>Reconstruction of the broken En√ªma Eli≈° tablet seems to define the rarely attested <i>Sapattu<sup>m</sup></i> or <i>Sabattu<sup>m</sup></i> as the <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">full moon</a>. This word is cognate or merged with Hebrew <i><a href="/wiki/Shabbat" title="Shabbat">Shabbat</a></i> (cf. <a class="external text" href="http://www.biblica.com/en-us/bible/online-bible/?osis=niv:Genesis.2:2‚Äì3:2" rel="nofollow">Genesis 2:2-3</a>), but is monthly rather than weekly; it is regarded as a form of <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> <i>sa-bat</i> ("mid-rest"), attested in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">Akkadian</a> as <i>um nuh libbi</i> ("day of mid-repose"). This conclusion is a contextual restoration of the damaged tablet, which is read as "[Sa]bbath shalt thou then encounter, mid[month]ly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ere_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ere-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
