<p><b>Eniwa</b> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">恵庭市</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Eniwa-shi</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> (<small>Japanese pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Japanese" title="Help:IPA for Japanese">[eniw͍a]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Cities_of_Japan" title="Cities of Japan">city</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ishikari_Subprefecture" title="Ishikari Subprefecture">Ishikari Subprefecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido" title="Hokkaido">Hokkaido</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. It is on the Ishikari plain, 8 km north of <a href="/wiki/Chitose,_Hokkaido" title="Chitose, Hokkaido">Chitose</a> and 26 km south of the prefectural capital <a href="/wiki/Sapporo" title="Sapporo">Sapporo</a>. It is reached through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Route_36_(Japan)" title="Route 36 (Japan)">route 36</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chitose_Line" title="Chitose Line">Chitose Railway Line</a>. The town is separated into three major areas: Eniwa in the south, Megumino in the center, and Shimamatsu in the north.</p>
<p>Many farms are located around Eniwa, and the town has many manufacturing businesses, including the <a href="/wiki/Sapporo_Brewery" title="Sapporo Brewery">Sapporo Brewery</a> Hokkaido factory. There are three <a href="/wiki/Japan_Ground_Self-Defense_Force" title="Japan Ground Self-Defense Force">Japan Ground Self-Defense Force</a> camps in the city.</p>
<p>Eniwa's 2012 population of 68,883 makes it the fourth largest city in the <a href="/wiki/Ishikari_Subprefecture" title="Ishikari Subprefecture">Ishikari Subprefecture</a> and the 13th largest in Hokkaido.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eniwa,_Hokkaido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town's name is taken from the nearby <a href="/wiki/Mount_Eniwa" title="Mount Eniwa">Mount Eniwa</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Shikotsu-T%C5%8Dya_National_Park" title="Shikotsu-Tōya National Park">Shikotsu-Tōya National Park</a>. The name in <a href="/wiki/Ainu_language" title="Ainu language">Ainu</a>, <i>e-en-iwa</i> (<span lang="ain">エエンイワ</span>), means "sharp mountain."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The name was transliterated into Japanese <a href="/wiki/Ateji" title="Ateji">ateji</a> to mean <i>blessed garden</i>. The Japanese transliteration was chosen because of the homonyms <i>niwa</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">庭</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, "garden")</span> and <i>niwa</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">二輪</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, "two rings")</span>, the later referring to the two rivers that pass through the city, the Shimamatsu River and the Izari River, as well as the "blessings" (<i>e</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">恵</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>) between the two rivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OfficialOutline_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-OfficialOutline-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eniwa,_Hokkaido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The first known settlement of Eniwa was in the Initial <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Dmon_period" title="Jōmon period">Jōmon period</a> in 7000 BC, at the Karinba Ruins <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">カリンバ遺跡</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Karinba Iseki</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KarinbaPamphlet_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-KarinbaPamphlet-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The settlement received a surge of people in 2000 B,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup> and continued being settled for many years. Many artifacts have been found, including lacquered combs, beads, earthenware and stone accessories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KarinbaPamphlet_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-KarinbaPamphlet-3">[3]</a></sup> Historical <a href="/wiki/Satsumon_culture" title="Satsumon culture">Satsumon culture</a> (700–1200 <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">CE</a>) graves dating have been found around Eniwa, at the Moizari <a href="/wiki/Kofun" title="Kofun">Kofun</a> Site <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">茂漁古墳群</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Moizari kofun-gun</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The style is similar to those at the <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%B1%9F%E5%88%A5%E5%8F%A4%E5%A2%B3%E7%BE%A4" title="ja:江別古墳群">Ebetsu Kofun Site</a> and northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dhoku" title="Tōhoku">Tōhoku</a> historic graves. During the <a href="/wiki/Ainu_people" title="Ainu people">Ainu</a> settlement period (1200 CE until the Meiji era), there is historical evidence for settlements in the villages and further away on the plains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KarinbaPamphlet_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-KarinbaPamphlet-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Matsumae_clan" title="Matsumae clan">Matsumae clan</a> settled on the southern tip of Hokkaido in 1590, they traded goods with the Ainu who lived in the area. In the <a href="/wiki/Edo_period" title="Edo period">Edo period</a>, one of the 13 trading locations across the Ishikari plain was <i>Shuma-mappu Location</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">シュママップ場所</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Shumamappu Basho</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> (<a href="/wiki/Ainu_language" title="Ainu language">Ainu</a>: Shuma-o-mappu (<span lang="ain">シュマ・オ・マップ</span>)), which corresponds to the modern-day Shimamatsu River basin. The trading area was active until the end of the Edo era. Early Japanese contact with the area included in 1755, when <a href="/wiki/Picea_jezoensis" title="Picea jezoensis">jezo spruce</a> trees were harvested along the Izari river, and in 1805, when the river was farmed for salmon and trout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1857, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hakodate" title="Hakodate">Hakodate</a> <a href="/wiki/Bugy%C5%8D" title="Bugyō">magistrate</a> decreed that a road between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otaru,_Hokkaido" title="Otaru, Hokkaido">Otaru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chitose,_Hokkaido" title="Chitose, Hokkaido">Chitose</a> be developed, leading to the development of the Ishikari Plain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup> When Hokkaido became a part of Japan in the early <a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">Meiji period</a>, the area around Eniwa was incorporated into <a href="/wiki/Iburi_Province" title="Iburi Province">Iburi Province</a> in 1869.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Settlers from <a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dchi_Prefecture" title="Kōchi Prefecture">Kōchi Prefecture</a> initially settled in Eniwa in 1870 in two villages: Izari Village <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">漁村</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Izari-mura</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in the south and Shimamatsu Village <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">島松村</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Shimamatsu-mura</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in the north.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OfficialOutline_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-OfficialOutline-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1873, the <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%9C%AD%E5%B9%8C%E6%9C%AC%E9%81%93" title="ja:札幌本道">Sapporo Highway</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">札幌本道</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Sapporo Hondō</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, the a road linking Hakodate with <a href="/wiki/Sapporo" title="Sapporo">Sapporo</a> was completed, and it was built through both villages. In 1873, rice farming started in Shimamatsu, as well as the first postal service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1880, the Chitose town hall began administering five surrounding villages to Chitose, including Izari and Shimamatsu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChitoseHistory_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChitoseHistory-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1886, 65 families from <a href="/wiki/Waki,_Yamaguchi" title="Waki, Yamaguchi">Waki, Yamaguchi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iwakuni,_Yamaguchi" title="Iwakuni, Yamaguchi">Iwakuni, Yamaguchi</a> moved to the shores of the Izari river, which greatly increased the size of Izari.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OfficialOutline_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-OfficialOutline-2">[2]</a></sup> A year later, the Izari town hall was built, meaning Chitose no longer administered Izari or Shimamatsu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OfficialOutline_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-OfficialOutline-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChitoseHistory_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ChitoseHistory-8">[8]</a></sup> At this point, Eniwa had grown to 572 residents, and the first elementary school was opened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OfficialOutline_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-OfficialOutline-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Shinto_shrine" title="Shinto shrine">Shinto shrines</a> were constructed in 1901: Toyosaka Shrine in Izari and Shimamatsu Shrine. The Buddhist temple <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%A4%A9%E8%9E%8D%E5%AF%BA" title="ja:天融寺">Ten'yū-ji</a>'s main building was constructed in 1904.</p>
<p>In 1906, Izari and Shimamatsu were merged to form Eniwa Village <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">恵庭村</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Eniwa-mura</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, a second class municipality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OfficialOutline_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-OfficialOutline-2">[2]</a></sup> The village also administered <a href="/wiki/Kitahiroshima,_Hokkaido" title="Kitahiroshima, Hokkaido">Hiroshima Village</a>; in 1943 the village seceded. In 1922, the village received electricity, after the Izari River was used for <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric power</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup> By 1923, the town had grown enough to become a first class municipality. Eniwa was first connected to by rail in 1926, after the <a href="/wiki/Chitose_Line" title="Chitose Line">Sapporo Line</a> (now known as the Chitose Line) was completed, and <a href="/wiki/Eniwa_Station" title="Eniwa Station">Eniwa Station</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shimamatsu_Station" title="Shimamatsu Station">Shimamatsu Station</a> were opened.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1930s, Eniwa became a site for mining gold and silver. In 1935, a small-scale private mine called the Kōryū Mine <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">光竜鉱山</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kōryū Kōzan</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> was opened in 1935 and run by the <a href="/wiki/Fujita_(company)" title="Fujita (company)">Fujita</a> company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watanabe-EniwaCityHistory_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watanabe-EniwaCityHistory-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1939, the nationally run <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%81%B5%E5%BA%AD%E9%89%B1%E5%B1%B1" title="ja:恵庭鉱山">Eniwa Gold Mine</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">恵庭鉱山</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Eniwa Kōzan</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> opened. A mining town to the north-northwest of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Eniwa" title="Mount Eniwa">Mount Eniwa</a> was constructed, and Kōryū Mine was expanded. The town featured around 40 five-family apartments and additional buildings for administration and amenities, however, no restaurants or entertainment areas were constructed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Two elementary schools were operated for the area. Both mines were closed in 1943, due to the <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E9%87%91%E9%89%B1%E5%B1%B1%E6%95%B4%E5%82%99%E4%BB%A4" title="ja:金鉱山整備令">Order for Gold Mine Consolidation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> By the end of the Eniwa Mine's operations, a total of 700 kg of gold and 3,500 kg of silver had been mined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The mines and buildings were dismantled, though the Kōryū Mine was reopened in 1949 by Yutani Mining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watanabe-EniwaCityHistory_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Watanabe-EniwaCityHistory-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Japan" title="Occupation of Japan">Occupation of Japan</a> beginning in 1945, many agricultural reforms were undertaken that made the farms around Eniwa more prosperous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup> In September 1950, a military camp for training police personnel was built in Kashiwagi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1951, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Company_C,_52d_Infantry_Regiment_(Anti-Tank)" title="Company C, 52d Infantry Regiment (Anti-Tank)">Company C, 52d Infantry Regiment (Anti-Tank)</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> held military practices at the camp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> When the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Ground_Self-Defense_Force" title="Japan Ground Self-Defense Force">Japan Ground Self-Defense Force</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Northern_Army_(Japan)" title="Northern Army (Japan)">Northern Army</a> was established in 1952, they took over running the camp and established the South Eniwa Camp in 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>By 1951, the village's population was significant enough to be upgraded to a town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OfficialOutline_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-OfficialOutline-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1970 after the town had 34,500 residents, it was upgraded to Eniwa City, after a piece of regional legislation allowed Eniwa, <a href="/wiki/Noboribetsu,_Hokkaido" title="Noboribetsu, Hokkaido">Noboribetsu</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Date,_Hokkaido" title="Date, Hokkaido">Date</a> to become cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OfficialOutline_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-OfficialOutline-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1979, part of the farmland between Eniwa and Shimamatsu was developed into Megumino, a new suburb of the city. By 1982, the <a href="/wiki/Megumino_Station" title="Megumino Station">Megumino Station</a> was built, along with the <a href="/wiki/Ito-Yokado" title="Ito-Yokado">Ito-Yokado</a> shopping center and the Megumino Elementary School.</p>
<p>In 1987, residents exceeded 50,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OfficialOutline_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-OfficialOutline-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Sapporo_Brewery" title="Sapporo Brewery">Sapporo Brewery</a> Hokkaido factory was built in the south of Eniwa in 1989,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeerFactory_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-BeerFactory-16">[16]</a></sup> and a dedicated train station, the <a href="/wiki/Sapporo_Beer_Teien_Station" title="Sapporo Beer Teien Station">Sapporo Beer Teien Station</a> was built in 1990. In 1993, residents in Eniwa exceeded 60,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OfficialOutline_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-OfficialOutline-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2006, Eniwa's first community radio station began broadcast. It was originally called <i>FM Pumpkin</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">FMパンキン</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, but the name was changed to <i><a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%81%88%E3%81%AB%E3%82%8F%E3%82%B3%E3%83%9F%E3%83%A5%E3%83%8B%E3%83%86%E3%82%A3%E6%94%BE%E9%80%81" title="ja:えにわコミュニティ放送">E-Niwa</a></i> in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2013, construction began on East Garden Megumino <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">イーストガーデン恵み野</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Īsuto Gāden Megumino</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, a 4.6 hectare housing development in east Megumino. The first houses were completed in May 2013, with the entire area expected to be completed in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The development of 25 hectares west of Megumino Station is being planned. Development consent was given in July 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>As of October 2013, the city has an estimated <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> of 68,883 residents, with 31,005 <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">households</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">density</a> of 233.60 persons per km². 51% of the population is female.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dem-AreaBreakdown_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dem-AreaBreakdown-20">[20]</a></sup> Eniwa's population is 99.7% Japanese, with the remaining 0.3% being foreign residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dem-AgeBreakdown_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dem-AgeBreakdown-21">[21]</a></sup> 13.8% of residents are under 15 years of age, and the workforce comprises 64.4% of residents. 21.8% of people are over age 65. In the 2012 population records, 22 residents are listed as <a href="/wiki/Centenarian" title="Centenarian">centenarians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dem-AgeBreakdown_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dem-AgeBreakdown-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The bulk of the population, 68%, lives in central Eniwa, while 17.5% live in Megumino and 13.2% live in Shimamatsu. The remainder 1.3% of Eniwa residents live in the surrounding farmland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dem-AreaBreakdown_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dem-AreaBreakdown-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_climate">Geography and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eniwa,_Hokkaido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eniwa_helicopter_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Eniwa_helicopter_01.jpg/220px-Eniwa_helicopter_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Eniwa_helicopter_01.jpg/330px-Eniwa_helicopter_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Eniwa_helicopter_01.jpg/440px-Eniwa_helicopter_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eniwa_helicopter_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Eniwa receives an average of 576 cm of snow per year.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Eniwa is on the <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E7%9F%B3%E7%8B%A9%E5%B9%B3%E9%87%8E" title="ja:石狩平野">Ishikari Plain</a>, amongst farmland. The town is 8 km from <a href="/wiki/Chitose,_Hokkaido" title="Chitose, Hokkaido">Chitose</a> (and the <a href="/wiki/New_Chitose_Airport" title="New Chitose Airport">New Chitose Airport</a>), and to the north is <a href="/wiki/Kitahiroshima,_Hokkaido" title="Kitahiroshima, Hokkaido">Kitahiroshima</a> City. 26 km north of Eniwa is Sapporo, the largest city and prefectural capital of Hokkaido. All of these cities are connected by the <a href="/wiki/Chitose_Line" title="Chitose Line">Chitose Line</a> railway and by the <a href="/wiki/Japan_National_Route_36" title="Japan National Route 36">Japan National Route 36</a>. The town is on the Izari River and the Shimamatsu River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OfficialOutline_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-OfficialOutline-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The area administered to by Eniwa extends north to the Shimamatsu river and stops at the border to <a href="/wiki/Naganuma,_Hokkaido" title="Naganuma, Hokkaido">Naganuma</a> township in <a href="/wiki/Sorachi_Subprefecture" title="Sorachi Subprefecture">Sorachi Subprefecture</a>. Eniwa borders Chitose city along its south border, with the cities being separated by as little as 750 m in some places. To the west of Eniwa is the <a href="/wiki/Shikotsu-T%C5%8Dya_National_Park" title="Shikotsu-Tōya National Park">Shikotsu-Tōya National Park</a>. Eight mountains in the park are considered a part of Eniwa, including <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%BC%81%E5%B2%B3" title="ja:漁岳">Mount Izari</a> and Mount Soranuma. In the area is the hydroelectric <a href="/wiki/Izarigawa_Dam" title="Izarigawa Dam">Izarigawa Dam</a> that dams the Izari river.</p>
<p>The city of Eniwa is separated into three major areas: Eniwa in the south, Megumino <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">恵み野</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> in the center and Shimamatsu in the north. Shimamatsu is separated from Eniwa and Megumino by approximately 300 meters.</p>
<p>Winters in Eniwa are colder than the surrounding areas, due to the town being inland. The average daily low temperature is between 5-6 degrees lower than <a href="/wiki/Sapporo" title="Sapporo">Sapporo</a>, 26 km to the north near the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Japan" title="Sea of Japan">Sea of Japan</a>, and 4-5 degrees lower than in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tomakomai" title="Tomakomai">Tomakomai</a>, 28 km to the south on the coast of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. Summers are slightly more milder than in Sapporo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MeteorlogicalAgency_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-MeteorlogicalAgency-22">[22]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eniwa,_Hokkaido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eniwa_Rice_Types.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Eniwa_Rice_Types.JPG/220px-Eniwa_Rice_Types.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Eniwa_Rice_Types.JPG/330px-Eniwa_Rice_Types.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Eniwa_Rice_Types.JPG/440px-Eniwa_Rice_Types.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eniwa_Rice_Types.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Eniwa grows many types of rice, including <i>Yume Pirika</i> and <i>Fukkurinko</i>. (pictured)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Traditionally, Eniwa's economy was based around farming, with the first major rice farms created in 1873.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup> Eniwa has two main crops: flowers and rice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farming_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farming-23">[23]</a></sup> 3,800ha of land are dedicated to flowers, most of which are cut flowers. 2,700 ha of land are dedicated to rice farming, mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ley_farming" title="Ley farming">ley farming</a>, though <a href="/wiki/Paddy_field" title="Paddy field">paddy field</a> rice exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farming_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Farming-23">[23]</a></sup> Rice in Eniwa is generally made up of the <i><a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%82%86%E3%82%81%E3%81%B4%E3%82%8A%E3%81%8B" title="ja:ゆめぴりか">Yume Pirika</a></i>, <i><a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%81%AA%E3%81%AA%E3%81%A4%E3%81%BC%E3%81%97" title="ja:ななつぼし">Nanatsu Boshi</a></i>, <i><a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%81%8A%E3%81%BC%E3%82%8D%E3%81%A5%E3%81%8D" title="ja:おぼろづき">Oborozuki</a></i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%81%B5%E3%81%A3%E3%81%8F%E3%82%8A%E3%82%93%E3%81%93" title="ja:ふっくりんこ">Fukkurinko</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riceblog_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riceblog-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eniwa also grows many vegetables. Vegetables with 100 ha or more dedicated space include <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybeans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daikon" title="Daikon">daikon</a> (Japanese radish). Other farmed vegetables include <a href="/wiki/Kabocha" title="Kabocha">ebisu kabocha</a> pumpkins, carrots, <a href="/wiki/Adzuki_bean" title="Adzuki bean">adzuki beans</a> and cabbages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farming_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Farming-23">[23]</a></sup> The ebisu kabocha is the city vegetable. Pumpkin-flavored <a href="/wiki/Soft_serve" title="Soft serve">soft serve</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manj%C5%AB" title="Manjū">manjū</a> and soup can be bought at the <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E9%81%93%E3%81%AE%E9%A7%85%E8%8A%B1%E3%83%AD%E3%83%BC%E3%83%89%E3%81%88%E3%81%AB%E3%82%8F" title="ja:道の駅花ロードえにわ">Flower Road Eniwa</a> <a href="/wiki/Roadside_station" title="Roadside station">roadside station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heisei_era" title="Heisei era">Heisei era</a>, manufacturing has increasingly become an important industry. In 1989, an area in southern Eniwa became dedicated to manufacture, called the Eniwa Techno Park <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">恵庭テクノパーク</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Eniwa Tekuno Pāku</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup> In the same year, the <a href="/wiki/Sapporo_Brewery" title="Sapporo Brewery">Sapporo Brewery</a> Hokkaido factory was built. It deals with 120 million liters of beer per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BeerFactory_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-BeerFactory-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> There are many food production factories are in Eniwa, including ones for Sanmaruko, the restaurant chain Tonden, <a href="/wiki/Oden" title="Oden">oden</a> producer Horikawa, Hoshio Milk, <a href="/wiki/Yamazaki_Baking" title="Yamazaki Baking">Yamazaki Baking</a>, Kibun Foods and Robapan. <a href="/wiki/Morinaga_Milk_Industry" title="Morinaga Milk Industry">Morinaga Milk Industry</a> built its Sapporo Factory in Eniwa in 1961; in April 2013 it halted all manufacturing there, leaving the site as a delivery depot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>There are mechanical factories for Sanwa Holdings, MSK Farm Machinery, <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Electric" title="Mitsubishi Electric">Mitsubishi</a>'s customized machinery division and <a href="/wiki/Oji_Paper_Company" title="Oji Paper Company">Oji Paper</a>.</p>
<p>Three camps for the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Ground_Self-Defense_Force" title="Japan Ground Self-Defense Force">Japan Ground Self-Defense Force</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Northern_Army_(Japan)" title="Northern Army (Japan)">Northern Army</a> are in Eniwa — Camp Shimamatsu, Camp Kita Eniwa, and Camp Minami Eniwa — where the Northern Army performs military exercises, including the 1st Tank Group's tank practice.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eniwa,_Hokkaido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eniwa-Ten%27yuji-Temple-side.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2422" data-file-width="3633" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Eniwa-Ten%27yuji-Temple-side.jpg/220px-Eniwa-Ten%27yuji-Temple-side.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Eniwa-Ten%27yuji-Temple-side.jpg/330px-Eniwa-Ten%27yuji-Temple-side.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Eniwa-Ten%27yuji-Temple-side.jpg/440px-Eniwa-Ten%27yuji-Temple-side.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eniwa-Ten%27yuji-Temple-side.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ten'yū-ji, the largest Buddhist temple in Eniwa</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>There are a variety of <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_temples_in_Japan" title="Buddhist temples in Japan">Buddhist temples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shinto_shrine" title="Shinto shrine">Shinto shrines</a> and Christian churches in Eniwa. Most of the institutions were established in the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">Meiji period</a>. The most recent Shinto shrine was built in 1908.</p>
<p>There are seven Buddhist temples in Eniwa. The largest is <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%A4%A9%E8%9E%8D%E5%AF%BA" title="ja:天融寺">Ten'yū-ji</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otani-ha" title="Otani-ha">Otani-ha</a> temple established in 1886.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Daian-ji was initially established as a <a href="/wiki/Terakoya" title="Terakoya">terakoya</a> school for the children of Eniwa in 1887, but grew to be a temple in 1911.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daian-ji_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daian-ji-29">[29]</a></sup> The Eniwa Buddhist temples follow a variety of schools. Two of the temples are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otani-ha" title="Otani-ha">Otani-ha</a>, two are <a href="/wiki/Hongan-ji" title="Hongan-ji">Hongan-ji</a>, Kōryū-ji is a <a href="/wiki/Kompira" title="Kompira">Kompira</a> worshiping <a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dyasan_Shingon-sh%C5%AB" title="Kōyasan Shingon-shū">Kōyasan Shingon-shū</a> temple, Daian-ji is <a href="/wiki/S%C5%8Dt%C5%8D" title="Sōtō">Sōtō</a> and Myōshō-ji is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nichiren_Sh%C5%AB" title="Nichiren Shū">Nichiren Shū</a>.</p>
<p>There are four Shinto shrines around Eniwa. <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E8%B1%8A%E6%A0%84%E7%A5%9E%E7%A4%BE_(%E6%81%B5%E5%BA%AD%E5%B8%82)" title="ja:豊栄神社 (恵庭市)">Toyosaka Shrine</a> was first established in 1874 as an area dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Inari_%C5%8Ckami" title="Inari Ōkami">Inari Ōkami</a>, with a small shrine for <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ckuninushi" title="Ōkuninushi">Ōkuninushi</a> built at the site in 1891.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> A second shrine, built to accommodate settlers from <a href="/wiki/Toyama_Prefecture" title="Toyama Prefecture">Toyama</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ishikawa_Prefecture" title="Ishikawa Prefecture">Ishikawa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%B3%B6%E6%9D%BE%E7%A5%9E%E7%A4%BE" title="ja:島松神社">Shimamatsu Shrine</a> was established in 1901 from donations from the people who lived in Shimamatsu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The fourth, Kashiwagi Shrine, was established in 1908. Much of the shrine was demolished in 1982 due to dilapidation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In Eniwa, there are six Shinto gods who have been enshrined: <a href="/wiki/Toyouke-%C5%8Cmikami" title="Toyouke-Ōmikami">Toyouke-Ōmikami</a> (at Toyosaka and Shimamatsu), <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ckuninushi" title="Ōkuninushi">Ōkuninushi</a> (at Toyosaka), <a href="/wiki/Amenominakanushi" title="Amenominakanushi">Amenominakanushi</a> (at Kashiwagi) and <a href="/wiki/Amaterasu" title="Amaterasu">Amaterasu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inari_%C5%8Ckami" title="Inari Ōkami">Inari Ōkami</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%98%A5%E6%97%A5%E7%A5%9E" title="ja:春日神">Kasuga Ōkami</a> at Eniwa Shrine.</p>
<p>There are three <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_Japan" title="Christianity in Japan">Christian</a> churches in Eniwa, the Catholic Eniwa Parish, Eniwa Evangelical Christian Church and the Eniwa <a href="/wiki/Japan_Evangelical_Lutheran_Church" title="Japan Evangelical Lutheran Church">Evangelical Lutheran</a> church. In addition, there is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witness" title="Jehovah's Witness">Jehovah's Witness</a> church, as well as a church for the Chitose and Eniwa <a href="/wiki/Ward_(LDS_Church)" title="Ward (LDS Church)">ward</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints_in_Japan" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in Japan">the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parks_and_recreation">Parks and recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eniwa,_Hokkaido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Parks and recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ecorin-mura.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Ecorin-mura.jpg/220px-Ecorin-mura.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Ecorin-mura.jpg/330px-Ecorin-mura.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Ecorin-mura.jpg/440px-Ecorin-mura.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ecorin-mura.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <i>Tomato Forest</i> at Ecorin Village (pictured 2007)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Eniwa has five public parks: <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%81%B5%E5%BA%AD%E5%85%AC%E5%9C%92" title="ja:恵庭公園">Eniwa Park</a>, Nakajima Park, Furusato Park, Megumino Central Park and Technopark Central Park. The parks are mostly around central and southern Eniwa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The largest, Eniwa Park, is 411,000m². In addition, the land around the banks of the Izari River is considered <a href="/wiki/Urban_open_space" title="Urban open space">urban open space</a>. Parks in Eniwa mostly consist of open spaces and woodland, though several feature sporting facilities, such as Nakajima Park's jogging track.</p>
<p>In 2006, an agricultural theme park called <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%81%88%E3%81%93%E3%82%8A%E3%82%93%E6%9D%91" title="ja:えこりん村">Ecorin Village</a> was built in Eniwa. At the gardening center of the theme park is a greenhouse housing <i>Tomato no Mori</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">とまとの森</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, "Tomato Forest")</span>, which in November 2013 was awarded the <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">Guinness World Records</a></i> award for the largest tomato plant in the world, measuring 85.46m<sup>2</sup> at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>Within the urban borders of Eniwa are several <a href="/wiki/Park_golf" title="Park golf">park golf</a> grounds, a sport created in Hokkaido. Outside of the city, the Eniwa Country Club features three nine-hole <a href="/wiki/Golf_course" title="Golf course">golf courses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Shikotsu-T%C5%8Dya_National_Park" title="Shikotsu-Tōya National Park">Shikotsu-Tōya National Park</a> to the west, many of the mountains such as Mount Izari and Mount Soranuma feature walking trails to their summits. The man-made Lake Eniwa <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">えにわ湖</span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>Eniwa-ko</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span> lake is behind the Izari Dam in the national park,.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eniwa,_Hokkaido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%E3%83%8F%E3%82%A4%E3%83%86%E3%82%AF%E8%88%AA%E7%A9%BA%E5%86%99%E7%9C%9F.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="342" data-file-width="480" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/%E3%83%8F%E3%82%A4%E3%83%86%E3%82%AF%E8%88%AA%E7%A9%BA%E5%86%99%E7%9C%9F.jpg/220px-%E3%83%8F%E3%82%A4%E3%83%86%E3%82%AF%E8%88%AA%E7%A9%BA%E5%86%99%E7%9C%9F.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/%E3%83%8F%E3%82%A4%E3%83%86%E3%82%AF%E8%88%AA%E7%A9%BA%E5%86%99%E7%9C%9F.jpg/330px-%E3%83%8F%E3%82%A4%E3%83%86%E3%82%AF%E8%88%AA%E7%A9%BA%E5%86%99%E7%9C%9F.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/%E3%83%8F%E3%82%A4%E3%83%86%E3%82%AF%E8%88%AA%E7%A9%BA%E5%86%99%E7%9C%9F.jpg/440px-%E3%83%8F%E3%82%A4%E3%83%86%E3%82%AF%E8%88%AA%E7%A9%BA%E5%86%99%E7%9C%9F.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%E3%83%8F%E3%82%A4%E3%83%86%E3%82%AF%E8%88%AA%E7%A9%BA%E5%86%99%E7%9C%9F.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hokkaido High-Technology College, one of the major tertiary education centers in Eniwa</div>

<p>Eniwa has two public high schools, five junior high schools, and eight elementary schools. In 2012, the city had 3,935 students enrolled at elementary schools and 2,079 at junior high schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In 2008, 300 students were enrolled at Eniwa North High School and 200 at Eniwa South High School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eniwa's first school was opened in 1887, when Buddhist priest Kyūzō Nakayama established a <a href="/wiki/Terakoya" title="Terakoya">terakoya</a> for the children of Eniwa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daian-ji_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Daian-ji-29">[29]</a></sup> In 1897, the temple school was moved and became a public school, Eniwa Elementary School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Three more elementary schools were opened in the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">Meiji period</a>. By the early 1940s, Eniwa had eight elementary schools. In 1947, four new junior high schools were created, later amalgamating into two by 1964. The two high schools were opened in 1951. In the 1960s and 1970s, five elementary schools and one junior high school were closed or merged. With the building of Megumino in the late 1970s and increasing growth in the city, three new junior high schools and six new elementary schools were built between 1970 and 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eniwa has one university and three <a href="/wiki/Vocational_school#Japan" title="Vocational school">vocational schools</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido_Bunkyo_University" title="Hokkaido Bunkyo University">Hokkaido Bunkyo University</a>'s main campus is in Eniwa. The university has two departments, foreign languages and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Healthcare_science" title="Healthcare science">health sciences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The three vocational schools are in Megumino. The largest, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hokkaido_High-Technology_College&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hokkaido High-Technology College (page does not exist)">Hokkaido High-Technology College</a>, is a multi-discipline school, with four faculties: technology, medicine, education and recovery/sports science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hokkaido_Eco_Communication_College&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hokkaido Eco Communication College (page does not exist)">Hokkaido Eco Communication College</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_medicine" title="Veterinary medicine">veterinary</a> school,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nihon_Fukushi_Rehabilitation_Gakuin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nihon Fukushi Rehabilitation Gakuin (page does not exist)">Nihon Fukushi Rehabilitation Gakuin</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Physical_medicine_and_rehabilitation" title="Physical medicine and rehabilitation">physical medicine and rehabilitation</a> school. In addition to these, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinki_University" title="Kinki University">Kinki University</a> has its Hokkaido seminar house for natural resource research in Eniwa.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eniwa,_Hokkaido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eniwa is connected to the <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido_Railway_Company" title="Hokkaido Railway Company">Hokkaido Railway</a> network on the <a href="/wiki/Chitose_Line" title="Chitose Line">Chitose Line</a>. There are four train stations (from north to south): <a href="/wiki/Shimamatsu_Station" title="Shimamatsu Station">Shimamatsu Station</a>, <a href="/wiki/Megumino_Station" title="Megumino Station">Megumino Station</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eniwa_Station" title="Eniwa Station">Eniwa Station</a>, as well as the electronically manned <a href="/wiki/Sapporo_Beer_Teien_Station" title="Sapporo Beer Teien Station">Sapporo Beer Teien Station</a>. The Eniwa Station is a designated stop for <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%82%A8%E3%82%A2%E3%83%9D%E3%83%BC%E3%83%88_(%E5%88%97%E8%BB%8A)" title="ja:エアポート (列車)">Rapid Airport</a> trains, though not a stop for <a href="/wiki/Limited_express#Japan" title="Limited express">limited express</a> trains such as the <a href="/wiki/Super_%C5%8Czora" title="Super Ōzora">Super Ōzora</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Super_Tokachi" title="Super Tokachi">Super Tokachi</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Japan_National_Route_36" title="Japan National Route 36">Japan National Route 36</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan_National_Route_453" title="Japan National Route 453">Japan National Route 453</a> run through Eniwa. There are two <a href="/wiki/Controlled-access_highway" title="Controlled-access highway">toll express</a> roads through Eniwa, the <a href="/wiki/Hokkaid%C5%8D_Expressway" title="Hokkaidō Expressway">Hokkaidō Expressway</a> and the <a href="/wiki/D%C5%8Dt%C5%8D_Expressway" title="Dōtō Expressway">Dōtō Expressway</a> which begins at the Chitose-Eniwa junction. There are two bus services in Eniwa. The Hokkaido Chuo Bus transports passengers around Hokkaido and passes through Eniwa. The Eniwa Community Bus was established in 2004 and circuits around Eniwa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OS-history_5-13"><a href="#cite_note-OS-history-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Eniwa is serviced by the <a href="/wiki/New_Chitose_Airport" title="New Chitose Airport">New Chitose Airport</a> for air travel, 15 km away. It is an international airport, with destinations mainly in Asia such as <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taipei" title="Taipei">Taipei</a>. However, the bulk of its traffic is Japanese domestic travelers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_work">Community work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eniwa,_Hokkaido&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Community work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
