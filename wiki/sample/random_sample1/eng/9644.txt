<p><b>Édouard Kodjovi Kodjo</b>, better known as <b>Edem Kodjo</b> (born May 23, 1938), is a <a href="/wiki/Togo" title="Togo">Togolese</a> politician and diplomat. He was Secretary-General of the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_of_African_Unity" title="Organisation of African Unity">Organisation of African Unity</a> from 1978 to 1983; later, in Togo, he was a prominent opposition leader after the introduction of multi-party politics. He served as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Togo" title="Prime Minister of Togo">Prime Minister</a> from 1994 to 1996 and again from 2005 to 2006. Kodjo is currently the President of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriotic_Pan-African_Convergence" title="Patriotic Pan-African Convergence">Patriotic Pan-African Convergence</a> (CPP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_and_diplomatic_career_from_the_1960s_to_the_1980s">Political and diplomatic career from the 1960s to the 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edem_Kodjo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Political and diplomatic career from the 1960s to the 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kodjo was born in <a href="/wiki/Sokod%C3%A9" title="Sokodé">Sokodé</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tchaoudjo" title="Tchaoudjo">Tchaoudjo Prefecture</a>, Togo on May 23, 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CENI_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-CENI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Profil_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Profil-3">[3]</a></sup> He had his secondary school education at West Africa Secondary School in Ghana. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CENI_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-CENI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Profil_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Profil-3">[3]</a></sup> After completing his studies in France, he was an administrator at the <i><a href="/wiki/Office_de_Radiodiffusion_T%C3%A9l%C3%A9vision_Fran%C3%A7aise" title="Office de Radiodiffusion Télévision Française">Office de Radiodiffusion Télévision Française</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Profil_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Profil-3">[3]</a></sup> from November 1964 to June 1967. He then returned to Togo and was appointed by President <a href="/wiki/Gnassingb%C3%A9_Eyad%C3%A9ma" title="Gnassingbé Eyadéma">Gnassingbé Eyadéma</a> as Secretary-General of the Ministry of Finance in July 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Visage_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Visage-4">[4]</a></sup> Kodjo participated in the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Rally_of_the_Togolese_People" title="Rally of the Togolese People">Rally of the Togolese People</a> (RPT) ruling party in late 1969 and became the new party's Secretary-General.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Profil_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Profil-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Visage_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Visage-4">[4]</a></sup> He also wrote the "Green Book", which served as the ideological basis for the establishment of the RPT's single-party rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDD_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDD-5">[5]</a></sup> He was removed from his position as RPT Secretary-General in 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Profil_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Profil-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kodjo served in Eyadéma's government as <a href="/wiki/Finance_minister" title="Finance minister">Minister of Finance</a> from 1973 to 1976 and as <a href="/wiki/Foreign_minister" title="Foreign minister">Minister of Foreign Affairs</a> from 1976 to 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Profil_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Profil-3">[3]</a></sup> He was elected as the Secretary-General of the Organisation of African Unity (OAU) at its summit in <a href="/wiki/Khartoum" title="Khartoum">Khartoum</a> on July 18–22, 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> One of the key issues facing the OAU during Kodjo's five-year tenure was the status of the <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic</a> (SADR), which claimed independence for the former colony of <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Sahara" title="Spanish Sahara">Spanish Sahara</a>, at that time partly occupied by Morocco. Kodjo controversially allowed the SADR to be seated as a member of the OAU on February 28, 1982, over the objections of Morocco and various other African countries that supported the Moroccan position. According to Kodjo, that decision was based simply on the fact that a majority of OAU member states had recognized the SADR, but it led to a serious crisis within the OAU, with a number of member states boycotting OAU meetings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegalese</a> President <a href="/wiki/Abdou_Diouf" title="Abdou Diouf">Abdou Diouf</a> accused Kodjo of "mischief-making".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>After leaving his post as OAU Secretary-General in 1983,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fief_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fief-9">[9]</a></sup> Kodjo lived in France, where he taught at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">Sorbonne</a>, wrote for <i><a href="/wiki/Jeune_Afrique" title="Jeune Afrique">Jeune Afrique</a></i>, and founded a magazine, <i>Afrique 2000</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Demo_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Demo-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1985 he published <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Africa_Tomorrow&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Africa Tomorrow (page does not exist)">Africa Tomorrow</a></i> in France, which was later translated into English by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=E._B._Khan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="E. B. Khan (page does not exist)">E. B. Khan</a> and published in the United States in 1987.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career_since_the_early_1990s">Political career since the early 1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edem_Kodjo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career since the early 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1991, a few months before the National Conference, Kodjo returned to Togo and founded a new opposition political party, the <a href="/wiki/Togolese_Union_for_Democracy" title="Togolese Union for Democracy">Togolese Union for Democracy</a> (UTD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Demo_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Demo-10">[10]</a></sup> On July 20, 1993,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chron_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chron-11">[11]</a></sup> he was designated by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Collective_of_Democratic_Opposition&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Collective of Democratic Opposition (page does not exist)">Collective of Democratic Opposition</a> (COD II) as its sole candidate for the <a href="/wiki/Togolese_presidential_election,_1993" title="Togolese presidential election, 1993">presidential election</a> of August 25, 1993,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Profil_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Profil-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chron_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chron-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> although <a href="/wiki/Gilchrist_Olympio" title="Gilchrist Olympio">Gilchrist Olympio</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Union_of_the_Forces_of_Change" title="Union of the Forces of Change">Union of the Forces of Change</a> (UFC) did not accept this decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chron_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chron-11">[11]</a></sup> Along with fellow opposition leaders <a href="/wiki/Yawovi_Agboyibo" title="Yawovi Agboyibo">Yawovi Agboyibo</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Djobo_Boukari&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Djobo Boukari (page does not exist)">Djobo Boukari</a>, Kodjo announced on August 22 that he was withdrawing his candidacy and boycotting the election due to the number of registered voters being considered too high—a possible sign of preparations to rig the election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Along with other opposition leaders, Kodjo pressured Eyadéma to hold a free and fair <a href="/wiki/Togolese_parliamentary_election,_1994" title="Togolese parliamentary election, 1994">parliamentary election in 1994</a>. In this election, the <a href="/wiki/Action_Committee_for_Renewal" title="Action Committee for Renewal">Action Committee for Renewal</a> (CAR) and the UTD together won an initial majority in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Togo" title="National Assembly of Togo">National Assembly</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-South_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-South-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Named_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Named-15">[15]</a></sup> the CAR with 36 seats and the UTD with seven;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Named_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Named-15">[15]</a></sup> Kodjo himself won a seat from <a href="/wiki/Lom%C3%A9" title="Lomé">Lomé</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The CAR and UTD initially agreed to nominate CAR President Yawovi Agboyibo as Prime Minister. However, after the election in three constituencies was cancelled, the two parties lost their narrow majority, and Eyadéma invited Kodjo to form a government,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-South_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-South-14">[14]</a></sup> announcing his appointment as Prime Minister on April 22, 1994;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chron_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chron-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Named_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Named-15">[15]</a></sup> he took office on April 25.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chron_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Chron-11">[11]</a></sup> His Press Secretary was Kodjo Epou, a well known journalist of the national television station (TVT)in Togo.The CAR regarded Kodjo's appointment as a violation of the parties' agreement and refused to participate in his government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-South_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-South-14">[14]</a></sup> His acceptance of the position of Prime Minister, in addition to his earlier role in the establishment of the RPT regime, discredited him in the eyes of many opposition supporters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDD_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDD-5">[5]</a></sup> His government was announced on May 25, 1994; it included the RPT, the UTD, and some smaller parties not represented in the National Assembly. Although headed by Kodjo, the composition of the government was strongly dominated by the RPT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kodjo served as Prime Minister of Togo until August 20, 1996. He resigned as Prime Minister after the RPT won the elections that were held over again in the constituencies where the results had been annulled, giving the RPT and its allies a parliamentary majority; a new government under <a href="/wiki/Kwassi_Klutse" title="Kwassi Klutse">Kwassi Klutse</a> of the RPT was formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-South_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-South-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the night of August 13, 1997, <a href="/wiki/Tear_gas" title="Tear gas">tear gas</a> canisters were thrown at Kodjo's house while he was exiting it with guests. The UTD alleged that the canisters were thrown from a police vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Kodjo announced on May 4, 1998 that he would not be a candidate in the <a href="/wiki/Togolese_presidential_election,_1998" title="Togolese presidential election, 1998">June 1998 presidential election</a>, stressing the need for opposition unity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He backed the leading opposition candidate, UFC President Gilchrist Olympio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kodjo subsequently became the leader of a new party, the Patriotic Pan-African Convergence (CPP), which was created in August 1999<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Briefing_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Briefing-21">[21]</a></sup> through the merger of four parties,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Briefing_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Briefing-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PP_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-PP-22">[22]</a></sup> including the UTD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PP_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-PP-22">[22]</a></sup> Kodjo ran as the CPP's candidate in the <a href="/wiki/Togolese_presidential_election,_2003" title="Togolese presidential election, 2003">June 2003 presidential election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PP_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-PP-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Security_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Security-23">[23]</a></sup> Kodjo criticized Eyadéma for not honoring his pledge to step down in the 2003 election and again called for the opposition to put forward a single candidate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> During the campaign, the CPP called for a debate on television between Kodjo and Eyadéma after the RPT engaged in what the CPP considered personal attacks on Kodjo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Security_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Security-23">[23]</a></sup> In the election, Kodjo received 0.96% of the vote according to official results<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dem_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dem-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sans_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sans-26">[26]</a></sup> and took fifth place;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dem_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dem-25">[25]</a></sup> he denounced the results as fraudulent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the disputed <a href="/wiki/Togolese_presidential_election,_2005" title="Togolese presidential election, 2005">April 2005 presidential election</a>, which occurred shortly after Eyadema's death, Eyadema's son and successor <a href="/wiki/Faure_Gnassingb%C3%A9" title="Faure Gnassingbé">Faure Gnassingbé</a> named Kodjo, a representative of the moderate opposition, as Prime Minister again on June 8, 2005, choosing him instead of a candidate from the radical opposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sans_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sans-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> He took office on June 9, succeeding <a href="/wiki/Koffi_Sama" title="Koffi Sama">Koffi Sama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an announcement on September 16, 2006, Gnassingbé accepted Kodjo's resignation and named <a href="/wiki/Yawovi_Agboyibo" title="Yawovi Agboyibo">Yawovi Agboyibo</a> as Prime Minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> On September 25, Gnassingbé appointed Kodjo by decree as Minister of State to the Presidency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Togolese_parliamentary_election,_2007" title="Togolese parliamentary election, 2007">October 2007 parliamentary election</a>, Kodjo ran for a seat in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Togo" title="National Assembly of Togo">National Assembly</a> as a candidate of the CPP in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Av%C3%A9,_Togo" title="Avé, Togo">Avé Prefecture</a>, where he was the first name on the party's candidate list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CENI_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-CENI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fief_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fief-9">[9]</a></sup> The CPP did not win any seats in the election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Results_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Results-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>At a CPP congress in late April 2009, Kodjo announced that he was retiring from day-to-day politics in order to make way for younger leadership. He also said that he would not be a candidate in the <a href="/wiki/Togolese_presidential_election,_2010" title="Togolese presidential election, 2010">2010 presidential election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Acting as the Special Envoy of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Francophonie" title="La Francophonie">La Francophonie</a>, he arrived in <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> to help <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malagasy_political_crisis,_2009" title="Malagasy political crisis, 2009">mediate in that country's political crisis</a> on May 8, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In an interview with <i>Jeune Afrique</i>, published in May 2009, he said that he had served his country in all possible capacities, except that of President: "I have made my contribution to the construction of my country". According to Kodjo, he had lost interest in "internal politics" and preferred to devote himself to Pan-Africanism by working to facilitate a cooperative approach to finding solutions to African problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
