<p><i><b>Eden</b></i> <i>(Abduction of Eden)</i> is a 2012 American dramatic film about <a href="/wiki/Human_trafficking" title="Human trafficking">human trafficking</a>. It was directed by <a href="/wiki/Megan_Griffiths" title="Megan Griffiths">Megan Griffiths</a>, who co-wrote the screenplay with Richard B. Phillips and stars <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Chung" title="Jamie Chung">Jamie Chung</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matt_O%27Leary" title="Matt O'Leary">Matt O'Leary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beau_Bridges" title="Beau Bridges">Beau Bridges</a>. The film was produced by Colin Harper Plank and Jacob Mosler through Plank's Centripetal Films production company. It was inspired by the story of Chong Kim, who claims that she was kidnapped and sold into a domestic human trafficking ring in the mid 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holden_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holden-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirklandreporter1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-kirklandreporter1-3">[3]</a></sup> It had its world premiere at the 2012 <a href="/wiki/South_by_Southwest" title="South by Southwest">South by Southwest</a> Film Festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014, the non-profit organization Breaking Out claimed they had investigated Kim's story and concluded it was false and aimed to defraud charitable organizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-salon_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-salon-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmader_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schmader-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reason_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-reason-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-truth_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-truth-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eden_(2012_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hyun Jae, or Jae for short, is an 18-year-old Korean-American girl living in New Mexico in 1994 with her immigrant parents, working at their taxidermy shop. After convincing her reluctant mother to let her stay out late on a Friday, she joins her friend Abbie at a local bar with a fake ID. Jae meets a good-looking young firefighter and accepts his offer to drive her home after Abbie vanishes. In the car, Jae notices there is a policeman costume in the backseat and that the firefighter badge is fake. Jae attempts to escape from the car but is caught. She is bound, gagged, and thrown into the trunk of a second car driven by a second man. The Scenes cut to a U.S Marshal named Bob Gault who follows a tracking bracelet tracking devise to a scene where an officer is investigating the death of a dead girl who is fitted with an ankle tracking bracelet in a ditch. Bob Gault questions the officer if he reported the girl, after the officer responds no, Bob shoots the officer as well as a bystander.</p>
<p>Jae is taken to an isolated warehouse in the middle of the desert, where she is put in a makeshift hospital room , where she is sedated and a nurse forcible removes her braces and other belongings disposed of and is fitted with an ankle tracking bracelet. Jae meets Bob, who is the presumed leader of the organization that captured her and his overseer, the violently short-tempered and drug-addicted Vaughan who uses his connections to the government to obtain information on her and her family. After Bob confirms her suspicions that she is now part of a <a href="/wiki/Human_trafficking" title="Human trafficking">sex-trafficking</a> ring, Gault christens her with a new name, Eden, after the place where she used to live with her family, and tells her that any acts of trouble could end with the harm of her family. Bob tells her that she has a few days to accept this reality and, after that, she will start working for him. Living with many young girls from all parts of the world who are frequently picked out for “work,". Eden is forced to take pregnancy tests and given mystery injections by the organization's nurse along with all the other girls. Eden is first forced to star in an adult video with her bunk mate Priscilla where they are beaten in BDSM fashion causing Eden to have a breakdown where she is comforted by Priscilla. Later, Vaughan takes Eden to meet her first "private client," where she attempts to escape by injuring the client and running to a neighboring house for help. Vaughan quickly captures Eden and, after convincing the startled neighbors not to call police, takes her back to the warehouse, where she is punished by being forced to lie in a tub full of ice for several hours, while Vaughn is scolded by Bob for letting Eden escape as well as the girl Bob found in the ditch.</p>
<p>A year passes, and Eden has full befriended Priscilla, who warns her not to let the leaders of the organization know her real age. Before Eden can learn more, Priscilla is abruptly taken away after a pregnancy test. Eden now knows she has little time left before she is no longer useful to her captors and may be killed. Eden notices that Vaughan's trustee Svetlana has the ring her father gave her the day she was abducted. Eden hides a makeshift weapon from a heel and attacks Svetlana one night in an attempt to get her ring back from Svetlana. After the fight is broken up and Eden recovers her ring, Vaughn forces Eden to eat the ring as punishment. Eden then proceeds to tell Vaughan that he’s being consistently scammed by their clients and offers to become his new trustee to keep track of things. At a frat party where she and 2 other girls were hired to perform, Eden starts acting as Vaughn's cashier and assists Vaghaun in securing full payment owed to him and later assist in recapturing the 2 girls who attempted to escape during the frat party confrontation effectively gaining Vaughan's trust. Eden's ring passes through her system and she recovers it. Vaughn draws Eden out of sex work and makes her clean his house and then has her take over Svetlana's job of answering the phones to Sventlana's protest who is told she will start driving now. When Sventlana leaves, Eden is instructed how to run the phones and hears a gunshot in the distance. Vaughn's last order is that Eden is to hang up the phone if any client asks for a price as it is probably the feds.</p>
<p>Eden starts working regularly with Vaughn and assists Vaughn in transporting girls and drugs. Bob is shown giving a lecture on drug trafficking at a law enforcement conference where another officer questions him on the disappearance of an officer a year ago as a GPS on Bob's phone places him at the location of the officer when he vanished where Bob denies any knowledge. While working the phones, Eden gets a call from a man asking about the price, Eden hesitantly gives the price to the customer who Vaughn sends one of his henchmen, Ivan to deal with the order. The next morning, Vaughn takes Eden to a fishing trip with Bob where Vaughn has brought 3 bodies to be disposed of in the lake including Sventlana's as Eden looks on in grief at the bag that contains Sventlana's remains. While on the lake, after Bob makes fun of Vaughn for his low up bringing, Vaughn proceeds to murder Bob and declares that it was an order from the top (presumably because of his implication in the murder of the officer).</p>
<p>Vaughan eventually takes Eden to the house of a man named Mario to drop off a girl and pick one up. Recognizing Mario as the one who brought her to the warehouse in the trunk of his car, Eden grows suspicious and explores his house, discovering a pregnant Priscilla, who is pleasantly ignorant of the fate of her unborn child (Mario's house is where pregnant captives give birth, and their babies are sold). Eden leaves visibly disturbed. Vaughan makes her prove her loyalty to the organization by forcing her to almost execute one of the other girls, stopping her at the last second.</p>
<p>When Eden and Vaughan return to the warehouse, they are told Ivan betrayed the organization (presumably because of Eden sent Ivan to a federal sting). All of the girls are packed into vans and their belongings are burned and disposed of. Eden and Vaughan go to a safe house to collect what they need before the trip. Vaughan reveals to Eden that they are moving to <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a>, before leaving her on the couch. Eden decides that she must make her escape. Eden takes Vaughan’s meth pipe from the table and sprays over-the-counter cleaning solution into the tube before placing it in the same spot. When Vaughan returns, he tells her that she doesn’t have to worry about Dubai and that they are “partners.” He takes a hit from his pipe, begins to choke and dies. Eden then cuts her ankle tracking bracelet from her ankle and drives to Mario’s house, where she tricks him into leading her to Priscilla’s room before injecting him with morphine, killing him. Running into Priscilla’s room, Eden reveals that her baby was sold and that they have to leave. They drive off into the desert until they reach a pay phone. The final scene is Eden summoning the courage to call her parents with her hanging up the phone a few times until eventually calling her Mother and finally being addressed by her birth name by her crying in joy mother while Hyun Jae looks at her distorted image in the payphone reflection before the screen cuts out.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eden_(2012_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Eden</i> was produced by Centripetal Films and filmed in Washington State in the summer of 2011, with a largely local crew and funding assistance from Washington Filmworks, a government supported non-profit organization that provides financial support for films produced in Washington State. The idea for the film came into being when screenwriter Richard B. Phillips contacted Chong Kim after reading a newspaper article about her account of being sold into and escaping a domestic human trafficking ring. Phillips then proposed the idea of writing a screenplay based on Kim's story. Kim agreed, and the two of them set about writing a story for the film. That story was later turned into a screenplay by Phillips. Plank then hired Megan Griffiths to revise the script, and later hired Griffiths to direct the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirklandreporter1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-kirklandreporter1-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eden_(2012_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Eden</i> has a score of 82% on <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> based on 33 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Robert Abele of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> said the film was "never less than suspenseful, but rather than sentimentally pander to easy outrage, or indulge in icky women-in-distress titillation, the movie...zeros in on the details of how dignity can be stripped like bark from a tree, and the queasy determination it takes to stay alive in a living hell."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Shannon" title="Jeff Shannon">Jeff Shannon</a> from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seattle_Times" title="Seattle Times">Seattle Times</a></i> awarded the film with 3 out of 4 stars and said that "Cruelty, bloodletting and death are evident throughout (frequently occurring just outside the frame), and Griffith's laudable discretion actually intensifies their impact."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Holden" title="Stephen Holden">Stephen Holden</a> of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> said that "Enough films about human trafficking have been made in recent years that the outlines of <i>Eden</i> should be painfully familiar. But that familiarity doesn’t cushion this movie’s excruciating vision."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holden_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holden-1">[1]</a></sup> Along with Holden's review Eden was also selected as one of New York Times Critics' Pick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holden_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holden-1">[1]</a></sup> Stephanie Carrie of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Village_Voice" title="Village Voice">Village Voice</a></i> said "overall, this is a powerful addition to the small collection of films dedicated to spreading awareness of this horrific crime."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many critics particularly praised the performance of Jamie Chung. Peter Bradshaw of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> said that "a very strong performance from Chung impresses".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Matthew Turner of <i>ViewLondon</i> said "Chung duly steps up, delivering an emotionally complex performance that requires her to keep her true feelings closely guarded (we also see her distance herself from her work by adopting a different persona)" and ended by saying that it was a "terrific central performance from Jamie Chung".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kauffmann" title="Stanley Kauffmann">Stanley Kauffmann</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Republic" title="The New Republic">The New Republic</a></i> wrote "a quite moving performance comes from Jamie Chung as Eden, repulsion sliding into fearful acceptance without the extinction of hope."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some reviews were negative. Farran Smith Nehme of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Post" title="New York Post">New York Post</a></i> called it "a Lifetime movie gone upscale-grindhouse" and criticized the South by Southwest festival's decision to give the film an award. Nehme also questioned the film's authenticity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> David Fear of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Out_(magazine)" title="Time Out (magazine)">Time Out</a></i> said the film's second half "head[s] south of ludicrous, [and] Eden’s goodwill dissipates." Fear also questioned the film's authenticity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accusations_of_fraud">Accusations of fraud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eden_(2012_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Accusations of fraud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On June 4, 2014, the non-profit anti-trafficking organization Breaking Out announced on their <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a> page that, after a year-long investigation of Chong Kim and her life, they had "found no truth to her story. In fact, we found a lot of fraud, lies, and most horrifically capitalizing and making money on an issue where so many people are suffering from [sic]." Breaking Out also accused Kim of defrauding several organizations out of charitable donations and announced a coming lawsuit. Later that day, Griffiths commented on her <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> account, "I just heard about this today and it came as a shock. I am deeply concerned about it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmader_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schmader-6">[6]</a></sup> <i>Eden'</i>s official website claims that "A portion of the net profits from <i>EDEN</i> will benefit anti-slavery organizations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Kim responded on her Facebook page, stating "I don't appreciate you spreading lies about me … Whatever you claim to have I have the right to see it otherwise I will send you and your organization a formal complaint."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reason_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-reason-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>After these accusations arose, <a href="/wiki/Noah_Berlatsky" title="Noah Berlatsky">Noah Berlatsky</a> of <i>Salon</i> compared Kim to <a href="/wiki/Somaly_Mam" title="Somaly Mam">Somaly Mam</a> and commented that the film was thickly layered with exploitation tropes and improbable scenarios. Berlatsky found it shocking that "anyone took this clearly fanciful, clearly derivative fiction for fact".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-salon_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-salon-5">[5]</a></sup> Journalist Elizabeth Nolan Brown criticized "the authors who repeated Kim's story, the journalists who interviewed her, the organizations that brought her on as a speaker, or any of the myriad people behind the 'based on a true story' <i>Eden</i>" for not checking and verifying Kim's claims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reason_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-reason-7">[7]</a></sup> Journalist Mike Ludwig argued that the narrative promoted by the film harmed consensual sex workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-truth_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-truth-8">[8]</a></sup> Seattle sex worker <a href="/wiki/Mistress_Matisse" title="Mistress Matisse">Mistress Matisse</a> had been questioning the veracity of the film since 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmader_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schmader-6">[6]</a></sup> Matisse stated that Colin Plank and Megan Griffiths "perpetrated a fraud in their movie called Eden." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graves_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graves-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Stranger_(newspaper)" title="The Stranger (newspaper)">The Stranger</a></i> journalist David Schmader had previously called the film a masterpiece and the best film ever made in Seattle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmader2013_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schmader2013-20">[20]</a></sup> but has since called its back story "bullshit."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmader_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Schmader-6">[6]</a></sup> <i>Stranger</i> writer Jen Graves was highly critical of Griffith's lack of research into Kim's story and regretted her initial support of the film. Since Breaking Out first made their allegations, Griffiths and others directly associated with the film have refused to comment further,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graves_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Graves-19">[19]</a></sup> but the film's official website continues to promote the film as "Inspired by the harrowing true story of Chong Kim."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 2012 Griffiths won the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Stranger_Genius_Awards_winners" title="List of Stranger Genius Awards winners">Stranger Film Genius Award</a>. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Mudede" title="Charles Mudede">Charles Mudede</a>, a <i>Stranger</i> writer and former winner of the award, wrote that <i>Eden</i> had "secured her nomination" and was "a masterpiece".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On June 25, 2014 Kim's PR firm Bradshaw &amp; Co. publicly denied the allegations and referenced a pending lawsuit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Since its initial allegation, Breaking Out has continued to release information about Kim on their Facebook page, including court documents detailing Kim’s 2009 felony charge of theft by swindle, for which Kim was ordered to pay $15,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graves_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Graves-19">[19]</a></sup> "to another human trafficking victim and activist in Minnesota."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-truth_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-truth-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_accolades">Awards and accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eden_(2012_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Awards and accolades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film premiered at the 2012 <a href="/wiki/South_by_Southwest" title="South by Southwest">South by Southwest</a> film festival, where it won the Audience Award for Best Narrative Feature, Special Jury Recognition for Best Actress, Jamie Chung, and the award for Emergent Narrative Female Director, Megan Griffiths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>It also won the <a href="/wiki/Seattle_International_Film_Festival#Golden_Space_Needle_.28Best_Film.29_and_SIFF_Awards_for_Best_Short_and_Documentary" title="Seattle International Film Festival">Golden Space Needle Award</a> for Best Actress, Jamie Chung and the Lena Sharpe Award for Persistence of Vision; Seattle Reel NW Award at the 2012 Seattle International Film Festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>It also won the Audience Award for Best Film at the 2012 <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Asian_Film_Festival" title="San Diego Asian Film Festival">San Diego Asian Film Festival</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eden_(2012_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Eden</i> was released theatrically in North America by Phase 4 Films on March 20, 2013 at Film Forum in New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> It also played theatrically in Los Angeles and Seattle, followed by a release on VOD on April 20, 2013. It was released on DVD on June 11, 2013. The DVD and Blu-ray were released in the UK by Queensway Digital Ltd on September 9, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>A May 2013 screening in Seattle included a panel discussion about human trafficking sponsored by <a href="/wiki/Hope_for_Justice" title="Hope for Justice">Hope for Justice</a> and the <a href="/wiki/World_Affairs_Council_of_Seattle" title="World Affairs Council of Seattle">World Affairs Council of Seattle</a>, with panelists including Washington State senator <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_Kohl-Welles" title="Jeanne Kohl-Welles">Jeanne Kohl-Welles</a> and YouthCare social worker Leslie Briner. Kohl-Welles had sponsored an anti-sex trafficking bill in the Washington State congress earlier that year<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and would be a major supporter of the Motion Picture Competitiveness Bill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Kohl-Welles and Griffiths were later panelists of the 2014 Seattle Film Summit, which called Kohl-Welles "the most vocal and reliable supporters of the State’s film industry",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and Griffiths spoke at an October 2014 fundraiser in support of Kohl-Welles's re-election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Writing_better_articles#Stay_on_topic" title="Wikipedia:Writing better articles"><span title="The paragraph near this tag may contain information that is not relevant to the article's main topic. (May 2015)">relevant?</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Eden_(2012_film)" title="Talk:Eden (2012 film)">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The international distribution rights were licensed by <a href="/wiki/Cinema_Management_Group" title="Cinema Management Group">Cinema Management Group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
