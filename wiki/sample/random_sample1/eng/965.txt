<p><b>Football Club Nordsjælland</b> (<b>FCN</b>; <small>Danish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Danish" title="Help:IPA for Danish">[ˈnoɐ̯ɕɛlɑnˀ]</a></span>) is a professional Danish <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> team from the <a href="/wiki/North_Zealand" title="North Zealand">North Zealand</a> town of <a href="/wiki/Farum" title="Farum">Farum</a>. Founded as <i>Farum Boldklub</i> from the merger of the town's two football clubs Farum IK and Stavnsholt BK in 1991, the club changed its name to FC Nordsjælland in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>FCN plays in the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Superliga" title="Danish Superliga">Danish Superliga</a>, winning its first medal in the <a href="/wiki/2002%E2%80%9303_Danish_Superliga" title="2002–03 Danish Superliga">2002–03 season</a>, taking third place. Since then, the <i>Wild Tigers</i> have made four appearances in Europe under both the old <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Europa_League" title="UEFA Europa League">UEFA Cup</a> format in <a href="/wiki/2003%E2%80%9304_UEFA_Cup" title="2003–04 UEFA Cup">2003–04</a>, <a href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_UEFA_Cup" title="2008–09 UEFA Cup">2008–09</a> and in the UEFA Europa League during the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_UEFA_Europa_League" title="2010–11 UEFA Europa League">2010–11</a> and <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_UEFA_Europa_League" title="2011–12 UEFA Europa League">2011–12</a> seasons. In <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_Danish_Cup" title="2009–10 Danish Cup">2010</a>, the club won its first <a href="/wiki/Danish_Cup" title="Danish Cup">Danish Cup</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and successfully defended it the following year in <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_Danish_Cup" title="2010–11 Danish Cup">2011</a>, beating <a href="/wiki/FC_Midtjylland" title="FC Midtjylland">Midtjylland</a> in both finals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> FCN won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011-12_Danish_Superliga" title="2011-12 Danish Superliga">2011-12 Danish Superliga</a> in May 2012 which qualified the team to participate in the <a href="/wiki/2012%E2%80%9313_UEFA_Champions_League" title="2012–13 UEFA Champions League">2012–13 UEFA Champions League</a>.</p>
<p>Nordsjælland plays its home matches at <a href="/wiki/Farum_Park" title="Farum Park">Right to Dream Park</a>, which has a capacity of 10,100 of which 9,800 seating and 300 standing spectators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-farum_park_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-farum_park-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The club is known for focusing on youth and talent development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> Their playing style is often referred to as aggressive and attacking minded football, where they play a 4–2–3–1 system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FC_Nordsj%C3%A6lland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Farum_BK_.281991.E2.80.932003.29">Farum BK (1991–2003)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FC_Nordsj%C3%A6lland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Farum BK (1991–2003)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Farum_Boldklub_logo.png"><img alt="refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="200" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/92/Farum_Boldklub_logo.png" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Farum_Boldklub_logo.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Farum Boldklub badge.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Established on 1 January 1991, from the merger of two football clubs, Farum Idræts Klub (formed in 1910) and Stavnsholt Boldklub af 1974,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup> both from the then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farum_municipality" title="Farum municipality">Farum municipality</a> (now consolidated with <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A6rl%C3%B8se" title="Værløse">Værløse</a> as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fures%C3%B8_municipality" title="Furesø municipality">Furesø municipality</a>), Farum BK would become one of the few merger success stories in Danish football, but not without controversy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup> The club was an initiative local <a href="/wiki/Farum" title="Farum">Farum</a> residence<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fcn_history1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-fcn_history1-11">[11]</a></sup> and of then local <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Brixtofte" title="Peter Brixtofte">Peter Brixtofte</a>, whom took a personal interest in the club, as well as garnered sponsorship for the team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brixtofte_scandal_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-brixtofte_scandal-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-totalbold_fcn1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-totalbold_fcn1-13">[13]</a></sup> The club kit colours became a combination of the two merged clubs, the red and white of Stavnsholt with the yellow and blue of F.I.K. combined into a kit with yellow and red striped shirts and dark blue shorts and socks, which is still used in some form to this day. Colours were not the only thing the newly formed club inherited, as Thomas Andreasen who had been with Stavnsholt BK was carried over into the new Farum squad, Andreasen would go on to make a record 295 appearances, playing from the <a href="/wiki/Denmark_Series" title="Denmark Series">Denmark Series</a> all the way to the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Superliga" title="Danish Superliga">Danish Superliga</a>, until his departure from the club in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fcn_history1_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-fcn_history1-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Farum BK was placed in the second group of the <a href="/wiki/Denmark_Series" title="Denmark Series">Denmark Series</a>, the fourth tier in the <a href="/wiki/Football_in_Denmark" title="Football in Denmark">Danish football pyramid</a>, though it gained promotion to the first group after the club's maiden season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup> Jørgen Andersen, a former goalkeeper for <a href="/wiki/Hvidovre_IF" title="Hvidovre IF">Hvidovre</a>, took over as the club's first head coach in 1992; the club stayed in the Denmark Series first group for six years. Under the guidance of manager Jørgen Tideman, who took over in 1994, Farum qualified for promotion into the <a href="/wiki/Danish_2nd_Division" title="Danish 2nd Division">2nd Division</a> in the 1997–98 season and subsequently turned professional for the first time in club history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fcn_history1_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-fcn_history1-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Farum's first full season as a professional club was a fruitful one, edging out <a href="/wiki/Aalborg_Chang" title="Aalborg Chang">Aalborg Chang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skive_IK" title="Skive IK">Skive</a> to fifth place by one point, gaining promotion for the second time in two years into the <a href="/wiki/Danish_1st_Division" title="Danish 1st Division">1st Division</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup> The club's meteoric rise was slowed at first with the new challenge of playing in the Danish second tier, though it was not stopped however, ending the 1999–2000 campaign with a respectable eighth-place finish, winning the same amount of games as it lost. Farum, however, remained unable to make any sort of impact in the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Cup" title="Danish Cup">Danish Cup</a>, having lost in the first round for the last three years since making its debut in the tournament in the 1997–98 season. The following year saw some progression in both the league and cup, improving on the previous year's finish by finishing fifth, three spots and nine points behind second-placed promotion winners <a href="/wiki/Vejle_Boldklub" title="Vejle Boldklub">Vejle</a>, as well as breaking out of the first round of the Danish Cup to eventually fall to <a href="/wiki/Fremad_Amager" title="Fremad Amager">Fremad Amager</a> in the third round following a 2–1 loss.</p>
<p>With the <a href="/wiki/2001%E2%80%9302_Danish_1st_Division" title="2001–02 Danish 1st Division">2001–02</a> season, unconventional coach <a href="/wiki/Christian_Andersen_(footballer)" title="Christian Andersen (footballer)">Christian Andersen</a> was brought in to manage the team, building on the gradual footholds the club had been making in the 1st Division. <a href="/wiki/Jeppe_Tengbjerg" title="Jeppe Tengbjerg">Jeppe Tengbjerg</a> played a pivotal role, brought in from <a href="/wiki/Boldklubben_af_1893" title="Boldklubben af 1893">B.93</a> the previous year; he scored 16 goals, becoming Farum's top goalscorer of the season and third overall in the league. The team went on to earn promotion into the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Superliga" title="Danish Superliga">Superliga</a> after finishing in second place, 11 points clear of third-placed <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B8nderjyskE_Fodbold" title="SønderjyskE Fodbold">Sønderjylland</a>, one point behind first-place winners <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B8ge_BK" title="Køge BK">Køge</a>, scoring a team total of 69 goals, the highest that season and losing only four games.</p>
<p>Farum's <a href="/wiki/2002%E2%80%9303_Danish_Superliga" title="2002–03 Danish Superliga">2002–03</a> appearance in the top flight of Danish football would be its first and last under the Farum BK name. it ended the season in third place, thus qualifying for the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Europa_League" title="UEFA Europa League">UEFA Cup</a> for the first time in its history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup> The club's achievement, however, was overshadowed by the controversy involving Peter Brixtofte, who had helped form and fund the club, with deals involving his municipality deliberately overpaying for welfare services bought from private companies who in return would sponsor the Farum football team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brixtofte_scandal_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-brixtofte_scandal-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Brixtofte was forced to step down as chairman and the club became close to bankruptcy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fcn_history1_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-fcn_history1-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FC_Nordsj.C3.A6lland_.282003.E2.80.93present.29">FC Nordsjælland (2003–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FC_Nordsj%C3%A6lland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: FC Nordsjælland (2003–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Full_time_score_01.JPG"><img alt="refer to caption" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="393" data-file-width="550" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Full_time_score_01.JPG/220px-Full_time_score_01.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Full_time_score_01.JPG/330px-Full_time_score_01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Full_time_score_01.JPG/440px-Full_time_score_01.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Full_time_score_01.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The full-time score at Farum Park.</div>

<p>In March 2003, Farum BK was bought by AKP Holding, the holdings company of local businessman <a href="/wiki/Allan_K._Pedersen" title="Allan K. Pedersen">Allan K. Pedersen</a>, and in an effort to distance the club from the Brixtofte scandal, Farum BK was re-branded as FC Nordsjælland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fcn_history1_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-fcn_history1-11">[11]</a></sup> named after the <a href="/wiki/North_Zealand" title="North Zealand">North Zealand</a> (Danish: Nordsjælland) region to clarify the club was to represent both the region as well as the town in which the club is based.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup> To reinforce the status as a regional team, a network of local football clubs from the surrounding area was created, consisting of around 66 teams, with the aim to highlight young talent in the region and bring it to national attention via FC Nordsjælland. The network is known as Fodbold Samarbejde Nordsjælland (FSN).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-history-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The club's second year in the Superliga, first as Nordsjælland, struggled to improve on the previous year's outing. With its worst goal difference since turning professional, not one FCN player ended in the top ten goal scorers, fighting to avoid relegation for most of the season, ending the campaign in ninth place. The <i>Wild Tiger</i> fans, however, were rewarded with European football with the club's first appearance in the UEFA Cup; it beat Armenian team <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shirak_F.C." title="Shirak F.C.">Shirak</a> 6–0 on aggregate in the qualifying round, but were eliminated in the first round by Greek team <a href="/wiki/Panionios_F.C." title="Panionios F.C.">Panionios</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup> Christian Andersen was sacked at the end of the <a href="/wiki/2003%E2%80%9304_Danish_Superliga" title="2003–04 Danish Superliga">2003–04 season</a>, replaced by <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Petersen" title="Johnny Petersen">Johnny Petersen</a> as head coach.</p>
<p>The following two years under Johnny Petersen were spent avoiding relegation, with no success to speak of in either the Superliga or Danish Cup. Petersen's reign was not completely amiss, as he was noted for creating a good young team and the emergence of players <a href="/wiki/Mads_Junker" title="Mads Junker">Mads Junker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anders_Due" title="Anders Due">Anders Due</a>. For the <a href="/wiki/2006%E2%80%9307_Danish_Superliga" title="2006–07 Danish Superliga">2006–07 season</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morten_Wieghorst" title="Morten Wieghorst">Morten Wieghorst</a> was promoted from assistant to head coach,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-totalbold_fcn1_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-totalbold_fcn1-13">[13]</a></sup> a position the former <a href="/wiki/Celtic_F.C." title="Celtic F.C.">Celtic</a> player would retain for five years.</p>
<p>Wieghorst's first dilemma in charge of FCN was to fill the gap left by top scorer Mads Junker, sale to Dutch side <a href="/wiki/SBV_Vitesse" title="SBV Vitesse">Vitesse</a> the previous winter. <a href="/wiki/Morten_Nordstrand" title="Morten Nordstrand">Morten Nordstrand</a> came in on a free from nearby <a href="/wiki/Lyngby_Boldklub" title="Lyngby Boldklub">Lyngby</a> after scoring 29 goals in the 1st Division that previous season. Nordstrand would go on to make an instant impact, topping the goal scorers charts for the first half of the 2006–07 season and earning himself a call up to the <a href="/wiki/Denmark_national_football_team" title="Denmark national football team">Denmark national team</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-totalbold_fcn1_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-totalbold_fcn1-13">[13]</a></sup> Helping the club to a fifth-place finish, Nordstrand ended the season with 18 goals after appearing in every league match that season. <a href="/wiki/List_of_Danish_football_champions" title="List of Danish football champions">Danish champions</a> <a href="/wiki/F.C._Copenhagen" title="F.C. Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a> purchased the player at the end of the season for a then record 15 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danish_kroner" title="Danish kroner">Danish kroner</a>, becoming the largest transfer fee ever paid for a player between two Danish clubs.</p>
<p>With lower-than-average attendance and issues still arising from the Brixtofte scandal, chairman Allan Kim Pedersen confirmed there had been discussions to move the club north to <a href="/wiki/Hiller%C3%B8d" title="Hillerød">Hillerød</a>, where it would be able expand to other sports such as <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The move, however, never materialized.</p>
<p>Nordsjælland would find itself in a similar position the following season, again having sold its star striker <a href="/wiki/Martin_Bernburg" title="Martin Bernburg">Martin Bernburg</a> to Copenhagen. The team only managed a ninth-place finish, yet qualified for the UEFA Cup for the second time via the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Respect_Fair_Play_ranking" title="UEFA Respect Fair Play ranking">UEFA Respect Fair Play rankings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_Danish_Superliga" title="2008–09 Danish Superliga">2008–09</a> would become a good year for the <i>Wild Tigers</i> in terms of cup competitions, making it to the quarter-finals in the Danish Cup for the second time in its history, and improving on its previous European outing with wins over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FC_TVMK_Tallinn" title="FC TVMK Tallinn">TVMK Tallinn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queen_of_the_South_F.C." title="Queen of the South F.C.">Queen of the South</a>, though later being knocked out by Greek side <a href="/wiki/Olympiacos_F.C." title="Olympiacos F.C.">Olympiacos</a> 0–7 aggregate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-13"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2008, Allan K. Pedersen sold FC Nordsjælland from AKP Holding to himself for a reported 500,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danish_kroner" title="Danish kroner">Danish kroner</a>, shortly before his holding company went bankrupt, a price Pedersen, however, denies. Following an investigation from his creditors, it was found that the sale was forced through without the bank's consent, and that the value for which the club was sold was too low, which ultimate reduced the finances the creators received for the sale. FCN was reevaluated to be worth 35 million kroner at the time of sale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The case has gone to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_(Denmark)" title="Supreme Court (Denmark)">Supreme Court</a> and is yet to be resolved; it speculated that it could take one-to-four years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_Danish_Superliga" title="2009–10 Danish Superliga">2009–10 season</a> saw Nordsjælland lift its first trophy, the <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_Danish_Cup" title="2009–10 Danish Cup">Danish Cup</a>. FCN was not drawn against another Superliga team until the quarter-final meeting with <a href="/wiki/Silkeborg_IF" title="Silkeborg IF">Silkeborg</a>, where it won 3–1 in extra time. The team would go on to face <a href="/wiki/FC_Midtjylland" title="FC Midtjylland">Midtjylland</a> in Nordsjælland's first cup final, winning in extra time<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-14"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup> 2–0 with goals from new signing <a href="/wiki/Nicolai_Stokholm" title="Nicolai Stokholm">Nicolai Stokholm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bajram_Fetai" title="Bajram Fetai">Bajram Fetai</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-danish_cup1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-danish_cup1-20">[20]</a></sup> and qualifying for European competition in the newly remodeled UEFA Europa League. The team would repeat this feat the following season, facing Midtjylland once again in the finals of the Danish Cup and winning the trophy for the second time<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-15"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup> with a 3–2 win. This would be Morten Wieghorst's last trophy with the <i>Wild Tigers</i>, however; he moved to manage the <a href="/wiki/Denmark_national_under-21_football_team" title="Denmark national under-21 football team">Denmark under-21 national team</a> at the end of the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_Danish_Superliga" title="2010–11 Danish Superliga">2010–11 campaign</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kasper_Hjulmand" title="Kasper Hjulmand">Kasper Hjulmand</a> was named Wieghorst's successor in June 2011, promoted from the coaching staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-16"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup> In preparation for the <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_Danish_Superliga" title="2011–12 Danish Superliga">2011–12 season</a>, the former Lyngby head coach brought in two Danish internationals in <a href="/wiki/Mikkel_Beckmann" title="Mikkel Beckmann">Mikkel Beckmann</a> (from relegated <a href="/wiki/Randers_FC" title="Randers FC">Randers</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Mtiliga" title="Patrick Mtiliga">Patrick Mtiliga</a> )on a free from <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga_CF" title="Málaga CF">Málaga</a>). FCN sought to improve on the previous season's sixth-place finish and to defend its Danish Cup title for the second year running. It would go on to play in the Europa League for second year in a row, exiting the competition in the third qualifying round after losing to <a href="/wiki/Sporting_Clube_de_Portugal" title="Sporting Clube de Portugal">Sporting CP</a> 2–1 aggregate, which had also eliminated Nordsjælland from Europe the previous season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-17"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>FCN started the new season in good form, peaking as high as second in the Superliga, and an undefeated run at home in all competitions until the 30 October, including an impressive 0–0 draw with Portuguese side Sporting CP, making the start of the 2011–12 campaign one of its best starts in recent years. For the first time in club history, a total of five players were called up to the Denmark national team<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-18"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup> to face <a href="/wiki/Sweden_national_football_team" title="Sweden national football team">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finland_national_football_team" title="Finland national football team">Finland</a> in November: <a href="/wiki/Mikkel_Beckmann" title="Mikkel Beckmann">Mikkel Beckmann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Bjelland" title="Andreas Bjelland">Andreas Bjelland</a> and debutantes <a href="/wiki/Tobias_Mikkelsen" title="Tobias Mikkelsen">Tobias Mikkelsen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jesper_Hansen" title="Jesper Hansen">Jesper Hansen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jores_Okore" title="Jores Okore">Jores Okore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> FCN ended the season as Superliga champions for the first time in its history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nord_5-19"><a href="#cite_note-nord-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2012–13, for the first time FCN participated in the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a>, where it was drawn into a difficult group alongside defending champions <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_F.C." title="Chelsea F.C.">Chelsea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serie_A" title="Serie A">Serie A</a> champions <a href="/wiki/Juventus_F.C." title="Juventus F.C.">Juventus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Premier_League" title="Ukrainian Premier League">Ukrainian Premier League</a> champions <a href="/wiki/FC_Shakhtar_Donetsk" title="FC Shakhtar Donetsk">Shakhtar Donetsk</a>. FCN played all its home matches at the Danish national stadium, <a href="/wiki/Parken_Stadium" title="Parken Stadium">Parken</a>. It gained one point from the group stage – playing 1–1 against Juventus at home thanks to a direct free-kick goal from Mikkel Beckmann. In the last group match, a controversial goal became the talking point of the match, where Shakhtar Donetsk forward <a href="/wiki/Luiz_Adriano" title="Luiz Adriano">Luiz Adriano</a> scored after a fair play situation to level the score; he received a one match ban from <a href="/wiki/UEFA" title="UEFA">UEFA</a> after the match.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Club_captains">Club captains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FC_Nordsj%C3%A6lland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Club captains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2001, seven players have held the position as club captain for Farum BK or FC Nordsjælland. The first recorded captain was Michael Elbæk. All recorded captains to date have been of Danish nationality. The captain to have lifted the most trophies for FCN is <a href="/wiki/Nicolai_Stokholm" title="Nicolai Stokholm">Nicolai Stokholm</a>, who won the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Cup" title="Danish Cup">Danish Cup</a> on two occasions. Stokholm is also the current and longest-serving captain, having taken over from <a href="/wiki/Henrik_Kildentoft" title="Henrik Kildentoft">Henrik Kildentoft</a> when the former arrived at the club in 2009.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stadium">Stadium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FC_Nordsj%C3%A6lland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Stadium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nordsjælland plays its home matches in <a href="/wiki/Farum_Park" title="Farum Park">Farum Park</a>, which has a capacity of 10,100 attendances (9,800 seated). The stadium is the first in Denmark with artificial turf.</p>

<p>The <b>Fodbold Samarbejde Nordsjælland</b> (English: Football Cooperation North Zealand <b>or</b> FSN in short) is a network of affiliated clubs headed by FC Nordsjælland, in which to highlight talent, youth development, cooperation and community in the <a href="/wiki/North_Zealand" title="North Zealand">North Zealand</a> region. Where clubs participating receive benefits from FCN such as loan moves, friendlies, tickets to games, merchandise, coach visits, training camps and coaching courses. In return, FC Nordsjælland get access to a large scouting network of youth players, which has helped develop many young talent to become youth internationals, who have gone on to play professionally in the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Superliga" title="Danish Superliga">Danish Superliga</a> and beyond. FSN has also played an important role in the attendance rise in Farum Park.</p>
