<p>The works of American author <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a> (January 19, 1809 – October 7, 1849) include many <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">poems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">short stories</a>, and one <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novel</a>. His fiction spans multiple genres, including <a href="/wiki/Horror_fiction" title="Horror fiction">horror fiction</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adventure_novel" title="Adventure novel">adventure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">detective fiction</a>, a genre he is credited with inventing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silverman171_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silverman171-1">[1]</a></sup> These works are generally considered part of the <a href="/wiki/Dark_romanticism" title="Dark romanticism">Dark romanticism</a> movement, a literary reaction to <a href="/wiki/Transcendentalism" title="Transcendentalism">Transcendentalism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Poe's writing reflects his literary theories: he disagreed with <a href="/wiki/Didacticism" title="Didacticism">didacticism</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Meaning in literature, he said in his criticism, should be an undercurrent just beneath the surface; works whose meanings are too obvious cease to be art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Poe pursued originality in his works, and disliked proverbs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He often included elements of popular <a href="/wiki/Pseudoscience" title="Pseudoscience">pseudosciences</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Phrenology" title="Phrenology">phrenology</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Physiognomy" title="Physiognomy">physiognomy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> His most recurring themes deal with questions of death, including its physical signs, the effects of decomposition, concerns of <a href="/wiki/Premature_burial" title="Premature burial">premature burial</a>, the reanimation of the dead, and <a href="/wiki/Mourning" title="Mourning">mourning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Though known as a masterly practitioner of <a href="/wiki/Gothic_fiction" title="Gothic fiction">Gothic fiction</a>, Poe did not invent the genre; he was following a long-standing popular tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Poe's literary career began in 1827 with the release of 50 copies of <i><a href="/wiki/Tamerlane_and_Other_Poems" title="Tamerlane and Other Poems">Tamerlane and Other Poems</a></i> credited only to "a Bostonian", a collection of early poems that received virtually no attention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In December 1829, Poe released <i>Al Aaraaf, Tamerlane, and Minor Poems</i> in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> before delving into <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">short stories</a> for the first time with "<a href="/wiki/Metzengerstein" title="Metzengerstein">Metzengerstein</a>" in 1832.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silverman88_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silverman88-13">[13]</a></sup> His most successful and most widely read prose during his lifetime was "<a href="/wiki/The_Gold-Bug" title="The Gold-Bug">The Gold-Bug</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> which earned him a $100 prize, the most money he received for a single work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> One of his most important works, "<a href="/wiki/The_Murders_in_the_Rue_Morgue" title="The Murders in the Rue Morgue">The Murders in the Rue Morgue</a>", was published in 1841 and is today considered the first modern detective story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meyers.2C_123_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meyers.2C_123-16">[16]</a></sup> Poe called it a "tale of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ratiocination" title="wikt:ratiocination">ratiocination</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silverman171_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Silverman171-1">[1]</a></sup> Poe became a household name with the publication of "<a href="/wiki/The_Raven" title="The Raven">The Raven</a>" in 1845,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> though it was not a financial success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The publishing industry at the time was a difficult career choice and much of Poe's work was written using themes specifically catered for mass market tastes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
