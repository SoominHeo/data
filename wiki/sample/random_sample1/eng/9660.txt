<p><b>Edward Gierek</b> (<small>Polish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Polish" title="Help:IPA for Polish">[ˈɛdvart ˈɡʲɛrɛk]</a></span>; 6 January 1913 – 29 July 2001) was a <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> <a href="/wiki/Communism_in_Poland" title="Communism in Poland">communist politician</a>. Gierek replaced <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_Gomu%C5%82ka" title="Władysław Gomułka">Władysław Gomułka</a> as <a href="/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party" title="General Secretary of the Communist Party">First Secretary</a> of the ruling <a href="/wiki/Polish_United_Workers%27_Party" title="Polish United Workers' Party">Polish United Workers' Party</a> (PZPR) in the <a href="/wiki/Polish_People%27s_Republic" title="Polish People's Republic">Polish People's Republic</a>. Gierek is known for opening communist Poland to Western influence and for his economic reform, based on foreign loans, which ultimately failed. He was removed from power after labor strikes led to the <a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk_Agreement" title="Gdańsk Agreement">Gdańsk Agreement</a> between the communist state and workers of the emerging <a href="/wiki/Solidarity_(Polish_trade_union)" title="Solidarity (Polish trade union)">Solidarity free trade union</a> movement.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Youth_and_early_career">Youth and early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Gierek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Youth and early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Edward Gierek was born in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Por%C4%85bka,_Sosnowiec&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Porąbka, Sosnowiec (page does not exist)">Porąbka</a> near <a href="/wiki/Sosnowiec" title="Sosnowiec">Sosnowiec</a>, into a coal mining family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gajdzinski_94_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gajdzinski_94-1">[1]</a></sup> He lost his father to a mining accident in a pit at the age of four. His mother remarried and emigrated to northern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, where he lived from the age of 10 and worked in a coal mine from the age of 13. Gierek joined the <a href="/wiki/French_Communist_Party" title="French Communist Party">French Communist Party</a> in 1931 and in 1934 was deported to Poland for organizing a strike.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gajdzinski_68_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gajdzinski_68-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_260.E2.80.93262_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_260.E2.80.93262-3">[3]</a></sup> After completing compulsory military service in <a href="/wiki/Stryi" title="Stryi">Stryi</a> in southeastern Poland (1934–36), Gierek married Stanisława Jędrusik, but was unable to find employment. The Giereks went to <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, where Edward worked in the coal mines of <a href="/wiki/Genk" title="Genk">Waterschei</a>, acquiring <a href="/wiki/Pneumoconiosis" title="Pneumoconiosis">pneumoconiosis</a> (black lung disease). In 1939 Gierek joined the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Belgium" title="Communist Party of Belgium">Communist Party of Belgium</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/German_occupation_of_Belgium_during_World_War_II" title="German occupation of Belgium during World War II">German occupation</a>, he participated in communist anti-<a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi</a> <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Resistance" title="Belgian Resistance">Belgian resistance activities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_260.E2.80.93262_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_260.E2.80.93262-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gajdzinski_53_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gajdzinski_53-4">[4]</a></sup> After the war Gierek was politically active, primarily among Polish immigrants. He was a co-founder of the Belgian branch of the <a href="/wiki/Polish_Workers%27_Party" title="Polish Workers' Party">Polish Workers' Party</a> (PPR) and a chairman of the National Council of Poles in Belgium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_260.E2.80.93262_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_260.E2.80.93262-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polish_United_Workers.27_Party_activist">Polish United Workers' Party activist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Gierek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Polish United Workers' Party activist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Edward_Gierek_Dzie%C5%84_Czynu_Partyjnego_1974.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2540" data-file-width="3827" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Edward_Gierek_Dzie%C5%84_Czynu_Partyjnego_1974.jpg/250px-Edward_Gierek_Dzie%C5%84_Czynu_Partyjnego_1974.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Edward_Gierek_Dzie%C5%84_Czynu_Partyjnego_1974.jpg/375px-Edward_Gierek_Dzie%C5%84_Czynu_Partyjnego_1974.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Edward_Gierek_Dzie%C5%84_Czynu_Partyjnego_1974.jpg/500px-Edward_Gierek_Dzie%C5%84_Czynu_Partyjnego_1974.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Edward_Gierek_Dzie%C5%84_Czynu_Partyjnego_1974.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gierek and wife contribute on Party Volunteer Labor Day</div>

<p>Gierek, who in 1948 was 35 and had spent 22 years abroad, was directed by the PPR authorities to return to Poland, which he did with his wife and their two sons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_260.E2.80.93262_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_260.E2.80.93262-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gajdzinski_57_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gajdzinski_57-5">[5]</a></sup> Working in the <a href="/wiki/Katowice" title="Katowice">Katowice</a> district PPR organization, in December 1948, as a <a href="/wiki/Sosnowiec" title="Sosnowiec">Sosnowiec</a> delegate he participated in the PPR-<a href="/wiki/Polish_Socialist_Party" title="Polish Socialist Party">PPS</a> unification congress, which resulted in the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Polish_United_Workers%27_Party" title="Polish United Workers' Party">Polish United Workers' Party</a> (PZPR). In 1949, he was designated for and attended a 2-year higher party course in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>, where he was judged to be poorly qualified for intellectual endeavors but highly motivated for party work. In 1951 <a href="/wiki/Roman_Zambrowski" title="Roman Zambrowski">Roman Zambrowski</a> sent Gierek to a striking coal mine, charging him with restoring order. Gierek was able to resolve the situation using persuasion and a use of force was avoided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_262.E2.80.93264_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_262.E2.80.93264-6">[6]</a></sup> He was a member of the <i><a href="/wiki/Sejm" title="Sejm">Sejm</a></i>, Polish parliament, from 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gajdzinski_70_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gajdzinski_70-7">[7]</a></sup> During the II Congress of the PZPR (March 1954), he was elected a member of the party's <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee" title="Central Committee">Central Committee</a>. As chief of the Central Committee's Heavy Industry Division, he worked directly under First Secretary <a href="/wiki/Boles%C5%82aw_Bierut" title="Bolesław Bierut">Bolesław Bierut</a> in Warsaw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_262.E2.80.93264_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_262.E2.80.93264-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 1956, when <a href="/wiki/Edward_Ochab" title="Edward Ochab">Edward Ochab</a> became the party's first secretary, Gierek became a secretary of the Central Committee, even though he publicly expressed doubts about his own qualifications. On 28 June 1956 he was sent to <a href="/wiki/Pozna%C5%84" title="Poznań">Poznań</a>, where a <a href="/wiki/Pozna%C5%84_1956_protests" title="Poznań 1956 protests">worker revolt</a> was taking place. Afterwards, delegated by the <a href="/wiki/Politburo" title="Politburo">Politburo</a>, he headed the commission charged with investigating the causes and course of the Poznań events. They presented their report on 7 July, blaming a hostile anti-socialist foreign inspired conspiracy that took advantage of worker discontent in Poznań enterprises. In July Gierek became a member of the PZPR Politburo, but lasted in that position only until October, when <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_Gomu%C5%82ka" title="Władysław Gomułka">Władysław Gomułka</a> <a href="/wiki/Polish_October" title="Polish October">replaced Ochab as first secretary</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a> criticized Gomułka for not retaining in the Politburo Gierek, who remained a Central Committee secretary responsible for economic affairs, however. He would return to the Politburo in March 1959, at the III Congress of the PZPR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_264.E2.80.93268_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_264.E2.80.93268-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Katowice_industrial_district_leader">Katowice industrial district leader</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Gierek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Katowice industrial district leader">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:237px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gierek_in_Rz%C4%85%C5%9Bnik.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1308" data-file-width="1544" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Gierek_in_Rz%C4%85%C5%9Bnik.jpg/235px-Gierek_in_Rz%C4%85%C5%9Bnik.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Gierek_in_Rz%C4%85%C5%9Bnik.jpg/353px-Gierek_in_Rz%C4%85%C5%9Bnik.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Gierek_in_Rz%C4%85%C5%9Bnik.jpg/470px-Gierek_in_Rz%C4%85%C5%9Bnik.jpg 2x" width="235"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

<p>In March 1957, in addition to his Central Committee duties, Gierek became first secretary of the <a href="/wiki/Katowice_Voivodeship" title="Katowice Voivodeship">Katowice Voivodeship</a> PZPR organization, and he kept this job until 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_264.E2.80.93268_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_264.E2.80.93268-8">[8]</a></sup> He created a personal power base in the Katowice region and became the nationally recognized leader of the young technocrat faction of the party. On the one hand Gierek was regarded as a pragmatic, non-ideological and economic progress-oriented manager, on the other he was known for his servile attitude toward the Soviet leaders, for whom he was a source of information concerning the PZPR and its personalities. Both the industrial supremacy of Gierek's well-run <a href="/wiki/Upper_Silesia" title="Upper Silesia">Upper Silesia</a> territory and the special relationship with the Soviets he cultivated made many believe that Gierek was a likely successor to Gomułka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_268.E2.80.93272_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_268.E2.80.93272-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Gierek may have tried to make his move during the <a href="/wiki/1968_Polish_political_crisis" title="1968 Polish political crisis">1968 Polish political crisis</a>. Soon after the student rally on 8 March in Warsaw, on 14 March in Katowice he led a mass gathering of 100,000 party members from the entire province. He was the first Politburo member speaking publicly on the issue of the protests currently taking place and later he claimed that his motivation was to demonstrate support for Gomułka's rule, threatened by <a href="/wiki/Mieczys%C5%82aw_Moczar" title="Mieczysław Moczar">Mieczysław Moczar</a>'s intra-party conspiring. Gierek used strong language to condemn the purported "enemies of <a href="/wiki/Polish_People%27s_Republic" title="Polish People's Republic">People's Poland</a>" who were "disturbing the peaceful <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesian</a> water". He showered them with propaganda epithets and alluded to their bones being crushed (by that Silesian water) if they persevere in their attempts to turn the nation away from its chosen course. Gierek was supposedly embarrassed when participants of the party conference in Warsaw on 19 March shouted his name along with that of Gomułka, as an expression of support. The 1968 events strengthened Gierek's position, also in the eyes of his sponsors in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_272.E2.80.93275_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_272.E2.80.93275-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_secretary_of_the_PZPR">First secretary of the PZPR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Gierek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First secretary of the PZPR">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:237px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Osiedle_Ustronie_w_Radomiu_3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1897" data-file-width="2301" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Osiedle_Ustronie_w_Radomiu_3.jpg/235px-Osiedle_Ustronie_w_Radomiu_3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Osiedle_Ustronie_w_Radomiu_3.jpg/353px-Osiedle_Ustronie_w_Radomiu_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Osiedle_Ustronie_w_Radomiu_3.jpg/470px-Osiedle_Ustronie_w_Radomiu_3.jpg 2x" width="235"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Osiedle_Ustronie_w_Radomiu_3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gierek participated in hundreds of field trips and visitations, meeting ordinary Poles and seeking their feedback<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285-11">[11]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/1970_Polish_protests" title="1970 Polish protests">1970 Polish protests</a> were violently suppressed, Gierek replaced Gomułka as first secretary of the party and had thus become the most powerful politician in Poland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_275.E2.80.93277_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_275.E2.80.93277-12">[12]</a></sup> In late January 1971, he put his new authority on the line and traveled to <a href="/wiki/Szczecin" title="Szczecin">Szczecin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk" title="Gdańsk">Gdańsk</a>, to bargain personally with the striking workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_255.E2.80.93260_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_255.E2.80.93260-13">[13]</a></sup> Consumer price increases that triggered the recent revolt were rescinded. Among Gierek's popular moves was the decision to rebuild the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Castle,_Warsaw" title="Royal Castle, Warsaw">Royal Castle in Warsaw</a>, destroyed during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and not included in the post-war restoration of the city's <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Old_Town" title="Warsaw Old Town">Old Town</a>. State controlled media stressed his Western upbringing and his fluent facility with the French language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The arrival of the Gierek team meant the final generational replacement of the ruling communist elite, a process begun in 1968 under Gomułka. Many thousands of party activists, including important elder leaders with background in the prewar <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Poland" title="Communist Party of Poland">Communist Party of Poland</a>, were removed from positions of responsibility and replaced with people whose careers formed after World War II. Much of the overhaul was accomplished before, during and after the VI Congress of the PZPR, convened in December 1971. The resulting <a href="/wiki/Nomenklatura" title="Nomenklatura">governing class</a> was one of the youngest in Europe. The role of the administration was expanded at the expense of the party, according to the maxim "the party leads, the government governs". Throughout the 1970s, the most highly visible member of the top leadership after Gierek was Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Piotr_Jaroszewicz" title="Piotr Jaroszewicz">Piotr Jaroszewicz</a>. From May 1971, Gierek's rival party politician Mieczysław Moczar was increasingly marginalized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to historian <a href="/wiki/Krzysztof_Pomian" title="Krzysztof Pomian">Krzysztof Pomian</a>, early in his term Gierek abandoned the regime's long-standing practice of on-and-off confrontation with the Polish <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, and opted for cooperation. The policy resulted in a privileged position of the Church and its leaders for the duration of the communist rule in Poland. The Church markedly expanded its physical infrastructure and also became a crucial political third force, often involved in mediating conflict between the authorities and opposition activists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krzysztof_Pomian_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krzysztof_Pomian-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_expansion_and_decline">Economic expansion and decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Gierek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economic expansion and decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Huta_Katowice_20081030.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="298" data-file-width="1280" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Huta_Katowice_20081030.jpg/400px-Huta_Katowice_20081030.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Huta_Katowice_20081030.jpg/600px-Huta_Katowice_20081030.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Huta_Katowice_20081030.jpg/800px-Huta_Katowice_20081030.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Huta_Katowice_20081030.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Katowice_Steelworks" title="Katowice Steelworks">Katowice Steelworks</a>, Gierek's major industrial project</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Since the riots that brought down Gomułka were caused primarily by economic difficulties, Gierek promised economic reform and instituted a program to modernize industry and increase the availability of consumer goods. His reform was based primarily on large scale foreign borrowing, not accompanied by major systemic restructuring. The need for deeper reform was obscured by the investment boom the country was enjoying in the first half of the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285-11">[11]</a></sup> The first secretary's good relations with Western leaders, especially France's <a href="/wiki/Val%C3%A9ry_Giscard_d%27Estaing" title="Valéry Giscard d'Estaing">Valéry Giscard d'Estaing</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Schmidt" title="Helmut Schmidt">Helmut Schmidt</a>, were a catalyst for his receiving Western aid and loans. Gierek is widely credited with opening Poland to political and economic influence from the West. He himself extensively traveled abroad and received important foreign guests in Poland, including three presidents of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285-11">[11]</a></sup> Gierek also was trusted by <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev" title="Leonid Brezhnev">Leonid Brezhnev</a>, which meant that he was able to pursue his policies (<a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a> of Poland's economy) without much <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> interference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_255.E2.80.93260_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_255.E2.80.93260-13">[13]</a></sup> He had readily granted the Soviets concessions that his predecessor Gomułka would consider contrary to the Polish national interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Standard_of_living" title="Standard of living">Standard of living</a> increased markedly in Poland in the first half of the 1970s, and for a time Gierek was hailed as a miracle-worker. Poles, to an unprecedented degree, were able to purchase desired consumer items such as compact cars, travel to the West rather freely, and even a solution to the intractable housing supply problem seemed to be on the horizon. Decades later many remembered the period as the most prosperous in their lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_285.E2.80.93286_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_285.E2.80.93286-15">[15]</a></sup> The economy, however, began to falter during the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a>, and by 1976 price increases became necessary. The <a href="/wiki/June_1976_protests" title="June 1976 protests">June 1976 protests</a> were forcibly suppressed, but the planned price increases were canceled. The greatest accumulation of foreign debt occurred in the late 1970s, as the regime struggled to counter the effects of the crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_277.E2.80.93285-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crisis.2C_protests.2C_organized_opposition">Crisis, protests, organized opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Gierek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Crisis, protests, organized opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>The period of Gierek's rule is notable for the rise of organized opposition in Poland. Changes in the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Polish_People%27s_Republic" title="Constitution of the Polish People's Republic">constitution</a>, proposed by the regime, caused considerable controversy at the turn of 1975 and 1976. The intended amendments included formalizing the "socialist character of the state", the leading role of the PZPR and the Polish-Soviet alliance. The widely opposed alterations resulted in numerous protest letters and other actions, but were supported at the VII Congress of the PZPR in December 1975 and largely implemented by the <i><a href="/wiki/Sejm" title="Sejm">Sejm</a></i> in February 1976. Organized opposition circles developed gradually and reached 3000–4000 members by the end of the decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_289.E2.80.93290_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_289.E2.80.93290-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of the deteriorating economic situation, at the end of 1975 the authorities announced that the frozen from 1971 food prices would have to increase. Prime Minister Jaroszewicz forced the price rise combined with financial compensation favoring upper income people, the policy ultimately adopted despite strong objections voiced by the Soviet leadership. The increase, supported by Gierek, was announced by Jaroszewicz in the <i>Sejm</i> on 24 June 1976. Strikes broke out the following day, with particularly serious disturbances, brutally pacified by the police, taking place in <a href="/wiki/Radom" title="Radom">Radom</a>, at Warsaw's <a href="/wiki/Ursus_Factory" title="Ursus Factory">Ursus Factory</a> and in <a href="/wiki/P%C5%82ock" title="Płock">Płock</a>. On 26 June, Gierek engaged in the traditional party crisis-confronting mode of operation, ordering mass public gatherings in Polish cities to demonstrate people's supposed support for the party and condemn the "trouble makers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_290.E2.80.93296_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_290.E2.80.93296-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ordered by Brezhnev not to attempt any further manipulations with prices, Gierek and his government undertook other measures to rescue the market destabilized in the summer of 1976. In August, sugar "merchandise coupons" were introduced to ration the product. The politics of "dynamic development" was over, as evidenced by such <a href="/wiki/Ration_stamp" title="Ration stamp">ration cards</a>, which would remain a part of Poland's daily reality until July 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_290.E2.80.93296_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_290.E2.80.93296-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the aftermath of the June 1976 protests, a major opposition group, the <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_Defence_Committee" title="Workers' Defence Committee">Workers' Defence Committee</a> (KOR), commenced its activities in September to help the persecuted worker protest participants. Other opposition organizations were also established in 1977–79, but historically the KOR turned out to be of particular importance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_296.E2.80.93300_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_296.E2.80.93300-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1979, Poland's ruling communists reluctantly allowed <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> to make his first papal visit to Poland (2–10 June), despite Soviet advice to the contrary. Gierek, who had previously met <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_VI" title="Pope Paul VI">Pope Paul VI</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican</a>, talked also with the Polish pope on the occasion of his visit in Poland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_296.E2.80.93300_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_296.E2.80.93300-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Downfall">Downfall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Gierek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Downfall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Although Gierek, distressed by the 1976 price increase policy failure, was persuaded by his colleagues not to resign, divisions within his team intensified. One faction, led by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Babiuch" title="Edward Babiuch">Edward Babiuch</a> and Piotr Jaroszewicz, wanted him to remain at the helm, while another, led by <a href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_Kania" title="Stanisław Kania">Stanisław Kania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wojciech_Jaruzelski" title="Wojciech Jaruzelski">Wojciech Jaruzelski</a>, was less interested in preserving his leadership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_290.E2.80.93296_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_290.E2.80.93296-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 1980, after the <a href="/wiki/Soviet%E2%80%93Afghan_War" title="Soviet–Afghan War">Soviet invasion of Afghanistan</a> and the subsequent Western boycott of the Soviet Union, Gierek arranged a meeting between <a href="/wiki/Val%C3%A9ry_Giscard_d%27Estaing" title="Valéry Giscard d'Estaing">Valéry Giscard d'Estaing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev" title="Leonid Brezhnev">Leonid Brezhnev</a> in Warsaw. As was the case with <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_Gomu%C5%82ka" title="Władysław Gomułka">Władysław Gomułka</a> a decade earlier, a foreign policy success created an illusion that the Polish party leader was secure in his statesman aura, while the paramount political facts were being determined by the deteriorating economic situation and the resultant labor unrest. In July Gierek went to the <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a>, his usual vacation spot. For the last time he talked there with his friend Brezhnev. He responded to Brezhnev's gloomy assessment of the situation in Poland (including the out-of-control indebtedness) with his own upbeat predictions, possibly not fully cognizant of the country's and his own predicament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_300.E2.80.93304_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_300.E2.80.93304-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>High foreign debts, food shortages, and an outmoded industrial base were among the factors that compelled at that time a new round of economic reforms. Once again, in the summer of 1980 price increases set off protests across the country, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk" title="Gdańsk">Gdańsk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Szczecin" title="Szczecin">Szczecin</a> <a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk_Shipyard" title="Gdańsk Shipyard">shipyards</a>. Unlike on previous occasions, the regime decided not to resort to forcible suppression of the strike activity. In the <a href="/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk_Agreement" title="Gdańsk Agreement">Gdańsk Agreement</a> and other accords reached with Polish workers, Gierek was forced to concede their right to strike and the <a href="/wiki/Solidarity_(Polish_trade_union)" title="Solidarity (Polish trade union)">Solidarity</a> labor union had its beginnings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_300.E2.80.93304_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_300.E2.80.93304-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>Shortly thereafter, in early September 1980, he was replaced by the Central Committee's VI <a href="/wiki/Plenary_session" title="Plenary session">Plenum</a> as party first secretary by <a href="/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_Kania" title="Stanisław Kania">Stanisław Kania</a> and removed from power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_300.E2.80.93304_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_300.E2.80.93304-19">[19]</a></sup> A popular and trusted leader in the early 1970s, Gierek left surrounded by infamy and ridicule, deserted by most of his collaborators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_255.E2.80.93260_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_255.E2.80.93260-13">[13]</a></sup> The VII Plenum in December 1980 held Gierek and Jaroszewicz personally liable for the situation in the country and removed them from the Central Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_305.E2.80.93311_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_305.E2.80.93311-20">[20]</a></sup> The extraordinary IX Congress of the PZPR, in an unprecedented move, voted in July 1981 to expel Gierek and his close associates from the party, as the delegates considered them responsible for the Solidarity-related crisis in Poland, and First Secretary Kania was unable to prevent their action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_368.E2.80.93369_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_368.E2.80.93369-21">[21]</a></sup> The next first secretary of the PZPR, General <a href="/wiki/Wojciech_Jaruzelski" title="Wojciech Jaruzelski">Wojciech Jaruzelski</a>, introduced <a href="/wiki/Martial_law_in_Poland" title="Martial law in Poland">martial law in Poland</a> on 13 December 1981. Gierek was <a href="/wiki/Internment" title="Internment">interned</a> for a year from December 1981. Unlike in case of the also-interned opposition activists, the internment status brought Gierek no social respect and he ended up as the martial law's main <a href="/wiki/Outcast_(person)" title="Outcast (person)">pariah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_305.E2.80.93311_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_305.E2.80.93311-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Edward Gierek died in July 2001 of the miner's lung illness in a hospital in <a href="/wiki/Cieszyn" title="Cieszyn">Cieszyn</a>, near the southern mountain resort of <a href="/wiki/Ustro%C5%84" title="Ustroń">Ustroń</a> where he spent his last years. From the perspective of time his rule was now seen in a more positive light and over ten thousand people attended his funeral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_305.E2.80.93311_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_305.E2.80.93311-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>With his lifelong wife, Stanisława <i>née</i> Jędrusik, Gierek had two sons,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_260.E2.80.93262_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_260.E2.80.93262-3">[3]</a></sup> one of whom is <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">MEP</a> <a href="/wiki/Adam_Gierek" title="Adam Gierek">Adam Gierek</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Gierek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1990 two books, based on extended interviews with Gierek by Janusz Rolicki, were published in Poland and became bestsellers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rzeczpospolita_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rzeczpospolita-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Polish society is divided in its assessment of Gierek. His government is fondly remembered by some for the improved living standards the Poles enjoyed in the 1970s under his rule. Uniquely among the PZPR leaders, the Polish public has shown signs of Gierek nostalgia, discernible especially after the former first secretary's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_255.E2.80.93260_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_255.E2.80.93260-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Others emphasize that the improvements were only made possible by the unwise and unsustainable policies based on huge foreign loans, which led directly to the economic crises of the 1970s and 1980s. Judged by the outcome, the total sum of over 24 <a href="/wiki/Billion" title="Billion">billion</a> borrowed (in 1970s dollars) was not well-spent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_255.E2.80.93260_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_255.E2.80.93260-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon becoming first secretary in December 1970, Gierek promised himself that under his watch people would not be shot on streets. In 1976 the security forces intervened, but only after having to give up their firearms. In 1980, they did not use force at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisler_siedmiu_305.E2.80.93311_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Eisler_siedmiu_305.E2.80.93311-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to sociologist Maciej Gdula, the social and cultural transformation that took place in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Poland_(1945%E2%80%9389)#Gierek_decade_.281970.E2.80.9380.29" title="History of Poland (1945–89)">Poland in the 1970s</a> was even more fundamental than the one which occurred in the 1990s, following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Poland_(1945%E2%80%9389)#Politics_of_the_People.27s_Republic.27s_last_years_and_the_transition_period" title="History of Poland (1945–89)">political transition</a>. Regarding the politics of alliance of the political and later also money elites with the <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle class</a> at the expense of the <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working class</a>, he said "the general idea of the relationship of forces in our society has remained the same from the 1970s, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Poland_(1945%E2%80%9389)#Final_decade_of_the_Polish_People.27s_Republic_.281980.E2.80.9390.29" title="History of Poland (1945–89)">period of mass solidarity</a> was an exception" ("mass solidarity" being the years 1980–81). Since the time of Gierek, Polish society has been hegemonized by cultural perceptions and norms of the (at that time emerging) middle class. Terms like management, initiative, personality, or the <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualistic</a> maxim "get educated, work hard and get ahead in life", combined with orderliness, replaced <a href="/wiki/Class_consciousness" title="Class consciousness">class consciousness</a> and the socialist <a href="/wiki/Egalitarianism" title="Egalitarianism">egalitarian</a> concept, as workers were losing their symbolic status, to be eventually separated into a marginalized stratum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gdula_Sutowski_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gdula_Sutowski-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
