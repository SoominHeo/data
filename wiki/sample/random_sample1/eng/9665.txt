<p><b>Edward John Smith</b>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reserve_Decoration" title="Reserve Decoration">RD</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ʊ/ short 'oo' in 'foot'">ʊ</span><span title="/ɑːr/ 'ar' in 'far'">ɑːr</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/dʒ/ 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="/θ/ 'th' in 'thigh'">θ</span></span>/</a></span></span>; 27 January 1850 – 15 April 1912) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Merchant_Navy" title="British Merchant Navy">British Merchant Navy</a> officer. He served as master of numerous <a href="/wiki/White_Star_Line" title="White Star Line">White Star Line</a> vessels. He is best known as the captain of the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></a>, perishing when the ship sank on its maiden voyage.</p>
<p>Raised in a working environment, he left school early to join the Merchant Navy and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_Reserve" title="Royal Naval Reserve">Royal Naval Reserve</a>. After earning his master's ticket, he entered the service of the <a href="/wiki/White_Star_Line" title="White Star Line">White Star Line</a>, a prestigious British company. He quickly rose through the ranks, and graduated in 1887 his first command aboard the <a href="/wiki/SS_Celtic_(1872)" title="SS Celtic (1872)">SS <i>Celtic</i></a>. He served as commanding officer of numerous White Star Line vessels,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-igi_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-igi-1">[1]</a></sup> including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS_Majestic_(1890)" title="SS Majestic (1890)"><i>Majestic</i></a> (which he commanded for nine years) and attracted a strong and loyal following amongst passengers.</p>
<p>In 1904, Smith became the commodore of the White Star Line, and was responsible for controlling its flagships. He successfully commanded the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Baltic_(1903)" title="RMS Baltic (1903)"><i>Baltic</i></a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RMS_Adriatic_(1907)" title="RMS Adriatic (1907)"><i>Adriatic</i></a> and the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Olympic" title="RMS Olympic"><i>Olympic</i></a>. In 1912, he was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_Captain" title="Sea Captain">captain</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Maiden_voyage" title="Maiden voyage">maiden voyage</a> of the RMS <i>Titanic</i>, which <a href="/wiki/Sinking_of_the_RMS_Titanic" title="Sinking of the RMS Titanic">struck an iceberg and sank</a> on 15 April 1912; Smith and over 1,500 others perished in the sinking. For his stoicism and fortitude in the face of adversity, Smith became an icon of British "<a href="/wiki/Stiff_upper_lip" title="Stiff upper lip">stiff upper lip</a>" spirit and discipline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span></h2>
<p>Edward John Smith was born on 27 January 1850 on Well Street, <a href="/wiki/Hanley,_Staffordshire" title="Hanley, Staffordshire">Hanley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Staffordshire" title="Staffordshire">Staffordshire</a>, England to Edward Smith, a potter, and Catherine Hancock, born Marsh, who married on 2 August 1841 in Shelton, Staffordshire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC17447183_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC17447183-3">[3]</a></sup> His parents later owned a shop.</p>
<p>Smith attended the <a href="/wiki/Etruria,_Staffordshire" title="Etruria, Staffordshire">Etruria</a> British School until the age of 13, when he left and operated a steam hammer at the Etruria Forge. In 1867, aged 17 he went to <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> in the footsteps of his half-brother Joseph Hancock, a captain on a sailing ship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC17181461_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC17181461-4">[4]</a></sup> He began his apprenticeship on <i>Senator Weber</i>, owned by A Gibson &amp; Co. of Liverpool.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_children">Marriage and children</span></h2>
<p>On 13 January 1887, Smith married Sarah Eleanor Pennington at <a href="/wiki/St_Oswald%27s_Church,_Winwick" title="St Oswald's Church, Winwick">St Oswald's Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winwick,_Cheshire" title="Winwick, Cheshire">Winwick</a>, Cheshire. Their daughter, Helen Melville Smith, was born in <a href="/wiki/Waterloo,_Merseyside" title="Waterloo, Merseyside">Waterloo</a>, Liverpool on 2 April 1898. The family lived in an imposing red brick, twin-gabled house, named "Woodhead", on Winn Road, <a href="/wiki/Highfield,_Southampton" title="Highfield, Southampton">Highfield, Southampton</a>, Hampshire.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_commands">Early commands</span></h3>
<p>Edward Smith joined the <a href="/wiki/White_Star_Line" title="White Star Line">White Star Line</a> in March 1880 as the Fourth Officer of <a href="/wiki/SS_Celtic_(1872)" title="SS Celtic (1872)">SS <i>Celtic</i></a>. He served aboard the company's liners to Australia and to New York City, where he quickly rose in status. In 1887, he received his first White Star command, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS_Republic_(1872)" title="SS Republic (1872)"><i>Republic</i></a>. In 1888, Smith earned his Extra Master's Certificate and joined the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_Reserve" title="Royal Naval Reserve">Royal Naval Reserve</a>, receiving a commission as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_(Royal_Navy)" title="Lieutenant (Royal Navy)">Lieutenant</a>, which entitled him to add the letters "RNR" after his name. This meant that in a time of war he could be called upon to serve in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>. Later, after he had been promoted a <a href="/wiki/Commander_(Royal_Navy)" title="Commander (Royal Navy)">Commander</a> in the Royal Naval Reserve, Smith's ship had the distinction of being able to fly the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Ensign" title="Blue Ensign">Blue Ensign</a> of the RNR; British merchant vessels generally flew the <a href="/wiki/Red_Ensign" title="Red Ensign">Red Ensign</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bigger_commands">Bigger commands</span></h3>
<p>Smith was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS_Majestic_(1890)" title="SS Majestic (1890)"><i>Majestic</i></a><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s captain for nine years commencing in 1895. When the <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Boer War</a> started in 1899, <i>Majestic</i> was called upon to transport troops to <a href="/wiki/Cape_Colony" title="Cape Colony">Cape Colony</a>. Smith made two trips to South Africa, both without incident, and in 1903, for his service, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_VII_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Edward VII of the United Kingdom">King Edward VII</a> awarded him the <a href="/wiki/Transport_Medal" title="Transport Medal">Transport Medal</a>, showing the "South Africa" clasp. Smith was regarded as a "safe captain". As he rose in seniority, he gained a following amongst passengers with some only sailing the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> on a ship he captained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Smith even became known as the "Millionaires' Captain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> From 1904 on, Smith commanded the White Star Line's newest ships on their maiden voyages. In 1904, he was given command of what was then the largest ship in the world, the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Baltic_(1903)" title="RMS Baltic (1903)"><i>Baltic</i></a>. Her maiden voyage from Liverpool to New York, sailing 29 June 1904, went without incident. After three years with <i>Baltic</i>, Smith was given his second new "big ship," the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RMS_Adriatic_(1907)" title="RMS Adriatic (1907)"><i>Adriatic</i></a>. Once again, the maiden voyage went without incident. During his command of <i>Adriatic</i>, Smith received the long service <a href="/wiki/Decoration_for_Officers_of_the_Royal_Naval_Reserve" title="Decoration for Officers of the Royal Naval Reserve">Decoration for Officers of the Royal Naval Reserve</a> (RD) along with a promotion to Commander.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Olympic_class_command"><i>Olympic</i> class command</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EJ_Smith.jpg"><img alt="Photograph of a bearded man wearing a white captain's uniform, standing on a ship with his arms crossed." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="545" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/EJ_Smith.jpg/220px-EJ_Smith.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/EJ_Smith.jpg/330px-EJ_Smith.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/EJ_Smith.jpg/440px-EJ_Smith.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EJ_Smith.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Edward J. Smith, on <i>Olympic</i>, in 1911</div>

<p>As one of the world's most experienced sea captains, Smith was called upon to take first command of the lead ship in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympic_class_ocean_liner" title="Olympic class ocean liner">new class</a> of ocean liners, the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Olympic" title="RMS Olympic"><i>Olympic</i></a> – again, the largest vessel in the world at that time. The maiden voyage from Southampton to New York was successfully concluded on 21 June 1911, but as the ship was docking in New York harbour, a small incident took place. Docking at Pier 59 under the command of Captain Smith with the assistance of a harbour pilot, <i>Olympic</i> was being assisted by twelve tugs when one got caught in the backwash of <i>Olympic</i>, spun around, collided with the bigger ship, and for a moment was trapped under <i>Olympic's</i> stern, finally managing to work free and limp to the docks.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Hawke_incident">The <i>Hawke</i> incident</span></h3>
<p>On 20 September 1911, <i>Olympic</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s first major mishap occurred during a collision with a British warship, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Hawke_(1891)" title="HMS Hawke (1891)">HMS <i>Hawke</i></a>, in which the warship lost her <a href="/wiki/Prow" title="Prow">prow</a>. Although the collision left two of <i>Olympic</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s <a href="/wiki/Ship_floodability" title="Ship floodability">compartments</a> filled and one of her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propeller_shaft" title="Propeller shaft">propeller shafts</a> twisted, she was able to limp back to <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>. At the resultant inquiry, the Royal Navy blamed <i>Olympic</i>, alleging that her massive size generated a suction that pulled <i>Hawke</i> into her side. Captain Smith had been on the bridge during the events.</p>
<p>The <i>Hawke</i> incident was a financial disaster for White Star, and the out-of-service time for the big liner made matters worse. <i>Olympic</i> returned to <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> and, to speed up the repairs, <a href="/wiki/Harland_and_Wolff" title="Harland and Wolff">Harland and Wolff</a> was forced to delay <i>Titanic</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s completion, in order to use one of her propeller shafts and other parts for <i>Olympic</i>. Back at sea in February 1912, <i>Olympic</i> lost a <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">propeller</a> blade and once again returned to her builder for emergency repairs. To get her back to service immediately, Harland and Wolff again had to pull resources from <i>Titanic</i>, delaying her <a href="/wiki/Maiden_voyage" title="Maiden voyage">maiden voyage</a> from 20 March to 10 April.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="RMS_Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sinking_of_the_RMS_Titanic" title="Sinking of the RMS Titanic">Sinking of the RMS Titanic</a></div>
<p>Despite the past trouble, Smith was again appointed to be in command of the newest ship in the Olympic class when the RMS <i>Titanic</i> left Southampton for her maiden voyage. Although some sources state that he had decided to retire after completing <i>Titanic</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s maiden voyage, an article in the Halifax <i>Morning Chronicle</i> on 9 April 1912 stated that Smith would remain in charge of <i>Titanic</i> "until the Company (White Star Line) completed a larger and finer steamer."</p>
<p>On 10 April 1912, Smith, wearing a bowler hat and a long overcoat, took a taxi from his home to Southampton docks. He came aboard <i>Titanic</i> at 7 am to prepare for the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a> muster at 8:00 am. He immediately went to his cabin to get the sailing report from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Officer" title="Chief Officer">Chief Officer</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Tingle_Wilde" title="Henry Tingle Wilde">Henry Wilde</a>. After departure at noon, the huge amount of water displaced by <i>Titanic</i> as she passed caused the laid-up <a href="/wiki/SS_City_of_New_York" title="SS City of New York"><i>New York</i></a> to break from her moorings and swing towards <i>Titanic.</i> Quick action from Smith helped to avert a premature end to the maiden voyage.</p>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Titanic_the_sinking.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Illustration of the sinking of the <i>Titanic</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first four days of the voyage passed without incident, but shortly after 11:40 pm on 14 April Smith was informed by First Officer <a href="/wiki/William_McMaster_Murdoch" title="William McMaster Murdoch">William Murdoch</a> that the ship had just collided with an iceberg. It was soon apparent that the ship was seriously damaged; designer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Andrews_(shipbuilder)" title="Thomas Andrews (shipbuilder)">Thomas Andrews</a> reported that all of the first five of the ship's watertight compartments had been breached and that <i>Titanic</i> would sink in under two hours. During the evacuation, Captain Smith, aware that there were not enough lifeboats for all of the passengers and crew, did all in his power to prevent panic and did his best to assist in the evacuation; Major <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Godfrey_Peuchen" title="Arthur Godfrey Peuchen">Arthur Godfrey Peuchen</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Yacht_Club" title="Royal Canadian Yacht Club">Royal Canadian Yacht Club</a> said "He was doing everything in his power to get women in these boats, and to see that they were lowered properly. I thought he was doing his duty in regard to the lowering of the boats".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-titanicinquiry.org_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-titanicinquiry.org-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Williams_Daniel" title="Robert Williams Daniel">Robert Williams Daniel</a>, a first class passenger also said:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Captain Smith was the biggest hero I ever saw. He stood on the bridge and shouted through a megaphone, trying to make himself heard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></i></dd>
</dl>
<p>Just minutes before the ship started its final plunge, Smith was still busy releasing <i>Titanic'</i>s crew from their duties; he went to the Marconi operators room and released Junior Marconi Officer <a href="/wiki/Harold_Bride" title="Harold Bride">Harold Bride</a> and senior wireless operator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_George_Phillips_(wireless_officer)" title="John George Phillips (wireless officer)">John "Jack" Phillips</a> from their duties. He then carried out a final tour of the deck, telling crew members: "Now it's every man for himself."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> At 2:10 a.m., Steward Edward Brown saw the captain approach with a megaphone in his hand. He heard him say "Well boys, do your best for the women and children, and look out for yourselves.” He saw the Captain walk onto the bridge alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This was the last reliable sighting of Smith. A few minutes later Trimmer Samuel Hemming found the bridge apparently empty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Five minutes later, the ship disappeared beneath the ocean. Smith perished that night along with around 1,500 others, and his body was never recovered.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/The_captain_goes_down_with_the_ship" title="The captain goes down with the ship">The captain goes down with the ship</a></div>
<p>There are conflicting accounts of Smith's death. Some survivors<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> said they saw Smith enter the ship's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pilothouse" title="Pilothouse">wheelhouse</a> on the bridge, and die there when it was engulfed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Herald" title="New York Herald">New York Herald</a></i> in its 19 April 1912 edition quoted <a href="/wiki/Robert_Williams_Daniel" title="Robert Williams Daniel">Robert Williams Daniel</a>, who jumped from the stern immediately before the ship sank, in its 19 April 1912 edition as have claimed to have witnessed Captain Smith drown in the ship's wheelhouse. <i>"I saw Captain Smith on the bridge. My eyes seemingly clung to him. The deck from which I had leapt was immersed. The water had risen slowly, and was now to the floor of the bridge. Then it was to Captain Smith's waist. I saw him no more. He died a hero."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Captain Smith himself made statements hinting that he would go down with his ship if he was ever confronted with a disaster. A friend of Smith's, Dr. Williams, asked Captain Smith what would happen if the Adriatic struck a concealed reef of ice and was badly damaged. "Some of us would go to the bottom with the ship," was Smith's reply. A boyhood friend, William Jones said, "Ted Smith passed away just as he would have loved to do. To stand on the bridge of his vessel and go down with her was characteristic of all his actions when we were boys together."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seaofglass_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-seaofglass-17">[17]</a></sup> Because of these factors as well as the accounts of Smith going inside the wheelhouse, this has remained the iconic image which has remained of Smith and has been perpetuated by film portrayals.</p>
<p>When working to free Collapsible B, Junior Marconi Officer <a href="/wiki/Harold_Bride" title="Harold Bride">Harold Bride</a> said he saw Captain Smith dive from the bridge into the sea just as Collapsible B was levered off the roof of the officers' quarters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> a story which was corroborated by first class passenger Mrs Eleanor Widener, who was in Lifeboat No.4 (the closest to the sinking ship) at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Also second class passenger William John Mellors, who survived aboard collapsible B, stated that Smith jumped from the bridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shots_in_the_dark_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shots_in_the_dark-20">[20]</a></sup> Tim Maltin, author of <i>101 Things You Thought You Knew About The Titanic - But Didn't!</i> affirms that the witnesses "could here be mistaking Captain Smith for Lightoller, who we know did exactly this at this time, first swimming towards the crow's nest."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.co.uk_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.co.uk-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several accounts say that Smith may have been seen in the water near the overturned Collapsible B during or after the sinking. Colonel Archibald Gracie reported that an unknown swimmer came near the capsized and overcrowded lifeboat, and that one of the men on board told him "Hold on to what you have, old boy. One more of you aboard would sink us all,"; in a powerful voice, the swimmer replied "All right boys. Good luck and God bless you.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Gracie did not see this man, nor was able to identify him, but some other survivors later claimed to have recognised this man as Smith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Another man (or possibly the same) never asked to come aboard the boat, but instead cheered its occupants saying "Good boys! Good lads!" with "the voice of authority".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANTR_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-ANTR-25">[25]</a></sup> One of the Collapsible B survivors, fireman Walter Hurst, tried to reach him with an oar, but the rapidly rising swell carried the man away before he could reach him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANTR_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-ANTR-25">[25]</a></sup> Hurst said he was certain this man was Smith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ANTR_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-ANTR-25">[25]</a></sup> Some of these accounts also describe Smith carrying a child to the boat. Harry Senior, one of Titanic's stokers, and second class passenger Charles Eugene Williams, who both survived aboard Collapsible B, stated that Smith <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shots_in_the_dark_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shots_in_the_dark-20">[20]</a></sup> swam with a child in his arms to Collapsible B, which Smith presented to a steward, after which he apparently swam back to the rapidly foundering ship. Williams' account differs slightly, claiming that, after Smith handed the child over to the steward, he asked what had become of First Officer Murdoch. Upon hearing news of Murdoch's demise, Smith "pushed himself away from the lifeboat, threw his lifebelt from him and slowly sank from our sight. He did not come to the surface again." These accounts are almost certainly apocryphal, according to historians featured in the A&amp;E Documentary <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titanic:_Death_of_a_Dream" title="Titanic: Death of a Dream">Titanic: Death of a Dream</a></i>. Lightoller who survived on Collapsible B never reported seeing Smith in the water or receiving a child from him. There is also no way in which survivors on Collapsible B would have been able to verify the identity of the individual concerned under such dimly lit and chaotic circumstances. It is more likely based upon wishful thinking that the person they saw was the Captain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Captain Smith's fate will probably remain uncertain.</p>
<p>For many years, there were also conflicting accounts of Smith's last words. Newspaper reports said that as the final plunge began, Smith advised those on board to "<i>Be British boys, be British!"</i> Although this is engraved on his memorial and portrayed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titanic_(1996_TV_miniseries)" title="Titanic (1996 TV miniseries)">1996 TV miniseries</a>, it was just a myth popularised by the British press at the time; Smith was an experienced transatlantic captain and a cosmopolitan, sophisticated man. Had he been prone to this type of jingoistic statement, he certainly wouldn't have been so popular with the prominent Americans and Canadians who preferred to travel on ships he captained and to dine with him while on board. If he said these words to anyone, it would have been to the crew, but not one of the surviving crew members claimed he said this. Since Steward Brown's account of Smith giving orders before walking onto the bridge was the last reliable sighting, this would make Smith's last words simply "Well boys, do your best for the women and children, and look out for yourselves.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.co.uk_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.co.uk-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span></h2>

<p>A statue, sculpted by <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Scott" title="Kathleen Scott">Kathleen Scott</a>, wife of Antarctic explorer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Falcon_Scott" title="Robert Falcon Scott">Robert Falcon Scott</a>, was unveiled in July 1914 at the western end of the Museum Gardens in <a href="/wiki/Beacon_Park" title="Beacon Park">Beacon Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lichfield" title="Lichfield">Lichfield</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The pedestal is made from Cornish granite and the figure is bronze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noz_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-noz-28">[28]</a></sup> Lichfield was chosen as the location for the monument because Smith was a Staffordshire man and Lichfield was the centre of the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Lichfield" title="Diocese of Lichfield">diocese</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LM_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-LM-29">[29]</a></sup> The statue originally cost £740 (£60,000 with inflation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-30">[30]</a></sup>) raised through local and national contributions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LM_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-LM-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>For his stoicism—the “<a href="/wiki/Stiff_upper_lip" title="Stiff upper lip">stiff upper lip</a>” self-discipline, fortitude and remaining calm in the face of adversity, which popular culture rendered into a British character trait, the plaque below his memorial statue states;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2010, as part of the 'Parks for People' programme, the statue was restored and the green <a href="/wiki/Patina" title="Patina">patina</a> removed from its surface at a cost of £16,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LM_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-LM-29">[29]</a></sup> In 2011 an unsuccessful campaign was started to get the statue moved back to Captain Smith's home town of Hanley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmslsa2_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmslsa2-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smith had already been commemorated in Hanley's Town Hall with a plaque reading:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>This tablet is dedicated to the memory of Commander  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] Edward John Smith RD, RNR. Born in Hanley, 27th Jany 1850, died at sea, 15th April 1912. Be British.</p>
<p>Whilst in command of the White Star SS <i>Titanic</i> that great ship struck an iceberg in the Atlantic Ocean during the night and speedily sank with nearly all who were on board. Captain Smith having done all man could do for the safety of passengers and crew remained at his post on the sinking ship until the end. His last message to the crew was "Be British."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The plaque was removed in 1961, given to a local school and then returned to the Town Hall but remounted in the interior of the building in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Titanic_Brewery" title="Titanic Brewery">Titanic Brewery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Burslem" title="Burslem">Burslem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stoke-on-Trent" title="Stoke-on-Trent">Stoke-on-Trent</a> is in honour of him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decorations">Decorations</span></h2>
<p>As a member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_Reserve" title="Royal Naval Reserve">Royal Naval Reserve</a>, Smith wore his two decorations when in uniform: the <a href="/wiki/Decoration_for_Officers_of_the_Royal_Naval_Reserve" title="Decoration for Officers of the Royal Naval Reserve">Decoration for Officers of the Royal Naval Reserve</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Transport_Medal" title="Transport Medal">Transport Medal</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span></h2>
<p>Smith’s half-sister Thyrza died in 1921 and his widow, Sarah Eleanor Smith, was hit and killed by a taxi in London in 1931.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cooper2011_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cooper2011-36">[36]</a></sup> Their daughter, Helen Melville, married and gave birth to twins, Simon and Priscilla. Simon, a pilot in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a>, was killed in World War II. Priscilla died from polio three years later; neither of them had children. Helen died in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC17447183_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC17447183-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
