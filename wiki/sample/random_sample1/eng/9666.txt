<p><b>Edward Reilly Stettinius Jr.</b> (October 22, 1900 – October 31, 1949) was an American businessman who served as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">United States Secretary of State</a> under Presidents <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Harry S. Truman</a> from 1944 to 1945, and as U.S. Ambassador to the United Nations from 1945 to 1946.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Stettinius_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Edward Reilly Stettinius was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois" title="Chicago, Illinois">Chicago, Illinois</a> on October 22, 1900, the younger of two sons and third of four children of <a href="/wiki/Edward_R._Stettinius" title="Edward R. Stettinius">Edward R. Stettinius</a> and Judith Carrington. His mother was a <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginian</a> of colonial English ancestry. His father was of German descent, and was a native of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chernow_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-chernow-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The younger Stettinius grew up in a mansion on the family's estate on <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a> and graduated from the <a href="/wiki/Pomfret_School" title="Pomfret School">Pomfret School</a> in 1920, after which he attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a> until 1924, leaving without a degree; while at Virginia he became a member of the secret <a href="/wiki/Seven_Society" title="Seven Society">Seven Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sevens_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sevens-2">[2]</a></sup> On May 15, 1926, Stettinius married Virginia Gordon Wallace, daughter of a prominent family of <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond, Virginia</a>. They had three children: Edward Reilly, and the twins Wallace and Joseph.</p>
<p>In 1926, Stettinius began working at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Motors_Corporation" title="General Motors Corporation">General Motors</a> as a stock clerk, but his connections made for rapid advancement. He became assistant to <a href="/wiki/John_Lee_Pratt" title="John Lee Pratt">John Lee Pratt</a>, a friend of the family, and by 1931 he had become vice president, in charge of public and industrial relations. At General Motors he worked to develop unemployment relief programs, and through this he came into contact with Franklin D. Roosevelt.</p>
<p>During the 1930s, Stettinius' work in the private sector alternated with public service. He served on the Industrial Advisory Board of the National Recovery Administration (1933), but in 1934 he returned to the private sector when he joined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Steel_Corporation" title="United States Steel Corporation">US Steel</a> where he eventually become chairman of the nation's largest corporation in 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>He then returned to public service, serving on the National Defense Advisory Commission, as chairman of the War Resources Board (1939) and administrator of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lend-lease" title="Lend-lease">Lend-lease</a> Program (1941).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He held the latter position until he became undersecretary of state in 1943. Due to the poor health of Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/Cordell_Hull" title="Cordell Hull">Cordell Hull</a>, he chaired the 1944 <a href="/wiki/Dumbarton_Oaks_Conference" title="Dumbarton Oaks Conference">Dumbarton Oaks Conference</a>, and in December 1944, succeeded Hull as Secretary of State. Stettinius was a member of the US delegation to the 1945 <a href="/wiki/Yalta_Conference" title="Yalta Conference">Yalta Conference</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Stettinius chaired the United States delegation to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_International_Organization" title="United Nations Conference on International Organization">United Nations Conference on International Organization</a> held in San Francisco from April 25 to June 26, 1945, which brought together delegates from 50 Allied nations to create the United Nations. He resigned as Secretary of State to take up the position of the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassadors_to_the_United_Nations" title="United States Ambassadors to the United Nations">United States Ambassador to the United Nations</a>. Stettinius resigned from this post in June 1946, after which he became critical of what he saw as Truman's refusal to use the UN as a tool to resolve tensions with the Soviet Union. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_W._Yost" title="Charles W. Yost">Charles W. Yost</a>, Stettinius' aide, in the State Department and at the conference, followed him as UN Ambassador twenty-six years later.</p>
<p>Prematurely white-haired, with dark eyebrows, blue eyes, tanned face, and a quick smile, Stettinius was striking in appearance and inspired goodwill.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For three years after his return to private life he served as rector of the University of Virginia. A longtime friend of <a href="/wiki/William_Tubman" title="William Tubman">William Tubman</a>, the president of <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>, he helped form (1947) and headed as board chairman the Liberia Company, a partnership between the Liberian government and American financiers to provide funds for the development of that African nation. He lived during his retirement at his estate, The Horseshoe, on the <a href="/wiki/Rapidan_River" title="Rapidan River">Rapidan River</a>, Virginia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He died of a coronary <a href="/wiki/Thrombosis" title="Thrombosis">thrombosis</a> on October 31, 1949, at the home of a sister in <a href="/wiki/Greenwich,_Connecticut" title="Greenwich, Connecticut">Greenwich, Connecticut</a>, at the age of 49, and was buried in the family plot in Locust Valley Cemetery, <a href="/wiki/Locust_Valley,_New_York" title="Locust Valley, New York">Locust Valley, New York</a>.</p>
<p>Stettinius was a character in <a href="/wiki/Robert_Conroy" title="Robert Conroy">Robert Conroy</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Inferno:_1945" title="Red Inferno: 1945">Red Inferno: 1945</a></i>.</p>
<p>Stettinius's voluminous archive of more than 1,000 boxes resides at the <a href="/wiki/Albert_and_Shirley_Small_Special_Collections_Library" title="Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library">Albert and Shirley Small Special Collections Library</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a>.</p>
