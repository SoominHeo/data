<p><b>Edward Palmer Thompson</b> (3 February 1924 – 28 August 1993), usually cited as <b>E. P. Thompson</b>, was a British historian, writer, socialist and peace campaigner. He is probably best known today for his historical work on the British radical movements in the late 18th and early 19th centuries, in particular <i><a href="/wiki/The_Making_of_the_English_Working_Class" title="The Making of the English Working Class">The Making of the English Working Class</a></i> (1963).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CollsStillRelevant_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-CollsStillRelevant-1">[1]</a></sup> He also published influential biographies of <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a> (1955) and (posthumously) <a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">William Blake</a> (1993) and was a prolific journalist and essayist. He also published the novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sykaos_Papers" title="The Sykaos Papers">The Sykaos Papers</a></i> and a collection of poetry. His work is considered to have been among the most important contributions to <a href="/wiki/Labor_history_(discipline)" title="Labor history (discipline)">labour history</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_history" title="Social history">social history</a> in the latter twentieth-century, with a global impact, including on scholarship in Asia and Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thompson was one of the principal intellectuals of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Great_Britain" title="Communist Party of Great Britain">Communist Party</a> in Great Britain. Although he left the party in 1956 over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1956_Hungarian_Revolution" title="1956 Hungarian Revolution">Soviet invasion</a> of Hungary, he nevertheless remained a "historian in the <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Marxist</a> tradition", calling for a rebellion against <a href="/wiki/Stalinism" title="Stalinism">Stalinism</a> as a prerequisite for the restoration of communists' "confidence in our own revolutionary perspectives".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Thompson played a key role in the first <a href="/wiki/New_Left" title="New Left">New Left</a> in Britain in the late 1950s. He was a vociferous left-wing socialist critic of the Labour governments of 1964–70 and 1974–79, and an early and constant supporter of the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Nuclear_Disarmament" title="Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament">Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament</a>, becoming during the 1980s the leading intellectual light of the movement against nuclear weapons in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GriffinUnconventional_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-GriffinUnconventional-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span></h2>
<p>E.P. Thompson was born in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodist" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> missionary parents: His father, <a href="/wiki/Edward_John_Thompson" title="Edward John Thompson">Edward John Thompson</a> (1886–1946) was a poet and admirer of the Nobel Prize–winning poet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tagore" title="Tagore">Tagore</a>. His older brother was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Frank_Thompson" title="William Frank Thompson">William Frank Thompson</a> (1919–1944), a British officer in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, who was captured and shot aiding the Bulgarian anti-fascist partisans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GhodseeVagabond_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-GhodseeVagabond-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thompson attended two independent schools, <a href="/wiki/Dragon_School" title="Dragon School">The Dragon School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingswood_School" title="Kingswood School">Kingswood School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>. Like many he left school in 1941 to fight in World War II. He served in a tank unit in the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Campaign_(World_War_II)" title="Italian Campaign (World War II)">Italian campaign</a>, including at the last battle of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Monte_Cassino" title="Battle of Monte Cassino">Cassino</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>After his military service, he studied at <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi_College,_Cambridge" title="Corpus Christi College, Cambridge">Corpus Christi College</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>, where he joined the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Great_Britain" title="Communist Party of Great Britain">Communist Party of Great Britain</a>. In 1946, E.P. Thompson formed the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_Historians_Group" title="Communist Party Historians Group">Communist Party Historians Group</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Edward_Christopher_Hill" title="John Edward Christopher Hill">Christopher Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Hobsbawm" title="Eric Hobsbawm">Eric Hobsbawm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Hilton" title="Rodney Hilton">Rodney Hilton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dona_Torr" title="Dona Torr">Dona Torr</a> and others. In 1952 they launched the influential journal <i><a href="/wiki/Past_%26_Present_(journal)" title="Past &amp; Present (journal)">Past and Present</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GriffinUnconventional_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-GriffinUnconventional-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="William_Morris"><i>William Morris</i></span></h3>
<p>Thompson's first major work of scholarship was his biography of <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a>, written while he was a member of the Communist Party. Subtitled <i>From Romantic to Revolutionary</i>, it was part of an effort by the Communist Party Historians' Group, inspired by Torr, to emphasise the domestic roots of Marxism in Britain at a time when the Communist Party was under attack for always following the Moscow line. It was also an attempt to take Morris back from the critics who for more than 50 years had emphasised his art and downplayed his politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although Morris's political work is well to the fore, Thompson also used his literary talents to comment on aspects of Morris's work, such as his early Romantic poetry, which had previously received relatively little consideration. As Thompson noted in his preface to the second edition (1976), the first edition (1955) appears to have received relatively little attention from the literary establishment because of its then-unfashionable Marxist point of view. However, the somewhat rewritten second edition was much better received.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_first_New_Left">The first New Left</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:5709-newreasoner-cover-v1n01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2594" data-file-width="1707" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f8/5709-newreasoner-cover-v1n01.jpg/200px-5709-newreasoner-cover-v1n01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f8/5709-newreasoner-cover-v1n01.jpg/300px-5709-newreasoner-cover-v1n01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f8/5709-newreasoner-cover-v1n01.jpg/400px-5709-newreasoner-cover-v1n01.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:5709-newreasoner-cover-v1n01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Thompson launched the dissident Marxist journal <i>The New Reasoner</i> in the summer of 1957. The publication would merge to form <i>New Left Review</i> in 1960.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/On_the_Personality_Cult_and_its_Consequences" title="On the Personality Cult and its Consequences">"secret speech"</a> to the 20th Congress of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Communist Party of the Soviet Union</a> in 1956, which revealed that the Soviet party leadership had long been aware of Stalin's crimes, Thompson (with <a href="/wiki/John_Saville" title="John Saville">John Saville</a> and others) started a dissident publication inside the CP, called <i>The Reasoner</i>. Six months later, he and most of his comrades left the party in disgust at the Soviet invasion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1956_Hungarian_Revolution" title="1956 Hungarian Revolution">Hungary</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>But Thompson remained what he called a "socialist humanist". With Saville and others, he set up the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Reasoner" title="New Reasoner">New Reasoner</a></i>, a journal that sought to develop a democratic socialist alternative to what its editors considered the ossified official <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxism</a> of the Communist and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trotskyist" title="Trotskyist">Trotskyist</a> parties and the managerialist cold war social democracy of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> and its international allies. The <i>New Reasoner</i> was the most important organ of what became known as the "<a href="/wiki/New_Left" title="New Left">New Left</a>", an informal movement of dissident leftists closely associated with the nascent movement for nuclear disarmament in the late 1950s and early 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i>New Reasoner</i> combined with the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universities_and_Left_Review" title="Universities and Left Review">Universities and Left Review</a></i> to form <i><a href="/wiki/New_Left_Review" title="New Left Review">New Left Review</a></i> in 1960, though Thompson and others fell out with the group around <a href="/wiki/Perry_Anderson" title="Perry Anderson">Perry Anderson</a> who took over the journal in 1962. The fashion ever since has been to describe the Thompson <i>et al.</i> New Left as "the first New Left" and the Anderson <i>et al.</i> group, which by 1968 had embraced <a href="/wiki/Tariq_Ali" title="Tariq Ali">Tariq Ali</a> and various Trotskyists, as the second.</p>
<p>Thompson subsequently allied himself with the annual <i><a href="/wiki/Socialist_Register" title="Socialist Register">Socialist Register</a></i> publication. With <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Williams" title="Raymond Williams">Raymond Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Hall_(cultural_theorist)" title="Stuart Hall (cultural theorist)">Stuart Hall</a>, he was one of the editors of the 1967 <i>May Day Manifesto</i>, one of the key left-wing challenges to the 1964–70 Labour government of <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BryanPalmer_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-BryanPalmer-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Making_of_the_English_Working_Class"><i>The Making of the English Working Class</i></span></h3>
<p>Thompson's most influential work was and remains <i><a href="/wiki/The_Making_of_the_English_Working_Class" title="The Making of the English Working Class">The Making of the English Working Class</a></i>, published in 1963 while he was working at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">University of Leeds</a>. The massive book, over 800 pages, was a watershed in the foundation of the field of <a href="/wiki/Social_history" title="Social history">social history</a>. By exploring the ordinary cultures of working people through their previously ignored documentary remains, Thompson told the forgotten history of the first working-class political left in the world in the late-18th and early-19th centuries. Reflecting on the importance of the book for its 50th anniversary, Emma Griffin explained that Thompson "uncovered details about workshop customs and rituals, failed conspiracies, threatening letters, popular songs, and union club cards. He took what others had regarded as scraps from the archive and interrogated them for what they told us about the beliefs and aims of those who were not on the winning side. Here, then, was a book that rambled over aspects of human experience that had never before had their historian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GriffinUnconventional_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-GriffinUnconventional-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Making of the English Working Class</i> had a profound effect on the shape of British historiography, and still endures as a staple on university reading lists more than 50 years after its first publication in 1963. Writing for the <a href="/wiki/Times_Higher_Education" title="Times Higher Education">Times Higher Education</a> in 2013, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Colls" title="Robert Colls">Robert Colls</a> recalled the power of Thompson's book for his generation of young British leftists:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I bought my first copy in 1968 – a small, fat bundle of <a href="/wiki/Pelican_Books" title="Pelican Books">Pelican</a> with a picture of a Yorkshire miner on the front – and I still have it, bandaged up and exhausted by the years of labour. From the first of its 900-odd pages, I knew, and my friends at the University of Sussex knew, that this was something else. We talked about it in the bar and on the bus and in the refectory queue. Imagine that: young male students more interested in a book than in gooseberry tart and custard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CollsStillRelevant_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-CollsStillRelevant-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In his preface to this book, E.P. Thompson set out his approach to writing history from below:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I am seeking to rescue the poor stockinger, the <a href="/wiki/Luddite" title="Luddite">Luddite</a> cropper, the "obsolete" hand-loom weaver, the "Utopian" artisan, and even the deluded follower of <a href="/wiki/Joanna_Southcott" title="Joanna Southcott">Joanna Southcott</a>, from the enormous condescension of posterity. Their crafts and traditions may have been dying. Their hostility to the new industrialism may have been backward-looking. Their communitarian ideals may have been fantasies. Their insurrectionary conspiracies may have been foolhardy. But they lived through these times of acute social disturbance, and we did not. Their aspirations were valid in terms of their own experience; and, if they were casualties of history, they remain, condemned in their own lives, as casualties.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Thompson's thought was also original and significant because of the way he defined "class." To Thompson, class was not a structure, but a relationship:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>And class happens when some men, as a result of common experiences (inherited or shared), feel and articulate the identity of their interests as between themselves, and as against other men whose interests are different from (and usually opposed to) theirs. The class experience is largely determined by the productive relations into which men are born—or enter involuntarily. Class-consciousness is the way in which these experiences are handled in cultural terms: embodied in traditions, value-systems, ideas, and institutional forms. If the experience appears as determined, class-consciousness does not. We can see a logic in the responses of similar occupational groups undergoing similar experiences, but we cannot predicate any law. Consciousness of class arises in the same way in different times and places, but never in just the same way.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>By re-defining class as a relationship that changed over time, Thompson proceeded to demonstrate how class was worthy of historical investigation. He opened the gates for a generation of labour historians, such as <a href="/wiki/David_Montgomery_(historian)" title="David Montgomery (historian)">David Montgomery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Gutman" title="Herbert Gutman">Herbert Gutman</a>, who made similar studies of the American working classes.</p>
<p>A major work of research and synthesis, the book was also important in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historiographical" title="Historiographical">historiographical</a> terms: with it, Thompson demonstrated the power of a historical Marxism rooted in the experience of real flesh-and-blood workers. Thompson wrote the book while living in Siddal, <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_West_Yorkshire" title="Halifax, West Yorkshire">Halifax, West Yorkshire</a> and based some of the work on his experiences with the local Halifax population.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Freelance_polemicist">Freelance polemicist</span></h3>
<p>Thompson left the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Warwick" title="University of Warwick">University of Warwick</a> in protest at the commercialisation of the academy, documented in the book <i>Warwick University Limited</i> (1971). He continued to teach and lecture as a visiting professor, particularly in the United States. However, he increasingly worked as a freelance writer, contributing many essays to <i><a href="/wiki/New_Society" title="New Society">New Society</a></i>, <i>Socialist Register</i> and historical journals. In 1978 he published <i>The Poverty of Theory</i> which attacked the <a href="/wiki/Structural_Marxism" title="Structural Marxism">structuralist Marxism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Louis_Althusser" title="Louis Althusser">Louis Althusser</a> and his followers in Britain on <i>New Left Review</i> (famously saying: "...all of them are <i>Geschichtenscheissenschlopff</i>, unhistorical shit"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>). The title echoes that of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a>'s 1847 polemic against <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Joseph_Proudhon" title="Pierre-Joseph Proudhon">Pierre-Joseph Proudhon</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Poverty_of_Philosophy" title="The Poverty of Philosophy">The Poverty of Philosophy</a></i> and that of philosopher <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Karl Popper</a>'s 1936 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Poverty_of_Historicism" title="The Poverty of Historicism">The Poverty of Historicism</a></i>. Thompson's polemic provoked a book-length response from <a href="/wiki/Perry_Anderson" title="Perry Anderson">Perry Anderson</a>, <i>Arguments Within English Marxism</i>.</p>
<p>During the late 1970s Thompson acquired a large public audience as a critic of the then Labour government's disregard of civil liberties; his writings from this time are collected in <i>Writing By Candlelight</i> (1980).</p>
<p>From 19891 Thompson was a frequent contributor to the American magazine <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nation" title="The Nation">The Nation</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thenation_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-thenation-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice_of_the_peace_movement">Voice of the peace movement</span></h3>
<p>From 1980, Thompson was the most prominent intellectual of the revived movement for <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_disarmament" title="Nuclear disarmament">nuclear disarmament</a>, revered by activists throughout the world. In Britain, his pamphlet <i>Protest and Survive</i>, a parody on the government leaflet <i><a href="/wiki/Protect_and_Survive" title="Protect and Survive">Protect and Survive</a></i>, played a major role in the revived strength of the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Nuclear_Disarmament" title="Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament">Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BryanPalmer_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-BryanPalmer-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Just as important, Thompson was, with <a href="/wiki/Ken_Coates" title="Ken Coates">Ken Coates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Kaldor" title="Mary Kaldor">Mary Kaldor</a> and others, an author of the 1980 <i>Appeal for European Nuclear Disarmament</i>, calling for a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear-free_zone" title="Nuclear-free zone">nuclear-free</a> Europe from Poland to Portugal, which was the founding document of <a href="/wiki/European_Nuclear_Disarmament" title="European Nuclear Disarmament">European Nuclear Disarmament</a>. Confusingly, END was both a Europe-wide campaign that comprised a series of large public conferences (the END Conventions), and a small British pressure group.</p>

</div>
</div>
<p>Thompson played a key role in both END and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CND" title="CND">CND</a> throughout the 1980s, speaking at many public meetings, corresponding with hundreds of fellow activists and sympathetic intellectuals, and doing more than his fair share of committee work. He had a particularly important part in opening a dialogue between the west European peace movement and dissidents in Soviet-dominated eastern Europe, particularly in Hungary and <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>, for which he was denounced as a tool of American imperialism by the Soviet authorities.</p>
<p>He wrote dozens of polemical articles and essays during this period, which are collected in the books <i>Zero Option</i> (1982) and <i>The Heavy Dancers</i> (1985). He also wrote an extended essay attacking the ideologists on both sides of the cold war, <i>Double Exposure</i> (1985) and edited a collection of essays opposing <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defense_Initiative" title="Strategic Defense Initiative">Strategic Defense Initiative</a>, <i>Star Wars</i> (1985).</p>
<p>An excerpt from a speech given by Thompson featured in the computer game <a href="/wiki/Deus_Ex_Machina_(video_game)" title="Deus Ex Machina (video game)">Deus Ex Machina</a> (1984). Thompson's own haunting recitation of his 1950 poem of "apocalyptic expectation, "The Place Called Choice," appeared on the 1984 vinyl recording "The Apocalypso", by Canadian pop group <a href="/wiki/Singing_Fools" title="Singing Fools">Singing Fools</a>, released by A&amp;M Records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="William_Blake">William Blake</span></h3>
<p>The last book Thompson finished was <i><a href="/wiki/Witness_Against_the_Beast" title="Witness Against the Beast">Witness Against the Beast: William Blake and the Moral Law</a></i> (1993). The product of years of research and published shortly after his death, it shows how far Blake was inspired by dissident religious ideas rooted in the thinking of the most radical opponents of the monarchy during the English civil war.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span></h2>
<p>In 1948 Thompson married <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Thompson_(historian)" title="Dorothy Thompson (historian)">Dorothy Towers</a>, whom he met at Cambridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> A fellow left-wing historian, she wrote studies on women in the <a href="/wiki/Chartism" title="Chartism">Chartist</a> movement, and the biography <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victoria_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Victoria of the United Kingdom">Queen Victoria</a>: Gender and Power</i>; she was Professor of History at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Birmingham" title="University of Birmingham">University of Birmingham</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The Thompsons had three children, the youngest of whom is the award-winning children's writer, <a href="/wiki/Kate_Thompson_(author)" title="Kate Thompson (author)">Kate Thompson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thompson was a user of marijuana, although he never made his habit public during his lifetime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thompson died at the age of 69 in <a href="/wiki/Worcester" title="Worcester">Worcester</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="William_Frank_Thompson">William Frank Thompson</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Frank_Thompson" title="William Frank Thompson">William Frank Thompson</a></div>
<p>Thompson's older brother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Frank_Thompson" title="William Frank Thompson">William Frank Thompson</a> (1920–1944), was also a member of the British Communist Party during World War II. A gifted linguist, Frank Thompson parachuted into fascist-occupied Bulgaria as part of a "Phantom Brigade" during <i>Operation Mulligatawny</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConradiVeryEnglish_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ConradiVeryEnglish-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> There, he supported the resistance as a liaison officer. Frank Thompson was captured and on 10 June 1944 he was executed. His body was buried in the War Cemetery of Sofia. After the war, the Bulgarians erected a statue in his honour. The nearby villages of Livage, Lipata, Tsarevi Stragi, Malak Babul, Babul and Zavoya were merged and renamed to <a href="/wiki/Thompson,_Bulgaria" title="Thompson, Bulgaria">Thompson</a> in the British officer's honour.</p>
<p>E. P. Thompson and his mother wrote <i>There is a Spirit in Europe: A Memoir of Frank Thompson</i> (1947). Frank Thompson was also a friend and confidant of <a href="/wiki/Iris_Murdoch" title="Iris Murdoch">Iris Murdoch</a>, the philosopher and novelist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> E. P. Thompson wrote another book about his brother, published in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span></h2>
<p>Although Thompson left the Communist Party of Great Britain, but remained committed to Marxist ideals, <a href="/wiki/Leszek_Ko%C5%82akowski" title="Leszek Kołakowski">Leszek Kołakowski</a> wrote a very harsh criticism of Thompson in his 1974 essay "My Correct Views on Everything."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tony_Judt" title="Tony Judt">Tony Judt</a> considered this rejoinder so authoritative that he claimed that "no one who reads it will ever take E.P. Thompson seriously again". Kolakowski's portrait of Thompson elicited some protests from readers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and other left-wing journals came to Thompson's defence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> On the 50th anniversary of the landmark publication of <i>The Making of the English Working Class</i>, journalists celebrated E.P. Thompson as one of the preeminent historians of his day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CollsStillRelevant_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-CollsStillRelevant-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wade Matthews argued in 2013:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Numerous books, special collections, and journal articles on E.P. Thompson's scholarly work and legacy appeared soon after his death in 1993. Since then, however, interest in Thompson has waned. The reasons for this are perhaps easily enough summarized. Today, Thompson's histories are viewed as old-fashioned, while his socialist politics are believed extinct. Class is considered neither a fruitful concept of historical analysis nor an appropriate basis for an emancipatory politics. Nuclear weapons proliferate, but no anti-nuclear movement grows up alongside their proliferation. Civil liberties are a minority, and increasingly "radical," interest in the age of the "war on terror." Internationalism, as ideology and practice, is the preserve of capital not labour. At the beginning of the twenty-first century, then, Thompson seems out of place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
