<p><b>Edirne</b> (<small>Turkish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Turkic_dialects" title="Help:IPA for Turkic dialects">[eˈdiɾne]</a></span>), historically known as <i>Adrianople</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgarian</a> known as <i>Odrin</i>, is a city in the northwestern <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkish</a> province of <a href="/wiki/Edirne_Province" title="Edirne Province">Edirne</a>, in the region of <a href="/wiki/East_Thrace" title="East Thrace">East Thrace</a>, close to Turkey's borders with <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>. Edirne served as the third capital city of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> from 1363 to 1453,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> before <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>) <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople" title="Fall of Constantinople">became</a> the empire's fourth and final capital. At present, Edirne is the capital of <a href="/wiki/Edirne_Province" title="Edirne Province">Edirne Province</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Thrace" title="East Thrace">Turkish Thrace</a>. The city's estimated population in 2014 was 165,979.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edirne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city was founded as <i>Hadrianopolis</i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Aδριανούπολις</span> in <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>), named for the Roman Emperor <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">Hadrian</a>. This name is still used in the <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">Modern Greek</a> (<span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Αδριανούπολη</span>). The name <i>Adrianople</i> was used in English, until the Turkish adoption of Latin alphabet in 1928 made Edirne the internationally recognized name. The <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr"><i>Edirne</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>: <span lang="bg" xml:lang="bg">Одрин (<i>Odrin</i>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">['odrin]</span>)</span>, <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a>: <span lang="sq" xml:lang="sq"><i>Edrêne</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">Macedonian</a>: <span lang="mk" xml:lang="mk">Одрин (<i>Odrin</i>)</span> and <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a>: <span lang="sr" xml:lang="sr">Једрене / Jedrene</span> are adapted forms of the name <i>Hadrianopolis</i> or of its Turkish version; see also its <a href="/wiki/Names_of_European_cities_in_different_languages:_E%E2%80%93H#E" title="Names of European cities in different languages: E–H">other names</a>.</p>

<p>The area around Edirne has been the site of <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Adrianople_(disambiguation)" title="Battle of Adrianople (disambiguation)">no fewer than 16 major battles or sieges</a>, from the days of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greeks" title="Ancient Greeks">ancient Greeks</a>. Military historian <a href="/wiki/John_Keegan" title="John Keegan">John Keegan</a> identifies it as "the most contested spot on the globe" and attributes this to its geographical location.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edirne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Greek mythology, <a href="/wiki/Orestes" title="Orestes">Orestes</a>, son of king <a href="/wiki/Agamemnon" title="Agamemnon">Agamemnon</a>, built this city as <i>Orestias</i>, at the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/Tundzha" title="Tundzha">Tonsus</a> (Toundja) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arda_River_(Maritsa)" title="Arda River (Maritsa)">Ardiscus</a> (Arda) with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrus" title="Hebrus">Hebrus</a> (Maritza). The city was (re)founded eponymously by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Emperor" title="Roman Emperor">Roman Emperor</a> Hadrian on the site of a previous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_ancient_Thracian_cities" title="List of ancient Thracian cities">Thracian settlement</a> known as <i>Uskadama</i>, <i>Uskudama</i>, <i>Uskodama</i> or <i>Uscudama</i>. It was the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Bessi" title="Bessi">Bessi</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odrysians" title="Odrysians">Odrysians</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">Hadrian</a> developed it, adorned it with monuments, changed its name to <i>Hadrianopolis</i> after himself (which would be corrupted into <i>Adrianopolis</i>, <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">Anglicised</a> as <i>Adrianople</i>), and made it the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Thracia" title="Thracia">Roman province of Thrace</a>. <a href="/wiki/Licinius" title="Licinius">Licinius</a> was defeated there by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I" title="Constantine I">Constantine I</a> in 323, and Emperor <a href="/wiki/Valens" title="Valens">Valens</a> was killed by the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a> in 378 during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Adrianople" title="Battle of Adrianople">Battle of Adrianople</a> (378).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_period">Medieval period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edirne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Medieval period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cihan-numa-kasri.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="367" data-file-width="400" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Cihan-numa-kasri.jpg/220px-Cihan-numa-kasri.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Cihan-numa-kasri.jpg/330px-Cihan-numa-kasri.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bc/Cihan-numa-kasri.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cihan-numa-kasri.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Historical image of Cihannüma Kasrı (Panoramic Pavilion), part of <a href="/wiki/Edirne_Palace" title="Edirne Palace">Edirne Palace</a> complex</div>

<p>In 813, the city was seized by <a href="/wiki/Khan_(title)" title="Khan (title)">Khan</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krum_of_Bulgaria" title="Krum of Bulgaria">Krum of Bulgaria</a> who moved its inhabitants to the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_lands_across_the_Danube" title="Bulgarian lands across the Danube">Bulgarian lands towards the north of the Danube</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>During the existence of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Empire" title="Latin Empire">Latin Empire</a> of Constantinople, the Crusaders were decisively defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgarian</a> Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaloyan" title="Kaloyan">Kaloyan</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Adrianople_(1205)" title="Battle of Adrianople (1205)">Battle of Adrianople (1205)</a>. Later <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Komnenos_Doukas" title="Theodore Komnenos Doukas">Theodore Komnenos</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Despot_of_Epirus" title="Despot of Epirus">Despot of Epirus</a>, took possession of it in 1227, but three years later was defeated at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Klokotnitsa" title="Battle of Klokotnitsa">Klokotnitsa</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Asen_II_of_Bulgaria" title="Ivan Asen II of Bulgaria">Asen</a>, Emperor of the Bulgarians.</p>
<p>In 1369, the city was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Adrianople" title="Conquest of Adrianople">conquered</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> sultan <a href="/wiki/Murad_I" title="Murad I">Murad I</a>. He renamed it "Edirne".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The city remained the Ottoman capital for 90 years until 1453, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mehmed_II" title="Mehmed II">Mehmed II</a> moved the capital to <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>). Edirne is famed for its many mosques, domes, minarets, and palaces from the Ottoman period.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_period">Modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edirne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:280713-2114AA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="782" data-file-width="1094" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/280713-2114AA.jpg/220px-280713-2114AA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/280713-2114AA.jpg/330px-280713-2114AA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/280713-2114AA.jpg/440px-280713-2114AA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Under Ottoman rule, Edirne was the principal city of the administrative unit, the eponymous <a href="/wiki/Eyalet_of_Adrianople" title="Eyalet of Adrianople">Eyalet of Edirne</a>, and after land reforms in 1867, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vilayet_of_Adrianople" title="Vilayet of Adrianople">Vilayet of Edirne</a>. Sultan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mehmed_II" title="Mehmed II">Mehmed II</a>, the conqueror of Constantinople, was born in Edirne. It was here that he fell under the influence of some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurufi" title="Hurufi">Hurufis</a> dismissed by Taş Köprü Zade in the <i>Şakaiki Numaniye</i> as "<a href="/wiki/Certain_accursed_ones_of_no_significance" title="Certain accursed ones of no significance">Certain accursed ones of no significance</a>", who were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burned_at_the_stake" title="Burned at the stake">burnt</a> as <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heretics</a> by a certain Mahmud Pasha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sultan <a href="/wiki/Mehmed_IV" title="Mehmed IV">Mehmed IV</a> left the palace in Constantinople and died in Edirne in 1693.</p>
<p>During his exile in the Ottoman Empire, the Swedish king <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_XII" title="Charles XII">Charles XII</a> stayed in the city during most of 1713.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h" title="Bahá'u'lláh">Bahá'u'lláh</a>, the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'í Faith</a>, lived in Edirne from 1863 to 1868. He was exiled there by the Ottoman Empire before being banished further to the Ottoman penal colony in <a href="/wiki/Acre,_Israel" title="Acre, Israel">Akka</a>. He referred to Edirne in his writings as the "Land of Mystery".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Edirne was a <a href="/wiki/Sanjak" title="Sanjak">sanjak</a> centre during the Ottoman period and was bound to, successively, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rumeli_Eyalet" title="Rumeli Eyalet">Rumeli Eyalet</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silistre_Eyalet" title="Silistre Eyalet">Silistre Eyalet</a> before becoming a provincial capital of the Eyalet of Edirne at the beginning of the 19th century; until 1878, the Eyalet of Edirne comprised the <a href="/wiki/Sanjak" title="Sanjak">sanjaks</a> of Edirne, <a href="/wiki/Tekirda%C4%9F" title="Tekirdağ">Tekfurdağı</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gelibolu" title="Gelibolu">Gelibolu</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filibe" title="Filibe">Filibe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sliven" title="Sliven">İslimye</a>.</p>
<p>Edirne was briefly occupied by imperial Russian troops in 1829 during the <a href="/wiki/Greek_War_of_Independence" title="Greek War of Independence">Greek War of Independence</a> and in 1878 during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russo-Turkish_War_(1877%E2%80%9378)" title="Russo-Turkish War (1877–78)">Russo-Turkish War of 1877–1878</a>. The city suffered a fire in 1905. In 1905 it had about 80,000 inhabitants, of whom 30,000 were Turks; 22,000 Greeks; 10,000 Bulgarians; 4,000 Armenians; 12,000 Jews; and 2,000 more citizens of non-classifiable ethnic/religious backgrounds.<small>[citation required]</small></p>
<p>Edirne was a vital fortress defending Ottoman <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Thrace" title="Eastern Thrace">Eastern Thrace</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Balkan_Wars" title="Balkan Wars">Balkan Wars</a> of 1912–13. It was briefly occupied by the Bulgarians in 1913, following the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Adrianople_(1912%E2%80%9313)" title="Siege of Adrianople (1912–13)">Siege of Adrianople</a>. The Great Powers–Britain, Italy, France, and Russia–forced the Ottoman Empire to cede Edirne to <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> at the end of <a href="/wiki/First_Balkan_War" title="First Balkan War">First Balkan War</a>, which created a political scandal in the Ottoman government in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> (as Edirne was a former capital of the Empire), leading to the <a href="/wiki/1913_Ottoman_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="1913 Ottoman coup d'état">1913 Ottoman coup d'état</a>. Although it was victorious in the coup, the <a href="/wiki/Committee_of_Union_and_Progress" title="Committee of Union and Progress">Committee of Union and Progress</a> was unable to keep Edirne, but under <a href="/wiki/Enver_Pasha" title="Enver Pasha">Enver Pasha</a> (who proclaimed himself the "second conqueror of Edirne", after <a href="/wiki/Murad_I" title="Murad I">Murad I</a>), it retook it from the Bulgarians soon after the <a href="/wiki/Second_Balkan_War" title="Second Balkan War">Second Balkan War</a> began.</p>
<p>It was occupied by the Greeks between the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_S%C3%A8vres" title="Treaty of Sèvres">Treaty of Sèvres</a> in 1920 and their defeat at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Turkish_War_(1919%E2%80%9322)" title="Greco-Turkish War (1919–22)">Greco-Turkish War</a>, also known as the Western Front of the larger <a href="/wiki/Turkish_War_of_Independence" title="Turkish War of Independence">Turkish War of Independence</a>, in 1922.</p>
<p>According to the 2007 census, <a href="/wiki/Edirne_Province" title="Edirne Province">Edirne Province</a> had a population of 382,222 inhabitants. The city is a thriving center of commerce for woven textiles, silks, carpets and agricultural products.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecclesiastical_history">Ecclesiastical history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edirne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ecclesiastical history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Edirne_7349_Nevit.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="592" data-file-width="896" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Edirne_7349_Nevit.JPG/250px-Edirne_7349_Nevit.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Edirne_7349_Nevit.JPG/375px-Edirne_7349_Nevit.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Edirne_7349_Nevit.JPG/500px-Edirne_7349_Nevit.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>

<p>Adrianople was made the seat of a Greek metropolitan and of a Gregorian Armenian bishop. Adrianople is also the centre of a Bulgarian diocese, but not recognized and deprived of a bishop. The city also had some Protestants. The Latin Catholics, foreigners for the most part, and not numerous, were dependent on the vicariate-apostolic of Constantinople. At Adrianople itself were the parish of St. Anthony of Padua (Minors Conventual) and a school for girls conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Sisters_of_Charity" title="Sisters of Charity">Sisters of Charity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Zagreb" title="Zagreb">Agram</a>. In the suburb of <a href="/wiki/Karaa%C4%9Fa%C3%A7,_Edirne" title="Karaağaç, Edirne">Karaağaç</a> were a church (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_Conventuals" title="Minor Conventuals">Minor Conventuals</a>), a school for boys (Assumptionists) and a school for girls (Oblates of the Assumption). Each of its mission stations, at <a href="/wiki/Tekirda%C4%9F" title="Tekirdağ">Tekirdağ</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexandroupoli" title="Alexandroupoli">Alexandroupoli</a>, had a school (Minor Conventuals), and there was one at <a href="/wiki/Gallipoli" title="Gallipoli">Gallipoli</a> (the Assumptionists).</p>
<p>Around 1850, from the standpoint of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Catholic_Churches" title="Eastern Catholic Churches">Eastern Catholic Churches</a>, Adrianople was the residence of a Bulgarian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vicar-apostolic" title="Vicar-apostolic">vicar-apostolic</a> for the 4,600 Eastern Catholics of the Ottoman vilayet (province) of Thrace and after 1878 - of the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Bulgaria" title="Principality of Bulgaria">principality of Bulgaria</a>. They had 18 parishes or missions, 6 of which were in the principality, with 20 churches or chapels, 31 priests, of whom 6 were <a href="/wiki/Assumptionists" title="Assumptionists">Assumptionists</a> and 6 were Resurrectionists; 11 schools with 670 pupils. In Adrianople itself were only a very few United Bulgarians, with an Episcopal church of St. Elias, and the churches of St. Demetrius and Sts. Cyril and Methodius. The last is served by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resurrectionist_Order" title="Resurrectionist Order">Resurrectionists</a>, who have also a college of 90 pupils. In the suburb of Karaağaç, the <a href="/wiki/Assumptionists" title="Assumptionists">Assumptionists</a> have a parish and a seminary with 50 pupils. Besides the Eastern Catholic Bulgarians, the above statistics included the Greek Catholic missions of Malgara (now Malkara) and Daoudili (now Davuteli village in Malkara), with 4 priests and 200 faithful, because from the civil point of view belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Exarchate" title="Bulgarian Exarchate">Bulgarian Vicariate</a>.</p>
<p>Later however, the Roman Catholic diocese was discontinued, and exists only in name as a <a href="/wiki/Titular_see" title="Titular see">titular metropolitan archbishopric</a>, under the full name <i>Hadrianopolis in Haemimonto</i> to distinguish it from several other titular sees named Hadrianopolis.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edirne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Edirne has a <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">humid subtropical climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a>: Cfa) with long, hot summers and cold and occasionally snowy winters.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Points_of_interest">Points of interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edirne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Points of interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GrandSynagogueEdirne_(2).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2328" data-file-width="3618" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/GrandSynagogueEdirne_%282%29.JPG/220px-GrandSynagogueEdirne_%282%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/GrandSynagogueEdirne_%282%29.JPG/330px-GrandSynagogueEdirne_%282%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/GrandSynagogueEdirne_%282%29.JPG/440px-GrandSynagogueEdirne_%282%29.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Situated 7 km (4.3 mi) near to the Greek and 20 km (12 mi) to the Bulgarian border, Edirne is famed for its many mosques, domes and <a href="/wiki/Minaret" title="Minaret">minarets</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selimiye_Mosque_(Edirne)" title="Selimiye Mosque (Edirne)">Selimiye Mosque</a>, built in 1575 and designed by Turkey's greatest master architect, <a href="/wiki/Mimar_Sinan" title="Mimar Sinan">Mimar Sinan</a> (c. 1489/1490–1588), is one of the most important monuments in the city. It has the highest <a href="/wiki/Minaret" title="Minaret">minarets</a> in Turkey, at 70.90 m (232.6 ft) and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cupol" title="Cupol">cupola</a> 3 or 4 ft (0.91 or 1.22 m) higher than that of <a href="/wiki/Hagia_Sophia" title="Hagia Sophia">Hagia Sophia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> Orthodox Cathedral (now museum) in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>. Carrying the name of the then reigning Ottoman Sultan <a href="/wiki/Selim_II" title="Selim II">Selim II</a> (r. 1566–1574), this mosque futures Turkish marble handicrafts, and it is covered with valuable tiles and fine paintings. Another notable mosques are Eski Cami (<a href="/wiki/Old_Mosque,_Edirne" title="Old Mosque, Edirne">Old Mosque</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evdb1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-evdb1-9">[9]</a></sup> and Burmalı Cami (Serpent Mosque), aka <a href="/wiki/%C3%9C%C3%A7_%C5%9Eerefeli_Mosque" title="Üç Şerefeli Mosque">Üç Şerefeli Mosque</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evdb4_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-evdb4-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Edirne has three historic covered <a href="/wiki/Bazaar" title="Bazaar">bazaars</a>: Arasta, next to Selimiye Mosque, Bedesten next to Eski Cami and Ali Paşa Çarşısı (Ali Pasha Bazaar).</p>
<p>Besides the mosques, there are visitor attractions in Edirne, all reflecting its rich past. The most prominent place being the <a href="/wiki/Edirne_Palace" title="Edirne Palace">Edirne Palace</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language" title="Ottoman Turkish language">Ottoman Turkish</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ota-Arab" xml:lang="ota-Arab">Saray-ı Cedid-i Amire</span>‎ for "New Imperial Palace") in Sarayiçi quarter, built during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Murad_II" title="Murad II">Murad II</a> (r. 1421–1444). Although the buildings of the palace and its bath (Kum Kasrı Hamamı) are in ruined form, the palace gate and the palace kitchen facility are restored. The <i>Kasr-ı Adalet</i> ("Justice Castle"), built as part of the palace complex, stands intact next to the small <a href="/wiki/Fatih_Bridge" title="Fatih Bridge">Fatih Bridge</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Tundzha" title="Tundzha">Tunca</a> river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evdb3saraylar_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-evdb3saraylar-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another notable building in the area is the <a href="/wiki/Trakya_University" title="Trakya University">Trakya University</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayezid_II_K%C3%BClliye_Health_Museum" title="Bayezid II Külliye Health Museum">Bayezid II Külliye Health Museum</a>, an important monument with its complex construction comprising many facilities used in those times.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Balkan_Wars_Memorial_Cemetery_in_Edirne" title="Balkan Wars Memorial Cemetery in Edirne">Balkan Wars Memorial Cemetery</a> is located close to the Edirne Palace, with an unknown soldier monument futuring an Ottoman soldier in front of its entrance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evdb2_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-evdb2-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The historic <a href="/wiki/Grand_Synagogue_of_Edirne" title="Grand Synagogue of Edirne">Grand Synagogue of Edirne</a>, abandoned and ruined, was restored and re-opened in March 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-s1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-s1-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnnt1_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnnt1-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-h1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-h1-15">[15]</a></sup> A Roman Catholic and two Bulgarian Orthodox churches are found in the city.</p>
<p>Edirne has several <a href="/wiki/Bridges_of_Edirne" title="Bridges of Edirne">historic arch bridges</a> crossing over the rivers <a href="/wiki/Maritsa" title="Maritsa">Meriç</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tundzha" title="Tundzha">Tundzha</a>, which flow around west and south of the city.</p>
<p>There are <a href="/wiki/Caravanserai" title="Caravanserai">caravansaries</a>, like the Rustem Pasha and Ekmekcioglu Ahmet Pasha caravansaries, which were designed to host travelers, in the 16th century.</p>
<p>The historic <a href="/wiki/Karaa%C4%9Fa%C3%A7_railway_station" title="Karaağaç railway station">Karaağaç railway station</a> hosts today, after redevelopment, the <a href="/wiki/Trakya_University" title="Trakya University">Trakya University</a>'s Faculty of Fine Arts in <a href="/wiki/Karaa%C4%9Fa%C3%A7,_Edirne" title="Karaağaç, Edirne">Karaağaç</a> suburb of Edirne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evdb2_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-evdb2-12">[12]</a></sup> Next to it, the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lausanne_Monument_and_Museum" title="Treaty of Lausanne Monument and Museum">Treaty of Lausanne Monument and Museum</a> are situated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evdb3_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-evdb3-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edirne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yagli_gures1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Yagli_gures1.jpg/220px-Yagli_gures1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Yagli_gures1.jpg/330px-Yagli_gures1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Yagli_gures1.jpg/440px-Yagli_gures1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yagli_gures1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Oil-wrestling at Kırkpınar</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Edirne is home to the traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil-wrestling" title="Oil-wrestling">oil-wrestling</a> tournament called <a href="/wiki/K%C4%B1rkp%C4%B1nar" title="Kırkpınar">Kırkpınar</a>. Held every year in June, it is considered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2012)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to be the oldest active sport organization after the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a> that were refounded only after centuries of inactivity.</p>
<p>Another international festival in Edirne is <a href="/wiki/Kakava#Kakava_in_Turkey" title="Kakava">Kakava</a>, a celebration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roma_people" title="Roma people">Roma people</a> held on May 5 each year.</p>
<p>A cultural partnership with <a href="/wiki/L%C3%B6rrach" title="Lörrach">Lörrach</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> has been started in 2006. The goal is to exchange pupils and students to improve their cultural skills and understanding.</p>
<p>Edirne is well known for the local dish "ciğer tava" (<a href="/wiki/Bread_crumbs#Breading" title="Bread crumbs">breaded</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deep_frying" title="Deep frying">deep-fried</a> <a href="/wiki/Liver_(food)" title="Liver (food)">liver</a>) served often with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cac%C4%B1k" title="Cacık">cacık</a>, diluted yogurt with chopped cucumber.</p>
<p>Handmade broom with mirror is one of the cultural images of the city. In old times, every bride brought a broom with mirror to her new home so it was an important part of the weddings. It is possible to see many small handmade brooms with mirror in souvenir shops.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edirne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AliPa%C5%9Fa%C3%87ar%C5%9F%C4%B1s%C4%B1Edirne_(2).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3696" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/AliPa%C5%9Fa%C3%87ar%C5%9F%C4%B1s%C4%B1Edirne_%282%29.JPG/220px-AliPa%C5%9Fa%C3%87ar%C5%9F%C4%B1s%C4%B1Edirne_%282%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/AliPa%C5%9Fa%C3%87ar%C5%9F%C4%B1s%C4%B1Edirne_%282%29.JPG/330px-AliPa%C5%9Fa%C3%87ar%C5%9F%C4%B1s%C4%B1Edirne_%282%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/AliPa%C5%9Fa%C3%87ar%C5%9F%C4%B1s%C4%B1Edirne_%282%29.JPG/440px-AliPa%C5%9Fa%C3%87ar%C5%9F%C4%B1s%C4%B1Edirne_%282%29.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AliPa%C5%9Fa%C3%87ar%C5%9F%C4%B1s%C4%B1Edirne_(2).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Ali Paşa Çarşısı (Ali Pasha Bazaar).</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Edirne's economy largely depends on agriculture. 73% of the working population work in agriculture, fishing, forests, hunting. There are lots of things that are cultivated here. Its lowlands are productive. The field crop cultivation has developed so much here. Corn, sugarbeet and sunflower are the first. Melon, watermelon and viniculture are advanced. For the last decade, the agricultural products have doubled. Corn, rice, sunflower, sugarbeet, tomato, eggplant, melon, watermelon, grape are cultivated so much.</p>
<p>The through highway with an important role in global transport that connects Europe to Middle East and <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolian</a> to <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> passes through Edirne.</p>
<p>Also, the existence of many historical and natural touristic places and events lead the fact that tourism has become a leading component of the economic growth of the city in recent years.</p>
<p>Industry has also been developing. Agriculture-based industries (agro-industries) are especially important for the city's economy.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edirne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: lower-alpha;">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-status-17"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-status_17-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Kosovo</a> is the subject of a territorial dispute between the <a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Republic of Kosovo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Republic of Serbia</a>. The Republic of Kosovo <a href="/wiki/2008_Kosovo_declaration_of_independence" title="2008 Kosovo declaration of independence">unilaterally declared independence</a> on 17 February 2008, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbia%27s_reaction_to_the_2008_Kosovo_declaration_of_independence" title="Serbia's reaction to the 2008 Kosovo declaration of independence">Serbia continues to claim</a> it as part of its <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_Province_of_Kosovo_and_Metohija" title="Autonomous Province of Kosovo and Metohija">own sovereign territory</a>. The two governments <a href="/wiki/Belgrade%E2%80%93Pristina_negotiations" title="Belgrade–Pristina negotiations">began to normalise relations</a> in 2013, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Brussels_Agreement_(2013)" title="Brussels Agreement (2013)">Brussels Agreement</a>. Kosovo has received recognition as an independent state from <a href="/wiki/International_recognition_of_Kosovo" title="International recognition of Kosovo">110</a> out of 193 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_states_of_the_United_Nations" title="Member states of the United Nations">member states</a>.</span></li>
</ol>
