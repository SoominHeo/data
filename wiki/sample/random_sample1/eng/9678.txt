<p><b>Dame Edith Louisa Sitwell</b> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">DBE</a> (7 September 1887 – 9 December 1964) was a British poet and critic and the eldest of <a href="/wiki/The_Sitwells" title="The Sitwells">the three literary Sitwells</a>.</p>
<p>Like her brothers <a href="/wiki/Osbert_Sitwell" title="Osbert Sitwell">Osbert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sacheverell_Sitwell" title="Sacheverell Sitwell">Sacheverell</a>, Edith reacted badly to her eccentric, unloving parents, and lived for much of her life with her governess. She never married, but became passionately attached to the gay Russian painter <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Tchelitchew" title="Pavel Tchelitchew">Pavel Tchelitchew</a>, and her home was always open to London's poetic circle, to whom she was unfailingly generous and helpful.</p>
<p>Sitwell published poetry continuously from 1913, some of it abstract and set to music. With her dramatic style and exotic costumes, she was sometimes labelled a poseur, but her work was praised for its solid technique and painstaking craftsmanship.</p>

<p>Edith Louisa Sitwell was born in <a href="/wiki/Scarborough,_North_Yorkshire" title="Scarborough, North Yorkshire">Scarborough</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="North Riding of Yorkshire">North Riding of Yorkshire</a>, the oldest child and only daughter of Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Sitwell" title="George Sitwell">George Sitwell, 4th Baronet</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Renishaw_Hall" title="Renishaw Hall">Renishaw Hall</a>; he was an expert on genealogy and landscaping.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-age_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-age-1">[1]</a></sup> Her mother was Lady Ida Emily Augusta (née Denison), a daughter of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Henry_Forester_Denison,_1st_Earl_of_Londesborough" title="William Henry Forester Denison, 1st Earl of Londesborough">Earl of Londesborough</a> and a granddaughter of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Somerset,_7th_Duke_of_Beaufort" title="Henry Somerset, 7th Duke of Beaufort">Henry Somerset, 7th Duke of Beaufort</a>. She claimed a descent through female lines from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plantagenets" title="Plantagenets">Plantagenets</a>.</p>
<p>Sitwell had two younger brothers, <a href="/wiki/Osbert_Sitwell" title="Osbert Sitwell">Osbert</a> (1892–1969) and <a href="/wiki/Sacheverell_Sitwell" title="Sacheverell Sitwell">Sacheverell Sitwell</a> (1897–1988) both distinguished authors, well-known literary figures in their own right, and long-term collaborators. Her relationship with her parents was stormy at best, not least because her father made her undertake a "cure" for her supposed spinal deformation, involving locking her into an iron frame. She wrote in her autobiography that her parents had always been strangers to her.</p>
<p>In 1914, 26-year-old Sitwell moved to a small, shabby flat in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pembridge_Mansions&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pembridge Mansions (page does not exist)">Pembridge Mansions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bayswater" title="Bayswater">Bayswater</a>, which she shared with Helen Rootham (1875–1938), her <a href="/wiki/Governess" title="Governess">governess</a> since 1903.</p>

<p>Sitwell never married, but in 1927 she allegedly fell in love with the homosexual Russian painter <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Tchelitchew" title="Pavel Tchelitchew">Pavel Tchelitchew</a>. The relationship lasted until 1928, the same year that Rootham underwent operations for cancer (eventually becoming an invalid). In 1932, Helen Rootham and Sitwell moved to Paris, where they lived with Rootham's younger sister, Evelyn Wiel.</p>
<p>Sitwell's mother died in 1937. Sitwell did not attend the funeral because of her displeasure with her parents during her childhood. Helen Rootham died of spinal cancer in 1938. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> Sitwell returned from France and retired to Renishaw with her brother Osbert and his lover, David Horner. She wrote under the light of oil lamps as the house had no electricity. She knitted clothes for their friends who served in the army. One of the beneficiaries was <a href="/wiki/Alec_Guinness" title="Alec Guinness">Alec Guinness</a>, who received a pair of <a href="/wiki/Seaboot" title="Seaboot">seaboot</a> stockings.</p>
<p>The poems she wrote during the war brought her back before the public. They include <i>Street Songs</i> (1942), <i>The Song of the Cold</i> (1945), and <i>The Shadow of Cain</i> (1947), all of which were much praised. <a class="external text" href="http://www.poetryarchive.org/poet/edith-sitwell" rel="nofollow">"Still Falls the Rain"</a> about the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Blitz" title="London Blitz">London Blitz</a>, remains perhaps her best-known poem; it was set to music by <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Britten" title="Benjamin Britten">Benjamin Britten</a> as <a href="/wiki/Canticle_III:_Still_falls_the_rain" title="Canticle III: Still falls the rain">Canticle III: Still Falls the Rain</a>. Her poem <i>The Bee-Keeper</i> was set to music by <a href="/wiki/Priaulx_Rainier" title="Priaulx Rainier">Priaulx Rainier</a>, as <i>The Bee Oracles</i> (1970), a setting for tenor, flute, oboe, violin, cello, and harpsichord. It was premiered by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Pears" title="Peter Pears">Peter Pears</a> in 1970. Poems from <i>The Canticle of the Rose</i> were set by composer <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Phibbs" title="Joseph Phibbs">Joseph Phibbs</a> in a song-cycle for high soprano with string quartet premiered in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1943, her father died in Switzerland, his wealth depleted. In 1948, a reunion with Tchelitchew, whom she had not seen since before the war, went badly. In 1948 Sitwell toured the United States with her brothers, reciting her poetry and, notoriously, giving a reading of <a href="/wiki/Lady_Macbeth" title="Lady Macbeth">Lady Macbeth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sleepwalking_scene" title="Sleepwalking scene">sleepwalking scene</a>. Her poetry recitals always were occasions; she made recordings of her poems, including two recordings of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fa%C3%A7ade_(poem)" title="Façade (poem)">Façade</a></i>, the first with <a href="/wiki/Constant_Lambert" title="Constant Lambert">Constant Lambert</a> as co-narrator, and the second with Peter Pears.</p>
<p>Tchelitchew died in July 1957. Her brother Osbert died in 1969, of Parkinson's disease, diagnosed in 1950. Sitwell became a Dame Commander (<a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">DBE</a>) in 1954. In August 1955 she converted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a> and asked author <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Waugh" title="Evelyn Waugh">Evelyn Waugh</a> to serve as her godfather.</p>
<p>Sitwell wrote two books about Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I of England</a>: <i>Fanfare for Elizabeth</i> (1946) and <i>The Queens and the Hive</i> (1962). She always claimed that she wrote prose simply for money and both these books were extremely successful, as were her <i>English Eccentrics</i> (1933) and <i>Victoria of England</i> (1936).</p>
<p>Sitwell was the subject of <i><a href="/wiki/This_Is_Your_Life_(UK_TV_series)" title="This Is Your Life (UK TV series)">This Is Your Life</a></i> in November 1962 when she was surprised by <a href="/wiki/Eamonn_Andrews" title="Eamonn Andrews">Eamonn Andrews</a> on the stage of the BBC Television Theatre in London.</p>
<p>Sitwell lived from 1961 until her death in a flat in <a href="/wiki/Hampstead" title="Hampstead">Hampstead</a> in London, which is now marked with an <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> <a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">blue plaque</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Last_years_and_death">Last years and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edith_Sitwell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Last years and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>About 1957 she began using a <a href="/wiki/Wheelchair" title="Wheelchair">wheelchair</a>, after battling with <a href="/wiki/Marfan_syndrome" title="Marfan syndrome">Marfan syndrome</a> throughout her life. Her last poetry reading was in 1962. She died of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cerebral_haemorrhage" title="Cerebral haemorrhage">cerebral haemorrhage</a> at <a href="/wiki/St_Thomas%27_Hospital" title="St Thomas' Hospital">St Thomas' Hospital</a> on 9 December 1964 at the age of 77. She is buried in the churchyard of <a href="/wiki/Weedon_Lois" title="Weedon Lois">Weedon Lois</a> in Northamptonshire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sitwell's papers are held at the <a href="/wiki/Harry_Ransom_Center" title="Harry Ransom Center">Harry Ransom Center</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="The University of Texas at Austin">The University of Texas at Austin</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poetry">Poetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edith_Sitwell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Poetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Sitwell published her first poem <i>The Drowned Suns</i> in the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mirror" title="Daily Mirror">Daily Mirror</a></i> in 1913 and between 1916 and 1921 she edited <i>Wheels</i>, an annual poetic anthology compiled with her brothers—a literary collaboration generally called "<a href="/wiki/The_Sitwells" title="The Sitwells">the Sitwells</a>".</p>
<p>In 1929 she published <i>Gold Coast Customs</i>, a poem about the artificiality of human behaviour and the barbarism that lies beneath the surface. The poem was written in the rhythms of the <a href="/wiki/Tom-tom_drum" title="Tom-tom drum">tom-tom</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, and shows considerable technical skill. Her early work reflects the strong influence of the French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbolists" title="Symbolists">symbolists</a>.</p>
<p>She became a proponent and supporter of innovative trends in English poetry and opposed what she considered the conventionality of many contemporary backward-looking poets. Her flat became a meeting place for young writers whom she wished to befriend and help: these later included <a href="/wiki/Dylan_Thomas" title="Dylan Thomas">Dylan Thomas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Denton_Welch" title="Denton Welch">Denton Welch</a>. She also helped to publish the poetry of <a href="/wiki/Wilfred_Owen" title="Wilfred Owen">Wilfred Owen</a> after his death. Her only novel, <i><a href="/wiki/I_Live_Under_a_Black_Sun" title="I Live Under a Black Sun">I Live Under a Black Sun</a></i>, based on the life of <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Swift" title="Jonathan Swift">Jonathan Swift</a>, was published in 1937.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publicity_and_controversy">Publicity and controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edith_Sitwell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Publicity and controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Sitwell had angular features resembling Queen Elizabeth I (they also had the same birthday) and stood six feet (183 cm) tall, but often dressed in an unusual manner with gowns of <a href="/wiki/Brocade" title="Brocade">brocade</a> or <a href="/wiki/Velvet" title="Velvet">velvet</a>, with gold <a href="/wiki/Turban" title="Turban">turbans</a>, and a plethora of rings – her jewellery may be seen in the jewellery galleries of the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a> in London. Her unusual appearance provoked critics almost as much as her verse, and throughout her life she was the subject of more or less virulent personal attacks from <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Grigson" title="Geoffrey Grigson">Geoffrey Grigson</a>, <a href="/wiki/F._R._Leavis" title="F. R. Leavis">F. R. Leavis</a>, and others, which she returned with vigour.</p>
<p>Her 'enemies' were treated with scorn; after <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Coward" title="Noël Coward">Noël Coward</a> wrote a skit on Sitwell and her two brothers as "The Swiss Family Whittlebot" for his 1923 revue <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Calling!" title="London Calling!">London Calling!</a></i>, she refused to speak to him until they were reconciled after her triumphant 70th birthday party at London's <a href="/wiki/Royal_Festival_Hall" title="Royal Festival Hall">Royal Festival Hall</a>. To her friends she showed great sweetness and invariable kindness.</p>
<p>Sitwell participated in the ongoing <i>UGH....</i> correspondence featured in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Times_Literary_Supplement" title="Times Literary Supplement">Times Literary Supplement</a> in 1963, an ongoing debate on the value of the work of <a href="/wiki/William_S._Burroughs" title="William S. Burroughs">William S. Burroughs</a> and the nature of literary criticism, initiated by critic John Willard. Sitwell stated she was delighted by Willard's wholly negative review of Burroughs' work, despite claiming to not know who Burroughs was. In the same letter she described <i><a href="/wiki/Lady_Chatterley%27s_Lover" title="Lady Chatterley's Lover">Lady Chatterley's Lover</a></i> as an "insignificant, dirty little book", and rounded out her letter with the statement that she preferred <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chanel_Number_5" title="Chanel Number 5">Chanel Number 5</a> to having her nose "nailed to other people's lavatories".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sitwell was most interested by the distinction between poetry and music, a matter explored in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fa%C3%A7ade_(poem)" title="Façade (poem)">Façade</a></i> (1922), a series of abstract poems the rhythms of which counterparted those of music, and which was set to music by <a href="/wiki/William_Walton" title="William Walton">William Walton</a>. <i>Façade</i> was performed behind a curtain with a hole in the mouth of a painted face (the painting was by <a href="/wiki/John_Piper_(artist)" title="John Piper (artist)">John Piper</a>) and the words were recited through the hole with the aid of a <a href="/wiki/Megaphone" title="Megaphone">Sengerphone</a>. The public received the first performance with bemusement, but there were many positive reactions.</p>
<p>As she lay dying, the critic <a href="/wiki/Julian_Symons" title="Julian Symons">Julian Symons</a> published the last of these attacks in <i><a href="/wiki/The_London_Magazine" title="The London Magazine">The London Magazine</a></i> of November 1964, accusing her of <i>"wearing other people's bleeding hearts on her own safe sleeve."</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
