<p><b>Mount Erebus</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small>pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɛr/ 'err' in 'merry'">ɛr</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is the second-highest <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a> in <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> (after <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sidley" title="Mount Sidley">Mount Sidley</a>) and the southernmost active volcano on Earth. It is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_islands_by_highest_point" title="List of islands by highest point">sixth highest</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultra_mountain" title="Ultra mountain">ultra mountain</a> on an island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gvp_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-gvp-1">[1]</a></sup> With a summit elevation of 3,794 metres (12,448 ft), it is located on <a href="/wiki/Ross_Island" title="Ross Island">Ross Island</a>, which is also home to three inactive volcanoes, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Terror_(Antarctica)" title="Mount Terror (Antarctica)">Mount Terror</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Bird" title="Mount Bird">Mount Bird</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Terra_Nova" title="Mount Terra Nova">Mount Terra Nova</a>.</p>
<p>The volcano has been active since c. 1.3 million years ago<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and is the site of the Mount Erebus Volcano Observatory run by the <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico_Institute_of_Mining_and_Technology" title="New Mexico Institute of Mining and Technology">New Mexico Institute of Mining and Technology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology_and_volcanology">Geology and volcanology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Erebus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology and volcanology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anorthoclase-219058.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="575" data-file-width="340" height="288" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Anorthoclase-219058.jpg/170px-Anorthoclase-219058.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Anorthoclase-219058.jpg/255px-Anorthoclase-219058.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/de/Anorthoclase-219058.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Anorthoclase-219058.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Anorthoclase" title="Anorthoclase">Anorthoclase</a> crystal from Mt. Erebus</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Mount Erebus is currently the most active <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a> in Antarctica and is the current eruptive zone of the <a href="/wiki/Erebus_hotspot" title="Erebus hotspot">Erebus hotspot</a>. The summit contains a persistent convecting <a href="/wiki/Phonolite" title="Phonolite">phonolitic</a> lava lake, one of five long-lasting <a href="/wiki/Lava_lake" title="Lava lake">lava lakes</a> on Earth. Characteristic eruptive activity consists of <a href="/wiki/Strombolian_eruption" title="Strombolian eruption">Strombolian eruptions</a> from the lava lake or from one of several subsidiary vents, all within the volcano's inner crater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The volcano is scientifically remarkable in that its relatively low-level and unusually persistent eruptive activity enables long-term volcanological study of a Strombolian eruptive system very close (hundreds of metres) to the active vents, a characteristic shared with only a few volcanoes on Earth, such as <a href="/wiki/Stromboli" title="Stromboli">Stromboli</a> in Italy. Scientific study of the volcano is also facilitated by their proximity to <a href="/wiki/McMurdo_Station" title="McMurdo Station">McMurdo Station</a> (U.S.) and <a href="/wiki/Scott_Base" title="Scott Base">Scott Base</a> (New Zealand), both sited on <a href="/wiki/Ross_Island" title="Ross Island">Ross Island</a> approximately thirty-five kilometres away.</p>
<p>Mount Erebus is classified as a <a href="/wiki/Polygenetic_volcanic_field" title="Polygenetic volcanic field">polygenetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcano</a>. The bottom half of the volcano is a shield and the top half is a stratocone. The composition of the current eruptive products of Erebus is <a href="/wiki/Anorthoclase" title="Anorthoclase">anorthoclase</a>-<a href="/wiki/Porphyritic" title="Porphyritic">porphyritic</a> <a href="/wiki/Tephrite" title="Tephrite">tephritic</a> phonolite and <a href="/wiki/Phonolite" title="Phonolite">phonolite</a>, which are the bulk of exposed lava flow on the volcano. The oldest eruptive products consist of relatively <a href="/wiki/Igneous_differentiation" title="Igneous differentiation">undifferentiated</a> and <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">non-viscous</a> <a href="/wiki/Basanite" title="Basanite">basanite</a> lavas that form the low broad platform shield of Erebus. Slightly younger basanite and <a href="/wiki/Phonotephrite" title="Phonotephrite">phonotephrite</a> lavas crop out on <a href="/wiki/Fang_Ridge" title="Fang Ridge">Fang Ridge</a>—an eroded remnant of an early Erebus volcano—and at other isolated locations on the flanks of Erebus. Erebus is the world's only presently erupting phonolite volcano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burg2012_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burg2012-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Lava flows of more viscous phonotephrite and <a href="/wiki/Trachyte" title="Trachyte">trachyte</a> erupted after the basanite. The upper slopes of Mount Erebus are dominated by steeply <a href="/wiki/Strike_and_dip" title="Strike and dip">dipping</a> (~30°) tephritic phonolite lava flows with large scale flow levees. A conspicuous break in slope at approximately 3,200 metres calls attention to a summit plateau representing a <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">caldera</a>. The summit caldera was created by an explosive <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_Explosivity_Index" title="Volcanic Explosivity Index">VEI</a>-6 eruption that occurred 18,000 ± 7,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It is filled with small volume tephritic phonolite and phonolite lava flows. In the center of the summit caldera is a small steep-sided cone composed primarily of decomposed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lava_bomb" title="Lava bomb">lava bombs</a> and a large deposit of <a href="/wiki/Anorthoclase" title="Anorthoclase">anorthoclase</a> crystals known as <a href="/wiki/Erebus_crystal" title="Erebus crystal">Erebus crystals</a>. The active lava lake in this summit cone undergoes continuous degassing.</p>
<p>Researchers spent more than three months during the 2007–08 field season installing an unusually dense array of <a href="/wiki/Seismometer" title="Seismometer">seismometers</a> around Mount Erebus to listen to waves of energy generated by small, controlled blasts from explosives they buried along its flanks and perimeter and to record scattered seismic signals generated by lava lake eruptions and local ice quakes. By studying the refracted and scattered seismic waves, the scientists produced an image of the uppermost (top few km) of the volcano to understand the geometry of its "plumbing" and how the <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> rises to the lava lake. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These results demonstrated a complex upper-volcano conduit system with appreciable upper-volcano magma storage to the northwest of the lava lake at depths hundreds of meters below the surface.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice_fumaroles">Ice fumaroles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Erebus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ice fumaroles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mt. Erebus is notable for its numerous ice <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fumaroles" title="Fumaroles">fumaroles</a> – ice towers that form around gases that escape from vents in the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The ice caves associated with the fumaroles are dark, in polar alpine environments starved in organics and with oxygenated hydrothermal circulation in highly reducing host rock. The life is sparse, mainly bacteria and fungi. This makes it of special interest for studying <a href="/wiki/Oligotroph" title="Oligotroph">oligotrophs</a> – organisms that can survive on minimal amounts of resources.</p>
<p>It is thought that ice caves may exist on Mars – ice preserved under the surface in cave systems protected from the surface conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WilliamsMcKay2010_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-WilliamsMcKay2010-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>The caves on Erebus are of especial interest for astrobiology as most surface caves are influenced by human activities, or by organics from the surface brought in by animals (e.g. bats) or ground water. The caves at Erebus are high altitude, yet accessible for study. There is almost no chance of photosynthetic based organics, or of animals in a food chain based on photosynthetic life, and no overlying soil to wash down into them.</p>
<p>They are dynamical systems that collapse and rebuild, but persist over decades. The air inside the caves has 80% to 100% humidity, and up to 3% CO<sub>2</sub>, and some CO and H<sub>2</sub>, but almost no CH<sub>4</sub> or H<sub>2</sub>S. Many of them are completely dark, so can't support photosynthesis. Organics can only come from the atmosphere, or from ice algae that grow on the surface in summer, which may eventually find their way into the caves through burial and melting. As a result, most micro-organisms there are chemolithoautotrophic i.e. microbes that get all of their energy from chemical reactions with the rocks, and that don't depend on any other lifeforms to survive. The organisms survive using CO<sub>2</sub> fixation and it's hypothesized that some use CO oxidization for the metabolism. The main types of microbe found there are <a href="/wiki/Chloroflexi_(phylum)" title="Chloroflexi (phylum)">Chloroflexi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acidobacteria" title="Acidobacteria">Acidobacteria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TeboDavis2015_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-TeboDavis2015-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Erebus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_naming">Discovery and naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Erebus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mount Erebus was discovered on January 27, 1841 (and observed to be in eruption)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> by polar explorer Sir <a href="/wiki/James_Clark_Ross" title="James Clark Ross">James Clark Ross</a> who named it and its companion, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Terror_(Antarctica)" title="Mount Terror (Antarctica)">Mount Terror</a>, after his ships, <i><a href="/wiki/HMS_Erebus_(1826)" title="HMS Erebus (1826)">Erebus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/HMS_Terror_(1813)" title="HMS Terror (1813)">Terror</a></i> (which were later used by Sir <a href="/wiki/John_Franklin" title="John Franklin">John Franklin</a> on his disastrous <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Franklin%27s_lost_expedition" title="Franklin's lost expedition">expedition</a>). Present with Ross on the Erebus was the young <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Dalton_Hooker" title="Joseph Dalton Hooker">Joseph Hooker</a>, future president of the Royal Society and close friend of Charles Darwin. <a href="/wiki/Erebus" title="Erebus">Erebus</a> was an <a href="/wiki/Greek_primordial_deities" title="Greek primordial deities">Ancient Greek primordial deity</a> of darkness, the son of <a href="/wiki/Chaos_(mythology)" title="Chaos (mythology)">Chaos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_sites">Historic sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Erebus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Historic sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The mountain was surveyed in December 1912 by a science party from <a href="/wiki/Robert_Falcon_Scott" title="Robert Falcon Scott">Scott’s</a> <a href="/wiki/Terra_Nova_Expedition" title="Terra Nova Expedition">Terra Nova Expedition</a> who also collected geological samples. Two of the camp sites they used have been recognised for their historic significance:</p>
<ul>
<li>Upper “Summit Camp” site (HSM 89) consists of part of a circle of rocks, which were probably used to weight the tent valances.</li>
<li>Lower “Camp E” site (HSM 90) consists of a slightly elevated area of gravel as well as some aligned rocks, which may have been used to weight the tent valances.</li>
</ul>
<p>They have been designated <a href="/wiki/Historic_Sites_and_Monuments_in_Antarctica" title="Historic Sites and Monuments in Antarctica">Historic Sites or Monuments</a> following a proposal by the United Kingdom, New Zealand and the United States to the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Treaty_System" title="Antarctic Treaty System">Antarctic Treaty Consultative Meeting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atcm_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-atcm-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climbing">Climbing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Erebus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climbing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mount Erebus' summit crater rim was first achieved by members of Sir <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Shackleton" title="Ernest Shackleton">Ernest Shackleton</a>'s party; <a href="/wiki/Edgeworth_David" title="Edgeworth David">Professor Edgeworth David</a>, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Mawson" title="Douglas Mawson">Sir Douglas Mawson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alistair_Mackay" title="Alistair Mackay">Dr Alister Mackay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jameson_Adams" title="Jameson Adams">Jameson Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Marshall" title="Eric Marshall">Dr Eric Marshall</a> and Phillip Brocklehurst (who did not reach the summit), in 1908. Its first known solo ascent and the first winter ascent was accomplished by British mountaineer Roger Mear on 7 June 1985, a member of the "In the Footsteps of Scott" expedition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> On January 19–20, 1991, Charles J. Blackmer, an iron-worker for many years at <a href="/wiki/McMurdo_Station" title="McMurdo Station">McMurdo Station</a> and the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a>, accomplished a solo ascent in approximately seventeen hours completely unassisted via snow mobile and on foot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheeler_Terra_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wheeler_Terra-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_Place_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson_Place-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Robotic_exploration">Robotic exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Erebus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Robotic exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1992 the inside of the volcano was explored by <i>Dante I</i>, an eight legged tethered robotic explorer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> <i>Dante</i> was designed to acquire gas samples from the magma lake inside the inner crater of Mount Erebus in order to understand the chemistry better through the use of the on-board gas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromatograph" title="Chromatograph">chromatograph</a> as well as measuring the temperature inside the volcano and the radioactivity of the materials present in such volcanoes. <i>Dante</i> successfully scaled a significant portion of the crater before technical difficulties emerged with the fibre-optic cable used for communications between the walker and base station. Unfortunately, <i>Dante I</i> had not yet reached the bottom of the crater and so no data of volcanic significance was recorded. However, the expedition proved to be highly successful in terms of robotic and computer science, and was possibly the first ever expedition by a robotic platform to Antarctica.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_New_Zealand_Flight_901">Air New Zealand Flight 901</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Erebus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Air New Zealand Flight 901">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Air_New_Zealand_Flight_901" title="Air New Zealand Flight 901">Air New Zealand Flight 901</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Air_New_Zealand_Flight_901" title="Air New Zealand Flight 901">Air New Zealand Flight 901</a> was a scheduled sightseeing service from <a href="/wiki/Auckland_Airport" title="Auckland Airport">Auckland Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> to <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and return with a scheduled stop at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christchurch_Airport" title="Christchurch Airport">Christchurch Airport</a> to refuel before returning to Auckland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holmes_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holmes-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Air_New_Zealand" title="Air New Zealand">Air New Zealand</a> flyover service, for the purposes of Antarctic sightseeing, was operated with <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_DC-10" title="McDonnell Douglas DC-10">McDonnell Douglas DC-10-30</a> aircraft and began in February 1977. The flight crashed into Mount Erebus in <a href="/wiki/Whiteout_(weather)" title="Whiteout (weather)">whiteout</a> conditions on November 28, 1979, killing all 257 people aboard. Further investigation of the crash shows an Air New Zealand navigational error and a cover-up that resulted in about $100 million in lawsuits. Air New Zealand discontinued its flyovers of Antarctica. Its final flight was in February 17, 1980. During the Antarctic summer, snow melt on the flanks of Mount Erebus continually reveals debris from the crash; it is visible from the air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holmes_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holmes-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
