<p><b>Baron Ernst Gideon von Laudon</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i>Ernst Gideon Freiherr von Laudon</i> (originally <i>Laudohn</i> or <i>Loudon</i>) (2 February 1717 – 14 July 1790) was an <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Monarchy" title="Habsburg Monarchy">Austrian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generalisimo" title="Generalisimo">generalisimo</a>, one of the most successful opponents of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Frederick_the_Great" title="Frederick the Great">Frederick the Great</a>, allegedly lauded by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Suvorov" title="Alexander Suvorov">Alexander Suvorov</a> as his teacher. He served the position of military governorship of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg-occupied_Serbia_(1788-92)" title="Habsburg-occupied Serbia (1788-92)">Habsburg Serbia</a> from his capture of Belgrade in 1789 until his death, cooperating with the resistance fighters of <a href="/wiki/Ko%C4%8Da_An%C4%91elkovi%C4%87" title="Koča Anđelković">Koča Anđelković</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_early_career">Background and early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_Gideon_von_Laudon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Laudohn family, of mixed <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latgalians" title="Latgalians">Latgalian</a> origin, had been settled in the estate of Tootzen, near <a href="/wiki/%C4%BBaudona" title="Ļaudona">Ļaudona</a> in Eastern <a href="/wiki/Livonia" title="Livonia">Livonia</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>) before 1432. Laudon himself claimed a kinship with the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scottish</a> Earls of <a href="/wiki/Loudoun" title="Loudoun">Loudoun</a>, which could not be established. His father Otto Gerhard von Laudohn was a lieutenant-colonel, retired on a meagre pension from the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Empire" title="Swedish Empire">Swedish</a> service. As upon the <a href="/wiki/Great_Northern_War" title="Great Northern War">Great Northern War</a> Livonia had been ceded to <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russia</a> according to the 1721 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Nystad" title="Treaty of Nystad">Treaty of Nystad</a>, the boy was sent to the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russian_Army" title="Imperial Russian Army">Imperial Russian Army</a> as a cadet in 1732. During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Polish_Succession" title="War of the Polish Succession">War of the Polish Succession</a> he took part in the 1734 <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Danzig_(1734)" title="Siege of Danzig (1734)">Siege of Danzig</a> led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feldmarschall" title="Feldmarschall">Feldmarschall</a> <a href="/wiki/Burkhard_Christoph_von_M%C3%BCnnich" title="Burkhard Christoph von Münnich">Burkhard Christoph von Münnich</a>, he marched against <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">French</a> troops up to the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> in 1735 and back to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dnieper_River" title="Dnieper River">Dnieper River</a> into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russo-Austrian-Turkish_War_(1735%E2%80%931739)" title="Russo-Austrian-Turkish War (1735–1739)">Turkish campaign</a>.</p>
<p>After the 1739 <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Belgrade" title="Treaty of Belgrade">Treaty of Belgrade</a> he returned to the Russian court at <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a>. Dissatisfied with his prospects and the conditions in the Russian Army, he finally resigned in 1741 and sought military employment elsewhere. He applied first to King Frederick the Great, who however declined his services. At <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> he had better fortune, being made a captain in the <i><a href="/wiki/Freikorps" title="Freikorps">Freikorps</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Baron_Franz_von_der_Trenck" title="Baron Franz von der Trenck">Franz von der Trenck</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Austrian_Succession" title="War of the Austrian Succession">War of the Austrian Succession</a>, he took part in its forays and marches, though not in its atrocities, until wounded and taken prisoner in <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a>. He was shortly released by the advance of the main Austrian army.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seven_Years.27_War">Seven Years' War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_Gideon_von_Laudon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Seven Years' War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His next active service, still under Trenck, was in the <a href="/wiki/Silesian_Wars" title="Silesian Wars">Silesian</a> mountains in 1745, in which campaign he greatly distinguished himself as a leader of light troops. He was present also at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Soor" title="Battle of Soor">Battle of Soor</a>. He retired shortly afterwards, owing to his distaste for the lawless habits of his comrades in the irregulars, and after long waiting in poverty for a regular commission he was at last made a captain in one of the frontier regiments, spending the next ten years in half-military, half-administrative work in the <a href="/wiki/Karlovac" title="Karlovac">Karlovac</a> district on the <a href="/wiki/Military_Frontier" title="Military Frontier">Military Frontier</a>. At <a href="/wiki/Buni%C4%87,_Croatia" title="Bunić, Croatia">Bunić</a>, where he was stationed, he built a church and planted an oak forest now called by his name. He had reached the rank of lieutenant-colonel when the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a> called him again into the field. From this point began his fame as a soldier. At first rejected by General <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Reinhard_von_Neipperg" title="Wilhelm Reinhard von Neipperg">Wilhelm Reinhard von Neipperg</a>, he soon was promoted colonel at the behest of Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Wenzel_Anton,_Prince_of_Kaunitz-Rietberg" title="Wenzel Anton, Prince of Kaunitz-Rietberg">Wenzel Anton Kaunitz</a> and distinguished himself repeatedly. In 1757 he fought in <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Saxony" title="Electorate of Saxony">Saxony</a> under Feldmarschall <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_Ulysses_Browne" title="Maximilian Ulysses Browne">Maximilian Ulysses Browne</a> and became a <i><a href="/wiki/Generalfeldwachtmeister" title="Generalfeldwachtmeister">Generalfeldwachtmeister</a></i> (equivalent to major-general) of cavalry as well as a knight of the newly founded <a href="/wiki/Military_Order_of_Maria_Theresa" title="Military Order of Maria Theresa">Maria Theresa Military Order</a>.</p>
<p>In the campaign of 1758 came his first opportunity for fighting an action as a commander-in-chief, and he used it so well that Frederick the Great was obliged to give up the siege of <a href="/wiki/Olomouc" title="Olomouc">Olomouc</a> and retire into Bohemia (<a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Domstadtl" title="Battle of Domstadtl">Battle of Domstadtl</a>, 30 June). He was rewarded with the grade of lieutenant-field-marshal and having again shown himself an active and daring commander in the campaign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hochkirk" title="Battle of Hochkirk">Hochkirch</a>, he was created a <a href="/wiki/Freiherr" title="Freiherr">Freiherr</a> in the Austrian nobility by Maria Theresa and in the peerage of the Holy Roman Empire by her husband the emperor Francis. Maria Theresa gave him, further, the grand cross of the order she had founded and an estate near <a href="/wiki/Kutn%C3%A1_Hora" title="Kutná Hora">Kutná Hora</a> in Bohemia.</p>

<p>He was placed in command of the Austrian contingent sent to join the Russians on the Oder, and participated in <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kunersdorf" title="Battle of Kunersdorf">Kunersdorf</a> alongside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyotr_Saltykov" title="Pyotr Saltykov">Pyotr Saltykov</a> where a joint Russo-Austrian contingent won a great victory. As a result, Laudon was promoted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feldzeugmeister" title="Feldzeugmeister">Feldzeugmeister</a> and made commander-in-chief in Bohemia, Moravia and Silesia. In 1760 he destroyed a whole corps of Frederick's army under <a href="/wiki/Fouqu%C3%A9" title="Fouqué">Fouqué</a> at <a href="/wiki/Landshut" title="Landshut">Landshut</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Glatz" title="Siege of Glatz">stormed the important fortress</a> of <a href="/wiki/K%C5%82odzko" title="Kłodzko">Glatz</a>. In 1760 he sustained a severe reverse at Frederick's hands in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Liegnitz_(1760)" title="Battle of Liegnitz (1760)">Battle of Liegnitz</a> (15 August 1760), which action led to bitter controversy with Daun and Lacy, the commanders of the main army, who, Laudon claimed, had left his corps unsupported. In 1761 he operated, as usual, in Silesia, but he found his Russian allies as timid as they had been after Kunersdorf, and all attempts against Frederick's entrenched camp of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bunzelwitz" title="Bunzelwitz">Bunzelwitz</a> failed. He brilliantly seized his one fleeting opportunity, however, and stormed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schweidnitz" title="Schweidnitz">Schweidnitz</a> on the night of September 30/October 1, 1761. His tireless activity continued to the end of the war, in conspicuous contrast with the temporizing strategy of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leopold_Josef_Graf_Daun" title="Leopold Josef Graf Daun">Daun</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franz_Moritz_Graf_von_Lacy" title="Franz Moritz Graf von Lacy">Lacy</a>. The student of the later campaigns of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a> will probably admit that there was need of more aggressiveness than Daun displayed, and of more caution than suited Laudon's genius. But neither recognized this, and the last three years of the war are marked by an ever-increasing friction between the "Fabius" and the "Marcellus," as they were called, of the Austrian army.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_career">Later career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_Gideon_von_Laudon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the peace, therefore, when Daun became the virtual commander-in-chief of the army, Laudon fell into the background. Offers were made, by Frederick the Great amongst others, to induce Laudon to transfer his services elsewhere. Laudon did not entertain these proposals, although negotiations went on for some years, and on Lacy succeeding Daun as president of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_War_(Austria)" title="Council of War (Austria)">Council of War</a>, Laudon was made inspector-general of infantry. Dissensions, however, continued between Laudon and Lacy, and on the accession of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph II, Holy Roman Emperor">Joseph II</a>, who was intimate with his rival, Laudon retired to his estate near <a href="/wiki/Kutn%C3%A1_Hora" title="Kutná Hora">Kutná Hora</a>.</p>
<p>Maria Theresa and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wenzel_Anton_Graf_Kaunitz" title="Wenzel Anton Graf Kaunitz">Kaunitz</a> caused him, however, to be made commander-in-chief in <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a> in 1769. This post he held for three years, and at the end of this time, contemplating retirement from the service, he settled again on his estate. Maria Theresa once more persuaded him to remain in the army, and, as his estate had diminished in value owing to agrarian troubles in Bohemia, she repurchased it from him, in 1776, on generous terms. Laudon then settled at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hadersdorf&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hadersdorf (page does not exist)">Hadersdorf</a> near Vienna, and shortly afterwards was made a field-marshal. Of this <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Carlyle" title="Thomas Carlyle">Carlyle</a> (<i>Frederick the Great</i>) records that when Frederick the Great met Laudon in 1776 he deliberately addressed him in the emperor's presence as "Herr Feldmarschall", but the hint was not taken until February 1778.</p>

</div>
<p>In 1778 came the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Bavarian_Succession" title="War of the Bavarian Succession">War of the Bavarian Succession</a>. Joseph and Lacy were now reconciled to Laudon and Laudon and Lacy commanded the two armies in the field. On this occasion, however, Laudon seems to have in a measure fallen below his reputation, while Lacy, who was opposed to Frederick's own army, earned new laurels.</p>
<p>For two years after this Laudon lived quietly at Hadersdorf. A new war, with Turkey, broke out in 1787 (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austro-Turkish_War_(1787-1791)" title="Austro-Turkish War (1787-1791)">Austro-Turkish War (1787-1791)</a> The generals charged with prosecuting this war did badly, and Laudon was thus called for the last time into the field. Though old and broken in health, he was commander-in-chief in fact as well as in name, and in 1789 he won a last brilliant success by capturing <a href="/wiki/Belgrade" title="Belgrade">Belgrade</a> in three weeks.</p>
<p>He died within the year, at Nový Jičín (Neu-Titschein) in Moravia, still on duty. His last appointment was that of commander-in-chief of the armed forces of Austria, which had been created for him by the <a href="/wiki/Leopold_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Leopold II, Holy Roman Emperor">new emperor Leopold</a>. Laudon was buried in the grounds of Hadersdorf. Eight years before his death the emperor Joseph had caused a marble bust of this great soldier to be placed in the chamber of the council of war.</p>
<p>His nephew <a href="/wiki/Johann_Ludwig_Alexius_von_Loudon" title="Johann Ludwig Alexius von Loudon">Johann Ludwig Alexius von Loudon</a> (1762–1822) fought in the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">Revolutionary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> with credit, and rose to the rank of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feldmarschall-Leutnant" title="Feldmarschall-Leutnant">Feldmarschall-Leutnant</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ernst_Gideon_von_Laudon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first battleship of the <i>Ersatz Monarch</i> class of the Austro-Hungarian Navy (officially known as <i>Schiff VIII</i>) was to be named <i>Laudon</i>. The ship was never completed due to the outbreak of World War I which interrupted all major warship construction in Austria-Hungary.</p>
<p>The phrase <i>fix Laudon</i> is a light curse sometimes used in Austria. It is said that it was first uttered by Maria Theresa upon her hearing of the loss of Silesia to Frederick the Great.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Regarding personal names: <i><a href="/wiki/Freiherr" title="Freiherr">Freiherr</a></i> is a former title (translated as <i><a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">Baron</a></i>), which is now legally a part of the last name. The feminine forms are <i><a href="/wiki/Freiherr" title="Freiherr">Freifrau</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Freiherr" title="Freiherr">Freiin</a></i>.</p>
