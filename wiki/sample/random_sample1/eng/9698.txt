<p><b>Eric James McCormack</b> (born April 18, 1963) is a Canadian-American actor known for his role as <a href="/wiki/Will_Truman" title="Will Truman">Will Truman</a> in the American sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Will_%26_Grace" title="Will &amp; Grace">Will &amp; Grace</a>.</i></p>
<p>Born in Toronto, McCormack began his acting career performing in high school plays. He left <a href="/wiki/Ryerson_University" title="Ryerson University">Ryerson University</a> in 1985 to accept a position with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratford_Shakespeare_Festival" title="Stratford Shakespeare Festival">Stratford Shakespeare Festival</a>, where he spent five years performing in numerous play productions.</p>
<p>For much of the late 1990s, he lived in Los Angeles and had minor roles. He made his feature film debut in the 1992 science-fiction adventure film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World_(1992_film)" title="The Lost World (1992 film)">The Lost World</a></i>. McCormack appeared in several television series, including <i><a href="/wiki/Top_Cops" title="Top Cops">Top Cops</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Street_Justice" title="Street Justice">Street Justice</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lonesome_Dove#Adaptations" title="Lonesome Dove">Lonesome Dove: The Series</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Townies" title="Townies">Townies</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Ally_McBeal" title="Ally McBeal">Ally McBeal</a></i>. McCormack later gained worldwide recognition for playing <a href="/wiki/Will_Truman" title="Will Truman">Will Truman</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Will_%26_Grace" title="Will &amp; Grace">Will &amp; Grace</a></i>, which premiered in September 1998. His performance earned him a <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Lead_Actor_in_a_Comedy_Series" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actor in a Comedy Series">Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actor in a Comedy Series</a> in 2001.</p>
<p>Aside from appearing in television, he made his <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> debut in the 2001 production of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Music_Man" title="The Music Man">The Music Man</a></i> and starred in the 2005 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sisters_(2005_film)" title="The Sisters (2005 film)">The Sisters</a></i>. Following the series conclusion of <i>Will &amp; Grace</i> in 2006, McCormack starred as the leading role in the New York production of <i><a href="/wiki/Some_Girl(s)" title="Some Girl(s)">Some Girl(s)</a></i>. He starred in the television miniseries <i><a href="/wiki/The_Andromeda_Strain_(miniseries)" title="The Andromeda Strain (miniseries)">The Andromeda Strain</a></i> (2008) and returned to television in 2009 in the <a href="/wiki/TNT_(TV_channel)" title="TNT (TV channel)">TNT</a> drama <i><a href="/wiki/Trust_Me_(TV_series)" title="Trust Me (TV series)">Trust Me</a></i>, which was cancelled after one season. Also in 2009, McCormack was cast in the science-fiction movie <i><a href="/wiki/Alien_Trespass" title="Alien Trespass">Alien Trespass</a></i>. In addition, he starred as Dr. Daniel Pierce for three seasons of the TNT crime drama <i><a href="/wiki/Perception_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Perception (U.S. TV series)">Perception</a></i>, and provided the voice of "Lucky" on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hub_Network" title="Hub Network">The Hub</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Pound_Puppies_(2010_TV_series)" title="Pound Puppies (2010 TV series)">Pound Puppies</a>.</i></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_McCormack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McCormack was born in Toronto, Ontario, Canada, the son of Doris (1932-2006), a homemaker, and Keith McCormack, an <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a> company financial analyst<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solidified_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-solidified-1">[1]</a></sup> who died from cancer in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He is the oldest of three siblings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-people_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-people-3">[3]</a></sup> McCormack has <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scottish</a> ancestry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hendry2006_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hendry2006-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> McCormack states while he was growing up, he was shy and did not play sports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-night_out_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-night_out-6">[6]</a></sup> McCormack attended Stephen Leacock Collegiate Institute High School in <a href="/wiki/Scarborough,_Toronto" title="Scarborough, Toronto">Scarborough</a>, Ontario. There, he enrolled in theatre and performed in high school productions of <i><a href="/wiki/Godspell" title="Godspell">Godspell</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pippin_(musical)" title="Pippin (musical)">Pippin</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcm_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcm-7">[7]</a></sup> and decided to pursue a career in acting. McCormack recalls after performing in <i>Godspell</i>, his feelings towards becoming an actor solidified. "...I remember after the first performance of that ... I knew where to fit in. That was the beginning of my life as an actor. It changed me in that the concept of any other options disappeared. From that moment there was no question. I knew exactly what I was going to do. I'm lucky that way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solidified_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-solidified-1">[1]</a></sup> He admits that he never felt cool growing up. "I was a bit of an outsider, but I discovered theatre very early on, which got me through."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hoggard_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-hoggard-8">[8]</a></sup> He then transferred to <a href="/wiki/Sir_John_A._Macdonald_Collegiate_Institute" title="Sir John A. Macdonald Collegiate Institute">Sir John A. Macdonald Collegiate Institute</a> in Scarborough, where he went to school with <a href="/wiki/David_Furnish" title="David Furnish">David Furnish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mclean_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-mclean-9">[9]</a></sup> He graduated in 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-people_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-people-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCormack enrolled at <a href="/wiki/Ryerson_University" title="Ryerson University">Ryerson University</a> School of Theatre in Toronto to further improve his acting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-actors_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-actors-10">[10]</a></sup> He left Ryerson in 1985, several months before graduating, to accept a position with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stratford_Shakespeare_Festival" title="Stratford Shakespeare Festival">Stratford Shakespeare Festival</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stratford,_Ontario" title="Stratford, Ontario">Stratford</a>, Ontario, where he spent five seasons performing. "It was all I wanted, to be a classical actor for the rest of my life, but during the last couple of years I was there, I started to realise that it wasn't for me. Perhaps I didn't have to give my Hamlet before I died, that the world might be an OK place without my Hamlet, in fact."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mclean_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-mclean-9">[9]</a></sup> McCormack appeared in the productions <i><a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream" title="A Midsummer Night's Dream">A Midsummer Night's Dream</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Henry_V_(play)" title="Henry V (play)">Henry V</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Murder_in_the_Cathedral" title="Murder in the Cathedral">Murder in the Cathedral</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Sisters_(play)" title="Three Sisters (play)">Three Sisters</a></i>. McCormack later performed with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manitoba_Theatre_Centre" title="Manitoba Theatre Centre">Manitoba Theatre Centre</a> in a production of <i><a href="/wiki/Burn_This" title="Burn This">Burn This</a></i>, as well as with Toronto's <a href="/wiki/Royal_Alexandra_Theatre" title="Royal Alexandra Theatre">Royal Alexandra Theatre</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Biloxi_Blues" title="Biloxi Blues">Biloxi Blues</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcm_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcm-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_McCormack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_work">Early work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_McCormack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McCormack made his Canadian television debut in the 1986 movie <i>The Boys from Syracuse</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcm_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-tcm-7">[7]</a></sup> McCormack moved to Los Angeles, California, and made his US television debut in a 1991 episode of the <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> crime series <i><a href="/wiki/Top_Cops" title="Top Cops">Top Cops</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcm_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-tcm-7">[7]</a></sup> He appeared in the 1992 theatrical film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World_(1992_film)" title="The Lost World (1992 film)">The Lost World</a></i>, based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conan_Doyle" title="Conan Doyle">Conan Doyle</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World_(Conan_Doyle_novel)" title="The Lost World (Conan Doyle novel)">novel of the same name</a> and starred in <i>The Lost World</i> sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Return_to_the_Lost_World" title="Return to the Lost World">Return to the Lost World</a></i>, also released in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcm_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-tcm-7">[7]</a></sup> By 1993, he landed a recurring role as a detective in the crime drama <i><a href="/wiki/Street_Justice" title="Street Justice">Street Justice</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-people_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-people-3">[3]</a></sup> Also in 1993, McCormack appeared in the television movie <i><a href="/wiki/Double,_Double,_Toil_and_Trouble" title="Double, Double, Toil and Trouble">Double, Double, Toil and Trouble</a></i>, playing <a href="/wiki/Mary-Kate_and_Ashley_Olsen" title="Mary-Kate and Ashley Olsen">Mary-Kate and Ashley Olsen</a>'s father.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renamed_from_1993_on_20101220232705_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-renamed_from_1993_on_20101220232705-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>He played the role of Colonel Francis Clay Mosby in 42 episodes of the <a href="/wiki/Western_(genre)" title="Western (genre)">Western</a> television series <i><a href="/wiki/Lonesome_Dove#Adaptations" title="Lonesome Dove">Lonesome Dove: The Series</a></i> (1994) and <i><a href="/wiki/Lonesome_Dove#Adaptations" title="Lonesome Dove">Lonesome Dove: The Outlaw Years</a></i> (1995).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-people_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-people-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-actors_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-actors-10">[10]</a></sup> McCormack commented that it was a "fantastic role".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mclean_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-mclean-9">[9]</a></sup> In an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> in 2003, McCormack admitted to auditioning "two or three times" for the part of <a href="/wiki/Ross_Geller" title="Ross Geller">Ross Geller</a>, which ultimately went to <a href="/wiki/David_Schwimmer" title="David Schwimmer">David Schwimmer</a>, for the situation comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Friends" title="Friends">Friends</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mclean_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-mclean-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1995, he appeared in the television film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Wouldn%27t_Die_(1995_film)" title="The Man Who Wouldn't Die (1995 film)">The Man Who Wouldn't Die</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He was cast in the 1997 made-for-television movie <i><a href="/wiki/Borrowed_Hearts" title="Borrowed Hearts">Borrowed Hearts</a></i>, where he portrayed a selfish businessman who learns to love, and in the HBO film <i>Exception to the Rule</i>, in which he played a cheating husband.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mclean_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-mclean-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in 1997, he had minor roles in the comedy shows <i><a href="/wiki/Townies" title="Townies">Townies</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Veronica%27s_Closet" title="Veronica's Closet">Veronica's Closet</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Ally_McBeal" title="Ally McBeal">Ally McBeal</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcm_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-tcm-7">[7]</a></sup> Originally, McCormack was scheduled to appear as a series regular in the <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Jenny_(TV_series)" title="Jenny (TV series)">Jenny</a></i>, but was fired after the pilot due to the network cutting his character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbs_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-pbs-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Will_.26_Grace"><i>Will &amp; Grace</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_McCormack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Will &amp; Grace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McCormack received his break-through role in 1998 when he was cast as <a href="/wiki/Gay" title="Gay">gay</a> lawyer <a href="/wiki/Will_Truman" title="Will Truman">Will Truman</a> on the <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Will_%26_Grace" title="Will &amp; Grace">Will &amp; Grace</a></i>. McCormack said that when the part came along, he was convinced he was right for the role. "At the end of the audition, <a href="/wiki/Max_Mutchnick" title="Max Mutchnick">Max Mutchnick</a>, co-creator and executive producer of the show said 'That was perfect. Just to let you know, you never have to be more gay than that.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mclean_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-mclean-9">[9]</a></sup> He explained that when he first read the script, "what hit me immediately was that this was me. I mean, <a href="/wiki/Sexual_orientation" title="Sexual orientation">sexual orientation</a> aside, Will was so much like me. He's a great host, he's relatively funny and he has great friends and he's a good friend to them ... the gay issue just wasn't really a big thing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson1998_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson1998-14">[14]</a></sup> The show debuted on September 21, 1998 and was watched by almost 8.6 million American viewers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renamed_from_1998_on_20101220232705_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-renamed_from_1998_on_20101220232705-15">[15]</a></sup> <i>Will &amp; Grace</i> quickly developed a loyal audience, with the show and McCormack receiving strong reviews. John Carman of the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> commented that McCormack and costar <a href="/wiki/Debra_Messing" title="Debra Messing">Debra Messing</a> (who played Will's best friend <a href="/wiki/Grace_Adler" title="Grace Adler">Grace Adler</a>) worked "nicely" together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carman1998_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carman1998-16">[16]</a></sup> Kay McFadden of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seattle_Times" title="The Seattle Times">The Seattle Times</a></i> also praised McCormack, Messing, and the supporting cast as "very funny".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mcfadden1998_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mcfadden1998-17">[17]</a></sup> For this performance, he earned four <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy Award</a> nominations (2000, 2001, 2003, 2005), one of which resulted in a win (2001), for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Lead_Actor_%E2%80%93_Comedy_Series" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actor – Comedy Series">Outstanding Lead Actor in a Comedy Series</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In addition, he received five <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award" title="Golden Globe Award">Golden Globe Award</a> nominations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in 1998, McCormack appeared in <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Herek" title="Stephen Herek">Stephen Herek</a>'s comedy film <i><a href="/wiki/Holy_Man" title="Holy Man">Holy Man</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klady1998_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klady1998-20">[20]</a></sup> The film was critically and financially unsuccessful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1998a_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-1998a-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1998b_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-1998b-22">[22]</a></sup> The following year he starred in the comedy movie <i><a href="/wiki/Free_Enterprise_(film)" title="Free Enterprise (film)">Free Enterprise</a></i> (1999), a movie about two filmmakers (McCormack and <a href="/wiki/Rafer_Weigel" title="Rafer Weigel">Rafer Weigel</a>) obsessed with actor <a href="/wiki/William_Shatner" title="William Shatner">William Shatner</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">Star Trek</a></i>. Film critic Kevin Thomas of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> wrote that McCormack and Weigel "both make a strong impression".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas1999_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas1999-23">[23]</a></sup> In 2000, McCormack appeared in the <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> television movie <i><a href="/wiki/The_Audrey_Hepburn_Story" title="The Audrey Hepburn Story">The Audrey Hepburn Story</a></i>, portraying actor <a href="/wiki/Mel_Ferrer" title="Mel Ferrer">Mel Ferrer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gallo2000_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gallo2000-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the 2001 <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> season, McCormack briefly portrayed Professor Harold Hill (replacing <a href="/wiki/Craig_Bierko" title="Craig Bierko">Craig Bierko</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/Susan_Stroman" title="Susan Stroman">Susan Stroman</a> revival of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Music_Man" title="The Music Man">The Music Man</a></i> at the <a href="/wiki/Neil_Simon_Theatre" title="Neil Simon Theatre">Neil Simon Theatre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dominguez2001_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dominguez2001-25">[25]</a></sup> In August 2002, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Bowl" title="Hollywood Bowl">Hollywood Bowl</a>'s summer concert series, he reprised the role of Harold Hill for a one-night only appearance in which he and other actors recreated the songs from the production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell2002_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell2002-26">[26]</a></sup> McCormack hosted the fourth episode of the 28th season of the sketch comedy show <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i> on November 2, 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-a-27">[27]</a></sup> In 2004, he had a recurring role as Ray Summers on <a href="/wiki/Showtime_(TV_network)" title="Showtime (TV network)">Showtime</a>'s comedy drama <i><a href="/wiki/Dead_Like_Me" title="Dead Like Me">Dead Like Me</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renamed_from_2004_on_20101220232705_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-renamed_from_2004_on_20101220232705-28">[28]</a></sup> The following year, McCormack starred in the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sisters_(2005_film)" title="The Sisters (2005 film)">The Sisters</a></i>, based on <a href="/wiki/Anton_Chekhov" title="Anton Chekhov">Anton Chekhov</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Sisters_(play)" title="Three Sisters (play)">Three Sisters</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert2006_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert2006-29">[29]</a></sup> The film premiered at the 2005 <a href="/wiki/Tribeca_Film_Festival" title="Tribeca Film Festival">Tribeca Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scheib2005_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scheib2005-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Will &amp; Grace</i>'s eighth and final season ended with the series finale on May 18, 2006. The finale garnered 18 million American viewers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renamed_from_2006_on_20101220232705_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-renamed_from_2006_on_20101220232705-31">[31]</a></sup> making it the most-watched entertainment telecast in six years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kissell2006_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kissell2006-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_Will_.26_Grace">After <i>Will &amp; Grace</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_McCormack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: After Will &amp; Grace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Following the end of <i>Will &amp; Grace</i>, McCormack starred on the New York stage opposite <a href="/wiki/Fran_Drescher" title="Fran Drescher">Fran Drescher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judy_Reyes" title="Judy Reyes">Judy Reyes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brooke_Smith_(actress)" title="Brooke Smith (actress)">Brooke Smith</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Maura_Tierney" title="Maura Tierney">Maura Tierney</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Neil_LaBute" title="Neil LaBute">Neil LaBute</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Off_Broadway" title="Off Broadway">Off Broadway</a> play <i><a href="/wiki/Some_Girl(s)" title="Some Girl(s)">Some Girl(s)</a></i> at the <a href="/wiki/Lucille_Lortel_Theatre" title="Lucille Lortel Theatre">Lucille Lortel Theatre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ben_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ben-33">[33]</a></sup> He plays a writer who is ready to settle down and marry, but decides to visit four ex-girlfriends first. For his performance, McCormack received critical reviews. <i>New York Times</i> contributor <a href="/wiki/Ben_Brantley" title="Ben Brantley">Ben Brantley</a>, in review of the production, wrote: "Playing a thoughtless, woman-despising heterosexual, Mr. McCormack isn't much different from when he was playing a thoughtful, woman-worshiping homosexual. As in <i>Will &amp; Grace</i>, he italicizes every other line for maximum comic spin and punctuates his dialogue by earnestly furrowing his features".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ben_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-ben-33">[33]</a></sup> Brantley went on to say that McCormack's interpretation of the character is "certainly a more slickly sustained performance" than the one delivered by <a href="/wiki/David_Schwimmer" title="David Schwimmer">David Schwimmer</a> in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ben_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-ben-33">[33]</a></sup> Melissa Rose Bernardo of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> commented that McCormack and Maura Tierney "have incredible chemistry".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernardo2006_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bernardo2006-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the same year, McCormack produced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lifetime_Television" title="Lifetime Television">Lifetime</a> comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Lovespring_International" title="Lovespring International">Lovespring International</a></i>, a show that revolves around six employees at Lovespring International, a dating agency located in California as an "elite Beverly Hills" company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wheat2006_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wheat2006-35">[35]</a></sup> The series debuted to ambivalent reviews,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006a_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-2006a-36">[36]</a></sup> with Matthew Gilbert of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i> commenting that <i>Lovespring International</i> is "a lively little cable exercise in over-the-top characters, bad taste, satire, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_incorrectness" title="Political incorrectness">political incorrectness</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilbert2006_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gilbert2006-37">[37]</a></sup> The show was cancelled that same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Umstead2006_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Umstead2006-38">[38]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 2008, McCormack co-starred in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A%26E_Network" title="A&amp;E Network">A&amp;E</a> television miniseries <i><a href="/wiki/The_Andromeda_Strain_(miniseries)" title="The Andromeda Strain (miniseries)">The Andromeda Strain</a></i>, a remake of the <a href="/wiki/The_Andromeda_Strain_(film)" title="The Andromeda Strain (film)">1971 movie</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Andromeda_Strain" title="The Andromeda Strain">based on the novel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Crichton" title="Michael Crichton">Michael Crichton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kathy_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-kathy-39">[39]</a></sup> In the miniseries, he played Jack Nash, a television reporter who battles an addiction to cocaine. <i>The Andromeda Strain</i> received mixed reviews,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renamed_from_2008_on_20101220232705_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-renamed_from_2008_on_20101220232705-40">[40]</a></sup> and McCormack's performance was criticized. Joanna Weiss of the <i>Boston Globe</i> wrote, "The presence of Eric McCormack, as an intrepid TV reporter, is especially extraneous (no disrespect to intrepid reporters)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weiss2008_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weiss2008-41">[41]</a></sup> Robert Bianco of <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i> commented, "The central cast is completed by ... poor Eric McCormack as a crusading, coke-addicted journalist who spends the second half of the movie playing Rambo in the desert. Let's just say McCormack does the best he can with what he's given, and leave it at that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bianco2008_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bianco2008-42">[42]</a></sup> On September 5, 2008, McCormack made a guest appearance in the seventh season and 100th episode of the television series <i><a href="/wiki/Monk_(TV_series)" title="Monk (TV series)">Monk</a></i>, where he played an unctuous host of a television crime docudrama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McDonough2008_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-McDonough2008-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008a_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-2008a-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2009, McCormack returned to television in the <a href="/wiki/TNT_(TV_channel)" title="TNT (TV channel)">TNT</a> drama <i><a href="/wiki/Trust_Me_(TV_series)" title="Trust Me (TV series)">Trust Me</a></i>, co-starring <a href="/wiki/Tom_Cavanagh" title="Tom Cavanagh">Tom Cavanagh</a>. The series, set around a fictional advertising firm, starred McCormack as Mason McGuire, the firm's newly promoted creative director, and deals with his best friend's (Cavanagh) unpredictable behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nussbaum2009_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nussbaum2009-45">[45]</a></sup> In an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Weekend" title="USA Weekend">USA Weekend</a></i>, McCormack revealed he was not afraid of being <a href="/wiki/Typecasting_(acting)" title="Typecasting (acting)">typecast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usa_weekend_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-usa_weekend-46">[46]</a></sup> McCormack's decision to do the show was due to "great writing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loud_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-loud-47">[47]</a></sup> The show debuted on January 26, 2009, and was watched by almost 3.4 million viewers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frankel2009_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frankel2009-48">[48]</a></sup> <i>Trust Me</i> debuted to very positive reviews, with Tim Goodman of the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> writing, "...the series is surprisingly solid."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodman2009_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodman2009-49">[49]</a></sup> Mary McNamara of the <i>Los Angeles Times</i> wrote that McCormack and Cavanagh "manage to keep their characters sharply defined but low-key. They are opposites but not in an ash-smudged, Windex-wielding Felix and Oscar way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McNamara2009_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-McNamara2009-50">[50]</a></sup> The series, however, was cancelled after one season, due to poor ratings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goodman2009a_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goodman2009a-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bryant2009_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bryant2009-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCormack starred in the science-fiction film <i><a href="/wiki/Alien_Trespass" title="Alien Trespass">Alien Trespass</a></i> (2009), in which he played Doctor Ted Lewis, who gets possessed by an alien marshal, Urp, after he crash-lands on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulture_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-vulture-53">[53]</a></sup> When asked about his interpretation on the character, McCormack commented that his first instinct was to make Ted Lewis more alien, sounding like <a href="/wiki/Spock" title="Spock">Spock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulture_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-vulture-53">[53]</a></sup> The film was critically and financially unsuccessful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renamed_from_2009_on_20101220232705_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-renamed_from_2009_on_20101220232705-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2009a_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-2009a-55">[55]</a></sup></p>

<p>In May 2009, he portrayed "El Gallo" in Reprise Theatre Company's revival of the 1960s musical <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fantasticks" title="The Fantasticks">The Fantasticks</a></i> at UCLA's Freud Playhouse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lacher2009_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lacher2009-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Verini2009_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Verini2009-57">[57]</a></sup> McCormack had a supporting role in <a href="/wiki/Richard_Loncraine" title="Richard Loncraine">Richard Loncraine</a>'s comedy <i><a href="/wiki/My_One_and_Only_(film)" title="My One and Only (film)">My One and Only</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2009_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anderson2009-58">[58]</a></sup> which was released in August 2009.</p>
<p>On September 30, 2009, he guest-starred on the police procedural drama <i><a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order:_Special_Victims_Unit" title="Law &amp; Order: Special Victims Unit">Law &amp; Order: Special Victims Unit</a></i> in the second episode of its <a href="/wiki/Law_%26_Order:_Special_Victims_Unit_(season_11)" title="Law &amp; Order: Special Victims Unit (season 11)">11th season</a> playing an owner of a dating website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gilbert2009_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gilbert2009-59">[59]</a></sup> In addition, McCormack had a recurring role in season five of the comedy series <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Adventures_of_Old_Christine" title="The New Adventures of Old Christine">The New Adventures of Old Christine</a></i>, in which he played a therapist and love interest for <a href="/wiki/Julia_Louis-Dreyfus" title="Julia Louis-Dreyfus">Julia Louis-Dreyfus</a>'s character, Christine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Porter2009_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Porter2009-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCormack portrayed con artist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clark_Rockefeller" title="Clark Rockefeller">Clark Rockefeller</a> in the Lifetime television movie <i><a href="/wiki/Who_Is_Clark_Rockefeller%3F" title="Who Is Clark Rockefeller?">Who Is Clark Rockefeller?</a></i>, which premiered on March 13, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renamed_from_2010_on_20101220232705_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-renamed_from_2010_on_20101220232705-61">[61]</a></sup> To prepare for the role, he read everything on the case,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albiniak2010_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Albiniak2010-62">[62]</a></sup> including coverage of the case and Rockefeller's jailhouse interview.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heslam2010_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heslam2010-63">[63]</a></sup> <i>Who Is Clark Rockefeller?</i> received mixed reaction, but McCormack's performance was favored by critics, with <i>Variety</i>'s Brian Lowry concluding, "...the real kitsch factor resides in Eric McCormack's performance as the suave charmer, which adds an element of high camp to the proceedings."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lowry2010_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lowry2010-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 2010, McCormack received the NBC Universal Canada Award of Distinction at the Banff TV Festival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Volmers2010_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Volmers2010-65">[65]</a></sup> In October 2010, McCormack received a star on <a href="/wiki/Canada%27s_Walk_of_Fame" title="Canada's Walk of Fame">Canada's Walk of Fame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quill2010_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Quill2010-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2010, it was reported that McCormack would star in a new TNT television drama, <i><a href="/wiki/Perception_(U.S._TV_series)" title="Perception (U.S. TV series)">Perception</a></i>, playing a crime-solving neuroscientist named Dr. Daniel Pierce, who works with the federal government to solve cases using his knowledge and imaginative view of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Exton2010_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Exton2010-67">[67]</a></sup> <i>Perception</i> premiered on July 9, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> McCormack also serves as producer for the show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baxton2010_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baxton2010-69">[69]</a></sup> He also provides the voice of "Lucky" on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Hub_(TV_channel)" title="The Hub (TV channel)">The Hub</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Pound_Puppies_(2010_TV_series)" title="Pound Puppies (2010 TV series)">Pound Puppies</a></i> series, which premiered October 10, 2010</p>
<p>In February 2015, he guest-starred on an episode of NBC's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mysteries_of_Laura" title="The Mysteries of Laura">The Mysteries of Laura</a></i> which stars <a href="/wiki/Debra_Messing" title="Debra Messing">Debra Messing</a>, his former co-star on <i>Will &amp; Grace</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_projects">Other projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_McCormack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McCormack has set up his own production company called Big Cattle Productions to develop ideas for television.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbs_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-pbs-13">[13]</a></sup> The projects produced by the company include <i><a href="/wiki/Lovespring_International" title="Lovespring International">Lovespring International</a></i> and <i>Imperfect Union</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006b_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-2006b-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zeitchik2007_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zeitchik2007-71">[71]</a></sup> In 2003, it was confirmed that he would write, direct, and star in the romantic comedy <i>What You Wish For</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brady2006_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brady2006-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-renamed_from_2003_on_20101220232705_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-renamed_from_2003_on_20101220232705-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCormack recorded a song, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Greatest_Discovery" title="The Greatest Discovery">The Greatest Discovery</a>", which was written by <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Taupin" title="Bernie Taupin">Bernie Taupin</a> in 1970, for the 2006 album <i><a href="/wiki/Unexpected_Dreams" title="Unexpected Dreams">Unexpected Dreams – Songs From the Stars</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Charkalis2006_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Charkalis2006-74">[74]</a></sup> He also wrote and sang a song called "Living with Grace" for the 2004 soundtrack to <i>Will &amp; Grace</i> with piano music provided by <a href="/wiki/Barry_Manilow" title="Barry Manilow">Barry Manilow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reighley2004_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reighley2004-75">[75]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_McCormack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>McCormack has been married to Janet Leigh Holden, whom he met on the set of <i>Lonesome Dove</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mclean_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-mclean-9">[9]</a></sup> since August 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pop_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-pop-76">[76]</a></sup> They have a son, Finnigan Holden McCormack, born on July 1, 2002, in Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hamersly2002_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hamersly2002-77">[77]</a></sup> McCormack maintains residences in Los Angeles and Vancouver.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brownlee2010_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brownlee2010-78">[78]</a></sup> He became a US citizen in 1999 and holds <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dual_citizenship" title="Dual citizenship">dual</a> Canadian and US citizenship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stafford1999_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stafford1999-79">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCormack is involved in many Los Angeles and Canadian-based charitable organizations including <a href="/wiki/Project_Angel_Food" title="Project Angel Food">Project Angel Food</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2008b_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-2008b-80">[80]</a></sup> The Wellness Community West Los Angeles Tribute to the Human Spirit Awards dinner presented an award to McCormack for his <a href="/wiki/Breast_cancer" title="Breast cancer">breast cancer</a> awareness advocacy. He shared with the audience how his comedy helped his mother, Doris McCormack, endure her breast cancer treatments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hartog2005_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hartog2005-81">[81]</a></sup> Doris was honored at the Lifetime's Breast Cancer Heroes Luncheon in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hibberd2004_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hibberd2004-82">[82]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2004a_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-2004a-83">[83]</a></sup> He serves as an honorary board member of the <a href="/wiki/Multiple_Myeloma_Research_Foundation" title="Multiple Myeloma Research Foundation">Multiple Myeloma Research Foundation</a> (MMRF) and was given the MMRF Spirit of Hope Award in October 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2006c_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-2006c-84">[84]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCormack sang both the American and Canadian national anthems at the <a href="/wiki/54th_National_Hockey_League_All-Star_Game" title="54th National Hockey League All-Star Game">2004 NHL All-Star game</a> in Minnesota.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gurtman2004_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gurtman2004-85">[85]</a></sup> McCormack is a supporter of <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a> and attended a march in <a href="/wiki/Fresno,_California" title="Fresno, California">Fresno, California</a>, on May 30, 2009, after the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_California" title="Supreme Court of California">Supreme Court of California</a> upheld a ban on same-sex marriage approved by voters in November by ballot <a href="/wiki/California_Proposition_8_(2008)" title="California Proposition 8 (2008)">Proposition 8</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fresno_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-fresno-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Garofoli2009_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Garofoli2009-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCormack identifies as a <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></p>
