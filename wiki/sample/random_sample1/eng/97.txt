<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Å</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Å</b> (lower case: <b>å</b>) represents various (although often very similar) <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">sounds</a> in several languages. It is considered a separate letter in the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_alphabet" title="Swedish alphabet">Swedish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danish_alphabet" title="Danish alphabet">Danish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_alphabet" title="Norwegian alphabet">Norwegian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finnish_alphabet" title="Finnish alphabet">Finnish</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Frisian_language" title="North Frisian language">North Frisian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walloon_alphabet" title="Walloon alphabet">Walloon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emiliano-Romagnolo" title="Emiliano-Romagnolo">Emiliano-Romagnolo</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chamorro_alphabet" title="Chamorro alphabet">Chamorro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Istro-Romanian_language" title="Istro-Romanian language">Istro-Romanian</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lule_Sami" title="Lule Sami">Lule Sami</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skolt_Sami" title="Skolt Sami">Skolt Sami</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Sami" title="Southern Sami">Southern Sami</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenlandic_alphabet" title="Greenlandic alphabet">Greenlandic</a> alphabets. Additionally, it is part of the <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabets</a> used for the <a href="/wiki/Alemannic_German" title="Alemannic German">Alemannic</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bavarian_German" title="Bavarian German">Bavarian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Austrian_German" title="Austrian German">Austrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_alphabet" title="German alphabet">German</a>.</p>
<p>Though Å is derived from an <a href="/wiki/A" title="A">A</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Ring_(diacritic)" title="Ring (diacritic)">ring</a>, it is considered a separate <a href="/wiki/Letter_(alphabet)" title="Letter (alphabet)">letter</a>. It developed as a form of semi-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ligature_(typography)" title="Ligature (typography)">ligature</a> of an <i>A</i> with a smaller <i>o</i> above it to denote a <a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">long</a> and darker A, similar to how the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umlaut_(diacritic)" title="Umlaut (diacritic)">umlaut mark</a> that distinguishes Ä from A, and Ö/Ø from O, developed from a small <i>e</i> written above the letter in question.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scandinavian_languages">Scandinavian languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scandinavian languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Swedish_keyboard_20050614.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Swedish_keyboard_20050614.jpg/220px-Swedish_keyboard_20050614.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Swedish_keyboard_20050614.jpg/330px-Swedish_keyboard_20050614.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Swedish_keyboard_20050614.jpg/440px-Swedish_keyboard_20050614.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Swedish_keyboard_20050614.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Swedish keyboard showing Å, Ä, and Ö</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The letter Å in Scandinavian <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabets</a> represents two sounds, one short and one long.</p>
<ul>
<li>The short version represents <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">IPA</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Open-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Open-mid back rounded vowel">ɔ</a>/</span>.</li>
<li>In Swedish, the long version represents <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">IPA</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Close-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close-mid back rounded vowel">o</a>ː/</span>. In Danish and Norwegian, the long version is pronounced <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">IPA</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɔː/</span>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical-comparative_linguistics" title="Historical-comparative linguistics">historical linguistics</a>, the Å-sound originally had the same origin as the long <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aː/</span> sound in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>Aal</i> and <i>Haar</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandinavian_language" title="Scandinavian language">Scandinavian</a> <i>ål</i>, <i>hår</i>, <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> eel, hair).</p>
<p>Historically, the letter Å derives from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> vowel <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%81" title="Á">á</a></i>. This was a long <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/aː/</span> sound, but over time, the vowel developed to an <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[<a href="/wiki/Open-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Open-mid back rounded vowel">ɔ</a>]</span> sound. Medieval writing often used doubled letters for long vowels, and the vowel continued to be written Aa. In <a href="/wiki/Old_Swedish" title="Old Swedish">Old Swedish</a> the use of the ligatures Æ and Œ that represented the sounds <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[æ]</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ø]</span> respectively were gradually replaced by new letters. Instead of using ligatures, a minuscule <a href="/wiki/E" title="E">E</a> was placed above the letters <a href="/wiki/A" title="A">A</a> and <a href="/wiki/O" title="O">O</a> to create new <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">graphemes</a>. They later evolved into the modern letters <a href="/wiki/%C3%84" title="Ä">Ä</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%96" title="Ö">Ö</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/E" title="E">E</a> was simplified into the two dots now referred to as <a href="/wiki/Diaeresis_(diacritic)" title="Diaeresis (diacritic)">umlaut</a>. This construction was also applied to construct a new grapheme where an "aa" previously had been used. A minuscule O was placed on top of an A to create a new letter. It was first used in <a href="/wiki/Publishing" title="Publishing">print</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Vasa_Bible" title="Gustav Vasa Bible">Gustav Vasa Bible</a> that was published in 1541 and replaced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aa_(digraph)" title="Aa (digraph)">Aa</a> in the 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In an attempt to modernize the orthography, linguists tried to introduce the Å to Danish and Norwegian writing in the 19th century. Most people felt no need for the new letter, although the letter group <i>Aa</i> had already been pronounced like Å for centuries all over Scandinavia. Aa was usually treated as a single letter, spoken like the present Å when spelling out names or words. Orthography reforms making Å official were carried out in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> in 1917 and in <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> in 1948. It has been argued that the Å only made its way to official Danish spelling due to anti-German and pro-Scandinavian sentiment after World War II. Danish had been the only language apart from German and <a href="/wiki/Luxembourgish_language" title="Luxembourgish language">Luxembourgish</a> to use <a href="/wiki/Capitalization#Nouns" title="Capitalization">capitalized nouns</a> in the last decades, but abolished them at the same occasion.</p>
<p>In a few names of Danish cities or towns, the old spelling has been retained as an option due to local resistance, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Aalborg" title="Aalborg">Aalborg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aabenraa" title="Aabenraa">Aabenraa</a>; however, Ålborg and Åbenrå are the spellings recommended by the <a href="/wiki/Dansk_Sprogn%C3%A6vn" title="Dansk Sprognævn">Danish Language Board</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Between 1948 and 2010, the city of <a href="/wiki/Aarhus" title="Aarhus">Aarhus</a> was officially spelled <i>Århus</i>. However, the city has changed to the <i>Aa</i> spelling starting 2011, in a controversial decision citing internationalization and <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">web</a> compatibility advantages.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Icelandic_orthography" title="Icelandic orthography">Icelandic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faroese_alphabet" title="Faroese alphabet">Faroese</a> are the only Nordic languages not to use the letter Å. The Old Norse letter <i>á</i> is retained, but has become a diphthong, pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[au]</span> in Icelandic and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɔa]</span> in Faroese. The short variation of Faroese á is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[<a href="/wiki/Open-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Open-mid back rounded vowel">ɔ</a>]</span>, though.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_names">Use in names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use in names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some names of geographical places, the old <i><span lang="no" xml:lang="no">Aa</span></i> spelling dominates, more often in Denmark than in Norway (where it has been abolished in official use since 1917). Locals of <i><span lang="no" xml:lang="no">Aalborg</span></i> and <i><span lang="no" xml:lang="no">Aabenraa</span></i> resist the Å, whereas <i><span lang="no" xml:lang="no">Ålesund</span></i> is rarely seen with Aa spelling. Official rules allow both forms in the most common cases, but Å is always correct.</p>
<p>Before 1917, when spelling with the double A was common, some Norwegian place names contained three or four consecutive A letters: for instance <i><span lang="no" xml:lang="no">Haaa</span></i> (now <i><span lang="no" xml:lang="no">Håa</span></i>, a river) and <i><span lang="no" xml:lang="no">Blaaaasen</span></i> (<i>Blååsen</i>, 'the blå/blue ås/ridge').</p>
<p>In family names, the bearer of the name uses Aa or Å according to their choice, but since family names are inherited they are resistant to change and the traditional Aa style is often kept. For instance, the last name <i><span lang="no" xml:lang="no">Aagaard</span></i> is much more common than <i><span lang="no" xml:lang="no">Ågård</span></i>. The surname Aa is always spelled with double A, never with the single letter Å. However, given names - which are less commonly inherited - have largely changed to the use of the Å. For instance, in Norway more than 12 000 male citizens spell their name <i>Håkon</i>, while only around 2500 are named <i><a href="/wiki/Haakon_(given_name)" title="Haakon (given name)">Haakon</a></i>.</p>
<p>Company names are sometimes spelled with the double A by choice, usually in order to convey an impression of old-fashionedness or traditionality. The double A, representing a single sound, is usually kept in initials e.g. for people whose surname begins with the double A. Accordingly, a person named "Hans Aagard Hauge" would spell his initials "H. Aa. H.", not "H. Å. H."</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Place_in_alphabet">Place in alphabet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Place in alphabet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pois_pakkoruotsi.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="997" data-file-width="1000" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Pois_pakkoruotsi.svg/220px-Pois_pakkoruotsi.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Pois_pakkoruotsi.svg/330px-Pois_pakkoruotsi.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Pois_pakkoruotsi.svg/440px-Pois_pakkoruotsi.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pois_pakkoruotsi.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The fact that Å is a common letter in Swedish while having no native use in Finnish has led to it being used as a concise symbol for the Swedish language in Finland, as in this campaign to rid Finnish schools of <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Swedish" title="Mandatory Swedish">Mandatory Swedish</a>. The phrase reads "Away with enforced Swedish".</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Correct alphabetization in Danish and Norwegian places Å as the last letter in the <a href="/wiki/Danish_and_Norwegian_alphabet" title="Danish and Norwegian alphabet">alphabet</a>, the sequence being <a href="/wiki/%C3%86" title="Æ">Æ</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%98" title="Ø">Ø</a>, Å. This is also true for the alternative spelling "Aa". Unless manually corrected, sorting algorithms of programs localised for Danish or Norwegian will place <i>e.g.,</i> <i>Aaron</i> after <i>Zorro</i>. In Danish and Norwegian books, a distinction is made between foreign and local words; thus, for example, the German city <i><a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a></i> is listed under <i>A,</i> but the Danish city <i><a href="/wiki/Aabenraa" title="Aabenraa">Aabenraa</a></i> is listed after Z, Æ and Ø.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_alphabet" title="Swedish alphabet">Swedish alphabet</a>, Å is <a href="/wiki/Collation" title="Collation">sorted</a> after <a href="/wiki/Z" title="Z">Z</a>, as the third letter from the end, the sequence being Å, <a href="/wiki/%C3%84" title="Ä">Ä</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%96" title="Ö">Ö</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_transcription">International transcription</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International transcription">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alternative spellings of the Scandinavian Å have become a concern because of globalization, and particularly because of the popularization of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>. This is to a large extent due to the fact that prior to the creation of <a href="/wiki/Internationalized_domain_name" title="Internationalized domain name">IDNA</a> system around 2005, internet domains containing Scandinavian letters were not recognized by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNS" title="DNS">DNS</a> system, and anyway do not feature on keyboards adapted for other languages. While it is recommended to keep the Å intact wherever possible, the next best thing is to use the older, double A spelling (e.g. "www.raade.com" instead of "www.råde.com"). This is because, as previously discussed, the Å/Aa indicates a separate sound. If the Å is represented as a common A without the ring (e.g. "www.rade.com") there is no indication that the A is supposed to represent another sound entirely. Even so, (mis-)representing the Å as just an A is particularly common in Sweden, as compared to Norway and Denmark.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Finnish">Finnish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Finnish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finnish_alphabet" title="Finnish alphabet">Finnish alphabet</a> is derived from the Swedish alphabet, Å is carried over, but it has no native use and is treated as in Swedish. Its usage is limited to names of Swedish, Danish or Norwegian origin. In Finland there are many both Swedish- and Finnish-speaking people with Swedish surnames, and many Swedish surnames include Å. The Finnish name of Å is <i>ruotsalainen O</i> ("Swedish O"), and is pronounced identically to <i>O,</i> which has the value <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[o̞]</span>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emilian-Romagnol">Emilian-Romagnol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Emilian-Romagnol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emiliano-Romagnolo" title="Emiliano-Romagnolo">Emilian-Romagnol</a>, å is used to represent the <a href="/wiki/Open-mid_back_unrounded_vowel" title="Open-mid back unrounded vowel">open-mid back unrounded vowel</a> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʌ]</span>, e.g. Modenese dialect <i>åmm</i>, <i>dånna</i> [ˈʌmː, ˈdʌnːa] "man, woman".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Walloon_writing">Walloon writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Walloon writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Å was introduced to some local variants of eastern-<a href="/wiki/Walloon_language" title="Walloon language">Walloon dialect</a> (a language related to French) at the beginning of the 20th century, initially to note the same sound as in Danish. Its use quickly spread to all the eastern-Walloon dialects, through the cultural influence of the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge_(city)" title="Liège (city)">Liège</a>, and covered three sounds, a long open <i>o</i>, a long close <i>o</i>, or a long <i>a</i>, depending on the local varieties. The use of a single <i>å</i> letter to cover those pronunciations has been embraced by the new pan-Walloon <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a>, that systemizes a unique orthography for words that are the same, regardless of the local phonetic variations.</p>
<p>In non-standardized writings outside the Liège area, words containing the <i>å</i> letter are written with <i>au</i>, <i>â</i> or <i>ô</i> depending on the pronunciation. For example, the word <i>måjhon</i> (house) in standardized orthography is written <i>môjo</i>, <i>mâhon</i>, <i>mohone</i>, <i>maujon</i> in dialectal writings.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Istro-Romanian">Istro-Romanian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Istro-Romanian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Istro-Romanian_language" title="Istro-Romanian language">Istro-Romanian</a> alphabet is based on the standard <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a> alphabet with three additional letters used to mark sounds specific only to this dialect: <b>å</b>, <b>ľ</b> and <b>ń</b>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chamorro">Chamorro</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Chamorro">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Å and <i>å</i> are also used in the practical orthography of <a href="/wiki/Chamorro_language" title="Chamorro language">Chamorro</a>, a language indigenous to the people of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Mariana_Islands" title="Northern Mariana Islands">Northern Mariana Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>. The capital of Guam is also called "Hagåtña".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbol_for_.C3.A5ngstr.C3.B6m">Symbol for ångström</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Symbol for ångström">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The letter "Å" (U+00C5) is also used throughout the world as the international symbol for the non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> unit <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">ångström</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_unit" title="Physical unit">physical unit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Length" title="Length">length</a> named after the <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> <a href="/wiki/Anders_Jonas_%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Anders Jonas Ångström">Anders Jonas Ångström</a>. It is always <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_case" title="Upper case">upper case</a> in this context (symbols for units named after persons are generally upper-case). The ångström is a unit of length equal to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6990100000000000000♠</span>10<sup>−10</sup> m</span> (one ten-billionth of a meter) or <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6990100000000000000♠</span>0.1 nm</span>.</p>
<p>Unicode also has encoded <span class="nowrap"><span style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">U+212B</span></span> <span style="font-size:125%">Å</span> <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">ANGSTROM SIGN</span>. However, that is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unicode_normalization" title="Unicode normalization">canonically equivalent</a> to the ordinary letter Å. The duplicate encoding at U+212B is due to round-trip mapping compatibility with an East-Asian <a href="/wiki/Character_encoding" title="Character encoding">character encoding</a>, but is otherwise not to be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incorrect_and_playful_uses_of_.C3.85">Incorrect and playful uses of Å</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%85&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Incorrect and playful uses of Å">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logo</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> team now known as the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels_of_Anaheim" title="Los Angeles Angels of Anaheim">Los Angeles Angels of Anaheim</a> is a capital "A" with a halo. Due to the resemblance, Angels fans are known to stylise the name as "Ångels". The logo of the <i><a href="/wiki/Stargate" title="Stargate">Stargate</a></i> series similarly features a stylized A with a circle above it, making it resemble an Å as in Stargåte; in Norwegian, "gåte" means "riddle". Similarly, Cirque du Soleil's Koozå production also uses this character in its logo, although it is pronounced by the main singer as a regular "a".</p>
<p>British producer and singer <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A5psley" title="Låpsley">Låpsley</a> uses å in her stage name.</p>
<p>A related phenomenon is the <a href="/wiki/Metal_umlaut" title="Metal umlaut">metal umlaut</a>, which unlike the previous examples is intentional use of diacritics.</p>
