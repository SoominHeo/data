<p><b>Ewa Kłobukowska</b> (born 1 October 1946) is a former <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> <a href="/wiki/Sprint_(running)" title="Sprint (running)">sprinter</a>. She competed at the 1964 Olympics in the 4×100 m relay and 100 m sprint and won a gold and a bronze medal, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sr_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sr-2">[2]</a></sup> She also won two gold and one silver medal at the 1966 European Championships. Kłobukowska set three world records, one in the 100 m (11.1 s, 9 July 1965 in <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>) and two in the 4 × 100 m relay (44.2 s, 13 September 1964, Lodz and 43.6 s, 21 October 1964, Tokyo). They were annulled by the <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Athletics_Federations" title="International Association of Athletics Federations">International Association of Athletics Federations</a> (IAAF) after she failed a gender identification test in 1967, though the test procedures were later found to be inadequate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ewa_K%C5%82obukowska&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kłobukowska was born in a family of intellectuals. In 1965 she graduated from a Technical School of Economics No. 6 and in 1972 from the Warsaw School of Economics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-bio-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender">Gender</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ewa_K%C5%82obukowska&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gender">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kłobukowska failed a traditional gender test for European Cup women's track and field competition in <a href="/wiki/Kiev" title="Kiev">Kiev</a> in 1967 and was subsequently banned from competing in professional sports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IAAF" title="IAAF">IAAF</a> she had "one chromosome too many." Medical publications revealed that Klobukowska is a genetic mosaic of XX/XXY. If she had been tested one year later at the Mexico Olympics she would have been eligible on the grounds that she was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barr_Body" title="Barr Body">Barr Body</a> positive. Klobukowska has a Barr Body in all of her cells. Athletes without such a Barr Body (inactive X-chromosome) were suspended from competition by 1968 in Mexico City. She gave birth to her son in 1968, and thus must have had a genetic abnormality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Her humiliation led to a change in the gender verification policies by the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a>, which from then on kept test results secret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>IAAF erased the three world records set by Kłobukowska, including the two team records in the 4×100 m relay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-bio-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
