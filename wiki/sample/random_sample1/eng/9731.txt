<p><b>Esen Taishi</b> (<a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">Mongolian</a>: <span lang="mn" xml:lang="mn">Эсэн тайш</span>; d. 1455)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> was a powerful <a href="/wiki/Oirats" title="Oirats">Oirat</a> <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_nobility" title="Mongolian nobility">Taishi</a> and <i>de facto</i> ruler of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Yuan_dynasty" title="Northern Yuan dynasty">Northern Yuan</a> in 15th century <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> . He is best known for capturing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zhengtong_Emperor" title="Zhengtong Emperor">Zhengtong Emperor</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a> in 1450 after the <a href="/wiki/Tumu_Crisis" title="Tumu Crisis">Battle of Tumu Fortress</a> and briefly reuniting the Mongols. The <a href="/wiki/Four_Oirat" title="Four Oirat">Four Oirat</a> reached the peak of their power under his rule.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Youth_and_early_career">Youth and early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esen_Taishi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Youth and early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Esen was born to his father, Toghan, the <a href="/wiki/Choros" title="Choros">Choros</a> taishi (grand <a href="/wiki/Preceptor" title="Preceptor">preceptor</a>, from 太師) who had expanded Oirat territory substantially, with more Mongol tribes acknowledging his supremacy. As an Oirat, Esen himself was not descended from Genghis Khan, which would hamper his claim to the title of great khan throughout his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his early campaigns he fought against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaghatayid_khans" title="Chaghatayid khans">Chaghatayid khans</a> of <a href="/wiki/Moghulistan" title="Moghulistan">Moghulistan</a>. Esen three times defeated and twice captured the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moghul" title="Moghul">Moghul</a> ruler <a href="/wiki/Uwais_Khan" title="Uwais Khan">Uwais Khan</a> (Ways Khan) (1418–1432).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Esen released him out of respect for his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinggisid" title="Chinggisid">Chinggisid</a> blood in both cases. The third time, Uwais Khan granted Esen his sister Makhtum Khanim, who bore his two sons. Esen nominally converted to Islam in order to marry the Muslim princess, but remained effectively a shamanist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>After his father died in 1438, Esen inherited his position, taishi, for the reigning khan <a href="/wiki/Taisun_Khan" title="Taisun Khan">Taisun Khan</a> (reigned 1433–52). Under Esen Taishi's leadership, the Mongols under Taisun Khan conquered the rest of Mongolia and received the submission of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jurchens" title="Jurchens">Jurchens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuvans" title="Tuvans">Tuvans</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a> and Eastern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. In the 1430s, Esen also took over control of the Mongol kingdom known as <a href="/wiki/Kara_Del" title="Kara Del">Kara Del</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hami_City" title="Hami City">Hami</a> <a href="/wiki/Oasis" title="Oasis">oasis</a> between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gobi" title="Gobi">Gobi</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Takla_Makan" title="Takla Makan">Takla Makan</a> deserts; after 1443–45 the Mongol empire reached the northern border of <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conflict_with_the_Ming">Conflict with the Ming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esen_Taishi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Conflict with the Ming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Esen's aggressive actions irritated and threatened the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a>. The Ming had for some time pursued a "<a href="/wiki/Divide_and_rule" title="Divide and rule">divide and rule</a>" strategy in dealings with their northern neighbors, maintaining trade and tribute relationships, functioning as a kind of state-subsidized monopoly, with multiple leaders who they could then turn against one another by inciting jealousy or suggesting intrigue. A unified Mongolia under one ruler was, however, much less susceptible to such tactics. Many of the tribes brought under Oirat dominion by conquest had inhabited areas claimed by the Ming, and other tribes had been pushed south into Chinese territory seeking to escape Oirat subjugation. The Chagatayid Hami oasis, furthermore, had paid tribute to the emperor before Esen convinced its ruler to pay tribute to the Oirats instead. Throughout the 1440s, Esen increased both the frequency of tribute missions to China and the number of representatives sent on each mission, meaning that the Chinese were obliged to provide ever-more expensive hospitality to the Mongols, irrespective of the actual trade or tribute being negotiated. According to surviving Chinese accounts, the Oirats demanded more and more lucrative tribute and trade agreements further skewed in the Mongol favor.</p>
<p>One Chinese tactic for dealing with the situation, provoking rivalry between Mongol leaders, failed completely as they underestimated the degree of power Esen wielded and chose "rivals" too far below him in status for the strategy to be effective. Efforts to make Taisun Khan an effective opponent against Esen Taishi failed. In addition, their other main tactic, meeting each demand for increased tribute or trade value with a decrease, backfired as well.</p>
<p>Esen encouraged hundreds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongol" title="Mongol">Mongol</a>, Hami, and <a href="/wiki/Samarkand" title="Samarkand">Samarkand</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> merchants to accompany his missions to the Ming Emperor. Beginning in 1439 Taisun Khan and Esen sent envoys to China, often numbering more than 1,000. In response to this inflation of numbers, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zhengtong_Emperor" title="Zhengtong Emperor">Zhengtong Emperor</a> (1427–64) decreased gifts to Esen and Taisun Khan, and closed border trade with the Mongols.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasion_of_Ming_China">Invasion of Ming China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esen_Taishi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Invasion of Ming China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In retaliation for real and perceived insults, Esen Taishi led an invasion of northern China in 1449 that culminated in the capture of the Ming emperor during the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tumu_Crisi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tumu Crisi (page does not exist)">Tumu Crisi</a>. The large-scale, three-pronged invasion began in July, with Taisun Khan leading the easternmost force to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liaodong" title="Liaodong">Liaodong</a>, the grand <a href="/wiki/Councillor" title="Councillor">councillor</a> Alag attacking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xuanfu" title="Xuanfu">Xuanfu</a>, and Esen himself leading the troops that sacked <a href="/wiki/Datong" title="Datong">Datong</a> in August. Another column of the Mongols invaded <a href="/wiki/Ganzhou" title="Ganzhou">Ganzhou</a>. Acting on extremely poor advice from one of his advisors, the emperor also chose to lead his own armies into battle, resulting in defeat and capture.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_Chinese_failures">Initial Chinese failures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esen_Taishi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Initial Chinese failures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The campaign was a series of routs and massacres of Ming forces, even though imperial troops in the region are estimated to have numbered as many as 500,000 and Esen Taishi had brought only 20,000 cavalry, expecting mainly to engage in traditional Oirat border raiding. Datong lay north of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Wall_of_China" title="Great Wall of China">Great Wall of China</a>, and thus beyond its protection. After the initial attack on Datong, Esen pretended to retreat back into the Mongolian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steppes" title="Steppes">steppes</a>. The emperor and his hastily raised army chased the invaders west and met an ambush upon arriving at Datong. Mongol horsemen harried the Chinese retreat back towards the wall for four days while hampered by thunderstorms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The imperial army eventually reached the Tumu Fortress. However rather than having secured a defensible position, the Chinese were trapped against the northern side of the fortress, and the Mongol horsemen annihilated the entire Ming army.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capture_of_the_Ming_emperor">Capture of the Ming emperor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esen_Taishi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Capture of the Ming emperor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zhengtong_Emperor.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="315" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Zhengtong_Emperor.jpg/200px-Zhengtong_Emperor.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Zhengtong_Emperor.jpg/300px-Zhengtong_Emperor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/67/Zhengtong_Emperor.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zhengtong_Emperor.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zhengtong_Emperor" title="Zhengtong Emperor">Zhengtong Emperor</a>, who was captured by Esen Taishi</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Most of the remaining soldiers, as well as all officers and courtiers of rank except the emperor himself, were slaughtered. Esen was still some distance away, near Xianfu. Six weeks later, when the captured emperor was brought to his camp, Esen attempted to ransom the emperor back to the Chinese. According to some accounts, it was at this point that Esen was granted the title "Taishi."</p>
<p>In any case, the Ming refused to negotiate a ransom, perhaps in part because the emperor's brother (a prince referred to as Zhu Qiyu, later the <a href="/wiki/Jingtai_Emperor" title="Jingtai Emperor">Jingtai Emperor</a>) was by then installed on the throne and not eager to give up his new position. <a href="/wiki/Yu_Qian" title="Yu Qian">Yu Qian</a>, the defense minister of Ming, who was organizing Chinese resistance, commented that the emperor's life is not as important as the fate of the country; he also believed that ransoming the emperor might boost the Mongols' morale and reduce that of the Ming.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laying_siege_to_Beijing">Laying siege to Beijing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esen_Taishi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Laying siege to Beijing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>His ransom demand rebuffed, Esen still considered the emperor more valuable alive than dead. Probably because the acting emperor was content not to obtain his brother's freedom, Esen began laying siege to Beijing. Esen offered the emperor his sister in marriage, but the emperor refused. The disheartened garrison in Beijing under the command of <a href="/wiki/Yu_Qian" title="Yu Qian">Yu Qian</a> soon turned their unfavorable situation around. Yu Qian ordered his forces to pretend that they had lost control of the city gate in order to lure Mongol horsemen into the city. Once a large portion of the Mongol force was inside, the gate was shut and the Mongols were ambushed. Esen's sworn <a href="/wiki/Blood_brother" title="Blood brother">blood brother</a> was killed in the attack. Having failed to take the city, Esen was forced to retreat under pressure from his own troops and by the arrival of Ming reinforcements.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Negotiations">Negotiations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esen_Taishi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Negotiations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Ming court elevated the <a href="/wiki/Jingtai_Emperor" title="Jingtai Emperor">Jingtai Emperor</a> (reigned 1449–57) to the throne. Esen sent the captured emperor back in 1450. Since the Mongol economy relied on their trade-and-tribute relationship with China, Esen was obligated to reopen negotiations, now under a much weaker position. While Ming-Mongol trade did not cease entirely during the Tumu Crisis, as the incident has come to be called, Esen had not only failed to win better terms than the prior arrangements, he was forced to accept less favorable terms in return for resumption of more regular relations with the Ming. Esen and Taisun Khan again invaded northern China later on, devastating the area around the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/Nen_River" title="Nen River">Nen River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Songhua_River" title="Songhua River">Songhua River</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reign_and_death">Reign and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esen_Taishi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reign and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Taisun Khan and Esen Taishi quarreled over the designation of the heir to the throne. Esen wanted a son of his sister to be the successor of Taisun Khan, but Taisun nominated another son of the eastern Mongol <a href="/wiki/Khatun" title="Khatun">khatun</a> as his heir instead. Taisun Khan supported the Three guards and openly led his own forces against Esen in 1451, but they were outnumbered by Oirat loyalists and the nominal khan was caught and killed by eastern tribesmen as he attempted to retreat. Taisun Khan's brother <a href="/wiki/Agbarjin" title="Agbarjin">Agbarjin</a> <a href="/wiki/Jinong" title="Jinong">jinong</a> (<a href="/wiki/Viceroy" title="Viceroy">viceroy</a>), who married Esen's daughter Tsetseg, deserted to the Oirats and was promised the title of khagan of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Yuan_dynasty" title="Northern Yuan dynasty">Northern Yuan dynasty</a>. However, Esen murdered him at a feast which he had been invited. Esen attempted to kill the baby of his daughter, Agbarjin's son, but she and Esen's grandmother, <a href="/wiki/Samur_Gunj" title="Samur Gunj">Samur Gunj</a>, hid the infant prince, Batu-Mongke, who would be a direct ancestor of <a href="/wiki/Dayan_Khan" title="Dayan Khan">Dayan Khan</a>. After eighteen months since his defeat of Taisun Khan, in 1453, Esen himself took the title of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Khan" title="Great Khan">Great Khan</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Yuan" title="Great Yuan">Great Yuan</a> (大元天盛可汗).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> At the same time the Oirats launched an invasion against <a href="/wiki/Moghulistan" title="Moghulistan">Moghulistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tashkent" title="Tashkent">Tashkent</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Transoxiana" title="Transoxiana">Transoxiana</a>.</p>
<p>The Ming emperor was among the first to acknowledge the new title, but the reaction of Esen's fellow Mongols, Oirat and otherwise, mostly ranged from disapproving to enraged. Though Esen's lineage was related to the royal line descended from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tem%C3%BCjin" title="Temüjin">Temüjin</a> (Genghis Khan) through his grandmother Samur gunji (<a href="/wiki/Princess" title="Princess">princess</a>), it was unlikely that he would have been considered eligible for election as Khan, and in any case Esen ignored the usual selection process. Rather than the title of khan falling automatically to the eldest eligible male of the line, as in <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">primogeniture</a>, Mongol leaders were traditionally chosen by means of the <a href="/wiki/Kurultai" title="Kurultai">kurultai</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Elective_monarchy" title="Elective monarchy">elective monarchy</a> system, with the members of a lineage voting to choose the title's successor from among themselves. This dissatisfaction soon escalated into open revolt against Esen's leadership.</p>
<p>Esen gave his son Amasanj the title of taishi, an action that led his powerful general Alag, who had expected to receive the title himself, into rebellion. Oirat leaders joined the rebellion against Esen, who was defeated in battle and murdered in 1455, a year after his assumption of the title of khan. After his death, the Oirat no longer held sway over Mongolia, which had come under their control through Esen's influence, and remained divided among themselves for many years. The 17th and 18th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zunghar" title="Zunghar">Zunghar</a> rulers considered themselves to be descendants of Esen Taishi.</p>
