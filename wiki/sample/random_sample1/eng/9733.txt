<p><b>Eskayan</b> is an artificial auxiliary language of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eskaya_clan" title="Eskaya clan">Eskaya clan</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bohol" title="Bohol">Bohol</a>, an island province of the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>. It is grammatically <a href="/wiki/Boholano_dialect" title="Boholano dialect">Boholano</a>, the native language of Bohol, with a substituted lexicon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ted_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ted-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly2012_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly2012-3">[3]</a></sup> While Eskayan has no mother-tongue speakers, it is taught by volunteers in at least three cultural schools in the southeast interior of the province.</p>

<p>Eskayan has a number of idiosyncrasies that have attracted wide interest. One of its most immediately remarkable features is its unique writing system of over 1,000 syllabic characters, said to be modeled on parts of the human body,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bibingka_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bibingka-4">[4]</a></sup> and its non-Philippine lexicon.</p>
<p>The earliest attested document in Eskayan provisionally dates from 1908, and was on display at the Bohol Museum until September 2006.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eskayan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to speakers, the Eskaya language and script were creations of Pinay, the ancestor of the Eskaya clan, who was inspired by human anatomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly2012_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly2012-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-classification_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-classification-5">[5]</a></sup> Pinay’s language was "rediscovered" in the early 20th century by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mariano_Datahan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mariano Datahan (page does not exist)">Mariano Datahan</a> (born Mariano Sumatra, ca. 1875–1949), a Messianic rebel soldier who transmitted it to his followers. Datahan had founded a utopian community in southeast Bohol in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Philippine%E2%80%93American_War" title="Philippine–American War">Philippine–American War</a>, in order to resist imperial claims and establish an indigenous nation in Bohol, and the Eskayan language and script were seen as the embodiment of this incipient national culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly2013_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly2013-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recent research strongly suggests that the Eskayan language was created in the period after Spanish contact had been established. Evidence of this includes the presence of "native" terms (i.e., not borrowed or calqued) for post-contact cultural categories such as "pope" and "aeroplane". Further, the language makes semantic distinctions that are made in Spanish and English but not in Visayan (such as between "moon" and "month"). It is highly plausible that Eskayan vocabulary was created by taking parallel Spanish-English-Visayan wordlists from textbooks, and replacing the Visayan layer with Eskayan. Finally, the Eskayan script bears strong similarities to 19th-century Copperplate handwriting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly2013_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly2013-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Indigenous auxiliary languages with accompanying creation myths are attested elsewhere in the world. One notable case is the <a href="/wiki/Damin" title="Damin">Damin</a> ceremonial language of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Carpentaria" title="Gulf of Carpentaria">Gulf of Carpentaria</a> which is said to have been the creation of the ancestor Kalthad; another are the <a href="/wiki/Pandanus_language" title="Pandanus language">pandanus languages</a> of the Medan region of Papua New Guinea.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eskayan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eskayan is a "sophisticated encryption" of the Cebuano language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly2012_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly2012-3">[3]</a></sup> It shows no lexical similarity to any of the indigenous languages of the Philippines, apart from a very few Cebuano words. Grammatically, however, it is Cebuano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Most of the words were invented, though with inspiration from Spanish and English vocabulary and phonotactics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly2013_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly2013-6">[6]</a></sup> Some Spanish words had their meanings changed, such as <i>astro</i> 'sun' (from 'star') and <i>tre</i> 'two' (from 'three').</p>
<p>Dr. Ernie Constantino (Professor of the Linguistics Department of the University of the Philippines) argued that the Visayan-Eskaya ethnolanguage is only a constructed language whose phonology, grammar, and/or vocabulary have been consciously devised by an individual or group, instead of having evolved naturally. This ethnolanguage would then be comparable to Esperanto and Ido.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system">Writing system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eskayan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Writing system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eskayan_script" title="Eskayan script">Eskayan script</a></div>
<p>The Eskayan script has both alphabetic<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and syllabic components. A basic 'alphabet' of 46 characters accounts for most of the common sounds and syllables used in Eskayan while a broader subset totalling over 1000 is used to represent the remaining syllables. The unusual diversity of consonant and vowel clusters accounts for this relatively large number of composite characters, which even includes superfluous symbols.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The symbols are said to be based on parts of the human anatomy, though many are clearly based on the cursive roman alphabet.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Romanised_orthography">Romanised orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eskayan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Romanised orthography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A romanised form of Eskayan is used in the cultural schools for the purpose of exposition. Although not strictly standardised, this orthography has elements in common with the Spanish system once used for transliterating Cebuano. E.g., the letters ⟨i⟩ and ⟨e⟩ are interchangeable symbols representing the sound <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɪ/</span>; the ‘ll’ combination is pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/lj/</span> and the letter ⟨c⟩ will be pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/s/</span> when it precedes a front vowel, as in Spanish. A notable innovation in Eskayan romanised orthography is the letter combination ‘chd’ which represents the sound <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/d͡ʒ/</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-classification_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-classification-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology_and_phonotactics">Phonology and phonotactics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eskayan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Phonology and phonotactics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eskayan shares all the same phonemes as Boholano-Visayan (the particular variety of Cebuano spoken on Bohol) and even includes the distinctive Boholano voiced palatal affricate <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/d͡ʒ/</span> that appears in Visayan words such as <i>maayo</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[maʔad͡ʒo]</span> (‘good’). With the exception of this phoneme, Eskayan shares the same basic phonology as Cebuano-Visayan, Tagalog and many other Philippine languages.</p>
<p>The phonotactics of Eskayan, on the other hand, are quite different from those of Boholano-Visayan and Philippine languages generally. This can be seen in Eskayan words such as <i>bosdipir</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[bosdɪpɪr]</span> (‘eel’), <i>guinposlan</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɡɪnposlan]</span> (‘face’), <i>ilcdo</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɪlkdo]</span> (‘knee’) and <i>estrapirado</i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɪstrapɪrado]</span> (‘flower’) that contain consonant sequences like <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sd/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/np/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/sl/</span>, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/lkd/</span> and <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/str/</span> which do not feature in Philippine languages. Furthermore, a significant number of Eskayan words have phonemic sequences that are common in Spanish or in Spanish loans into Boholano-Visayan but appear rarely, if ever, in non-borrowed words.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-classification_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-classification-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cebuano_influences">Cebuano influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eskayan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cebuano influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Eskayan words have a one-to-one correspondence with Cebuano, so that when two words are homophones in Cebuano, they are homophones in Eskayan as well. However, the verbal morphology is quite different: Cebuano has twenty-four verbal affixes which indicate <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">grammatical aspect</a> and other feature, whereas Eskayan has just five <i>(muy-, dil-, pur-, yu-, yi-)</i>, each of which can substitute for any of the Cebuano affixes. This often makes Eskayan grammar ambiguous, and dependent on the parallel Cebuano text. In addition, some Eskayan verbs are equivalent to specific inflections of Cebuano verbs despite not having any morphology. For example, Eskayan <i>imprus</i> 'was taken on', which is basic root, translates Cebuano <i>gipuslan</i>, where <i>gi-</i> indicates that the action is <a href="/wiki/Perfective_aspect" title="Perfective aspect">completed</a> and performed on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agent_trigger" title="Agent trigger">grammatical agent</a>. This is likely because the prototype for many Eskayan words was an early English–Spanish–Visayan trilingual, with the Visayan (Cebuano) glosses crossed out and replaced with Eskayan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelly2012_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly2012-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_influences">Spanish influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eskayan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Spanish influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the Eskayan lexicon bears a marked Spanish influence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> the loan-patterns are hard to map.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-classification_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-classification-5">[5]</a></sup> Some Spanish words appear to have been directly borrowed into Eskayan with virtually no semantic or phonetic alterations. E.g., the Eskayan word <i>merido</i>, meaning ‘husband’, is evidently loaned from the Spanish <i>marido</i>, also meaning ‘husband’. Others retain only a few of the semantic properties of the original. E.g., the word <i>astro</i> means ‘sun’ in Eskayan but ‘star’ in Spanish. In some interesting cases Eskayan lexical items appear to be borrowed but are assigned new meanings entirely. E.g., the Eskayan <i>memorya</i> (‘sky’) does not coincide semantically with the Spanish <i>memoria</i> (‘memory’). One of the most intriguing examples of such an ‘interrupted loan’ is that of the Eskayan <i>tre</i> (‘two’) seemingly derived from the Spanish <i>tres</i> (‘three’). Here the semantic property of ‘number’ was retained but the actual quantity it represented was reassigned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-classification_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-classification-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories_and_controversies">Theories and controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eskayan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Theories and controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1980s and 1990s, the Eskaya community attracted the interest of local mystics who promoted the notion that their language was of exotic origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-survey_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-survey-15">[15]</a></sup> Today, the few linguists who have examined Eskayan generally concur that it is structurally Cebuano but lexically innovative, suggesting that Eskayan is an auxiliary language or a highly sophisticated form of disguised speech encoded from Cebuano.</p>
