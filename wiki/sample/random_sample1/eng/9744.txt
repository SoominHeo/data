<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_Catholicism_in_Estonia">History of Catholicism in Estonia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catholic_Church_in_Estonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of Catholicism in Estonia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the beginning of the 13th century, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> was conquered by the German <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Order" title="Teutonic Order">Teutonic Order</a> and thus was one of the last territories in Europe to be Christianized. However, some archaeological evidence suggests that Christianity was already known centuries prior to the conquest. Based on archaeological relics, such as crosses and metal book corners, some areas of Estonia were Christian prior to the 13th century.</p>
<p>The whole of Estonia was subjugated by the year 1227 and, until the mid 16th century, Estonia was divided among feudal landlords and, thus, Catholic territory, although not yet unified.</p>
<p>During the Livonian War, medieval Estonia was conquered by the Swedes, initially occupying northern Estonia and, later, the southern part. Swedish rule, from 1561–1710, banned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a> for the benefit of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> Church.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Great_Northern_War" title="Great Northern War">Great Northern War</a> Sweden lost Estonia to Russia, which governed the land from 1710 to 1918. Imperial Russia granted vast privileges to the resident Baltic-German nobility of Estonia, including freedom to practice their Lutheran faith. During the 18th century, Polish and then Lithuanian Catholic noblemen started to make their own use of this right. The first Catholic mass, after more than a hundred years, was held on 18 January 1786. There were less than 300 Catholics in Estonia at that time. <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a> began its revival. On 26 December 1845, the new Catholic Church of <a href="/wiki/Tallinn" title="Tallinn">Tallinn</a> was consecrated, followed by the new Catholic Church of <a href="/wiki/Tartu" title="Tartu">Tartu</a> in 1899.</p>
<p>In 1918, when Estonia gained independence, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonian</a> citizens had complete freedom of religion. The <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> recognized <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> on 10 October 1921. In 1931 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eduard_Profittlich&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eduard Profittlich (page does not exist)">Eduard Profittlich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">S.J.</a> became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostolic_administrator" title="Apostolic administrator">apostolic administrator</a> for the Roman Catholic Church in Estonia. In 1936 he was consecrated as the first <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonian</a> Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a> since the Lutheran reformation in the 16th century. Before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> broke out, there were almost 5,000 Catholics in Estonia (Tallinn: 2.333, Tartu: 1.073, Narva: ca. 600, Valga: ca. 800).</p>
<p>In 1939 <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> was invaded by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviets</a> and organised religion was prohibited. The majority of the 5000 Catholic faithful either escaped the country or were imprisoned in Soviet prison camps. They arrested Bishop Profittlich who subsequently died in a Soviet prison in Siberia in 1942 after being sentenced to death as a Vatican spy. His cause for canonization as a <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">Martyr</a> has been opened. During the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> occupation, all <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia's</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> churches were closed and the Catholic population of Estonia decreased below 100 adherents and 1 underground priest who were all heavily persecuted by the KGB.</p>
<p>After the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, Estonia regained its independence and was re-recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> on 28 August 1991. <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> received its first Papal visit when <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> visited the country in September, 1993.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_state_of_Catholicism_in_Estonia">Current state of Catholicism in Estonia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catholic_Church_in_Estonia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current state of Catholicism in Estonia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> population of <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> is small, however has seen a rapid increase since the end of the Soviet rule. Currently the whole country has approximately 6,000 adherents. Most are of Estonian background but also many <a href="/wiki/Lithuanians" title="Lithuanians">Lithuanians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poles" title="Poles">Poles</a>. Most live in the major towns such as <a href="/wiki/Tallinn" title="Tallinn">Tallinn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tartu" title="Tartu">Tartu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Narva" title="Narva">Narva</a>. Estonia has no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioceses" title="Dioceses">dioceses</a>. Instead, the whole country forms an <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Administration_of_Estonia" title="Apostolic Administration of Estonia">apostolic administration</a>. Since 2005, the Apostolic Administrator of Estonia who resides in Tallinn is Bishop <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Jean-Charles_Jourdan" title="Philippe Jean-Charles Jourdan">Philippe Jean-Charles Jourdan</a>.</p>
