<p><i><b>Estia</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Ἑστία</span>) is a national <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a> published daily in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>. It is generally considered a broadsheet of a conservative, right-wing political alignment, and an advocate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-market" title="Free-market">free-market</a> policies. It is named after the goddess <a href="/wiki/Hestia" title="Hestia">Hestia</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1980s and 1990s, <i>Estia</i> was the only daily written in <a href="/wiki/Katharevousa" title="Katharevousa">katharevousa</a>, a purist and archaic form of <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">modern Greek</a> that was abolished as the official administrative language in 1976. (Before 1976, the use of <a href="/wiki/Katharevousa" title="Katharevousa">katharevousa</a> was favored by the conservative and centrist press, as opposed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimotiki" title="Dimotiki">dimotiki</a>, which was favored by the leftist press.) In recent years, however, <i>Estia</i> has adopted a conservative form of Standard Modern Greek. <i>Estia</i> is also the only daily employing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polytonic_orthography" title="Polytonic orthography">polytonic system of accentuation</a>, which was officially abandoned following legislation in 1982; <i>Estia</i>, nevertheless, uses a simplified polytonic orthography in which the <a href="/wiki/Grave_accent" title="Grave accent">grave accent</a> is replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">acute</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Format_and_layout">Format and layout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Format and layout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The paper's very first edition was misprinted, with Page 1 being on the back and Page 2 on the front. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Adonis_Kyrou_I.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Adonis Kyrou I. (page does not exist)">Adonis Kyrou I.</a> decided to keep printing the paper the same way, and the tradition continued to 1997, when it was abandoned because of technical difficulties arising from the change from linotype machines to computer-editing.</p>
<p><i>Estia</i> did not switch to a modern computer system until 1997. At that time <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a>-enabled software had become more widely available and it was possible to continue printing the newspaper in the polytonic system. Until then, the newspaper continued to be set and printed using <a href="/wiki/Linotype_machine" title="Linotype machine">Linotype machines</a>. <i>Estia</i> is one of the few Greek newspapers printed in <a href="/wiki/Broadsheet" title="Broadsheet">broadsheet</a> format. It normally contains only about eight pages a day. There are no pictures on the "front" page, and no colour photographs at all.</p>
<p>The paper's most popular column has always been the <a href="/wiki/Feuilleton" title="Feuilleton">feuilleton</a> "<i>Pennies, Eidisoules, Perierga</i>" (Strokes, small news, curiosities), noted for its dry, acerbic wit.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1876, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pavlos_Diomidis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pavlos Diomidis (page does not exist)">Pavlos Diomidis</a> founded a weekly publication named <i>Ἑστία</i>, which was a literary magazine similar to the present-day <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nea_Estia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nea Estia (page does not exist)">Nea Estia</a> (<i>Νέα Ἑστία</i>) rather than a news-focused paper. Not until 1894 did the well-known poet and journalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgios_Drosinis" title="Georgios Drosinis">Georgios Drosinis</a> transform it into a daily newspaper about politics, culture and finance. In 1941, during the occupation of Greece by the German army, <i>Estia</i> closed, but soon after the liberation it resumed its publication. <i>Estia</i> has been managed by the Kyrou family for more than 120 years. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Adonis_Kyrou_(publisher)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Adonis Kyrou (publisher) (page does not exist)">Adonis Kyrou</a> was its publisher from 1898 to 1918, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Achilleus_A._Kyrou&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Achilleus A. Kyrou (page does not exist)">Achilleus A. Kyrou</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kyros_A._Kyrou&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kyros A. Kyrou (page does not exist)">Kyros A. Kyrou</a> from 1918 to 1950, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kyros_A._Kyrou&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kyros A. Kyrou (page does not exist)">Kyros A. Kyrou</a> from 1950 to 1974, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Adonis_K._Kyrou&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Adonis K. Kyrou (page does not exist)">Adonis K. Kyrou</a> from 1974 until 1997, when the paper was taken over by Kyrou's nephew <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alexis_Zaousis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alexis Zaousis (page does not exist)">Alexis Zaousis</a>.</p>
