<p><b>Esperanza Emily Spalding</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> (born October 18, 1984)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibn-110214_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ibn-110214-3">[3]</a></sup> is an American <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> bassist and singer. Spalding was raised in <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a> and was a musical <a href="/wiki/Child_prodigy" title="Child prodigy">prodigy</a>, playing violin in the Chamber Music Society of Oregon at five years old. She was later both self-taught and -trained on a number of instruments, including guitar and bass. Her proficiency earned her scholarships to <a href="/wiki/Portland_State_University" title="Portland State University">Portland State University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Berklee_College_of_Music" title="Berklee College of Music">Berklee College of Music</a>.</p>
<p>She has won four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards" title="Grammy Awards">Grammy Awards</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_New_Artist" title="Grammy Award for Best New Artist">Grammy Award for Best New Artist</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/53rd_Grammy_Awards" title="53rd Grammy Awards">53rd Grammy Awards</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2011Grammy_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-2011Grammy-4">[4]</a></sup> making her the first jazz artist to win the award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IBT_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-IBT-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esperanza_Spalding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spalding was born in <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a>, to an African-American father and a mother of <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native American</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_people" title="Hispanic people">Hispanic</a> descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> She was raised in the <a href="/wiki/King,_Portland,_Oregon" title="King, Portland, Oregon">King, Alberta neighborhood</a> in North-East Portland<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BancudM-TPT-2001-12-14_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-BancudM-TPT-2001-12-14-9">[9]</a></sup> which at that time was at its height of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gang_violence" title="Gang violence">gang violence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chris_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-chris-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Her mother raised her and her brother as a single parent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-official-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spalding has an interest in the music of other cultures, including that of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CarpenterE-TNYT-2008-07-27_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-CarpenterE-TNYT-2008-07-27-13">[13]</a></sup> commenting: "With <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">Portuguese</a> songs, the phrasing of the melody is intrinsically linked with the language, and it's beautiful."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> Spalding's mother shares her interest in music, having nearly become a touring singer herself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-official-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28-15">[15]</a></sup> Spalding's mother took note of her musical proclivity when Spalding was able to reproduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beethoven" title="Beethoven">Beethoven</a> by ear on the family's piano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chris_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-chris-10">[10]</a></sup> Spalding has credited watching classical cellist <a href="/wiki/Yo-Yo_Ma" title="Yo-Yo Ma">Yo-Yo Ma</a> perform on an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Mister_Rogers%27_Neighborhood" title="Mister Rogers' Neighborhood">Mister Rogers' Neighborhood</a></i> as an integral part of her childhood, and what inspired her to pursue music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-official-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the time Spalding was five, she had taught herself to play the violin and was playing with the Chamber Music Society of Oregon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-official-12">[12]</a></sup> Spalding stayed with the group until she was fifteen years old, and left as <a href="/wiki/Concertmaster" title="Concertmaster">concertmaster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-official-12">[12]</a></sup> Due to a lengthy childhood illness, Spalding spent much of her elementary school years being <a href="/wiki/Homeschooling" title="Homeschooling">home-schooled</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-official-12">[12]</a></sup> but also attended King Elementary School in northeast Portland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BancudM-TPT-2001-12-14_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-BancudM-TPT-2001-12-14-9">[9]</a></sup> During this time, she also found the opportunity to pick up instruction in music by listening to her mother's college teacher instructor, who instructed her mother in guitar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> According to Spalding, when she was about eight, her mother briefly studied jazz guitar in college. Spalding says: "Going with her to her class, I would sit under the piano. Then I would come home and I would be playing her stuff that her teacher had been playing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> Spalding also played <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboe</a> and clarinet before discovering the double bass in high school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-official-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leggett_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-leggett-16">[16]</a></sup> She sings in English, Spanish and Portuguese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esperanza_Spalding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000.E2.80.9303:_Early_projects_and_education">2000–03: Early projects and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esperanza_Spalding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2000–03: Early projects and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Spalding began performing live in clubs in Portland, Oregon, as a teen-ager,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CarpenterE-TNYT-2008-07-27_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-CarpenterE-TNYT-2008-07-27-13">[13]</a></sup> securing her first gig at fifteen years old in a blues club, when she could play only one line on bass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-18">[18]</a></sup> One of the seasoned musicians with which she played that first night invited her to join the band's rehearsals "so she could actually learn something," and her rehearsals soon grew into regular performances spanning almost a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-18">[18]</a></sup> According to Spalding, it was a chance for her to stretch as a musician, reaching and growing beyond her experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> Her early contact with these "phenomenal resources," as she calls the musicians who played with her,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> fostered her sense of rhythm and helped nurture her interest in her instrument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>She does not consider herself a musical prodigy, having said, "I am surrounded by prodigies everywhere I go, but because they are a little older than me, or not a female, or not on a major label, they are not acknowledged as such."</p>
<p>Spalding had intended to play cello,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> but discovered the bass during a one-year stint at fourteen years old at the performing arts high school, <a href="/wiki/The_Northwest_Academy" title="The Northwest Academy">The Northwest Academy</a>, to which she had won a scholarship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeBarrosP-TST-2008-01-15_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-DeBarrosP-TST-2008-01-15-19">[19]</a></sup> The school was not a good fit, but the bass was.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-18">[18]</a></sup> Spalding found high school "easy –  and boring" and dropped out. When she was 15 or 16 years old, she started writing lyrics for music for the local indie rock/pop group Noise for Pretend, touching on any topic that came to mind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knight-20">[20]</a></sup> Although she had taken a few private voice lessons, which taught her how to project her voice, her primary singing experience had come from "singing in the shower," she said,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Knight-20">[20]</a></sup> before she started performing vocals for Noise for Pretend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> Her desire to perform live evolved naturally out of the compositional process, when she would sing and play simultaneously to see how melody and voice fit together, but she acknowledges that performing both roles can be challenging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCOLAmoeblog_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCOLAmoeblog-21">[21]</a></sup> In a 2008 interview, she said, "[W]hat can be difficult is being a singer, in the sense that you are engaged with the audience, and really responsible for emoting, and getting into the lyrics, melody, etc., and being an effective bassist/band leader."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCOLAmoeblog_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-PCOLAmoeblog-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spalding left high school at 16, and after completing her <a href="/wiki/General_Educational_Development" title="General Educational Development">GED</a>, enrolled in a music scholarship in the music program at <a href="/wiki/Portland_State_University" title="Portland State University">Portland State University</a>, where she remembers being "the youngest bass player in the program."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-official-12">[12]</a></sup> Although she lacked the training of her fellow students, she feels that her teachers nevertheless recognized her talent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-official-12">[12]</a></sup> She decided to apply to <a href="/wiki/Berklee_College_of_Music" title="Berklee College of Music">Berklee College of Music</a> on the encouragement of her bass teacher, and did well enough in her audition to receive a full scholarship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-18">[18]</a></sup> In spite of the scholarship, Spalding found meeting living expenses a challenge, so her friends arranged a benefit concert that paid her airfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-8"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_18-6"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spalding's savings did not last long,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humprhies_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Humprhies-22">[22]</a></sup> and she considered leaving music for political science,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28-15">[15]</a></sup> a move jazz guitarist and composer <a href="/wiki/Pat_Metheny" title="Pat Metheny">Pat Metheny</a> discouraged. He told her that she had "the 'X Factor'" and could make it if she applied herself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28-15">[15]</a></sup> During her time at Berklee, her primary bass instructor was John Lockwood.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2004.E2.80.9307:_Musical_beginnings.2C_teaching_and_Junjo">2004–07: Musical beginnings, teaching and <i>Junjo</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esperanza_Spalding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2004–07: Musical beginnings, teaching and Junjo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Gary_Burton" title="Gary Burton">Gary Burton</a>, Executive Vice President at Berklee, said in 2004 that Spalding had "a great time feel, she can confidently read the most complicated compositions, and she communicates her upbeat personality in everything she plays."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_18-7"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ben Ratliff wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> on July 9, 2006, that Spalding's voice is "light and high, up in <a href="/wiki/Blossom_Dearie" title="Blossom Dearie">Blossom Dearie</a>'s pitch range, and [that] she can sing quietly, almost in a daydream" and that Spalding "invents her own feminine space, a different sound from top to bottom."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCOLBenratliff_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCOLBenratliff-23">[23]</a></sup> Spalding was the 2005 recipient of the Boston Jazz Society scholarship for outstanding musicianship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-official_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-official-12">[12]</a></sup> Almost immediately after graduation from college later the same year, Spalding was hired by Berklee College of Music, becoming one of the youngest instructors in the institution's history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-9"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> at age 20.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.27.27EarlyShow.27.27_Grooves_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-.27.27EarlyShow.27.27_Grooves-24">[24]</a></sup> As a teacher, Spalding tries to help her students focus their practice through a practice journal, which can help them recognize their strengths and what they need to pursue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-10"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Junjo" title="Junjo">Junjo</a></i>, was released on April 18, 2006, on the Ayva Music label.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-26">[26]</a></sup> It was created to display the dynamic that she felt among her trio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Knight-20">[20]</a></sup> Though <i>Junjo</i> was released solely under her name, Spalding considers it "a collaborative effort."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-11"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008.E2.80.9310:_Esperanza">2008–10: <i>Esperanza</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esperanza_Spalding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2008–10: Esperanza">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>When asked in 2008 why she plays the bass instead of some other instrument, Spalding said that it was not a choice, but the bass "had its own arc" and resonated with her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Knight-20">[20]</a></sup> Spalding has said that, for her, discovering the bass was like "waking up one day and realizing you're in love with a co-worker."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-12"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> By the time she randomly picked up the bass in music class and began experimenting with it, she had grown bored with her other instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_18-8"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-norris_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-norris-27">[27]</a></sup> Her band teacher showed her a blues line for the bass that she later used to secure her first gig.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_18-9"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-18">[18]</a></sup> After that, she went in to play the bass daily and gradually fell in love.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-13"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ratliff wrote in <i>The New York Times</i> again, two years later, on May 26, 2008, that one of Spalding's central gifts is "a light, fizzy, optimistic drive that's in her melodic bass playing and her elastic, small-voiced singing," but that "the music is missing a crucial measure of modesty."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCOLRatliff_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCOLRatliff-28">[28]</a></sup> He added: "It's an attempt at bringing this crisscrossing [of <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Shorter" title="Wayne Shorter">Wayne Shorter</a>] to a new level of definition and power, but its vamps and grooves are a little obvious, and it pushes her first as a singer-songwriter, which isn't her primary strength."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCOLRatliff_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-PCOLRatliff-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pat_Metheny" title="Pat Metheny">Pat Metheny</a> said in 2008 it was immediately obvious "that she had a lot to say and was also unlike any musician I had ever run across before. Her unique quality is something that goes beyond her pretty amazing musical skills; she has that rare 'x' factor of being able to transmit a certain personal kind of vision and energy that is all her own."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humprhies_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Humprhies-22">[22]</a></sup> Andrés Quinteros wrote in the Argentinian periodical, <i>26Noticias</i> on October 28, 2008, that Spalding is one of the greatest new talents on the jazz scene today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCOL26notcias_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCOL26notcias-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Patti_Austin" title="Patti Austin">Patti Austin</a> hired Spalding to tour with her internationally after Spalding's first semester at Berklee,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_18-10"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-18">[18]</a></sup> where Spalding supported the singer on the <a href="/wiki/Ella_Fitzgerald" title="Ella Fitzgerald">Ella Fitzgerald</a> tribute tour "For Ella."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-14"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008 Spalding recalled the tour as educational, helping her learn to accompany a vocalist and also how to sustain energy and interest playing the same material nightly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-15"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> She continued to perform with Austin periodically for three years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-16"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> During the same period, while at Berklee, Spalding studied under saxophonist <a href="/wiki/Joe_Lovano" title="Joe Lovano">Joe Lovano</a>, before eventually touring with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-17"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> They began as a trio, expanding into a quartet before joining quintet US5 and traveling across the United States from New York to California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-18"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> As of 2008, she was also in the process of developing several courses for students at Berklee, including one that focuses "on transcribing as a tool for learning harmony and theory."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-19"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> Due to touring commitments, Spalding stopped giving classes at Berklee. She lives in New York and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austin" title="Austin">Austin</a>, Texas.</p>

<p><i><a href="/wiki/Esperanza_(Esperanza_Spalding_album)" title="Esperanza (Esperanza Spalding album)">Esperanza</a></i> is Spalding's second studio album. Being exposed to many different cultural impressions while growing up, Spalding sings in three languages: English, Spanish and Portuguese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>After Spalding's Grammy win in February 2011, the album entered the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> at 138. With <i>Esperanza</i>, Spalding's material was meant to be more reflective of herself as an artist, with musicians selected to best present that material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knight_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-Knight-20">[20]</a></sup> Ed Morales wrote in <a href="/wiki/PopMatters" title="PopMatters">PopMatters</a> on June 23, 2008, that <i>Esperanza</i> is "a sprawling collage of jazz fusion, Brazilian, and even a touch of hip-hop."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCOLPopmatters_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCOLPopmatters-31">[31]</a></sup> Siddhartha Mitter wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i> on May 23, 2008, that "the big change" in <i>Esperanza</i> "is the singing.... This makes it a much more accessible album, and in some ways more conventional."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCOLBostonglobe_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCOLBostonglobe-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 10 at the 2009 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> ceremonies, Spalding performed at <a href="/wiki/Oslo_City_Hall" title="Oslo City Hall">Oslo City Hall</a> in honor of the 2009 Laureate U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>, and again at the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize_Concert" title="Nobel Peace Prize Concert">Nobel Peace Prize Concert</a> the following day. She was personally selected by Obama, as per the tradition of one laureate-invited-artist to perform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-surprise_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-surprise-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spalding also was the featured final act for the opening night of the 2009 <a href="/wiki/Park_City_Jazz_Festival" title="Park City Jazz Festival">Park City Jazz Festival</a> in <a href="/wiki/Park_City,_Utah" title="Park City, Utah">Park City</a>, Utah. She closed the show with a number along with bass artists <a href="/wiki/Brian_Bromberg" title="Brian Bromberg">Brian Bromberg</a> and Sean O'Bryan Smith, who also performed earlier that day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> As a tribute to <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a>, Spalding was invited to sing along with <a href="/wiki/Patti_LaBelle" title="Patti LaBelle">Patti LaBelle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alicia_Keys" title="Alicia Keys">Alicia Keys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Janelle_Mon%C3%A1e" title="Janelle Monáe">Janelle Monáe</a>. Spalding performed the 1987 hit single "<a href="/wiki/If_I_Was_Your_Girlfriend" title="If I Was Your Girlfriend">If I Was Your Girlfriend</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-surprise_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-surprise-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 7, 2010, Spalding became the most searched person and second most searched item on <a href="/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google Search</a> as a result of her appearance the previous evening on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Broadcasting_Service" title="Public Broadcasting Service">PBS</a> television program <i><a href="/wiki/Austin_City_Limits" title="Austin City Limits">Austin City Limits</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011.E2.80.93present:_Chamber_Music_Society_and_Radio_Music_Society">2011–present: <i>Chamber Music Society</i> and <i>Radio Music Society</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esperanza_Spalding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2011–present: Chamber Music Society and Radio Music Society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2011, Spalding won "Jazz Artist of the Year" at the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Music_Awards" title="Boston Music Awards">Boston Music Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spalding collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Tineke_Postma" title="Tineke Postma">Tineke Postma</a> on the track '"Leave Me a Place Underground" from the album <i>The Dawn of Light</i> in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheDawnOfLight_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-TheDawnOfLight-39">[39]</a></sup> She also collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Terri_Lyne_Carrington" title="Terri Lyne Carrington">Terri Lyne Carrington</a> on the album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mosaic_Project_(album)" title="The Mosaic Project (album)">The Mosaic Project</a></i>, where she features on the track "Crayola".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Spalding also sang a duet with <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Payton" title="Nicholas Payton">Nicholas Payton</a> on the track "Freesia" from the 2011 album <i>Bitches of Renaissance</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 53rd <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011_Grammy_Awards" title="2011 Grammy Awards">2011 Grammy Awards</a>, Spalding won the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_New_Artist" title="Grammy Award for Best New Artist">Grammy Award for Best New Artist</a>, beating <a href="/wiki/Justin_Bieber" title="Justin Bieber">Justin Bieber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florence_and_the_Machine" title="Florence and the Machine">Florence and the Machine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mumford_and_Sons" title="Mumford and Sons">Mumford and Sons</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drake_(entertainer)" title="Drake (entertainer)">Drake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrammyWin_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-GrammyWin-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belieber" title="Belieber">Bieber's fans</a> targeted Spalding on the Internet, stating that she was not as popular as Bieber and that he should have won the award. They also vandalized her Wikipedia article.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Chamber_Music_Society" title="Chamber Music Society">Chamber Music Society</a></i> is the third album by Spalding. After her surprise Grammy win, the album re-entered the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> at number 34 with sales of 18,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> A video was made for the song "Little Fly".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The song is a poem by <a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">William Blake</a> set to music by Spalding. A vinyl version of the album was released in February 2011. Commenting on the album, <a href="/wiki/NPR_Music" title="NPR Music">NPR Music</a>'s Patrick Jarenwattananon wrote that, "the finished product certainly exudes a level of sophisticated intimacy, as if best experienced with a small gathering in a quiet, wood-paneled room."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCOLNPR_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCOLNPR-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spalding was the best-selling contemporary jazz artist of 2011, and her album <i>Chamber Music Society</i> was the best-selling contemporary jazz album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> On February 26, 2012, Spalding performed at the <a href="/wiki/84th_Academy_Awards" title="84th Academy Awards">84th Academy Awards</a>, singing the <a href="/wiki/Louis_Armstrong" title="Louis Armstrong">Louis Armstrong</a> standard <i><a href="/wiki/What_a_Wonderful_World" title="What a Wonderful World">What a Wonderful World</a></i>, alongside the Southern California Children's Chorus to accompany the video montage that celebrated the film industry greats who died in 2011 and early 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Radio_Music_Society" title="Radio Music Society">Radio Music Society</a></i> is Spalding's fourth studio album, released by record label Heads Up International on March 20, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Spalding hoped this album would showcase jazz musicians in an accessible manner suitable for mainstream radio,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GrammyWin_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-GrammyWin-43">[43]</a></sup> while incorporating her own musical compositions with covers of such artists as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beach_Boys" title="Beach Boys">Beach Boys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Shorter" title="Wayne Shorter">Wayne Shorter</a>.</p>
<p>Spalding also made guest appearances during this time, appearing on <a href="/wiki/Janelle_Mon%C3%A1e" title="Janelle Monáe">Janelle Monáe</a>'s 2013 album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Electric_Lady" title="The Electric Lady">The Electric Lady</a></i>, on the track "Dorothy Dandridge Eyes". She also sang a jazz duet on <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Mars" title="Bruno Mars">Bruno Mars</a>' album, <i><a href="/wiki/Unorthodox_Jukebox" title="Unorthodox Jukebox">Unorthodox Jukebox</a></i>, called "Old &amp; Crazy". In November 2013, Spalding released a single "We Are America" to protest the <a href="/wiki/Guant%C3%A1namo" title="Guantánamo">Guantánamo</a> prison camps, with cameo performances by Stevie Wonder and <a href="/wiki/Harry_Belafonte" title="Harry Belafonte">Harry Belafonte</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2016, she released her fifth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Emily%27s_D%2BEvolution" title="Emily's D+Evolution">Emily's D+Evolution</a></i>, released by Concord Records. She was also featured on the <a href="/wiki/Nova_(TV_series)" title="Nova (TV series)">NOVA</a> production <i>The Great Math Mystery</i>, talking about the connection between music and mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artistry">Artistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esperanza_Spalding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Artistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esperanza_Spalding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Spalding was mentored by <a href="/wiki/Thara_Memory" title="Thara Memory">Thara Memory</a>. She has cited jazz bassists <a href="/wiki/Ron_Carter" title="Ron Carter">Ron Carter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dave_Holland" title="Dave Holland">Dave Holland</a> as important influences on her music—Carter for the orchestration of his playing and Holland for the way his compositional method complements his personal style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth0612_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Booth0612-56">[56]</a></sup> She has described the saxophone player <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Shorter" title="Wayne Shorter">Wayne Shorter</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28-15">[15]</a></sup> and singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Milton_Nascimento" title="Milton Nascimento">Milton Nascimento</a>, as heroes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> She has also noted her preference for the <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Brazil" title="Music of Brazil">music of Brazil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Spalding has said she loves <a href="/wiki/Fusion_(music)" title="Fusion (music)">fusion music</a> and was influenced by a "wonderful arc that started 40 years ago where people kept incorporating modern sounds into their music."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCOLPopmatters_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-PCOLPopmatters-31">[31]</a></sup> Spalding, who has expressed a desire to be judged for her musicianship rather than sex appeal, believes that female musicians must take responsibility to avoid oversexualizing themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28_15-6"><a href="#cite_note-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCOLPopmatters_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-PCOLPopmatters-31">[31]</a></sup> And, to write original music, one must read and stay informed about the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28_15-7"><a href="#cite_note-PenaT-ES-JM-2008-05-28-15">[15]</a></sup> She has said she models her career on those of <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ornette_Coleman" title="Ornette Coleman">Ornette Coleman</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Booth_14-20"><a href="#cite_note-Booth-14">[14]</a></sup> and also cited <a href="/wiki/Joni_Mitchell" title="Joni Mitchell">Joni Mitchell</a> as a major musical inspiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Charity_work">Charity work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Esperanza_Spalding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Charity work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During her 2012 tour, Spalding donated a portion of proceeds from merchandise sales to the non-profit organization <a href="/wiki/Free_the_Slaves" title="Free the Slaves">Free the Slaves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The organization, based in Washington, D.C., works to combat <a href="/wiki/Human_trafficking" title="Human trafficking">human trafficking</a> around the world.</p>
