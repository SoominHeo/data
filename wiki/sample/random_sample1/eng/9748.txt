<p>The <a href="/wiki/Constructed_language" title="Constructed language">constructed</a> <a href="/wiki/International_auxiliary_language" title="International auxiliary language">international auxiliary language</a> <b><a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">Esperanto</a></b> was developed in the 1870s and 80s by <a href="/wiki/L._L._Zamenhof" title="L. L. Zamenhof">L. L. Zamenhof</a>, and first published in 1887. The number of speakers has grown gradually over time, although it has not had much support from governments and international bodies, and has sometimes been outlawed or otherwise suppressed.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standardized_Yiddish">Standardized Yiddish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Esperanto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Standardized Yiddish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Around 1880, while in Moscow and approximately simultaneously with working on Esperanto, Zamenhof made an aborted attempt to standardize <a href="/wiki/Yiddish" title="Yiddish">Yiddish</a>, based on his native Bialystok (Northeastern) dialect, as a unifying language for the Jews of the Russian Empire. He even used a Latin alphabet, with the letters <i>ć, h́, ś, ź</i> (the same as in early drafts of Esperanto, later <i>ĉ, ĥ, ŝ, ĵ</i>) and <i>ě</i> for <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">schwa</a>. However, he concluded there was no future for such a project, and abandoned it, dedicating himself to Esperanto as a unifying language for all humankind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Wexler_(linguist)" title="Paul Wexler (linguist)">Paul Wexler</a> proposed that Esperanto was not an arbitrary pastiche of major European languages but a Latinate <a href="/wiki/Relexification" title="Relexification">relexification</a> of Yiddish, a native language of its founder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This model is generally unsupported by mainstream linguists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spolsky_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spolsky-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_the_language_before_publication">Development of the language before publication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Esperanto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development of the language before publication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Proto-Esperanto" title="Proto-Esperanto">Proto-Esperanto</a>.</div>
<p>Zamenhof would later say that he had dreamed of a world language since he was a child. At first he considered a revival of <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, but after learning it in school he decided it was too complicated to be a common means of international communication. When he learned English, he realised that <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">verb conjugations</a> were unnecessary, and that grammatical systems could be much simpler than he had expected. He still had the problem of memorising a large vocabulary, until he noticed two Russian signs labelled <b>Швейцарская</b> (<i>švejtsarskaja,</i> a porter's lodge – from <b>швейцар</b> <i>švejtsar,</i> a porter) and <b>Кондитерская</b> (<i>konditerskaja,</i> a confectioner's shop – from <b>кондитер</b> <i>konditer,</i> a confectioner). He then realised that a judicious use of <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">affixes</a> could greatly decrease the number of root words needed for communication. He chose to take his vocabulary from <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a>, the languages that were most widely taught in schools around the world and would therefore be recognisable to the largest number of people.</p>
<p>Zamenhof taught an <a href="/wiki/Proto-Esperanto" title="Proto-Esperanto">early version of the language</a> to his high-school classmates. Then, for several years, he worked on translations and poetry to refine his creation. In 1895 he wrote, "I worked for six years perfecting and testing the language, even though it had seemed to me in 1878 that it was already completely ready." When he was ready to publish, the Czarist censors would not allow it. Stymied, he spent his time in translating works such as the Bible and <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>. This enforced delay led to continued improvement. In July 1887 he published his <i><a href="/wiki/Unua_Libro" title="Unua Libro">Unua Libro</a></i> (First Book), a basic introduction to the language. This was essentially the language spoken today.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Esperanto_history_from_publication_until_the_first_world_congress">Esperanto history from publication until the first world congress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Esperanto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Esperanto history from publication until the first world congress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At first the movement grew most in the Russian empire and eastern Europe, but soon spread to western Europe and beyond: to Argentina in 1889; to Canada in 1901; to Algeria, Chile, Japan, Mexico, and Peru in 1903; to Tunisia in 1904; and to Australia, the United States, Guinea, <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a>, New Zealand, <a href="/wiki/Tonkin" title="Tonkin">Tonkin</a>, and Uruguay in 1905.</p>
<p>In its first years Esperanto was used mainly in publications by Zamenhof and early adopters like <a href="/wiki/Antoni_Grabowski" title="Antoni Grabowski">Antoni Grabowski</a>, in extensive correspondence (mostly now lost), in the magazine <i>La Esperantisto</i>, published from 1889 to 1895 and only occasionally in personal encounters.</p>
<p>In 1894 under pressure from Wilhelm Trompeter, the publisher of the magazine <i>La Esperantisto</i>, and some other leading users, Zamenhof reluctantly put forward a radical reform to be voted on by readers. He proposed the reduction of the alphabet to 22 letters (by eliminating the accented letters and most of their sounds), the change of the plural to <i>-i</i>, the use of a positional accusative instead of the ending <i>-n</i>, the removal of the distinction between adjectives and adverbs, the reduction of the number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Participles" title="Participles">participles</a> from six to two, and the replacement of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Table_of_correlatives_(Esperanto)" title="Table of correlatives (Esperanto)">table of correlatives</a> with more Latinate words or phrases. These reforms were overwhelmingly rejected, but some were picked up in <a href="/wiki/Esperantido" title="Esperantido">subsequent reforms</a> (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ido_(language)" title="Ido (language)">Ido</a>) and criticisms of the language. In the following decade Esperanto spread into western Europe, especially France. By 1905 there were already 27 magazines being published (<a href="#References">Auld 1988</a>).</p>
<p>A small international conference was held in 1904, leading to the first world congress in August 1905 in <a href="/wiki/Boulogne-sur-Mer" title="Boulogne-sur-Mer">Boulogne-sur-Mer</a>, France. There were 688 Esperanto speakers present from 20 nationalities. At this congress, Zamenhof officially resigned his leadership of the Esperanto movement, as he did not want personal prejudice against himself (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-Semitism" title="Anti-Semitism">anti-Semitism</a>) to hinder the progress of the language. He proposed a <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Boulogne" title="Declaration of Boulogne">declaration</a> on founding principles of the Esperanto movement, which the attendees of the congress endorsed.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Esperanto_history_since_the_first_congress">Esperanto history since the first congress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Esperanto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Esperanto history since the first congress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>World congresses have been held every year since 1905, except during the two World Wars.</p>
<p>The autonomous territory of <a href="/wiki/Neutral_Moresnet" title="Neutral Moresnet">Neutral Moresnet</a>, between Belgium and Germany, had a sizable proportion of Esperanto-speakers among its small and multiethnic population. There was a proposal to make Esperanto its official language.</p>
<p>In the early 1920s, there was a proposal for the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> to accept Esperanto as their working language. Ten delegates accepted the proposal with only one voice against, the French delegate, <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Hanotaux" title="Gabriel Hanotaux">Gabriel Hanotaux</a>. Hanotaux did not like how the French language was losing its position as the international language and saw Esperanto as a threat. However, two years later the League recommended that its member states include Esperanto in their educational curricula. Many people see the 1920s as the heyday of the Esperanto movement.</p>
<p>In 1941, the Soviet Union started performing mass arrests, deportations, and killings of many Esperantists and their relatives for fear of an anti-nationalistic movement, but it was interrupted by the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa" title="Operation Barbarossa">Nazi invasion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hitler wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/Mein_Kampf" title="Mein Kampf">Mein Kampf</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> that it was created as a universal language to unite the Jewish <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diaspora</a>. The creation of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judenrein" title="Judenrein">Jew-free</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_German_Esperanto_League&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National German Esperanto League (page does not exist)">National German Esperanto League</a> was not enough to placate the Nazis. The teaching of Esperanto was not allowed in <a href="/wiki/List_of_prisoner-of-war_camps_in_Germany" title="List of prisoner-of-war camps in Germany">German prisoner-of-war camps</a> during World War II. Esperantists sometimes were able to get around the ban by convincing guards that they were teaching Italian, the language of Germany's closest ally.</p>

<p>In the early years of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, Esperanto was given a measure of government support, and an officially recognized Soviet Esperanto Association came into being.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harlow_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harlow-6">[6]</a></sup> However, in 1937, Stalin reversed this policy. He denounced Esperanto as "the language of spies" and had Esperantists exiled or executed. The use of Esperanto was effectively banned until 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harlow_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harlow-6">[6]</a></sup> While Esperanto itself was not enough cause for execution, its use was extended among Jews or trade unionists and encouraged contacts with foreigners.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861%E2%80%931946)#Fascism" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861–1946)">Fascist Italy</a>, on the other hand, made some efforts of promoting <a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_Italy" title="Tourism in Italy">tourism in Italy</a> through Esperanto leaflets and appreciated the similarities of Italian and Esperanto.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal)" title="Estado Novo (Portugal)">Portugal's right-wing governments</a> cracked down on the language from 1936 until the <a href="/wiki/Carnation_Revolution" title="Carnation Revolution">Carnation Revolution</a> of 1974.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> After the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francoist_Spain" title="Francoist Spain">Francoist Spain</a> cracked down on the <a href="/wiki/Anarchism_in_Spain" title="Anarchism in Spain">Anarchists</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalan_nationalist" title="Catalan nationalist">Catalan nationalists</a> among whom the speaking of Esperanto had been quite widespread; but in the 1950s, the Esperanto movement was tolerated again,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Del_Barrio_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Del_Barrio-7">[7]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Francisco Franco</a> accepting the honorary patronage of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Madrid_World_Esperanto_Congress&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Madrid World Esperanto Congress (page does not exist)">Madrid World Esperanto Congress</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, especially in the 1950s and 1960s, put a damper on the Esperanto movement as well, as there were fears on both sides that Esperanto could be used for enemy propaganda. However, the language experienced something of a renaissance in the 1970s and spread to new parts of the world, such as its veritable explosion in popularity in Iran in 1975. By 1991 there were enough African Esperantists to warrant a pan-African congress. The language continues to spread, although it is not officially recognised by any country, and is part of the state educational curriculum of only a few.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_of_the_language">Evolution of the language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Esperanto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Evolution of the language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Boulogne" title="Declaration of Boulogne">Declaration of Boulogne</a> <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.webcitation.org/query?url=http://www.geocities.com/Athens/Delphi/9061/bulonja.html&amp;date=2009-10-25+05:59:00" rel="nofollow">[1]</a> of 1905 limited changes to Esperanto. That declaration stated, among other things, that the basis of the language should remain the <i>Fundamento de Esperanto</i> ("Foundation of Esperanto", a group of early works by Zamenhof), which is to be binding forever: nobody has the right to make changes to it. The declaration also permits new concepts to be expressed as the speaker sees fit, but it recommends doing so in accordance with the original style.</p>
<p>Many Esperantists believe this declaration stabilising the language is a major reason why the Esperanto speaker community grew beyond the levels attained by other constructed languages and has developed a flourishing culture. Other constructed languages have been hindered from developing a stable speaking community by continual tinkering. Also, many developers of constructed languages have been possessive of their creation and have worked to prevent others from contributing to the language. One such ultimately disastrous case was <a href="/wiki/Johann_Martin_Schleyer" title="Johann Martin Schleyer">Schleyer</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Volap%C3%BCk" title="Volapük">Volapük</a>. In contrast, Zamenhof declared that "Esperanto belongs to the Esperantists", and moved to the background once the language was published, allowing others to share in the early development of the language.</p>
<p>The grammatical description in the earliest books was somewhat vague, so a consensus on usage (influenced by Zamenhof's answers to some questions) developed over time within boundaries set by the initial outline (<a href="#References">Auld 1988</a>). Even before the Declaration of Boulogne, the language was remarkably stable; only one set of lexical changes were made in the first year after publication, namely changing "when", "then", "never", "sometimes", "always" from <i>kian</i>, <i>tian</i>, <i>nenian</i>, <i>ian</i>, <i>ĉian</i> to <i>kiam</i>, <i>tiam</i>, <i>neniam</i> etc., to avoid confusion with the accusative forms of <i>kia</i> "what sort of", <i>tia</i> "that sort of", etc. Thus Esperanto achieved a stability of structure and grammar similar to that which natural languages enjoy by virtue of their native speakers and established bodies of literature. One could learn Esperanto without having it move from underfoot. Changes could and did occur in the language, but only by acquiring widespread popular support; there was no central authority making arbitrary changes, as happened with <a href="/wiki/Volap%C3%BCk" title="Volapük">Volapük</a> and some other languages.</p>
<p>Modern Esperanto usage may in fact depart from that originally described in the <i>Fundamento</i>, though the differences are largely semantic (involving changed meaning of words) rather than grammatical or phonological. The translation given for "I like this one", in the <a href="/wiki/Esperanto#Examples" title="Esperanto">sample phrases</a> in the main <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">Esperanto</a> article, offers a significant example. According to the <i>Fundamento</i>, <i>Mi ŝatas ĉi tiun</i> would in fact have meant "I esteem this one". The traditional usage is <i>Tiu ĉi plaĉas al mi</i> (literally, "this one is pleasing to me"), which reflects the phrasing of most European languages (French <i>celui-ci me plaît</i>, Spanish <i>éste me gusta</i>, Russian <i>это мне нравится</i> [eto mnye nravitsya], German <i>Das gefällt mir</i>, Italian <i>mi piace</i>). However, the original <i>Ĉi tiu plaĉas al mi</i> continues to be commonly used.</p>
<p>For later changes to the language, see <a href="/wiki/Modern_evolution_of_Esperanto" title="Modern evolution of Esperanto">Modern evolution of Esperanto</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects.2C_reform_projects_and_derived_languages">Dialects, reform projects and derived languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Esperanto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dialects, reform projects and derived languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dd><i>See also <a href="/wiki/Esperantido" title="Esperantido">Esperantido</a></i></dd>
</dl>
<p>Esperanto has not fragmented into regional <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a> through natural language use. This may be because it is the language of daily communication for only a small minority of its speakers. However at least three other factors work against dialects, namely the centripetal force of the Fundamento, the unifying influence of the Plena Vortaro and its successors, which exemplified usage from the works of Zamenhof and leading writers, and the transnational ambitions of the speech community itself. Slang and <a href="/wiki/Jargon" title="Jargon">jargon</a> have developed to some extent, but such features interfere with universal communication – the whole point of Esperanto – and so have generally been avoided.</p>
<p>However, in the early twentieth century numerous reform projects were proposed. Almost all of these "<a href="/wiki/Esperantido" title="Esperantido">esperantido</a>"s were stillborn, but the very first, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ido_(language)" title="Ido (language)">Ido</a> ("Offspring"), had significant success for several years. Ido was proposed by the <i><a href="/wiki/Delegation_for_the_Adoption_of_an_International_Auxiliary_Language" title="Delegation for the Adoption of an International Auxiliary Language">Delegation for the Adoption of an International Auxiliary Language</a></i> in Paris in October 1907. Its main reforms were in bringing the <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantics</a>, and some <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammatical</a> features into closer alignment with the <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a>, as well as removal of adjectival agreement and the accusative case except when necessary. At first a number of leading Esperantists put their support behind the Ido project, but the movement stagnated and declined, first with the accidental death of one of its main proponents and later as people proposed further changes, and the number of current speakers is estimated at between 250 and 5000. However, Ido has proven to be a rich source of Esperanto vocabulary.</p>
<p>Some more focused reform projects, affecting only a particular feature of the language, have gained a few adherents. One of these is "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riism" title="Riism">riism</a>", which modifies the language to incorporate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-sexist_language" title="Non-sexist language">non-sexist language</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender-neutral_pronoun" title="Gender-neutral pronoun">gender-neutral pronouns</a>. However, most of these projects are specific to individual nationalities (riism from English speakers, for example), and the only changes that have gained acceptance in the Esperanto community have been the minor and gradual bottom-up reforms discussed in the last section.</p>
<p>Esperanto is credited with influencing or inspiring several later competing language projects, such as <a href="/wiki/Occidental_language" title="Occidental language">Occidental</a> (1922) and <a href="/wiki/Novial" title="Novial">Novial</a> (1928). These always lagged far behind Esperanto in their popularity. By contrast, <a href="/wiki/Interlingua" title="Interlingua">Interlingua</a> (1951) has greatly surpassed Ido in terms of popularity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It shows little or no Esperanto influence, however.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeline_of_Esperanto">Timeline of Esperanto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_Esperanto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Timeline of Esperanto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>1859: <a href="/wiki/L._L._Zamenhof" title="L. L. Zamenhof">Lazar Zamenhof</a>, the creator of Esperanto, is born in <a href="/wiki/Bia%C5%82ystok" title="Białystok">Białystok</a>, Russia (now Poland).</li>
<li>1873: The Zamenhof family moves to <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>.</li>
<li>1878: Zamenhof celebrates the completion of his universal language project, <i><a href="/wiki/Proto-Esperanto#The_Lingwe_uniwersala_of_1878" title="Proto-Esperanto">Lingwe Uniwersala</a></i>, with high-school friends.</li>
<li>1879: Zamenhof attends medical school in Moscow. His father burns his language project while he's away. Meanwhile <a href="/wiki/Johann_Martin_Schleyer" title="Johann Martin Schleyer">Schleyer</a> publishes a sketch of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volap%C3%BCk_language" title="Volapük language">Volapük</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Constructed_language" title="Constructed language">constructed international auxiliary language</a> to acquire a number of speakers. Many Volapük clubs will later switch to Esperanto.</li>
<li>1881: Zamenhof returns to Warsaw to continue medical school, and starts to recreate his project.</li>
<li>1887: Zamenhof marries, and with his wife's help publishes <i><a href="/wiki/Unua_Libro" title="Unua Libro">Unua Libro</a></i>, the book introducing modern Esperanto.</li>
<li>1888: <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">Leo Tolstoy</a> becomes an early supporter.</li>
<li>1894: Zamenhof, reacting to pressure, puts a radical reform to a vote, but it is overwhelmingly rejected.</li>
<li>1901: Zamenhof publishes <a href="/wiki/Homaranismo" title="Homaranismo">his ideas on a universal religion</a>, based on the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Hillel_the_Elder" title="Hillel the Elder">Hillel the Elder</a>.</li>
<li>1905: The first <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Congress_of_Esperanto" title="World Congress of Esperanto">Universala Kongreso</a></i> (World Congress) is held in <a href="/wiki/Boulogne-sur-Mer" title="Boulogne-sur-Mer">Boulogne-sur-Mer</a>, with 688 participants and conducted entirely in Esperanto. The <i><a href="/wiki/Fundamento_de_Esperanto" title="Fundamento de Esperanto">Fundamento de Esperanto</a></i> is published.</li>
<li>1907: Twelve members of the British parliament nominate Zamenhof for the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a>. The <i>Ĉekbanko Esperantista</i> (Esperantist Checking Bank) is founded in London, using the <i><a href="/wiki/Spesmilo" title="Spesmilo">spesmilo</a></i>, an auxiliary Esperanto currency based on the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a>. A committee organised by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Couturat" title="Louis Couturat">Louis Couturat</a> in Paris proposes the Ido reform project, which provides significant competition for Esperanto until the First World War.</li>
<li>1908: <i><a href="/wiki/Universal_Esperanto_Association" title="Universal Esperanto Association">Universala Esperanto-Asocio</a></i>, the Universal Esperanto Association, is founded by <a href="/wiki/Hector_Hodler" title="Hector Hodler">Hector Hodler</a>, a 19-year-old Swiss Esperantist.</li>
<li>1909: The <i>International Association of Esperantist Railway Workers</i> is founded in <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a>.</li>
<li>1910: 42 members of the French parliament nominate Zamenhof for the Nobel Peace Prize.</li>
<li>1917: Zamenhof dies during World War I.</li>
<li>1910s: Esperanto is taught in state schools in the Republic of China, <a href="/wiki/Samos" title="Samos">Samos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(region)" title="Macedonia (region)">Macedonia</a>. (Today it is part of the curriculum of China, Hungary, and Bulgaria.)</li>
<li>1920: The first Esperanto magazine for the blind, <i>Aŭroro</i>, begins publishing in <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a>. It's still in print today.</li>
<li>1921: The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_des_Sciences" title="Académie des Sciences">French Academy of the Sciences</a> recommends using Esperanto for international scientific communication.</li>
<li>1922: Esperanto is banned from French schools. The French delegate to the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> vetoes the use of Esperanto as its working language, leaving English and French.</li>
<li>1924: The <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> recommends that member states implement Esperanto as an auxiliary language.</li>
<li>1920s: Offices of the Brazilian Ministry of Education use Esperanto for their international correspondence. <a href="/wiki/Lu_Xun" title="Lu Xun">Lu Xun</a>, the founder of modern Chinese literature, becomes a supporter of Esperanto. <a href="/wiki/Montagu_C._Butler" title="Montagu C. Butler">Montagu C. Butler</a> is the first to raise Esperanto-speaking children.</li>
<li>1933/34: Reorganisation of the international (neutral) Esperanto movement, under the name UEA.</li>
<li>1934: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encyclopedia_of_Esperanto" title="Encyclopedia of Esperanto">Encyclopedia of Esperanto</a> first published in <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>.</li>
<li>1935: <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1lm%C3%A1n_Kalocsay" title="Kálmán Kalocsay">Kalocsay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaston_Waringhien" title="Gaston Waringhien">Waringhien</a> publish the influential <i>Plena Gramatiko de Esperanto</i> (Complete Grammar of Esperanto).</li>
<li>1936: All Esperanto organisations in <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> prohibited.</li>
<li>1937: Leaders of the Esperanto organisation in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> arrested; Esperanto activities made impossible.</li>
<li>1938: The World Esperanto Youth Organisation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TEJO" title="TEJO">TEJO</a> is founded.</li>
<li>1939–1945: In World War II many countries are occupied by Germany and the Soviet Union, where Esperanto organisations often were prohibited or Esperanto activities were limited in other ways.</li>
<li>1948: The railway workers' association is refounded as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=IFEF&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="IFEF (page does not exist)">IFEF</a>, the <i>Internacia Fervojista Esperanto-Federacio</i> (International Railway Workers' Esperanto Federation) to foster the use of Esperanto in the administration of the railroads of the world (so far, of Eurasia).</li>
<li>1954: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Educational,_Scientific_and_Cultural_Organization" title="United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization">UNESCO</a> establishes consultative relations with the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Esperanto_Association" title="Universal Esperanto Association">Universal Esperanto Association</a>.</li>
<li>1966: The precursor to <i><a href="/wiki/Pasporta_Servo" title="Pasporta Servo">Pasporta Servo</a></i> is launched in Argentina. <i>Pasporta Servo</i> is a global network of Esperanto speakers who host Esperantists traveling through their countries.</li>
<li>1967: <a href="/wiki/Istv%C3%A1n_Nemere" title="István Nemere">István Nemere</a> founds the <i>Renkontiĝo de Esperanto-Familioj</i>, the first organisation for Esperanto-speaking families.</li>
<li>1975: The Esperanto movement spreads to Iran, with three thousand learning the language in Tehran.</li>
<li>1980: The <i>Internacia Junulara Kongreso</i> (International Youth Congress) in <a href="/wiki/Rauma,_Finland" title="Rauma, Finland">Rauma, Finland</a> makes explicit the view of many in the Esperanto movement that Esperanto is a goal in itself.</li>
<li>1985: UNESCO encourages UN member states to add Esperanto to their school curricula.</li>
<li>1987: 6000 Esperantists attend the 72nd <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Congress_of_Esperanto" title="World Congress of Esperanto">Universala Kongreso</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a> to mark Esperanto's centennial.</li>
<li>1991: The first pan-African Esperanto Conference is held in <a href="/wiki/Lom%C3%A9" title="Lomé">Lomé</a>, Togo.</li>
<li>1992: PEN International accepts an Esperanto section.</li>
<li>1999: The Esperanto poet <a href="/wiki/William_Auld" title="William Auld">William Auld</a> is nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a>.</li>
<li>2001: The <a class="extiw" href="https://eo.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vikipedio:%C4%88efpa%C4%9Do" title="eo:Vikipedio:Ĉefpaĝo">Vikipedio</a> project (<a href="/wiki/Esperanto_Wikipedia" title="Esperanto Wikipedia">Esperanto Wikipedia</a>) is launched, resulting in the first general encyclopedia written in a constructed language. It is now one of the most popular websites in Esperanto.</li>
<li>2004: The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europe_-_Democracy_-_Esperanto" title="Europe - Democracy - Esperanto">Europe - Democracy - Esperanto</a> party (E°D°E°) contests the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2004" title="European Parliament election, 2004">European Parliament elections</a> in France, on a platform of making Esperanto the second language of all EU member states, taking 0.15% of the vote.</li>
<li>2007: The State of Israel issues a stamp to commemorate 120 years of Esperanto (1887–2007). An image of Zamenhof is designed in a text describing his life, reproduced from the Wikipedia article on Esperanto. The corner of the tab shows the flag of the Esperanto movement.</li>
<li>2009: The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senate_of_Brazil" title="Senate of Brazil">Senate of Brazil</a> passed a bill which would make Esperanto an optional part of the curriculum in its <a href="/wiki/State_school" title="State school">state schools</a>. As of 2010 the bill has not yet been passed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Deputies_of_Brazil" title="Chamber of Deputies of Brazil">Chamber of Deputies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>2015: The 100th <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Congress_of_Esperanto" title="World Congress of Esperanto">Universala Kongreso</a></i> (World Congress) is held in Lille, France.</li>
</ul>
