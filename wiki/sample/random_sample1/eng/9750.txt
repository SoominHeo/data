<p><b>Enki</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɛ/ short 'e' in 'bed'">ɛ</span><span title="/ŋ/ 'ng' in 'sing'">ŋ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_language" title="Sumerian language">Sumerian</a>: <span lang="sux-Latn" xml:lang="sux-Latn"><sup><a href="/wiki/Dingir" title="Dingir">d</a></sup>EN.KI(G)</span><span lang="und-Xsux" style="font-size:125%;font-family:'Segoe UI Historic','Akkadian','Noto Sans Cuneiform','Noto Sans Sumero-Akkadian Cuneiform';" title="cuneiform text" xml:lang="und-Xsux">𒂗𒆠</span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Dingir" title="Dingir">god</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumerian_mythology" title="Sumerian mythology">Sumerian mythology</a>, later known as <b>Ea</b> in Akkadian and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonian_mythology" title="Babylonian mythology">Babylonian mythology</a>. He was originally patron god of the city of <a href="/wiki/Eridu" title="Eridu">Eridu</a>, but later the influence of his cult spread throughout <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> and to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canaanites" title="Canaanites">Canaanites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittites</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurrian" title="Hurrian">Hurrians</a>. He was the <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crafts" title="Crafts">crafts</a> (<i>gašam</i>); mischief; water, <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakewater</a> (<i>a</i>, <i>aba</i>, <i>ab</i>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intelligence_(trait)" title="Intelligence (trait)">intelligence</a> (<i>gestú</i>, literally "ear") and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creation_theory" title="Creation theory">creation</a> (<i>Nudimmud</i>: <i>nu</i>, likeness, <i>dim mud</i>, make beer). He was associated with the southern band of constellations called <i>stars of Ea</i>, but also with the constellation <b>AŠ-IKU</b>, <i>the Field</i> (<a href="/wiki/Pegasus_(constellation)" title="Pegasus (constellation)">Square of Pegasus</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Beginning around the second millennium BCE, he was sometimes referred to in writing by the numeric ideogram for "40," occasionally referred to as his "sacred number."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The planet Mercury, associated with Babylonian <i><a href="/wiki/Nabu" title="Nabu">Nabu</a></i> (the son of <a href="/wiki/Marduk" title="Marduk">Marduk</a>) was in <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> times, identified with Enki.</p>
<p>A large number of myths about Enki have been collected from many sites, stretching from Southern Iraq to the Levantine coast. He figures in the earliest extant cuneiform inscriptions throughout the region and was prominent from the third millennium down to Hellenistic times.</p>
<p>The exact meaning of his name is uncertain: the common translation is "Lord of the Earth". The Sumerian <i><a href="/wiki/EN_(cuneiform)" title="EN (cuneiform)">En</a></i> is translated as a title equivalent to "<a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">lord</a>" and was originally a title given to the High Priest. <i>Ki</i> means "earth", but there are theories that <i>ki</i> in this name has another origin, possibly <i>kig</i> of unknown meaning, or <i>kur</i> meaning "mound". The name Ea is allegedly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurrian" title="Hurrian">Hurrian</a> in origin while others <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> claim that his name 'Ea' is possibly of Semitic origin and may be a derivation from the West-Semitic root *hyy meaning "life" in this case used for "spring", "running water." In Sumerian E-A means "the house of water", and it has been suggested that this was originally the name for the shrine to the god at <a href="/wiki/Eridu" title="Eridu">Eridu</a>. It has also been suggested that the original non-anthropomorphic divinity at Eridu was not Enki but <a href="/wiki/Abzu" title="Abzu">Abzu</a>. The emergence of Enki as the divine lover of <a href="/wiki/Ninhursag" title="Ninhursag">Ninhursag</a>, and the divine battle between the younger Igigi divinities and Abzu, saw the Abzu, the underground waters of the Aquifer, becoming the place in which the foundations of the temple were built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Worship">Worship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Worship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main temple to Enki was called <i>E-abzu</i>, meaning "<a href="/wiki/Abzu" title="Abzu">abzu</a> temple" (also <i>E-en-gur-a</i>, meaning "house of the subterranean waters"), a <a href="/wiki/Ziggurat" title="Ziggurat">ziggurat</a> temple surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphratean</a> marshlands near the ancient <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> coastline at <a href="/wiki/Eridu" title="Eridu">Eridu</a>. It was the first temple known to have been built in Southern Iraq. Four separate excavations at the site of <a href="/wiki/Eridu" title="Eridu">Eridu</a> have demonstrated the existence of a shrine dating back to the earliest <a href="/wiki/Ubaid_period" title="Ubaid period">Ubaid period</a>, over 6,500 years ago. Over the following 4,500 years, the temple was expanded 18 times, until it was abandoned during the Persian period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> On this basis Thorkild Jacobsen<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> has hypothesized that the original deity of the temple was Abzu, with his attributes later being taken by Enki over time. P. Steinkeller believes that, during the earliest period, Enki had a subordinate position to a goddess (possibly <a href="/wiki/Ninhursag" title="Ninhursag">Ninhursag</a>), taking the role of divine consort or high priest,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> later taking priority. The Enki temple had at its entrance a pool of fresh water, and excavation has found numerous carp bones, suggesting collective feasts. Carp are shown in the twin water flows running into the later God Enki, suggesting continuity of these features over a very long period. These features were found at all subsequent Sumerian temples, suggesting that this temple established the pattern for all subsequent Sumerian temples. "All rules laid down at Eridu were faithfully observed".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iconography">Iconography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Iconography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Enki(Ea).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="306" data-file-width="265" height="254" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Enki%28Ea%29.jpg/220px-Enki%28Ea%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/87/Enki%28Ea%29.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Enki was the keeper of the divine powers called <i><a href="/wiki/Me_(mythology)" title="Me (mythology)">Me</a></i>, the gifts of <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a>. His image was a double-helix snake,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement"><span title="This claim has reliable sources with contradicting facts (October 2015)">disputed</span></a> <span class="metadata">– <a href="/wiki/Talk:Enki" title="Talk:Enki">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> or the <a href="/wiki/Caduceus" title="Caduceus">Caduceus</a>, sometimes confused with the <a href="/wiki/Rod_of_Asclepius" title="Rod of Asclepius">Rod of Asclepius</a> used to symbolize medicine<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. He is often shown with the horned crown of divinity dressed in the skin of a <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a>.</p>
<p>Enki was considered a god of life and replenishment, and was often depicted with two streams of water flowing into his shoulders, one the Tigris, the other the Euphrates. Alongside him were trees symbolising the female and male aspects of nature, each holding the female and male aspects of the 'Life Essence', which he, as apparent alchemist of the gods, would masterfully mix to create several beings that would live upon the face of the earth.</p>
<p>Considered the master shaper of the world, god of <a href="/wiki/Wisdom" title="Wisdom">wisdom</a> and of all <a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" title="Magic (paranormal)">magic</a>, Enki was characterized as the lord of the <a href="/wiki/Abzu" title="Abzu">Abzu</a> (Apsu in Akkadian), the freshwater sea or <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> located within the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">earth</a>. In the later Babylonian epic <i><a href="/wiki/En%C3%BBma_Eli%C5%A1" title="Enûma Eliš">Enûma Eliš</a></i>, Abzu, the "begetter of the gods", is inert and sleepy but finds his peace disturbed by the younger gods, so sets out to destroy them. His grandson Enki, chosen to represent the younger gods, puts a spell on Abzu "casting him into a deep sleep", thereby confining him deep underground. Enki subsequently sets up his home "<i>in the depths of the Abzu</i>." Enki thus takes on all of the functions of the Abzu, including his fertilising powers as lord of the waters and lord of <a href="/wiki/Semen" title="Semen">semen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Early royal inscriptions from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_millennium_BCE" title="Third millennium BCE">third millennium BCE</a> mention "the reeds of Enki". <a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">Reeds</a> were an important local building material, used for baskets and containers, and collected outside the city walls, where the dead or sick were often carried. This links Enki to the <i><a href="/wiki/Kur" title="Kur">Kur</a></i> or <a href="/wiki/Underworld" title="Underworld">underworld</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumerian_mythology" title="Sumerian mythology">Sumerian mythology</a>. In another even older tradition, <a href="/wiki/Nammu" title="Nammu">Nammu</a>, the goddess of the primeval creative matter and the mother-goddess portrayed as having <i>"given birth to the great gods,"</i> was the mother of Enki, and as the watery creative force, was said to preexist Ea-Enki.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Benito states "With Enki it is an interesting change of gender symbolism, the fertilising agent is also water, Sumerian <i>"a"</i> or <i>"Ab"</i> which also means "semen". In one evocative passage in a Sumerian hymn, Enki stands at the empty riverbeds and fills them with his 'water'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This may be a reference to Enki's <a href="/wiki/Hieros_gamos" title="Hieros gamos">hieros gamos</a> or sacred marriage with <a href="/wiki/Ki_(goddess)" title="Ki (goddess)">Ki</a>/<a href="/wiki/Ninhursag" title="Ninhursag">Ninhursag</a> (the Earth) (see below).</p>
<p>His symbols included a <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">goat</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, which later combined into a single beast, the <a href="/wiki/Capra_(genus)" title="Capra (genus)">goat</a> <a href="/wiki/Capricorn_(astrology)" title="Capricorn (astrology)">Capricorn</a>, recognised as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zodiacal_constellation" title="Zodiacal constellation">Zodiacal constellation</a> <a href="/wiki/Capricornus" title="Capricornus">Capricornus</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He was accompanied by an attendant <a href="/wiki/Isimud" title="Isimud">Isimud</a>. He was also associated with the planet Mercury in the Sumerian astrological system.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mythology">Mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enki_and_Ninhursag_and_the_Creation_of_Life_and_Sickness">Enki and Ninhursag and the Creation of Life and Sickness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Enki and Ninhursag and the Creation of Life and Sickness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The cosmogenic myth common in Sumer was that of the <a href="/wiki/Hieros_gamos" title="Hieros gamos">hieros gamos</a>, a sacred marriage where divine principles in the form of dualistic opposites came together as male and female to give birth to the cosmos. In the epic <i>Enki and Ninhursag</i>, Enki, as lord of <i>Ab</i> or fresh water (also the Sumerian word for semen), is living with his wife in the paradise of <a href="/wiki/Dilmun" title="Dilmun">Dilmun</a> where</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><i>"The land of Dilmun is a pure place, the land of Dilmun is a clean place,</i></dd>
<dd><i>The land of Dilmun is a clean place, the land of Dilmun is a bright place;</i></dd>
<dd><i>He who is alone laid himself down in Dilmun,</i></dd>
<dd><i>The place, after Enki is clean, that place is bright"</i></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Despite being a place where <i>"the raven uttered no cries"</i> and <i>"the lion killed not, the wolf snatched not the lamb, unknown was the kid-killing dog, unknown was the grain devouring boar"</i>, Dilmun had no water and Enki heard the cries of its Goddess, Ninsikil, and orders the sun-God Utu to bring fresh water from the Earth for Dilmun. As a result,</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><i>"Her City Drinks the Water of Abundance,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Dilmun Drinks the Water of Abundance,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Her wells of bitter water, behold they are become wells of good water,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Her fields and farms produced crops and grain,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Her city, behold it has become the house of the banks and quays of the land.</i>"</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Dilmun was identified with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahrein" title="Bahrein">Bahrein</a>, whose name in <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> means <i>"two seas"</i>, where the fresh waters of the Arabian <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a> mingle with the salt waters of the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>. This mingling of waters was known in Sumerian as <a href="/wiki/Nammu" title="Nammu">Nammu</a>, and was identified as the mother of Enki.</p>
<p>The subsequent tale, with similarities to the Biblical story of the forbidden fruit, repeats the story of how fresh water brings life to a barren land.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Enki, the Water-Lord then <i>"caused to flow the 'water of the heart"</i> and having fertilised his consort <a href="/wiki/Ninhursag" title="Ninhursag">Ninhursag</a>, also known as <a href="/wiki/Ki_(goddess)" title="Ki (goddess)">Ki</a> or Earth, after <i>"Nine days being her nine months, the months of 'womanhood'... like good butter, Nintu, the mother of the land, ...like good butter, gave birth to <a href="/wiki/Ninsar" title="Ninsar">Ninsar</a>, (Lady Greenery)"</i>. When Ninhursag left him, as Water-Lord he came upon Ninsar (Lady Greenery). Not knowing her to be his daughter, and because she reminds him of his absent consort, Enki then seduces and has intercourse with her. Ninsar then gave birth to <a href="/wiki/Ninkurra" title="Ninkurra">Ninkurra</a> (Lady Fruitfulness or Lady Pasture), and leaves Enki alone again. A second time, Enki, in his loneliness finds and seduces Ninkurra, and from the union Ninkurra gave birth to <a href="/wiki/Uttu" title="Uttu">Uttu</a> (weaver or spider, the weaver of the web of life).</p>
<p>A third time Enki succumbs to temptation, and attempts seduction of Uttu. Upset about Enki's reputation, Uttu consults Ninhursag, who, upset at the promiscuous wayward nature of her spouse, advises Uttu to avoid the riverbanks, the places likely to be affected by flooding, the home of Enki. In another version of this myth Ninhursag takes Enki's semen from Uttu's womb and plants it in the earth where eight plants rapidly germinate. With his two-faced servant and steward <a href="/wiki/Isimud" title="Isimud">Isimud</a>, <i>"Enki, in the swampland, in the swampland lies stretched out, 'What is this (plant), what is this (plant). His messenger Isimud, answers him; 'My king, this is the tree-plant', he says to him. He cuts it off for him and he (Enki) eats it". And so, despite warnings, Enki consumes the other seven fruit. Consuming his own semen, he falls pregnant (ill with swellings) in his jaw, his teeth, his mouth, his hip, his throat, his limbs, his side and his rib. The gods are at a loss to know what to do, chagrinned they</i> "sit in the dust"<i>. As Enki lacks a womb with which to give birth, he seems to be dying with swellings. The fox then asks Enlil King of the Gods,</i> "If I bring Ninhursag before thee, what shall be my reward?" <i>Ninhursag's sacred fox then fetches the goddess.</i></p>
<p>Ninhursag relents and takes Enki's Ab (water, or semen) into her body, and gives birth to gods of healing of each part of the body. Abu for the Jaw, Nintul for the Hip, Ninsutu for the tooth, Ninkasi for the mouth, Dazimua for the side, Enshagag for the Limbs. The last one, <a href="/wiki/Ninti" title="Ninti">Ninti</a> (Lady Rib), is also a pun on Lady Life, a title of Ninhursag herself. The story thus symbolically reflects the way in which life is brought forth through the addition of water to the land, and once it grows, water is required to bring plants to fruit. It also counsels balance and responsibility, nothing to excess.</p>
<p>Ninti, the title of Ninhursag, also means <i>"the mother of all living"</i>, and was a title given to the later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurrian" title="Hurrian">Hurrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Goddess" title="Goddess">goddess</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebat" title="Hebat">Kheba</a>. This is also the title given in the Bible to <a href="/wiki/Eve" title="Eve">Eve</a>, the Hebrew and Aramaic Ḥawwah (חוה), who was made from the rib of Adam, in a strange reflection of the Sumerian myth, in which Adam — not Enki — walks in the Garden of Paradise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kramer1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-kramer1-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enki_and_the_Making_of_Man">Enki and the Making of Man</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Enki and the Making of Man">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After six generations of gods, in the Babylonian <i>"<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enuma_Elish" title="Enuma Elish">Enuma Elish</a>"</i>, in the seventh generation, (Akkadian <i>"shapattu"</i> or sabath), the younger <a href="/wiki/Igigi" title="Igigi">Igigi</a> gods, the sons and daughters of Enlil and Ninlil, go on strike and refuse their duties of keeping the creation working. <a href="/wiki/Abzu" title="Abzu">Abzu</a> God of fresh water, co-creator of the cosmos, threatens to destroy the world with his waters, and the Gods gather in terror. Enki promises to help and puts Abzu to sleep, confining him in irrigation canals and places him in the Kur, beneath his city of <a href="/wiki/Eridu" title="Eridu">Eridu</a>. But the universe is still threatened, as <a href="/wiki/Tiamat" title="Tiamat">Tiamat</a>, angry at the imprisonment of Abzu and at the prompting of her son and vizier <a href="/wiki/Kingu" title="Kingu">Kingu</a>, decides to take back the creation herself. The gods gather again in terror and turn to Enki for help, but Enki who harnessed <a href="/wiki/Abzu" title="Abzu">Abzu</a>, Tiamat's consort, for irrigation refuses to get involved. The gods then seek help elsewhere, and the patriarchal <a href="/wiki/Enlil" title="Enlil">Enlil</a>, their father, God of <a href="/wiki/Nippur" title="Nippur">Nippur</a>, promises to solve the problem if they make him King of the Gods. In the Babylonian tale, Enlil's role is taken by <a href="/wiki/Marduk" title="Marduk">Marduk</a>, Enki's son, and in the Assyrian version it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asshur" title="Asshur">Asshur</a>. After dispatching Tiamat with the "arrows of his winds" down her throat and constructing the heavens with the arch of her ribs, Enlil places her tail in the sky as the Milky Way, and her crying eyes become the source of the Tigris and Euphrates. But there is still the problem of <i>"who will keep the cosmos working"</i>. Enki, who might have otherwise come to their aid, is lying in a deep sleep and fails to hear their cries. His mother <a href="/wiki/Nammu" title="Nammu">Nammu</a> (creatrix also of Abzu and Tiamat) <i>"brings the tears of the gods"</i> before Enki and says</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Oh my son, arise from thy bed, from thy (slumber), work what is wise,</dd>
<dd>Fashion servants for the Gods, may they produce their (bread?).</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Enki then advises that they create a servant of the gods, humankind, out of clay and blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Against Enki's wish the Gods decide to slay Kingu, and Enki finally consents to use Kingu's blood to make the first human, with whom Enki always later has a close relationship, the first of the seven sages, seven wise men or <i>"Abgallu"</i> (Ab = water, Gal = great, Lu = Man), also known as <a href="/wiki/Adapa" title="Adapa">Adapa</a>. Enki assembles a team of divinities to help him, creating a host of <i>"good and princely fashioners"</i>. He tells his mother</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Oh my mother, the creature whose name thou has uttered, it exists,</dd>
<dd>Bind upon it the (will?) of the Gods;</dd>
<dd>Mix the heart of clay that is over the Abyss,</dd>
<dd>The good and princely fashioners will thicken the clay</dd>
<dd>Thou, do thou bring the limbs into existence;</dd>
<dd><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ninmah" title="Ninmah">Ninmah</a> (the Earth-mother goddess (Ninhursag, his wife and consort) will work above thee</dd>
<dd>(<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintu" title="Nintu">Nintu</a>?) (goddess of birth) will stand by thy fashioning;</dd>
<dd>Oh my mother, decree thou its (the new born's) fate.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Adapa, the first man fashioned, later goes and acts as the advisor to the King of Eridu, when in the Sumerian Kinglist, the <i>"<a href="/wiki/Me_(mythology)" title="Me (mythology)">Me</a>"</i> of <i>"kingship descends on Eridu"</i>.</p>
<p>Samuel Noah Kramer, believes that behind this myth of Enki's confinement of Abzu lies an older one of the struggle between Enki and the Dragon Kur (the underworld)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kramer1_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-kramer1-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Atrahasis-Epos has it that Enlil requested from Nammu the creation of humans. And Nammu told him that with the help of Enki (her son) she can create humans in the image of gods.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Confuser_of_languages">Confuser of languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Confuser of languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Sumerian epic entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Enmerkar_and_the_Lord_of_Aratta" title="Enmerkar and the Lord of Aratta">Enmerkar and the Lord of Aratta</a></i>, in a speech of <a href="/wiki/Enmerkar" title="Enmerkar">Enmerkar</a>, an incantation is pronounced that has a mythical introduction. Kramer's translation is as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Once upon a time there was no snake, there was no scorpion,<br/></p>
<p>There was no hyena, there was no lion,<br/>
There was no wild dog, no wolf,<br/>
There was no fear, no terror,<br/>
Man had no rival.</p>
<p>In those days, the lands of <a href="/wiki/Subartu" title="Subartu">Subur</a> (and) <a href="/wiki/Hamazi" title="Hamazi">Hamazi</a>,<br/>
Harmony-tongued Sumer, the great land of the decrees of princeship,<br/>
Uri, the land having all that is appropriate,<br/>
The land <a href="/wiki/Amorites" title="Amorites">Martu</a>, resting in security,<br/>
The whole universe, the people in unison<br/>
To Enlil in one tongue [spoke].</p>
<p>(Then) Enki, the lord of abundance (whose) commands are trustworthy,<br/>
The lord of wisdom, who understands the land,<br/>
The leader of the gods,<br/>
Endowed with wisdom, the lord of <a href="/wiki/Eridu" title="Eridu">Eridu</a><br/>
Changed the speech in their mouths, [brought] contention into it,<br/>
Into the speech of man that (until then) had been one.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enki_and_the_Deluge">Enki and the Deluge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Enki and the Deluge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_creation_myth" title="Sumerian creation myth">Sumerian creation myth</a></div>
<p>According to Sumerian mythology, Enki also assisted humanity to survive the Deluge designed to kill them. In the later Legend of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atrahasis" title="Atrahasis">Atrahasis</a>, Enlil, the king of the gods, sets out to eliminate humanity, whose noise is disturbing his rest. He successively sends drought, famine and plague to eliminate humanity, but Enki thwarts his half-brother's plans by teaching Atrahasis how to counter these threats. Each time, Atrahasis asks the population to abandon worship of all gods, except the one responsible for the calamity, and this seems to shame them into relenting. Humans, however, proliferate a fourth time. Enraged, <a href="/wiki/Enlil" title="Enlil">Enlil</a> convenes a Council of Deities and gets them to promise not to tell <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humankind" title="Humankind">humankind</a> that he plans their total annihilation. Enki does not tell Atrahasis directly, but speaks to him in secret via a reed wall. He instructs Atrahasis to build a boat in order to rescue his family and other living creatures from the coming deluge. After the seven-day Deluge, the flood hero frees a swallow, a raven and a dove in an effort to find if the flood waters have receded. Upon landing, a sacrifice is made to the gods. Enlil is angry his will has been thwarted yet again, and Enki is named as the culprit. Enki explains that Enlil is unfair to punish the guiltless, and the gods institute measures to ensure that humanity does not become too populous in the future. This is one of the oldest of the surviving <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle Eastern</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flood_(mythology)" title="Flood (mythology)">Deluge myths</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enki_and_Inanna">Enki and Inanna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Enki and Inanna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his connections with <a href="/wiki/Inanna" title="Inanna">Inanna</a>, Enki shows other aspects of his non-<a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">Patriarchal</a> nature. The myth <i>Enki and Inanna</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gfixkf_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-gfixkf-19">[19]</a></sup> tells the story of the young goddess of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-anna" title="E-anna">É-anna</a> temple of <a href="/wiki/Uruk" title="Uruk">Uruk</a>, who visits the senior god of Eridu, and is entertained by him in a feast. The seductive god plies her with beer, and the young goddess maintains her virtue, whilst Enki proceeds to get drunk. In generosity he gives her all the gifts of his <i><a href="/wiki/Me_(mythology)" title="Me (mythology)">Me</a></i>, the gifts of civilized life. Next morning, with a hangover, he asks his servant <a href="/wiki/Isimud" title="Isimud">Isimud</a> for his <i>Me</i>, only to be informed that he has given them to Inanna. Upset at his actions, he sends <a href="/wiki/Gallu" title="Gallu"><i>Galla</i></a> demons to recover them. Inanna escapes her pursuers and arrives safely back at the quay at Uruk. Enki realises that he has been tricked in his hubris and accepts a peace treaty forever with Uruk.</p>
<p>Politically, this myth would seem to indicate events of an early period when political authority passed from Enki's city of Eridu to Inanna's city of Uruk.</p>
<p>In the myth of <i>Inanna's descent</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gfixkf_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-gfixkf-19">[19]</a></sup> Inanna, in order to console her grieving sister <a href="/wiki/Ereshkigal" title="Ereshkigal">Ereshkigal</a>, who is mourning the death of her husband <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gugalana" title="Gugalana">Gugalana</a> (<i>gu</i>, bull, <i>gal</i>, big, <i>ana</i>, sky/heaven), slain by <a href="/wiki/Gilgamesh" title="Gilgamesh">Gilgamesh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enkidu" title="Enkidu">Enkidu</a>, sets out to visit her sister. She tells her servant Ninshubur (Lady Evening), a reference to Inanna's role as the <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">evening star</a>, that if she does not return in three days, to get help from her father <a href="/wiki/Anu" title="Anu">Anu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enlil" title="Enlil">Enlil</a>, king of the gods, or Enki. When she does not return, Ninshubur approaches Anu only to be told that he understands that his daughter is strong and can take care of herself. Enlil tells Ninshubur he is much too busy running the cosmos. Enki immediately expresses concern and dispatches his Galla demons, Galaturra or Kurgarra, sexless beings created from the dirt from beneath the god's finger-nails, to recover the young goddess. These beings may be the origin of the Greco-Roman <i><a href="/wiki/Galli" title="Galli">Galli</a></i>, androgynous beings of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_sex" title="Third sex">third sex</a>, similar to the American Indian <a href="/wiki/Two-Spirit" title="Two-Spirit">berdache</a>, who played an important part in early religious ritual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the story <i>Inanna and Shukaletuda</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Shukaletuda, the gardener, set by Enki to care for the date palm he had created, finds Inanna sleeping under the palm tree and rapes the goddess in her sleep. Awaking, she discovers that she has been violated and seeks to punish the miscreant. Shukaletuda seeks protection from Enki, whom Bottero<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> believes to be his father. In classic Enkian fashion, the father advises Shukaletuda to hide in the city where Inanna will not be able to find him. Enki, as the protector of whoever comes to seek his help, and as the empowerer of Inanna, here challenges the young impetuous goddess to control her anger so as to be better able to function as a great judge.</p>
<p>Eventually, after cooling her anger, she too seeks the help of Enki, as spokesperson of the "assembly of the gods", the Igigi and the Anunnaki. After she presents her case, Enki sees that justice needs to be done and promises help, delivering knowledge of where the miscreant is hiding.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enki_and_the_Kur">Enki and the Kur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Enki and the Kur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the earliest surviving version of the myth of the slaying of the <a href="/wiki/Kur" title="Kur">Kur</a>, Enki is the hero responsible for the Kur being slain. Unfortunately, this myth is high fragmentary and what little that is known about it comes solely from the prologue at the beginning of the epic poem, "Gilgamesh, Enkidu, and the Nether World." In later versions of this myth, the hero is either <a href="/wiki/Ninurta" title="Ninurta">Ninurta</a> or Inanna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Based on what little has survived of the account, it seems that the legend begins with the Kur abducting the goddess, <a href="/wiki/Ereshkigal" title="Ereshkigal">Ereshkigal</a>, and dragging her down to the Netherworld. Enki sets out in a boat to attack the Kur and avenge the abduction of Ereshkigal. The Kur is described as defending itself using a storm of hailstones of all sizes and by attacking Enki using the waters beneath the boat. The account never actually tells who the winner of the fight is, but it can probably be assumed that Enki is the ultimate victor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Enki</b> and later <b>Ea</b> were apparently depicted, sometimes, as a man covered with the skin of a fish, and this representation, as likewise the name of his temple E-apsu, "house of the watery deep", points decidedly to his original character as a god of the waters (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oannes_(mythology)" title="Oannes (mythology)">Oannes</a>). Around the excavation of the 18 shrines found on the spot, thousands of <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a> bones were found, consumed possibly in feasts to the god. Of his cult at Eridu, which goes back to the oldest period of Mesopotamian history, nothing definite is known except that his temple was also associated with Ninhursag's temple which was called <i>Esaggila</i>, "the lofty head house" (<i><a href="/wiki/%C3%89_(temple)" title="É (temple)">E</a></i>, house, <i>sag</i>, head, <i>ila</i>, high; or Akkadian goddess = Ila), a name shared with Marduk's temple in Babylon, pointing to a staged tower or <a href="/wiki/Ziggurat" title="Ziggurat">ziggurat</a> (as with the temple of <a href="/wiki/Enlil" title="Enlil">Enlil</a> at <a href="/wiki/Nippur" title="Nippur">Nippur</a>, which was known as <i>E-kur</i> (<i>kur</i>, hill)), and that incantations, involving ceremonial rites in which water as a sacred element played a prominent part, formed a feature of his worship. This seems also implicated in the <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hieros_gamos" title="Hieros gamos">hieros gamos</a> or sacred <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a> of Enki and Ninhursag (above), which seems an <a href="/wiki/Etiology" title="Etiology">etiological</a> myth of the fertilisation of the dry ground by the coming of irrigation water (from Sumerian <i>a</i>, <i>ab</i>, water or semen). The early inscriptions of <a href="/wiki/Urukagina" title="Urukagina">Urukagina</a> in fact go so far as to suggest that the divine pair, Enki and Ninki, were the progenitors of seven pairs of gods, including Enki as god of <a href="/wiki/Eridu" title="Eridu">Eridu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enlil" title="Enlil">Enlil</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nippur" title="Nippur">Nippur</a>, and Su'en (or <a href="/wiki/Sin_(mythology)" title="Sin (mythology)">Sin</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Ur" title="Ur">Ur</a>, and were themselves the children of An (sky, heaven) and Ki (earth).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MastersThesis_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-MastersThesis-26">[26]</a></sup> The pool of the Abzu at the front of his temple was adopted also at the temple to Nanna (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">Akkadian</a> <a href="/wiki/Sin_(mythology)" title="Sin (mythology)">Sin</a>) the Moon, at <a href="/wiki/Ur" title="Ur">Ur</a>, and spread from there throughout the Middle East. It is believed to remain today as the sacred pool at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosques" title="Mosques">Mosques</a>, or as the holy water font in Catholic or Eastern Orthodox churches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Whether Eridu at one time also played an important political role in Sumerian affairs is not certain, though not improbable. At all events the prominence of "Ea" led, as in the case of Nippur, to the survival of Eridu as a sacred city, long after it had ceased to have any significance as a political center. Myths in which Ea figures prominently have been found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assurbanipal" title="Assurbanipal">Assurbanipal</a>'s library, and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hattusas" title="Hattusas">Hattusas</a> <a href="/wiki/Archive" title="Archive">archive</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittite</a> <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>. As Ea, Enki had a wide influence outside of Sumer, being equated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_(god)" title="El (god)">El</a> (at <a href="/wiki/Ugarit" title="Ugarit">Ugarit</a>) and possibly <a href="/wiki/Names_of_God_in_Judaism#Yah" title="Names of God in Judaism">Yah</a> (at <a href="/wiki/Ebla" title="Ebla">Ebla</a>) in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canaanite_mythology" title="Canaanite mythology">Canaanite</a> <a href="/wiki/Elohim" title="Elohim">'ilhm</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pantheon_(gods)" title="Pantheon (gods)">pantheon</a>, he is also found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurrian" title="Hurrian">Hurrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittite</a> mythology, as a god of contracts, and is particularly favourable to humankind. It has been suggested that etymologiically Ea was comes from the term <i>*hyy</i> (life), referring to Enki's waters as life giving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Opening_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Opening-28">[28]</a></sup> Enki/Ea is essentially a god of civilization, wisdom, and culture. He was also the creator and protector of man, and of the world in general. Traces of this version of Ea appear in the Marduk epic celebrating the achievements of this god and the close connection between the Ea cult at Eridu and that of Marduk. The correlation between the two rises from two other important connections: (1) that the name of Marduk's sanctuary at Babylon bears the same name, <i>Esaggila</i>, as that of a temple in Eridu, and (2) that Marduk is generally termed the son of Ea, who derives his powers from the voluntary abdication of the father in favour of his son. Accordingly, the incantations originally composed for the Ea cult were re-edited by the priests of Babylon and adapted to the worship of <a href="/wiki/Marduk" title="Marduk">Marduk</a>, and, similarly, the hymns to Marduk betray traces of the transfer to Marduk of attributes which originally belonged to Ea.</p>
<p>It is, however, as the third figure in the triad (the two other members of which were <a href="/wiki/Anu" title="Anu">Anu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enlil" title="Enlil">Enlil</a>) that Ea acquires his permanent place in the pantheon. To him was assigned the control of the watery element, and in this capacity he becomes the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B%C3%AAlu" title="Bêlu">shar</a> apsi</i>; i.e. king of the Apsu or "the deep". The Apsu was figured as the abyss of water beneath the earth, and since the gathering place of the dead, known as Aralu, was situated near the confines of the Apsu, he was also designated as <a href="/wiki/EN_(cuneiform)" title="EN (cuneiform)">En</a><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/En-Ki" title="En-Ki">-Ki</a>; i.e. "lord of that which is below", in contrast to Anu, who was the lord of the "above" or the heavens. The cult of Ea extended throughout Babylonia and <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyria</a>. We find temples and shrines erected in his honour, e.g. at <a href="/wiki/Nippur" title="Nippur">Nippur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Girsu" title="Girsu">Girsu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ur" title="Ur">Ur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sippar" title="Sippar">Sippar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nineveh" title="Nineveh">Nineveh</a>, and the numerous epithets given to him, as well as the various forms under which the god appears, alike bear witness to the popularity which he enjoyed from the earliest to the latest period of Babylonian-Assyrian history. The consort of Ea, known as Ninhursag, Ki, Uriash <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Damkina" title="Damkina">Damkina</a>, "lady of that which is below", or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Damgalnunna" title="Damgalnunna">Damgalnunna</a>, "big lady of the waters", originally was fully equal with Ea, but in more <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">patriarchal</a> Assyrian and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-Babylonian" title="Neo-Babylonian">Neo-Babylonian</a> times plays a part merely in association with her lord. Generally, however, Enki seems to be a reflection of pre-patriarchal times, in which relations between the sexes were characterised by a situation of greater <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a>. In his character, he prefers persuasion to conflict, which he seeks to avoid if possible.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ea_and_West_Semitic_deities">Ea and West Semitic deities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ea and West Semitic deities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1964, a team of Italian archaeologists under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Matthiae" title="Paolo Matthiae">Paolo Matthiae</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Rome_La_Sapienza" title="University of Rome La Sapienza">University of Rome La Sapienza</a> performed a series of excavations of material from the third-millennium BCE city of <a href="/wiki/Ebla" title="Ebla">Ebla</a>. Much of the written material found in these digs was later translated by <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Pettinato" title="Giovanni Pettinato">Giovanni Pettinato</a>.</p>
<p>Among other conclusions, he found a tendency among the inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Ebla" title="Ebla">Ebla</a>, after the reign of <a href="/wiki/Sargon_of_Akkad" title="Sargon of Akkad">Sargon of Akkad</a>, to replace the name of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_(god)" title="El (god)">El</a>, king of the gods of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canaanite_pantheon" title="Canaanite pantheon">Canaanite pantheon</a> (found in names such as Mikael and Ishmael), with <i>Ia</i> (Mikaia, Ishmaia).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Jean Bottero (1952)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and others<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> suggested that Ia in this case is a West Semitic (Canaanite) way of saying Ea, Enki's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akkadian_language" title="Akkadian language">Akkadian</a> name, associating the Canaanite theonym <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yahu" title="Yahu">Yahu</a>, and ultimately Hebrew <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/YHWH" title="YHWH">YHWH</a>. Some scholars remain skeptical of the theory while explaining how it might have been misinterpreted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> <i>Ia</i> has also been compared by William Hallo<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> with the <a href="/wiki/Ugarit" title="Ugarit">Ugaritic</a> <a href="/wiki/Yam_(god)" title="Yam (god)">Yamm</a> (sea), (also called Judge Nahar, or Judge River) whose earlier name in at least one ancient source was Yaw, or Ya'a.</p>
