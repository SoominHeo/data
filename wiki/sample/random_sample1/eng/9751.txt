<p><b>Air Siam</b> was an airline based in Thailand which operated from 1965 until 1976.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_Siam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The airline was established as <i>Varan Air-Siam</i> on 15 September 1965 by <a href="/wiki/Varananda_Dhavaj" title="Varananda Dhavaj">Prince Varanand</a>, who was the major shareholder at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI1974_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FI1974-2">[2]</a></sup> Operations started in 1970 using <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-4" title="Douglas DC-4">Douglas DC-4</a> equipment, initially flying the <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a>−<a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> route as a freighter service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI1974_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FI1974-2">[2]</a></sup> Following an agreement with <a href="/wiki/Overseas_National_Airways" title="Overseas National Airways">Overseas National Airways</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_lease" title="Aircraft lease">wet-lease</a> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-8-63" title="Douglas DC-8-63">Douglas DC-8-63</a> and operational assistance from <a href="/wiki/Trans_World_Airlines" title="Trans World Airlines">Trans World Airlines</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI1971-919_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FI1971-919-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI1971-444_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FI1971-444-4">[4]</a></sup> scheduled passenger services started in March 1971<span style="display:none"> (<span class="bday dtstart published updated">1971-03</span>)</span> serving Bangkok, <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI1974_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FI1974-2">[2]</a></sup> All these services were suspended in January 1972<span style="display:none"> (<span class="bday dtstart published updated">1972-01</span>)</span>; the Bangkok–Hong Kong sector was reactivated in May the same year with a leased <a href="/wiki/BAC_One-Eleven" title="BAC One-Eleven">BAC One-Eleven</a> and extended to Tokyo seven months later, in December 1972<span style="display:none"> (<span class="bday dtstart published updated">1972-12</span>)</span>, operated with a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_707" title="Boeing 707">Boeing 707</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI1974_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FI1974-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 1974<span style="display:none"> (<span class="bday dtstart published updated">1974-04</span>)</span>, the carrier placed an order for two <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A300" title="Airbus A300">Airbus A300s</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI1974-496_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FI1974-496-5">[5]</a></sup> one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A300B2" title="A300B2">A300B2</a> and one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A300B4" title="A300B4">A300B4</a>, taking an option for one more of the longer-range variant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI1974-513_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FI1974-513-6">[6]</a></sup> After taking delivery of the first of these aircraft on a lease basis from <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a> in October 1974<span style="display:none"> (<span class="bday dtstart published updated">1974-10</span>)</span>, Air Siam became the second operator worldwide in deploying the type on scheduled services, after <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Airliner_prices_index_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Airliner_prices_index-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:590</sup> Also in 1974, the company placed an order for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-10-30" title="Douglas DC-10-30">Douglas DC-10-30</a>, to be used on the Bangkok–Los Angeles route.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI1974-291_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FI1974-291-8">[8]</a></sup> Rights for the routes the airline intended to deploy the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A300" title="Airbus A300">A300s</a> on were never granted, and the A300B2 was returned to the lessor just a year after it was incorporated into the fleet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI1975-648_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FI1975-648-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Before Air Siam started flying long-haul services, Thailand's <a href="/wiki/Flag_carrier" title="Flag carrier">flag carrier</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thai_Airways_International" title="Thai Airways International">Thai International</a> was the country's single long-haul operator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Far_East_airline_market_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Far_East_airline_market-10">[10]</a></sup> Already in 1974, the airline had accused both Thai International and the Thai Civil Aeronautics Board for obstructing their expansion plans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI1974-828_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FI1974-828-11">[11]</a></sup> Despite various <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Thailand" title="Government of Thailand">governments</a> had considered the possibility of a merger, this never materialised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Far_East_airline_market_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Far_East_airline_market-10">[10]</a></sup> A new government taking office in 1976 had a different vision and decided that Thailand should have just one long-haul carrier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Far_East_airline_market_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Far_East_airline_market-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:1564</sup></p>
