<p><b>Airdrie</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɛər/ 'are' in 'bare'">ɛər</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>: <span lang="gd" xml:lang="gd"><i>An t-Àrd Ruigh</i></span>) is a town in <a href="/wiki/North_Lanarkshire" title="North Lanarkshire">North Lanarkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. It lies on a <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateau</a> roughly 400 ft (130 m) above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>, and is approximately 12 miles (19 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> city centre. Airdrie forms part of a conurbation with its neighbour <a href="/wiki/Coatbridge" title="Coatbridge">Coatbridge</a>, in the former district known as the <a href="/wiki/Monklands_(district)" title="Monklands (district)">Monklands</a>. As of 2012<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the town had a population of 37,130.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chapelhall" title="Chapelhall">Chapelhall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calderbank" title="Calderbank">Calderbank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caldercruix" title="Caldercruix">Caldercruix</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gartness,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gartness, North Lanarkshire (page does not exist)">Gartness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glenmavis" title="Glenmavis">Glenmavis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greengairs" title="Greengairs">Greengairs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Longriggend" title="Longriggend">Longriggend</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Moffat_Mills&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Moffat Mills (page does not exist)">Moffat Mills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plains,_North_Lanarkshire" title="Plains, North Lanarkshire">Plains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stand,_North_Lanarkshire" title="Stand, North Lanarkshire">Stand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Upperton,_North_Lanarkshire" title="Upperton, North Lanarkshire">Upperton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wattston" title="Wattston">Wattston</a> are generally considered satellite villages of Airdrie.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of Airdrie's name first appeared in the Register of the Great Seal of Scotland (Registrum Magni Sigilii Regum Scotorum) in 1373 as Ardre. By 1546 it had become Ardry and by 1587 it was known as Ardrie. In 1630 it finally appeared in the Register as Airdrie. Given the topography of the area, the most likely interpretation is that the name derives from the Gaelic <i>An Àrd Ruigh</i> meaning a level height or high pasture land. Another possibility is that it is from the Gaelic <i>An Àrd Àirighe</i> meaning a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sheiling" title="Sheiling">sheiling</a>, a summer pasture/shepherd's hut. A third possibility is the Gaelic <i>Ard Reidh</i> meaning a high plain.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The claim of a link between the modern town of Airdrie and the ancient battle of Arderyth has no evidence to back it up and is therefore best regarded as spurious. Unified the patronage of King Malcolm IVth Cistercian monks established an abbey at Melrose in 1136. Five years later a daughter house was founded at Newbattle in Lothian. In 1160 Malcolm granted to the monks of Newbattle lands in central Scotland which became known as the "Munklands" (Register of the Great Seal 1323). Malcolm's Charter constitutes the oldest documentary record of place-names in the Monklands. The area of land granted by the Charter is clearly defined by direct reference to geographical and topographical features thus: <i>Dunpeldre by its right boundaries, namely with Metheraugh and Mayeuth and Clarnephin as far as Dunduffes in the east.</i> The name <i>Dunpeldre</i> is found in the modern name Drumpellier; <i>Metheraugh</i> is now Medrox; <i>Mayeuth</i> is now Myvot; <i>Clarnephin</i> refers to the North Calder Water in the east of the parish (from old Brittonic name <i>claur n afon</i> meaning plain of the river); and, finally, <i>Dunduffes</i> has become directly translated into the modern Black Hill which, as the Charter states, lies at the eastern extremity of the parish. The one thing this Charter does not make any reference to is anything resembling Airdrie yet this is where Airdrie is located.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p><br/>
Airdrie owes its existence to its location on the 'Hogs Back' – a ridge of land running from east to west. One very important aspect of the town’s history were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cistercian" title="Cistercian">Cistercian</a> monks of <a href="/wiki/Newbattle_Abbey" title="Newbattle Abbey">Newbattle Abbey</a>, hence a name for the wider area; Monklands. The monks were farmers and most of the land they used is known today as 'The Four Isles' (a housing estate named after four Scottish islands): <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isle_of_Mull" title="Isle of Mull">Mull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islay" title="Islay">Islay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iona" title="Iona">Iona</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luing" title="Luing">Luing</a> in the Petersburn area of modern Airdrie. The <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monks</a> of Newbattle had numerous establishments throughout the area including a <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farm</a> grange at Drumpellier, Coatbridge, a court house at <a href="/wiki/Kipps" title="Kipps">Kipps</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Chapel" title="Chapel">chapel</a> in the area of Chapelhall and a number of <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a> <a href="/wiki/Mill_(grinding)" title="Mill (grinding)">mills</a>. The Monks were also expert in the <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> of <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">roads</a>. In the 12th century they established the original Glasgow to <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> road via Airdrie and <a href="/wiki/Bathgate" title="Bathgate">Bathgate</a>, to link up with their lands in Newbattle in <a href="/wiki/East_Lothian" title="East Lothian">East Lothian</a>.</p>
<p>In those days travelling was often dangerous. Horses were still very rare and could only be afforded by the rich. Low-lying ground was usually extremely difficult to navigate because of the numerous <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>, forests and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burn_(topography)" title="Burn (topography)">burns</a> – not to mention the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Ambush" title="Ambush">ambush</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Footpad" title="Footpad">footpad</a> or robber. Hence, it became much more practical to travel on the high ground (the 'High Way') where one could avoid the mud and the robbers. These roads (or rather tracks) became known as the King's Highway.</p>
<p>Definitive evidence of the existence of Airdrie as a tenantry was only made clear in 1503. The old monks' road was via Cliftonhill (an area now in neighbouring Coatbridge), Airdrie House (now the site of <a href="/wiki/Monklands_Hospital" title="Monklands Hospital">Monklands Hospital</a>), Aitchison Street, High Street, Hallcraig Street, Flowerhill Street and Colliertree Road. It was along this road that the first houses in Airdrie were built. Development was slow and it was only around 1650 that evidence of the number of <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">inhabitants</a> was known at around 500 for the Airdrie area. A large contingent of Airdrieonians fought at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bothwell_Brig" title="Battle of Bothwell Brig">Battle of Bothwell Brig</a> during the Covenanter Rebellion of 1679; their banner can still be viewed at the local library.</p>
<p>A significant event in Airdrie's history was the 1695 passing of a special Act of Parliament in the Scottish Parliament allowing Robert Hamilton of Airdrie to hold four fairs yearly and a weekly market in the town of 'Airdry'. This helped develop Airdrie from a 'farm town' into a thriving 'market town'.</p>
<p>However, Airdrie really came to prominence through its <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a> industry. Airdrie Weavers Society was founded in 1781 and <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a> was being grown in sixteen farms in and around the <a href="/wiki/Burgh" title="Burgh">burgh</a>. In the last decade of the eighteenth century, <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">coal mining</a> was in progress and around thirty <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">colliers</a> were employed. Weaving continued to flourish making up a substantial part of the population of over 2,500 around the turn of the 19th century. Given its large number of weavers, its geographic location, and a large number of unemployed soldiers following the end of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, Airdrie became a major centre of support for the <a href="/wiki/Radical_War" title="Radical War">Radical War</a> of 1820. The rapid pace of population growth continued and by 1821 there were 4,862 inhabitants. At this time the number of houses being built increased dramatically and in 1821, by a private <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a>, Airdrie became a free and independent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burgh_of_Barony" title="Burgh of Barony">Burgh of Barony</a>. Due to the fact it was 'independent', it had all the powers of a <a href="/wiki/Royal_burgh" title="Royal burgh">Royal Burgh</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Voting" title="Voting">Voting</a> in the early part of the nineteenth century was rather hit or miss as not only locals but residents outside the burgh were allowed to vote. In 1821 the first election of a town council took place and by August it had appointed an assessor, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Procurator_Fiscal" title="Procurator Fiscal">procurator fiscal</a>, master of police and a <a href="/wiki/Town_crier" title="Town crier">town crier</a>.</p>
<p>In 1824 it was decided to build the Town House, which was originally designed by Alexander Baird and is now a local landmark known as the 'town clock'. In 1832 the Town House was used as a hospital due to the <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> outbreak of this year.</p>
<p>By 1850, the population had grown to 12,418.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1850_to_1920">1850 to 1920</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1850 to 1920">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The enormous growth in population was not due to high birthrate, but instead due to an influx of residents from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Highlands</a> and predominantly <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. This followed the <a href="/wiki/Highland_Potato_Famine" title="Highland Potato Famine">Highland potato famine</a> of the mid-1840s and also reflected the change from cottage industry to <a href="/wiki/Heavy_industry" title="Heavy industry">heavy industry</a> in the area. Most of the Irish immigrant population were involved with <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> and labouring. This led to an increase in ironwork <a href="/wiki/Foundry" title="Foundry">foundries</a> around the area. Because of this explosion in industry, <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">railway</a> links were soon established (circa 1830) and by 1862, the Airdrie and Bathgate Junction Railway provided a direct link to Edinburgh with Airdrie South Station providing the starting point for trains to Glasgow.</p>
<p>In August the <a href="/wiki/Public_Libraries_Act_1850" title="Public Libraries Act 1850">Public Libraries Act (Scotland) 1853</a> was passed, and in November <a href="/wiki/Airdrie_Public_Library" title="Airdrie Public Library">Airdrie Public Library</a> became the first in Scotland.</p>
<p>The dramatic rise in population and industry prompted the need for more accessible water supplies. Until the mid-1800s, various wells were put in place feeding from surrounding streams in the area. These served to provide many houses with private wells. By 1846 Airdrie and Coatbridge Water Company was founded to construct (along with Forth and Clyde Canal Company) the reservoir at Roughrigg.</p>
<p>Around the mid-1800s, several local <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspapers</a> began appearing and notably the <a href="/wiki/Airdrie_%26_Coatbridge_Advertiser" title="Airdrie &amp; Coatbridge Advertiser">Airdrie &amp; Coatbridge Advertiser</a>, which is still the most popular local paper today. Also at this time, <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a> began to emerge as popular sports. Following the codification of association football rules a local team called Excelsior was formed in 1878 which would later be renamed <a href="/wiki/Airdrieonians_F.C._(1878)" title="Airdrieonians F.C. (1878)">Airdrieonians</a> Horse race meetings were also held in the town (1851–1870) but this land became the golf course for the newly formed Airdrie Golf Club in 1877.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">Education</a> posed a major problem with severe overcrowding in the few schools available, therefore three new school boards were established. Fees were routinely charged within the schools with the belief they should be self-supporting until a parliamentary act of 1889 relieved some of the infant classes in schools of this burden. <a href="/wiki/Airdrie_Academy" title="Airdrie Academy">Airdrie Academy</a> was built in 1895 and by 1919 all school boards were dissolved and Lanarkshire Education Authority took over responsibility for education throughout <a href="/wiki/Lanarkshire" title="Lanarkshire">Lanarkshire</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Airdrie_Public_Observatory" title="Airdrie Public Observatory">Airdrie Public Observatory</a>, one of only four public observatories in the UK (Second Oldest and Smallest)- all in Scotland, was founded in the first library building in 1896, and is still operated in the present building by the Airdrie Astronomical Association a Scottish <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">astronautic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> society and registered charity.</p>
<p>By the turn of the century <a href="/wiki/Variety_show" title="Variety show">variety shows</a> were becoming popular in the area and by 1911 the Pavilion in Graham Street was built which after initially being used as a music hall started showing cinematographic pictures. Unfortunately it was destroyed by fire in 1917 but was rebuilt in 1919 and finally closed in 1970. The New Cinema was opened in 1920 in Broomknoll Street but it too has since closed. The town had no suitable venue for larger functions so in 1912 the Sir John Wilson Town Hall was opened (following a generous offer from Sir John Wilson covering the total cost of £13,500). This still stands and is used for major events in the town.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1920_onwards">1920 onwards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1920 onwards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the end of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>, Airdrie was hard hit with many casualties from the war. Many inhabitants also chose to emigrate around this time. Consequently, the population only rose by 3% to around 26,000 by 1931. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">depression</a> years had made a great impact on the town and several well-known manufacturers ceased to exist and few replaced them. It was reported that 50% of the registered population were <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">unemployed</a>. Church groups tried to provide some comfort for the poor folk in the area and set up educational and work experience projects to help and by 1936 the Airdrie Churches Council had attracted national interest through their work culminating in a building in Graham Street being provided for them (the Mutual Service Club). This is now Airdrie Community Centre.</p>
<p>The current Airdrie Public Library building was eventually constructed on its present site in 1925 after years of moving from one site to another.</p>

<p>Conditions in the town did not really improve until well after the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> but in 1949 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boots_Group" title="Boots Group">Boots</a> <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_industry" title="Pharmaceutical industry">pharmaceutical company</a> and Banner Textiles Ltd were attracted to the town (between them employing 1200). With this impetus, new companies began to consider Airdrie as a viable option for business and in 1958 <a href="/wiki/Pye_Ltd." title="Pye Ltd.">Pye</a> opened employing over 1000 people. The emergence of industrial estates was also prevalent around this time (Newhouse, Chapelhall, and Brownsburn). The Airdrie Arts Centre opened in 1967 in the former Airdrie Library building and remained a popular venue for concerts and plays till it was closed in 2012 by North Lanarkshire Council.</p>
<p>The 1970s saw the opening of <a href="/wiki/Monklands_Hospital" title="Monklands Hospital">Monklands Hospital</a>, which replaced an older hospital on the Airdrie House estate that had been closed in 1962 and demolished in 1964.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Airdrie">Modern Airdrie</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Modern Airdrie">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Airdrie town centre has changed much in the last ten years with a new road scheme and a shift in emphasis with the type of shopping it offers. Graham Street, the main <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedestrianised" title="Pedestrianised">pedestrianised</a> street, has recently been refurbished and has had the pedestrian precinct area upgraded. New housing developments are being built around this suitably situated <a href="/wiki/Commuter_town" title="Commuter town">commuter town</a>, notably in Chapelhall, Rochsoles and Glenmavis, the former Boots factory site in Rawyards and the former Imperial Tube Works in Cairnhill.</p>
<p>Airdrie also has signage in the Scots Gaelic language around the town centre, (alongside English) this was first introduced for the 1993 <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_M%C3%B2d" title="Royal National Mòd">Royal National Mòd</a>. The signs were originally erected by Monklands District Council, but have been maintained by North Lanarkshire Council.</p>

<p>Scottish regiments returning from service in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> brought the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orange_Lodge" title="Orange Lodge">Orange Lodge</a> to Scotland in 1798 with warrants granted by the Grand Orange Lodge of Ireland. The first civilian lodge was set up in <a href="/wiki/Maybole" title="Maybole">Maybole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ayrshire" title="Ayrshire">Ayrshire</a>, in 1808 and a Grand Lodge was set up in Britain which issued its own warrants and its first Grand Master in 1821 was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/His_Royal_Highness" title="His Royal Highness">H.R.H.</a> <a href="/wiki/Prince_Frederick,_Duke_of_York_and_Albany" title="Prince Frederick, Duke of York and Albany">The Duke of York and Albany</a>, and the second being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/His_Royal_Highness" title="His Royal Highness">H.R.H.</a> <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Augustus,_King_of_Hanover" title="Ernest Augustus, King of Hanover">The 1st Duke of Cumberland and Teviotdale</a>. In 1824, L.O.L.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> 19 was established in Airdrie under the British Grand Lodge. When the Grand Secretary, Colonel Fairman, with other gentlemen visited Airdrie in 1833, their carriages were met a mile from town by a procession of Orangemen with banners and a band to parade into the town which was thronged with people. Two years later when the Orangemen of the town paraded in the streets to celebrate <a href="/wiki/The_Twelfth" title="The Twelfth">the Twelfth</a> they were met by a party of opposing <a href="/wiki/Ribbonism" title="Ribbonism">Ribbonmen</a>, most of whom came from Glasgow. The Orangemen returned to their Lodge whilst police and towns people dealt with<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the Ribbonmen. When the Grand Lodge was set up in 1836 Airdrie was given the honour of being granted its first District warrant hence District No 1 and of its new Lodge warrants No 1 Moodiesburn, No 2 Chryston, No 6 Drumgelloch, No 8 Gartsherrie and No 9 Shotts. It still retains the number of two of the Lodges that existed prior to 1836 namely L.O.L. 19 and L.O.L. 6.</p>
<p>The first united July Demonstration of Lodges was held in Airdrie District territory of Moodiesburn on 12 July 1857 led by Dr. Clements, Deputy Grand Master. Important years in the history of Airdrie District No 1 include 1891, when Bro.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Colonel Sanderson MP opened the Orange Hall in Greengairs and then traveled to Airdrie to lay the foundation stone of the Orange Halls in Baillies Lane, which opened later that year. On the 100th anniversary of the foundation being laid, Thursday 13 June 1991 the R.W.D.M.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Bro. Parks unveiled a plaque to commemorate this event. Monklands District Council afforded a civic reception to Airdrie District LOL 1 to mark the 200th anniversary of the foundation of the Orange Order. The District Lodge celebrated its 150th Anniversary in 1986 and after various functions the R.W.D.M. Bro. A. Blaney unveiled a plaque to commemorate their anniversary.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Angling">Angling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Angling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Airdrie is a popular destination for anglers from across the Central Belt, due to its lochs and reservoirs. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Airdrie &amp; District Angling Club based at <a href="/wiki/Hillend_Loch" title="Hillend Loch">Hillend Loch</a>, one of Scotland's top <a href="/wiki/Trout" title="Trout">trout</a> fishing locations.</li>
<li>The Lilly Loch, run by Clarkston Angling Club.</li>
<li>Roughrigg Reservoir, run by Roughrigg Angling Association.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.airdrieharriers.com/" rel="nofollow">Airdrie Harriers</a>, North Lanarkshire's largest athletics club.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bowls">Bowls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Bowls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are a number of bowling clubs in the town. All are members of the Lanarkshire Bowling Association and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Bowling_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Scottish Bowling Association (page does not exist)">Scottish Bowling Association</a>, district 27.</p>
<ul>
<li>Airdrie Bowling Club (founded 1852)</li>
<li>Calderbank Bowling Club – in nearby <a href="/wiki/Calderbank" title="Calderbank">Calderbank</a>.</li>
<li>Caldercraig Bowling Club</li>
<li>Central Bowling Club</li>
<li>Clarkston Bowling Club</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Football">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The town's major <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> club is <a href="/wiki/Airdrieonians_F.C." title="Airdrieonians F.C.">Airdrieonians F.C.</a>, who play in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Football_League_First_Division" title="Scottish Football League First Division">Scottish Football League First Division</a>, and are based at the <a href="/wiki/Excelsior_Stadium" title="Excelsior Stadium">Excelsior Stadium</a>. They were formed as a replacement for the original <a href="/wiki/Airdrieonians_F.C._(1878)" title="Airdrieonians F.C. (1878)">Airdrieonians</a>, who folded in May 2002; in the immediate aftermath of the liquidation of Airdrieonians, a local consortium quickly formed to establish a new club named Airdrie United. Its application to join the Scottish Football League, playing in the traditional Airdrieonians colours at the Excelsior Stadium (now also known as New Broomfield after the club's former home from 1892-1994), was rejected in favour of Gretna. The owning consortium swiftly devised a Plan B, and bought Clydebank FC from its administrators. Clydebank's sole asset of any value was its place in the third tier of Scottish professional football. The new owners gained permission to move that club to Airdrie and change the name to Airdrie United, which latter club formally inherited the short life history of Clydebank rather than Airdrieonians. The United part of the name was dropped informally in July 2012, but the club was still officially known as Airdrie United F.C., until a move back to the Airdrieonians name was finally achieved in June 2013.</p>
<p>The award winning Gartcairn Football Academy fields a number of teams, including a junior team, Gartcairn F.A. Juniors.</p>
<p>In addition there are a number of teams competing in the various <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Amateur_Football_Association" title="Scottish Amateur Football Association">Scottish Amateur Football Association</a> leagues.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_local_government_in_Scotland" title="History of local government in Scotland">History of local government in Scotland</a></div>
<p>Airdrie is represented by several tiers of elected government. <a href="/wiki/North_Lanarkshire" title="North Lanarkshire">North Lanarkshire Council</a>, the unitary <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local authority</a> for Airdrie, is based at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motherwell,_North_Lanarkshire" title="Motherwell, North Lanarkshire">Motherwell</a>, and is the <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deliberation" title="Deliberation">deliberative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislative</a> body responsible for <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Scotland" title="Local government in Scotland">local governance</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> is responsible for <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolved matters</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Scotland" title="Education in Scotland">education</a>, <a href="/wiki/NHS_Scotland" title="NHS Scotland">health</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">justice</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-devolvedmatters_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-devolvedmatters-5">[5]</a></sup> while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reserved_matters" title="Reserved matters">reserved matters</a> are dealt with by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Westminster">Westminster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Westminster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The town forms part of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_constituencies" title="United Kingdom constituencies">burgh constituency</a> of <a href="/wiki/Airdrie_and_Shotts_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Airdrie and Shotts (UK Parliament constituency)">Airdrie and Shotts</a>, electing one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">Member of Parliament</a> (MP) to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a>. In 2005, changes to the constituency boundaries saw part of its area transferred to <a href="/wiki/Motherwell_and_Wishaw_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Motherwell and Wishaw (UK Parliament constituency)">Motherwell and Wishaw</a>, offset by the addition of part of <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_North_and_Bellshill_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Hamilton North and Bellshill (UK Parliament constituency)">Hamilton North and Bellshill</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pamela_Nash" title="Pamela Nash">Pamela Nash</a> was MP for Airdrie and Shotts, elected at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010 general election</a> and was the youngest member of the House of Commons at that time. The location has been represented by several prominent Labour MPs in recent years:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Smith_(Labour_Party_leader)" title="John Smith (Labour Party leader)">John Smith</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> for North Lanarkshire 1970–1983 and Monklands East 1983–1994 (Eastern Coatbridge and Airdrie area). Former <a href="/wiki/Shadow_Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Shadow Chancellor of the Exchequer">Shadow Chancellor</a> and then leader of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> until his untimely death in 1994.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Helen_Liddell" title="Helen Liddell">Helen Liddell</a>, MP for Monklands East 1994–1997, Airdrie and Shotts 1997–2005, <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Scotland" title="Secretary of State for Scotland">Secretary of State for Scotland</a> and subsequently Britain's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_High_Commissioners_from_the_United_Kingdom_to_Australia" title="List of High Commissioners from the United Kingdom to Australia">High Commissioner to Australia</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Reid,_Baron_Reid_of_Cardowan" title="John Reid, Baron Reid of Cardowan">John Reid</a>, MP for Airdrie and Shotts 2005–2010, a high-profile minister including as the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a> to be appointed <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Northern_Ireland" title="Secretary of State for Northern Ireland">Secretary of State for Northern Ireland</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The current MP for the constituency is the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_National_Party%27s&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Scottish National Party's (page does not exist)">Scottish National Party's</a> Neil Grey. He won the seat in 2015's general election from the Labour party, which was always seen as a safe seat for Labour.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_Parliament">Scottish Parliament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Scottish Parliament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For the purposes of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>, Airdrie forms part of the <a href="/wiki/Airdrie_and_Shotts_(Scottish_Parliament_constituency)" title="Airdrie and Shotts (Scottish Parliament constituency)">Airdrie and Shotts</a> constituency. This has slightly different boundaries than that of the UK Parliament constituency of the same name. The current member of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> for <a href="/wiki/Airdrie_and_Shotts_(Scottish_Parliament_constituency)" title="Airdrie and Shotts (Scottish Parliament constituency)">Airdrie and Shotts</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alex_Neil_MSP" title="Alex Neil MSP">Alex Neil MSP</a> (<a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a>), who won this seat in the 2011 Scottish Parliament from the LAbour Party who had held the seat since the instatement of the Scottish Parliament in 1999.</p>
<p>In addition to this Airdrie is represented by seven <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_constituencies_and_regions" title="Scottish Parliament constituencies and regions">regional MSPs</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Central_Scotland_(Scottish_Parliament_electoral_region)" title="Central Scotland (Scottish Parliament electoral region)">Central Scotland electoral region</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> They are: <a href="/wiki/Linda_Fabiani" title="Linda Fabiani">Linda Fabiani</a> (<a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a>, SNP), <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Hepburn" title="Jamie Hepburn">Jamie Hepburn</a> (SNP), <a href="/wiki/Christina_McKelvie" title="Christina McKelvie">Christina McKelvie</a> (SNP), <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Mitchell_(Scottish_politician)" title="Margaret Mitchell (Scottish politician)">Margaret Mitchell</a> (<a href="/wiki/Scottish_Conservative_Party" title="Scottish Conservative Party">Scottish Conservative Party</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alex_Neil_MSP" title="Alex Neil MSP">Alex Neil</a> (SNP), <a href="/wiki/Hugh_O%27Donnell_(politician)" title="Hugh O'Donnell (politician)">Hugh O'Donnell</a> (<a href="/wiki/Scottish_Liberal_Democrats" title="Scottish Liberal Democrats">Scottish Liberal Democrats</a>), and <a href="/wiki/John_Wilson_(Scottish_politician)" title="John Wilson (Scottish politician)">John Wilson</a> (SNP).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_government">Local government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Local government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Up until 1975, Airdrie had its own <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Burgh_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Burgh Council (page does not exist)">Burgh Council</a>. Between 1975 and 1996, Airdrie came under <a href="/wiki/Monklands_(district)" title="Monklands (district)">Monklands District Council</a> operating in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Strathclyde" title="Strathclyde">Strathclyde Regional Council</a>. Monklands District Council was headquartered in the Coatbridge Municipal Building. Many Airdrieonians felt short-changed by MDC's actions and a significant political scandal known as <a href="/wiki/Monklandsgate" title="Monklandsgate">Monklandsgate</a> greatly tarnished the Council's reputation. After 1996, it came under the authority of the unitary <a href="/wiki/North_Lanarkshire" title="North Lanarkshire">North Lanarkshire Council</a>. Currently, the council is in control of the Labour group and the leader of the council is <a href="/wiki/Jim_McCabe" title="Jim McCabe">Jim McCabe</a>, councillor for Thorniewood.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Police">Police</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Police">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Policing in Airdrie is undertaken by <a href="/wiki/Police_Scotland" title="Police Scotland">Police Scotland</a>. Airdrie is part of Coatbridge Area Command<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Inspector" title="Chief Inspector">Chief Inspector</a> Kenny MacLeod as the Area Commander. Airdrie also forms part of NA (or Monklands) sub division which includes Coatbridge and the surrounding area. There is one police office in Airdrie and this is open 24 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sheriff_Court">Sheriff Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Sheriff Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie_Sheriff_Court&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Airdrie Sheriff Court (page does not exist)">Airdrie Sheriff Court</a> provides a comprehensive local court service for the area including civil actions and criminal cases. It is administered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Court_Service" title="Scottish Court Service">Scottish Court Service</a> and part of the South Strathclyde, Dumfries and Galloway <a href="/wiki/Sheriffdom" title="Sheriffdom">Sheriffdom</a> headed by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sheriff_Principal" title="Sheriff Principal">Sheriff Principal</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As outlined in the history section Airdrie's traditional economic activities of weaving, coal mining, and heavy industry have ceased to exist. Whilst the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glenflagler" title="Glenflagler">Glenflagler</a> Distillery is now closed the town still retains a strong involvement in the <a href="/wiki/Whisky" title="Whisky">whisky</a> industry. Airdrie was also home to a <a href="/wiki/Crimpy" title="Crimpy">Crimpy</a> Crisps factory. It is fair to say that, given its location near to Glasgow and other commercial or industrial areas, Airdrie might now be considered something of a commuter town. In fact housing construction in Airdrie has been very prominent in recent years with builders developing a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brownfield" title="Brownfield">brownfield</a> sites following the closure of various factories such as Boots who closed their factory in 2004. Nonetheless it does retain significant economic activity.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Airdrie_railway_station" title="Airdrie railway station">Airdrie railway station</a> is on the electrified <a href="/wiki/North_Clyde_Line" title="North Clyde Line">North Clyde Line</a>. This railway provides a frequent train service to <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> via <a href="/wiki/Coatbridge_Sunnyside_railway_station" title="Coatbridge Sunnyside railway station">Coatbridge Sunnyside</a> and <a href="/wiki/Easterhouse_railway_station" title="Easterhouse railway station">Easterhouse</a>. In 2010, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airdrie-Bathgate_Rail_Link" title="Airdrie-Bathgate Rail Link">Airdrie-Bathgate Rail Link</a> re-opened providing Airdrie with a direct commuter train service to <a href="/wiki/Bathgate_railway_station" title="Bathgate railway station">Bathgate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Livingston_North_railway_station" title="Livingston North railway station">Livingston North</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Waverley_railway_station" title="Edinburgh Waverley railway station">Edinburgh Waverley</a>. <a href="/wiki/Drumgelloch_railway_station" title="Drumgelloch railway station">Drumgelloch railway station</a> serves the eastern end of the town.</p>
<p>Airdrie has road links to <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Livingston,_West_Lothian" title="Livingston, West Lothian">Livingston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motherwell" title="Motherwell">Motherwell</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cumbernauld" title="Cumbernauld">Cumbernauld</a> and is situated close to the <a href="/wiki/M8_motorway_(Scotland)" title="M8 motorway (Scotland)">M8 motorway</a>. Bus services are largely undertaken by local operators, and links to Glasgow are provided by <a href="/wiki/Arriva" title="Arriva">Arriva</a>, <a href="/wiki/First_Glasgow" title="First Glasgow">First Glasgow</a> and <a href="/wiki/McGill%27s_Bus_Services" title="McGill's Bus Services">McGills</a>.</p>
<p>Airdrie is connected to the UK <a href="/wiki/National_Cycle_Network" title="National Cycle Network">National Cycle Network</a> by <a href="/wiki/National_Cycle_Route_75" title="National Cycle Route 75">National Cycle Route 75</a>. This route provides a path between Glasgow and Edinburgh. According to the Sustrans website, this path has yet to fully reopen <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.sustrans.org.uk/what-we-do/national-cycle-network/route-numbering-system/route-75" rel="nofollow">[2]</a>. Other than the Sustrans path, there are no cycle lanes in Airdrie.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Healthcare">Healthcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airdrie,_North_Lanarkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Healthcare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><a href="/wiki/NHS_Lanarkshire" title="NHS Lanarkshire">NHS Lanarkshire</a> is responsible for the healthcare of Airdrie residents. Airdrie is home to <a href="/wiki/Monklands_Hospital" title="Monklands Hospital">Monklands District General Hospital</a> with a 24-hour <a href="/wiki/Emergency_department" title="Emergency department">Accident &amp; Emergency</a> department. The hospital has over 400 beds and provides a comprehensive service with specialist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal" title="Renal">renal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_diseases" title="Infectious diseases">infectious diseases</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otolaryngology" title="Otolaryngology">ENT</a> departments. Wester Moffat Hospital provides long term care for the elderly.</p>
<p>Adjacent to Monklands Hospital is Maggie's Lanarkshire, part of the renowned <a href="/wiki/Maggie%27s_Centres" title="Maggie's Centres">Maggie's Centres</a> cancer support charity whilst the <a href="/wiki/Beatson_West_of_Scotland_Cancer_Centre" title="Beatson West of Scotland Cancer Centre">Beatson</a> Lanarkshire cancer treatment centre opened in 2015. St Andrew's Hospice is a palliative care unit with a strong emphasis on cancer care. It is operated by the <a href="/wiki/Sisters_of_Charity" title="Sisters of Charity">Sisters of Charity</a> and partly funded by NHS Lanarkshire.</p>
<p>The West Central division of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Ambulance_Service" title="Scottish Ambulance Service">Scottish Ambulance Service</a> provides accident and emergency, and patient transport services for the town.</p>
<p>The Parkinson's Self Help Group (North Lanarkshire) is based in the Weavers' Cottages (1780) and provides support for people with <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a>.</p>
<p>The SVGCA – Houses for Heroes, a charity providing suitable accommodation for permanently disabled armed forces, police and fire service personnel, maintains four houses in the town.</p>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Association_for_Mental_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Scottish Association for Mental Health (page does not exist)">Scottish Association for Mental Health</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lanarkshire_Association_for_Mental_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lanarkshire Association for Mental Health (page does not exist)">Lanarkshire Association for Mental Health</a> maintain offices in the town.</p>
<p>In addition there are several medical practices including the state of the art Airdrie Community Health Centre, dental surgeries, nursing homes and opticians throughout Airdrie.</p>
