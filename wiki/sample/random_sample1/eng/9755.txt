<p>The <b>Aeron chair</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Office_chair" title="Office chair">office chair</a> designed in 1994 by <a href="/wiki/Don_Chadwick" title="Don Chadwick">Don Chadwick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Stumpf" title="Bill Stumpf">Bill Stumpf</a>. It has been called "America's best-selling chair" (as of 2010)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> and is featured in the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a>'s permanent collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeron_chair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The chair was reportedly named after the Celtic god <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aeron_(Celtic_mythology)" title="Aeron (Celtic mythology)">Aeron</a>, as well as referring to <a href="/wiki/Aeration" title="Aeration">aeration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Market">Market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeron_chair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aeron chairs are produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herman_Miller_(office_equipment)" title="Herman Miller (office equipment)">Herman Miller</a> and were popular with <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">web</a> <a href="/wiki/Startup_company" title="Startup company">startups</a> during the late 1990s, when they were associated with the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>. A 2006 article in <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_(magazine)" title="New York (magazine)">New York</a></i> magazine called the Aeron "the Dot-Com Throne", writing that the chairs "became shorthand for the countless companies that didn't have a clue how to make money on the Internet, but, man, did they have the know-how to set up a cool office".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeron_chair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As described by <a href="/wiki/Galen_Cranz" title="Galen Cranz">Galen Kranz</a>, "one of the secrets of the success of that chair was finding that fabric they called ‘<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pellicle_(material)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pellicle (material) (page does not exist)">pellicle</a>.’ That sheer but resistant fabric hit on the right gestalt for where our culture was at."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup> The Aeron chair is made out of recycled materials, and 94 percent of the chair itself is recyclable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ergonomics">Ergonomics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aeron_chair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ergonomics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to a 2010 <a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_Businessweek" title="Bloomberg Businessweek">Bloomberg Businessweek</a> article, the Aeron chair "made a fetish of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lumbar_support" title="Lumbar support">lumbar support</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Galen Kranz has commented that while the company is aware that a perching position (facilitated by the chair's rounded front rail) is preferable, it put in the lumbar support to conform to public expectations - "because that’s what people think is required for it to be a scientifically 'good' chair".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sitting expert <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A._C._Mandal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A. C. Mandal (page does not exist)">A. C. Mandal</a> has criticized the Aeron as "far too low" and not offering enough height adjustment and opportunities for the sitter to move.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> According to the chair's co-designer Don Chadwick, the task given to him by Herman Miller was to update the company's previous best-seller, not to design the ideal chair for an <a href="/wiki/Eight-hour_day" title="Eight-hour day">eight-hour workday</a>. "We were given a brief and basically told to design the next-generation office chair," Chadwick said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
