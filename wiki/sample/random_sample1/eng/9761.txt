<p>The <b>Airbus A300-600ST</b> (Super Transporter) or <a href="/wiki/Beluga_whale" title="Beluga whale">Beluga</a>, is a version of the standard <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A300" title="Airbus A300">A300-600</a> <a href="/wiki/Wide-body_aircraft" title="Wide-body aircraft">wide-body</a> <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliner</a> modified to carry aircraft parts and oversized cargo. It received the official name of <i>Super Transporter</i> early on; however, the name <i><a href="/wiki/Beluga_whale" title="Beluga whale">Beluga</a></i>, a large whale, gained popularity and has since been officially adopted. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beluga_XL" title="Beluga XL">Beluga XL</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A330" title="Airbus A330">Airbus A330</a> with similar modifications and dimensions, is being developed by Airbus to replace the type around 2020.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airbus_Beluga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airbus_Beluga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aircraft_manufacturers" title="Aircraft manufacturers">aircraft manufacturers</a> are multinational, and it is not unusual for them to have plants in widely separated locations. <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a> is unique in that although it is today a standalone multinational corporation, it was originally a consortium formed by the major British, French, German, and Spanish aerospace companies. The geographic location of Airbus manufacturing is not only influenced by cost and convenience; it is also a matter of aviation history and national interest. Historically, each of the Airbus partners makes an entire aircraft section, which would then be transported to a central location for final assembly; even after the integration of Airbus into a single firm, the arrangement remained largely the same, with Airbus partners becoming subsidiaries or contractors of the multinational pan-European company. The details vary from one model to another, but the general arrangement is for the wings and landing gear to be made in the UK, the tail and doors in Spain, the fuselage in Germany, and the nose and centre-section in France, with final assembly in either <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">Toulouse</a>, France; <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, Germany; or <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>, Spain.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SuperGuppy-F-BPPA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="530" data-file-width="1024" height="114" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/SuperGuppy-F-BPPA.jpg/220px-SuperGuppy-F-BPPA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/SuperGuppy-F-BPPA.jpg/330px-SuperGuppy-F-BPPA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/SuperGuppy-F-BPPA.jpg/440px-SuperGuppy-F-BPPA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SuperGuppy-F-BPPA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Aero_Spacelines_Super_Guppy" title="Aero Spacelines Super Guppy">Super Guppy</a> F-BPPA, 1981</div>

<p>When Airbus started in 1970, road vehicles were initially used for the movement of components and sections; however, growth in production volume soon necessitated a switch to air transport. From 1972 onwards, a fleet of four highly modified "<a href="/wiki/Aero_Spacelines_Super_Guppy" title="Aero Spacelines Super Guppy">Super Guppies</a>" took over. These were former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_Stratocruiser" title="Boeing Stratocruiser">Boeing Stratocruisers</a> from the 1940s that had been converted with custom fuselages and the adoption of turbine engines to carry large volume loads for <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s space program in the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_46_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-king_46-2">[2]</a></sup> Airbus' use of the Super Guppies led to the jest that "every Airbus is delivered on the wings of a Boeing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-on_the_wings_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-on_the_wings-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-big_ideas_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-big_ideas-4">[4]</a></sup> As time went on, the Super Guppies grew increasingly unsatisfactory for Airbus's ferrying needs: their age meant that operating expenses were high and ever-increasing, and growing Airbus production required greater capacity than could be provided by the existing fleet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_46_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-king_46-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moxon_33_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-moxon_33-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Various options were studied to serve as a replacement transport medium for the Super Guppies, including methods of surface transportation by road, rail, and sea; these alternatives were discarded in favor of a principally air-based solution as they were considered to have reliability concerns and were time-consuming in operation; in addition, the assembly line in <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">Toulouse</a> was not conveniently accessible by any of the surface methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A key requirement of the new air transporter was the need to accommodate every major component being manufactured by Airbus, including the then-heaviest planned part, that being the wing of the larger variants of the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A340" title="Airbus A340">Airbus A340</a>. A speedy development program was also necessitated in order to begin introducing the prospective type in time to take over duties from the Super Guppy fleet, which was scheduled to draw down in the mid-1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moxon_33_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-moxon_33-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several different types of aircraft were examined for potential use, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonov_An-124" title="Antonov An-124">Antonov An-124</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonov_An-225" title="Antonov An-225">Antonov An-225</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ilyushin_Il-86" title="Ilyushin Il-86">Ilyushin Il-86</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boeing_767" title="Boeing 767">Boeing 767</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_C-5_Galaxy" title="Lockheed C-5 Galaxy">Lockheed C-5 Galaxy</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_C-17_Globemaster_III" title="McDonnell Douglas C-17 Globemaster III">McDonnell Douglas C-17 Globemaster III</a>; the use of any existing aircraft was eventually discounted due to a lack of internal space to accommodate the desired components, the use of a <a href="/wiki/Piggyback_(transportation)#Air_transport" title="Piggyback (transportation)">piggyback</a> arrangement was also dismissed as impractical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moxon_33_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-moxon_33-5">[5]</a></sup> Boeing made their own offer to convert several Boeing 767s for the requirement, but this was viewed as inferior to developing a purpose-built aircraft using Airbus' existing wide-body twin-engined <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A300" title="Airbus A300">Airbus A300-600R</a> instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moxon_33_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-moxon_33-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 1991, <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9rospatiale" title="Aérospatiale">Aérospatiale</a> and <a href="/wiki/DASA" title="DASA">DASA</a>, two of the major Airbus partners, formed a 50/50 joint venture company, <i>Super Airbus Transport International</i> (SATIC), based in <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">Toulouse</a>, France, to develop a new-build replacement for the Super Guppy fleet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_46_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-king_46-2">[2]</a></sup> The selected starting point for the design was the Airbus A300, leading to the new airframe being designated as the A300-600ST Super Transporter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_46_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-king_46-2">[2]</a></sup> Following a pre-design period by SATIC, detailed design work was performed by Aérospatiale and DASA while subcontractors were selected to complete the 15 separate work packages; amongst these subcontractors, <a href="/wiki/Construcciones_Aeron%C3%A1uticas_SA" title="Construcciones Aeronáuticas SA">CASA</a> was selected to produce the upper fuselage, <a href="/wiki/Dornier_Flugzeugwerke" title="Dornier Flugzeugwerke">Dornier</a> provided the <a href="/wiki/Hydraulics" title="Hydraulics">hydraulic</a> systems, and <a href="/wiki/Stelia_Aerospace" title="Stelia Aerospace">Sogerma</a> performed the final assembly work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The A300-600ST was not a like-for-like replacement, being larger, faster, and more efficient than the preceding Super Guppies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_46_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-king_46-2">[2]</a></sup> Airbus Industries elected to invest $1 billion into the program, this sum included the aircraft themselves, the cargo loading system, and program management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moxon_35_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-moxon_35-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Entry_into_service_and_replacement">Entry into service and replacement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airbus_Beluga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Entry into service and replacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In September 1992, construction work begun on the first aircraft; the <a href="/wiki/Maiden_flight" title="Maiden flight">maiden flight</a> of which took place in September 1994. Following a total of 335 flight hours being performed during the test program, restricted certification of the type was awarded by the <a href="/wiki/European_Aviation_Safety_Agency" title="European Aviation Safety Agency">European Aviation Safety Agency</a> (EASA) in October 1995, enabling the A300-600ST "Beluga" to enter service shortly thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In addition to the first aircraft, four more Belugas were constructed at a rate of roughly one per year; from start to finish, each airframe reportedly took roughly three years to complete. Modification work was performed at Toulouse using components provided by the Airbus assembly line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_46_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-king_46-2">[2]</a></sup> Originally a total of four aircraft were to be built along with an option for a fifth aircraft being available, which was later firmed up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_46_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-king_46-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moxon_35_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-moxon_35-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The fleet's primary task is to carry Airbus components ready for final assembly across Europe between Toulouse, Hamburg and nine other sites, and they do so 60 times per week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_46_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-king_46-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The Beluga fleet is owned by Airbus Transport International (ATI), a wholly owned subsidiary of Airbus Group that was established specifically to operate the type; through this organization, the fleet is made available for hire by third parties for <a href="/wiki/Charter" title="Charter">charter</a> flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-giz_mag_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-giz_mag-11">[11]</a></sup> In May 1998, ATI reportedly had an annual revenue target of $15 million to be achieved via leasing spare capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_46_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-king_46-2">[2]</a></sup> Over time, the Beluga has been used to carry a variety of special loads, including space station components, large and delicate artwork, industrial machinery, and intact helicopters. The A300-600ST's freight compartment is 7.4 m (24 ft) in diameter and 37.7 m (124 ft) long; maximum payload is 47 tonnes.</p>
<p>In late 1997, in response to the positive performance of the A300-600ST Super Transporter program, SATIC announced that they were in the process of evaluating several different prospective outsize freighter conversions; company chairman Udo Dräger indicated that a larger freighter based on the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A340" title="Airbus A340">Airbus A340</a> could be developed in a similar manner to that of the A300-based Beluga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_47_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-king_47-12">[12]</a></sup> At the time, derivatives of both the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A330" title="Airbus A330">Airbus A330</a> and the A340 were being studied, including combining the upward-swinging hinged door of the Beluga with a conventional upper deck as an alternative to a side-mounted cargo door in traditional freighter missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_47_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-king_47-12">[12]</a></sup> During the 1990s, as a result of reported inquiries to Airbus regarding the type, a niche market for selling Beluga-type aircraft to military customers and freight operators was also examined; but sales were considered 'unlikely' to take place by the late 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moxon_35_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-moxon_35-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_47_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-king_47-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In November 2014, Airbus announced that it was proceeding with the development of a larger replacement based on the Airbus A330-200; five of which will start operating from 2019, replacing the last old Beluga in 2025.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Airbus previously considered the A330-300 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A340-500" title="A340-500">A340-500</a>, but each require too much of the limited 1,663 m (5,460 ft) runway 04 at <a href="/wiki/Hawarden_Airport" title="Hawarden Airport">Hawarden Airport</a> near <a href="/wiki/Broughton,_Flintshire" title="Broughton, Flintshire">Broughton in Wales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In May 2015, Airbus confirmed that the new aircraft will have a 1 m (3.3 ft) wider cross-section than its predecessor and provide a 12% increase in payload. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beluga_XL" title="Beluga XL">Beluga XL</a> is intended primarily for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airbus_A350XWB" title="Airbus A350XWB">A350</a> work, and is designed with capacity to ship two A350 wings simultaneously. Five aircraft will be progressively assembled; the first two are considered essential to ramping-up production of the A350; the remaining three are to be introduced as the A300-600 Beluga fleet retires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airbus_Beluga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The A300-600ST Beluga shares many design similarities, although substantially differing in appearance, to the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A300" title="Airbus A300">Airbus A300</a> it was based upon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_46_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-king_46-2">[2]</a></sup> The wings, engines, landing gear, and the lower part of the fuselage remain identical to those used on the conventional A300, while the upper part of the fuselage forms an enormous horseshoe-shaped structure 7.7 m (25 ft) in diameter. In comparison with the Super Guppy, the payload was more than doubled and the volume increased by more than 30 per cent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_47_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-king_47-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_CF6#CF6-80C2" title="General Electric CF6">General Electric CF6-80C2</a> <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> engines used are slightly uprated from those used on the standard A300 as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moxon_35_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-moxon_35-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Vertical_stabilizer" title="Vertical stabilizer">vertical stabilizer</a> uses a modified <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A340" title="Airbus A340">Airbus A340</a> fin with a 1.12 m (3 ft 8 in) base extension while the <a href="/wiki/Tailplane" title="Tailplane">tailplane</a> was strengthened and fitted with auxiliary fins to maintain directional stability. The tailplane trim tank was also deleted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_46_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-king_46-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-on_the_wings_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-on_the_wings-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>To provide access to the cargo area from the front without having to disconnect all electrical, hydraulic and flight control connections (and also avoiding the lengthy recalibrations before each flight that reconnection entailed), the standard A300 cockpit was relocated down below the cargo floor level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moxon_35_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-moxon_35-8">[8]</a></sup> By relocating the cockpit in this manner, loading times were reportedly halved from those encountered using the Super Guppy, which had needed to disconnect and reconnect such systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_47_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-king_47-12">[12]</a></sup> Another reason for faster loading times was the adoption of a <a href="/wiki/Roll-on/roll-off" title="Roll-on/roll-off">roll-on/roll-off</a> loading system, as well as the ability to unload the aircraft in winds of up to 40 knots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_47_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-king_47-12">[12]</a></sup> The qualities and improved capabilities of the Beluga resulted in the costs associated with transporting Airbus components dropping to only one-third of those achieved by the Super Guppy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moxon_32_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-moxon_32-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cockpit of the Beluga is pressurized but the cargo deck is not, making it inaccessible during flight and unsuitable for cargoes that require a pressurized environment, such as live animals. However, the cargo deck is fitted with a heating module to keep the cargo within an appropriate temperature range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn2015_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnn2015-19">[19]</a></sup> On at least one occasion, a pressurized container has been used for cargo (<i>see <a href="/wiki/Airbus_Beluga#Operational_history" title="Airbus Beluga">Operational history</a></i>). The aircraft is operated by a crew of three - two pilots and a loadmaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn2015_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-cnn2015-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main deck cargo volume of the Beluga is greater than that of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-5_Galaxy" title="C-5 Galaxy">C-5 Galaxy</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonov_An-124" title="Antonov An-124">Antonov An-124</a> but still smaller than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonov_An-225" title="Antonov An-225">Antonov An-225</a>. However, it is restricted by cargo weight capacity of 47 tonnes, compared to 122.5 tonnes for the C-5 Galaxy and 150 tonnes for the An-124.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-on_the_wings_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-on_the_wings-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Despite this width, the Beluga cannot carry most fuselage parts of the <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">Airbus A380</a>, which are instead normally transported by ship and road. The Beluga has been used to transport some A380 components.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airbus_Beluga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In January 1996, the Beluga was formally placed into dedicated service, ferrying components from various aerospace sites to the final assembly lines in <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">Toulouse</a>, France and <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-giz_mag_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-giz_mag-11">[11]</a></sup> On 24 October 1997, the last of Airbus Industrie's Super Guppy freighters was retired and its outsize cargo mission from that point onwards being exclusively performed by the new A300-600ST fleet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_46_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-king_46-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1997, the second year of Beluga operations, the fleet accumulated in excess of 2,500 flight hours across more than 1,400 flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> By 2012, the fleet was performing roughly 5,000 flight hours per year; Airbus expected this figure to double by 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since entering service, the Beluga has been subject to several infrastructure upgrades. In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Pau_Pyr%C3%A9n%C3%A9es_Airport" title="Pau Pyrénées Airport">Pau Pyrénées Airport</a>, a site routinely visited by Belugas, became the first airport in Europe to deploy the <a href="/wiki/European_Geostationary_Navigation_Overlay_Service" title="European Geostationary Navigation Overlay Service">European Geostationary Navigation Overlay Service</a>, which accurately guides the Beluga and other aircraft during landing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2015, a dedicated Beluga loading station was opened at <a href="/wiki/Hawarden_Airport" title="Hawarden Airport">Hawarden Airport</a>, preventing high winds from disrupting future operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to its primary supply duties to Airbus' production facilities, Belugas have often performed charter flights for various purposes. In 1997, ATI claimed that it had to reject eight out of ten requests for commercial Beluga flights, the fleet only being able to spare 130 flight hours for such duties that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_47_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-king_47-12">[12]</a></sup> But as more Beluga aircraft were put into service, availability increased drastically, rising to 600 flight hours in 1998 and around 1,000 flight hours in 1999; this effectively enabled one of the five Beluga aircraft to spend much of its operating hours performing charter flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_47_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-king_47-12">[12]</a></sup> Amongst the early customers chartering Beluga flights was Boeing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_47_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-king_47-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 1997, a world record was set for the most voluminous payload to be carried by an aircraft when a Beluga was used to transport a chemical tank for a <a href="/wiki/Merchant_vessel" title="Merchant vessel">merchant vessel</a> from <a href="/wiki/Clermont-Ferrand" title="Clermont-Ferrand">Clermont-Ferrand</a> to <a href="/wiki/Le_Havre" title="Le Havre">Le Havre</a>, France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_47_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-king_47-12">[12]</a></sup> In February 2003, a single Beluga performed the farthest distance charter flight ever, having flown for 25 hours (not including refueling stops) to transport two complete <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NHI_NH90" title="NHI NH90">NHI NH90</a> helicopters along with a single <a href="/wiki/Eurocopter_Tiger" title="Eurocopter Tiger">Eurocopter Tiger</a> attack helicopter from <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a>, France to <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, Australia, for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avalon_Airshow" title="Avalon Airshow">Avalon Airshow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-giz_mag_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-giz_mag-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1999, a Beluga carried a large painting, <i><a href="/wiki/Liberty_Leading_the_People" title="Liberty Leading the People">Liberty Leading the People</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eugène Delacroix">Eugène Delacroix</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hibeam_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hibeam-26">[26]</a></sup> which had hung in the <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a> in Paris since 1874. It was flown from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris-Charles_de_Gaulle_Airport" title="Paris-Charles de Gaulle Airport">Paris</a> to <a href="/wiki/Narita_International_Airport" title="Narita International Airport">Tokyo</a> via <a href="/wiki/Bahrain_International_Airport" title="Bahrain International Airport">Bahrain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Netaji_Subhas_Chandra_Bose_International_Airport" title="Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose International Airport">Kolkata</a> in about 20 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Superguppy_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Superguppy-27">[27]</a></sup> The large canvas, measuring 2.99 metres (9.81 ft) high by 3.62 metres (11.88 ft) long,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Superguppy_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Superguppy-27">[27]</a></sup> was too large to fit into a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a>. It was transported in the vertical position inside a special pressurized container provided with isothermal protection and an anti-vibration device.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Superguppy_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Superguppy-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Beluga has seen recurrent use to transport bulky objects, including vehicles, for various different <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space programs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_47_12-10"><a href="#cite_note-king_47-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2001, sections of the unmanned <a href="/wiki/Automated_Transfer_Vehicle" title="Automated Transfer Vehicle">Automated Transfer Vehicle</a> (ATV) space vehicle were transported by a Beluga from <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">Turin</a>, Italy to <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In 2004, multiple Beluga flights were made to <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, to deliver <a href="/wiki/Astrium" title="Astrium">Astrium</a>-built satellites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-giz_mag_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-giz_mag-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2009, a Beluga was used to convey the <a href="/wiki/Tranquility_(ISS_module)" title="Tranquility (ISS module)">Tranquility module</a> of the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> from <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">Turin</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, a Beluga was used to deliver relief supplies to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> region following widespread devastation of coastal areas by a major <a href="/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2004 Indian Ocean earthquake and tsunami">tsunami</a>. In 2005, the Beluga was employed to transport humanitarian aid and medical supplies from the United Kingdom and France to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> in United States in support of disaster relief operations in the aftermath of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications_.28A300-600ST.29">Specifications (A300-600ST)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Airbus_Beluga&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Specifications (A300-600ST)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Crew:</b> Two<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCDS_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCDS-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Capacity:</b> 1,400 m<sup>3</sup> (49,000 cu ft)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freight_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-freight-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Payload" title="Payload">Payload</a>:</b> 47 t (103,616 lb)</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 56.15 m (184 ft 3 in)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wingspan" title="Wingspan">Wingspan</a>:</b> 44.84 m (147 ft 2 in)</li>
<li><b>Height:</b> 17.24 m (56 ft 7 in)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aspect_ratio_(aeronautics)" title="Aspect ratio (aeronautics)">Aspect ratio</a>:</b> 16.43</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Manufacturer%27s_empty_weight" title="Manufacturer's empty weight">Empty weight</a>:</b> 86.5 t (190698 lb)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Maximum_takeoff_weight" title="Maximum takeoff weight">Max. takeoff weight</a>:</b> 155 t (341,713 lb)</li>
<li><b>Cargo cross-section diameter:</b> 7.1 m (23 ft 4 in) in cargo compartment<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-freight_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-freight-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Maximum landing weight:</b> 140 t (308,644 lb)</li>
<li><b>Maximum zero fuel weight:</b> 133.5 t (294,314 lb)</li>
<li><b>Fuel capacity:</b> 23,860 - 34,430 Litres (6,303 - 9,095 US gal)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engine" title="Aircraft engine">Powerplant</a>:</b> 2 × <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_CF6" title="General Electric CF6">General Electric CF6</a>-80C2A8 turbofan, 25,740 daN<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCDS_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCDS-33">[33]</a></sup> (57,866 lbf) each</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span></p>
<ul>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Vne" title="V speeds">Never exceed speed</a>:</b> Mach 0.82 (473 kn)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Range_(aeronautics)" title="Range (aeronautics)">Range</a>:</b> 2,779 km (1,500 nmi) with 40 ton payload, 4,632 km (2,500 nmi) with 26 ton payload</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(aeronautics)" title="Ceiling (aeronautics)">Service ceiling</a>:</b> 35,000 ft<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCDS_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCDS-33">[33]</a></sup> (10,668 m)</li>
</ul>
