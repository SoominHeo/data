<p><b>AirTran Airways</b> was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Low-cost_carrier" title="Low-cost carrier">low-cost</a> <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airline</a> headquartered originally in <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando, Florida</a>, then in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dallas,_Texas" title="Dallas, Texas">Dallas, Texas</a>, after its acquisition by <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a>, into which it was integrated. AirTran operated nearly 700 daily flights, primarily in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_United_States" title="Eastern United States">eastern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">midwestern United States</a>, with its principal hub at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hartsfield-Jackson_Atlanta_International_Airport" title="Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport">Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport</a> where it operated nearly 200 daily departures. AirTran's fleet consisted of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_717-200" title="Boeing 717-200">Boeing 717-200</a> aircraft, of which it was the world's largest operator, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_737-700" title="Boeing 737-700">Boeing 737-700</a> aircraft. It was fully integrated into <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> on December 28, 2014.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation_.26_early_years">Foundation &amp; early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AirTran_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foundation &amp; early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/ValuJet_Airlines" title="ValuJet Airlines">ValuJet Airlines</a></div>
<p>The original AirTran Airways, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_737-200" title="Boeing 737-200">Boeing 737-200</a> operator with service to/from Orlando, was founded by AirTran Corporation, the holding company of <a href="/wiki/Mesaba_Airlines" title="Mesaba Airlines">Mesaba Airlines</a> of Minneapolis, Minnesota, operating as a <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlink" title="Northwest Airlink">Northwest Airlink</a> carrier with hubs in Minneapolis and Detroit. In 1994, AirTran Holdings purchased a start up 737 operator named <b>Conquest Sun</b> and renamed the airline AirTran Airways. Conquest Sun, similar to ValuJet, was an airline started by former <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Air_Lines" title="Eastern Air Lines">Eastern Air Lines</a> employees. The original AirTran Airways moved its headquarters to Orlando, Florida, and grew to 11 Boeing 737 aircraft serving 24 cities in the East and Midwest providing low-fare leisure travel to Orlando. In 1995, AirTran Airways was spun off by Mesaba and formed its own independent holding company named Airways Corporation.</p>
<p>On July 10, 1997, ValuJet, Inc., the holding company for ValuJet Airlines, Inc., announced plans to acquire Airways Corporation, Inc., the holding company for AirTran Airways, Inc. of Orlando, Florida. The deal was closed on November 17, 1997.</p>
<p>Following two serious accidents (flight 597 and <a href="/wiki/ValuJet_Flight_592" title="ValuJet Flight 592">flight 592</a>), both blamed on a lax corporate culture on safety at ValuJet,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> on September 24, 1997, ValuJet Airlines changed its name to AirTran Airlines, by, on November 17, 1997, acquiring Airways, Inc. and renaming the holding company AirTran Holdings, Inc. In the summer of 1998, the two airlines merged onto the same FAA certificate under the AirTran name. While the hub remained in Atlanta, the headquarters of the new entity was combined in Orlando, Florida, on January 28, 1998. While ValuJet was the nominal survivor and the merged company retained ValuJet's stock price history, it decided to take a new name to distance itself from its troubled past.</p>
<p>In January 1999, a new management team led by Joe Leonard, a veteran of Eastern Air Lines and <a href="/wiki/Robert_L._Fornaro" title="Robert L. Fornaro">Robert L. Fornaro</a>, of US Airways, took the reins at the airline. The two recruited a new senior management team including Stephen J. Kolski, Operations, Kevin P. Healy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Planning, and Loral Blinde, Human Resources. The immediate goals were to stabilize the balance sheet and prepare to refinance debt due in early 2000, fix the operations, increase and establish revenue streams and prepare for delivery and operation of the Boeing 717. AirTran was the launch customer and ultimately the largest operator of this brand new aircraft. At the same time, Leonard was determined to not only lead the turn around of the carrier, but establish a culture of trust and entrepreneurship at AirTran.</p>

<p>AirTran reported a $30 million operating profit for 1999.</p>
<p>On August 15, 2001, the company's stock began trading under the ticker symbol AAI on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>.</p>
<p>In 2002, AirTran created a regional brand, <a href="/wiki/AirTran_JetConnect" title="AirTran JetConnect">AirTran JetConnect</a>, operated by <a href="/wiki/Air_Wisconsin" title="Air Wisconsin">Air Wisconsin</a>.</p>
<p>In 2003, following an order for 100 <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737</a> aircraft, AirTran began service to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reagan_National_Airport" title="Reagan National Airport">Reagan National Airport</a> and to <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>.</p>
<p>On January 5, 2004, AirTran's last <a href="/wiki/McDonnell_Douglas_DC-9" title="McDonnell Douglas DC-9">McDonnell Douglas DC-9</a> was retired, leaving it with a fleet of more than 70 Boeing 717s. Shortly after, the first Boeing 737 entered AirTran's fleet in June 2004.</p>
<p>In August 2004, AirTran JetConnect (operated by Air Wisconsin) ceased all operations.</p>
<p>On May 23, 2006, AirTran accepted one of the last two Boeing 717s delivered in a ceremony with <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Airlines" title="Midwest Airlines">Midwest Airlines</a>, who accepted the other 717.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Boeing closed the 717 line due to an overlap with the 737.</p>
<p>In November 2007, <a href="/wiki/Robert_L._Fornaro" title="Robert L. Fornaro">Robert L. Fornaro</a> took over as CEO, as well as President.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Joe Leonard remained Chairman of the Board of Directors until June 2008. Upon his retirement, Fornaro then became Chairman making him Chairman, President and CEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airtranairways.com_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-airtranairways.com-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009, AirTran was the first major airline to have 100% of its fleet outfitted with <a href="/wiki/Gogo_Inflight_Internet" title="Gogo Inflight Internet">Gogo Inflight Internet</a>, although other airlines had begun adding Internet before AirTran.</p>
<p>By 2009 AirTran underwent major expansion in smaller cities such as <a href="/wiki/Yeager_Airport" title="Yeager Airport">Yeager Airport</a> (Charleston, WV); <a href="/wiki/Asheville_Regional_Airport" title="Asheville Regional Airport">Asheville Regional Airport</a>, NC; and <a href="/wiki/Harrisburg_International_Airport" title="Harrisburg International Airport">Harrisburg International Airport</a>, PA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Failed_acquisitions">Failed acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AirTran_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Failed acquisitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/AirTran_Holdings#Proposed_AirTran_Holding_acquisition_of_Midwest_Airlines_Group" title="AirTran Holdings">AirTran Holdings § Proposed AirTran Holding acquisition of Midwest Airlines Group</a></div>
<p>In 2004, AirTran sought a major expansion at Chicago-Midway Airport by buying the leases of <a href="/wiki/ATA_Airlines" title="ATA Airlines">ATA Airlines</a>' 14 gates. <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> made a higher bid for the gates, and AirTran lost the deal.</p>
<p>In December 2006, Air Tran Holdings announced that it had been trying to acquire <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Air_Group" title="Midwest Air Group">Midwest Air Group</a>. On August 12, 2007, AirTran announced its attempt to purchase <a href="/wiki/Midwest_Airlines" title="Midwest Airlines">Midwest Airlines</a> had expired, while <a href="/wiki/TPG_Capital" title="TPG Capital">TPG Capital</a>, in partnership with <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Airlines" title="Northwest Airlines">Northwest Airlines</a>, had entered into an agreement to purchase Midwest Airlines for an amount larger than the AirTran Airways' proposal. However, on August 14, 2007, AirTran increased its offer to the equivalent of $16.25 a share, slightly more than the $16 a share from TPG Capital investors group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, Midwest announced TPG would increase its offer to $17 per share and a definitive agreement had been reached late on August 16, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>On September 21, 2007, AirTran pilots, represented by the National Pilots Association, rejected the carrier's contract proposal. Two weeks earlier, the pilots voted to dump the union president and vice president. On April 10, 2009, 87% of the pilots at AirTran voted to merge the National Pilots Association with the world's largest pilot union, Air Line Pilots Association (ALPA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AirTran_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>On April 6, 2010 AirTran Airways opened their second crew base, at <a href="/wiki/General_Mitchell_International_Airport" title="General Mitchell International Airport">General Mitchell International Airport</a> in Milwaukee, the same day they officially announced Milwaukee as their second hub.</p>
<p>On July 27, 2010, AirTran Airways hosted the grand opening of their new System Operations Control (SOC) Center at Orlando International Airport. This 16,000-square-foot (1,500 m<sup>2</sup>), $6.9 million state-of-the-art command center serves as the 24-hour nerve center for the entire airline with over 700 flights per day. The company employs more than 1,000 crew members in central Florida at several facilities, including their corporate headquarters, the SOC and a maintenance facility in addition to passengers operations at the airport. After considering putting the SOC Center in Atlanta where AirTran has their largest hub, the decision was made to expand the facility in Orlando adjacent to AirTran’s headquarters.</p>
<p>In October 2010, a new crew base opened at <a href="/wiki/Orlando_International_Airport" title="Orlando International Airport">Orlando International Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The base initially employed 100 pilots, including a chief pilot.</p>
<p>Prior to the winding down of the airline, AirTran grew to serve more than 70 cities coast-to-coast as well as in the Caribbean and Mexico with more than 700 flights per day and over 8,500 crew members serving nearly 25 million passengers per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airtranairways.com_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-airtranairways.com-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Buyout_and_wind-down">Buyout and wind-down</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AirTran_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Buyout and wind-down">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>On September 27, 2010, <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> announced they would acquire AirTran Airways for a total cost of $1.4 billion. The acquisition gave Southwest a significant presence at many of AirTran’s hubs such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hartsfield-Jackson_Atlanta_International_Airport" title="Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport">Atlanta</a> (then the largest U.S. city without Southwest service), <a href="/wiki/General_Mitchell_International_Airport" title="General Mitchell International Airport">Milwaukee</a>, and expanded service in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore/Washington_International_Airport" title="Baltimore/Washington International Airport">Baltimore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orlando_International_Airport" title="Orlando International Airport">Orlando</a>. With the merger, Southwest added international service to several leisure destinations such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canc%C3%BAn,_Mexico" title="Cancún, Mexico">Cancún</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montego_Bay" title="Montego Bay">Montego Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a>. Southwest integrated AirTran's fleet of <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Next_Generation" title="Boeing 737 Next Generation">Boeing 737-700</a> series aircraft into Southwest Airlines brand and livery, and the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_717" title="Boeing 717">Boeing 717</a> fleet was then leased out to <a href="/wiki/Delta_Air_Lines" title="Delta Air Lines">Delta Air Lines</a> starting mid-2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The airlines planned to have the acquisition completed and finalized within two years with the two carriers operating as separate airlines in the interim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The deal closed on May 2, 2011 and a single operating certificate for the combined carrier was achieved March 1, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Total integration of all employee groups between the two carriers was completed in 2015.</p>
<p>On February 14, 2013, <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Airlines" title="Southwest Airlines">Southwest Airlines</a> announced that they had begun <a href="/wiki/Codeshare_agreement" title="Codeshare agreement">codesharing</a> with AirTran. They took the first step on January 26, 2013, by launching shared itineraries in five markets. Southwest continued launching shared itineraries with 39 more markets beginning February 25, 2013. By April 2013, shared itineraries were scheduled to be available in all Southwest and AirTran cities (domestic and international).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>AirTran's final flight was operated with a Boeing 717-200 on November 30, 2014.</p>
<p>Southwest announced that the integration would be completed on December 28, 2014, with AirTran Airways Flight 1 as the final scheduled departure for the airline flying from Atlanta's Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport (ATL) to Tampa International Airport (TPA). The flight used the callsign "Critter" as a nod to ValuJet. This route was ValuJet's first flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_affairs">Corporate affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AirTran_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Corporate affairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the acquisition, the corporate headquarters of AirTran were located in <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The airline moved its headquarters to Orlando in 1994. Prior to that period, the headquarters were in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minneapolis,_Minnesota" title="Minneapolis, Minnesota">Minneapolis, Minnesota</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Employee_relations">Employee relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AirTran_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Employee relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>AirTran adopted an approach to employee recruitment similar to Southwest Airlines with an emphasis on functional skills and relational competence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bamber.2C_G.J..2C_Gittell.2C_J.H..2C_Kochan.2C_T.A._.26_von_Nordenflytch.2C_A._2009_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bamber.2C_G.J..2C_Gittell.2C_J.H..2C_Kochan.2C_T.A._.26_von_Nordenflytch.2C_A._2009-21">[21]</a></sup> The airline had clear job specialization with the expectation of flexibility between jobs as required by day-to-day operational circumstances. AirTran’s training approach involved drawing the link between individual job performance, the airline’s overall financial performance and the importance of achieving high levels of customer service and efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bamber.2C_G.J..2C_Gittell.2C_J.H..2C_Kochan.2C_T.A._.26_von_Nordenflytch.2C_A._2009_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bamber.2C_G.J..2C_Gittell.2C_J.H..2C_Kochan.2C_T.A._.26_von_Nordenflytch.2C_A._2009-21">[21]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cabin">Cabin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AirTran_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cabin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>AirTran Airways operated a two-class configuration featuring Business Class and Economy Class. <a href="/wiki/Business_class" title="Business class">Business class</a> included rows 1–3 and coach began with row 10; rows 4–9 were skipped for numbering purposes and 13 was skipped due to <a href="/wiki/Triskaidekaphobia" title="Triskaidekaphobia">superstition</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Livery">Livery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AirTran_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Livery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>AirTran's <a href="/wiki/Livery" title="Livery">livery</a> was primarily white, with teal on the ventral side. The sections were divided by parallel red and pink stripes, which ran horizontal at the front, and started to curve upward at the wings until they reached the top side of the plane at the back of the vertical stabilizer. The <a href="/wiki/Nacelle" title="Nacelle">nacelles</a> were royal blue, with "airtran.com" written in white Helvetica font. The logo version of "AirTran" was written toward the front on either side in teal above the passenger windows and the vertical stabilizer was teal with a prominent white cursive "A", just like the beginning of the logo.</p>
<p>AirTran Airways also created several special <a href="/wiki/Livery" title="Livery">livery</a> aircraft. They included an aircraft featuring <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a> and <a href="/wiki/Danica_Patrick" title="Danica Patrick">Danica Patrick</a>. AirTran also partnered with the Orlando/Orange County CVB to create a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_717" title="Boeing 717">Boeing 717</a> aircraft emblazoned with a "Say YES to Orlando" logo on each side and a second <a href="/wiki/Boeing_717" title="Boeing 717">Boeing 717</a> saying "Orlando Makes Me Smile," which celebrated AirTran Airways' partnership with the OOCVB to promote travel to the city. The airline also had an aircraft paying tribute to the <a href="/wiki/The_Wizarding_World_of_Harry_Potter_(Universal_Orlando_Resort)" title="The Wizarding World of Harry Potter (Universal Orlando Resort)">Wizarding World of Harry Potter</a> at <a href="/wiki/Universal_Orlando" title="Universal Orlando">Universal Orlando</a>.</p>
<p>Several aircraft featured sports-related <a href="/wiki/Livery" title="Livery">liveries</a>. The teams represented were the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Falcons" title="Atlanta Falcons">Atlanta Falcons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Ravens" title="Baltimore Ravens">Baltimore Ravens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_Colts" title="Indianapolis Colts">Indianapolis Colts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Magic" title="Orlando Magic">Orlando Magic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Brewers" title="Milwaukee Brewers">Milwaukee Brewers</a>.</p>
<p>On February 12, 2010, AirTran Airways celebrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Little_Debbie" title="Little Debbie">Little Debbie</a>'s 50th anniversary by launching a one-of-a-kind, custom-designed <a href="/wiki/Boeing_717" title="Boeing 717">Boeing 717</a>, dubbed Little Debbie 1.</p>
