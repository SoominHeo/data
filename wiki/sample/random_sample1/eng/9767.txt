<p><a href="/wiki/Dom_(title)" title="Dom (title)">Dom</a> <b>Hélder Pessoa Câmara</b> (<small>Portuguese: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Portuguese" title="Help:IPA for Portuguese">[dõ ˈɛwdeɾ peˈsoɐ ˈkɐ̃mɐɾɐ]</a></span>; February 7, 1909 – August 27, 1999) was a Brazilian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> Archbishop. He was the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop" title="Archbishop">Archbishop</a> of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Olinda_e_Recife" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Olinda e Recife">Olinda and Recife</a>, serving from 1964 to 1985, during the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_military_government" title="Brazilian military government">military regime</a> of the country.</p>
<p>An advocate of <a href="/wiki/Liberation_theology" title="Liberation theology">liberation theology</a>, he is remembered for his social and political work for the poor and for Human Rights and democracy during the military regime. Câmara preached for a church closer to the disfavoured people and for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-violence" title="Non-violence">non-violence</a>. He is quoted as having said, "When I give food to the poor, they call me a saint. When I ask why they are poor, they call me a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">communist</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H%C3%A9lder_C%C3%A2mara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was born Hélder Pessoa Câmara in <a href="/wiki/Fortaleza" title="Fortaleza">Fortaleza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cear%C3%A1" title="Ceará">Ceará</a>, in the poor <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Region,_Brazil" title="Northeast Region, Brazil">Northeast Region</a> of Brazil. His father was an accountant and his mother was a primary school teacher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-2">[2]</a></sup> He was educated in local Catholic schools and entereded seminary in 1923.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H%C3%A9lder_C%C3%A2mara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Helder_Camara_1981.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2445" data-file-width="3168" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Helder_Camara_1981.jpg/280px-Helder_Camara_1981.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Helder_Camara_1981.jpg/420px-Helder_Camara_1981.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Helder_Camara_1981.jpg/560px-Helder_Camara_1981.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Helder_Camara_1981.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Hélder Câmara in 1984</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>He was ordained a priest in 1931, with direct authorization of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> over his premature age.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Câmara became auxiliary bishop of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archdiocese_of_S%C3%A3o_Sebasti%C3%A3o_do_Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Archdiocese of São Sebastião do Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> in 1952. During his first years as a priest he was a supporter of the far-right organization <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Integralism" title="Brazilian Integralism">Integralismo</a>, an ideological choice that he later rejected. He also founded two social organizations: the Ceará Legion of Work, in 1931, and the Women Workers' Catholic Union, in 1933. In 1959, he was appointed archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Olinda_e_Recife" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Olinda e Recife">Olinda e Recife</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his tenure, Câmara was informally called the 'Bishop of the slums' for his clear position on the side of the urban poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> With other clerics, he encouraged peasants to free themselves from their conventional fatalistic outlook by studying the gospels in small groups and proposing the search for social change from their readings. He was active in the formation of the <a href="/wiki/National_Conference_of_Bishops_of_Brazil" title="National Conference of Bishops of Brazil">Brazilian Bishops’ Conference</a> in 1952, and served as its first general secretary until 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O.27Connell_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-O.27Connell-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1959 he founded <a class="extiw" href="https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banco_da_Provid%C3%AAncia" title="pt:Banco da Providência">Banco da Providência</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a>, a philanthropic organization to fight poverty and social injustice by facilitating the contraction of loans by poorer populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bellos_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bellos-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>He attended all four sessions of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">Second Vatican Council</a> and played a significant role in drafting the <a href="/wiki/Gaudium_et_spes" title="Gaudium et spes"><i>Pastoral Constitution on The Church in the Modern World</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O.27Connell_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-O.27Connell-6">[6]</a></sup> On November 16, 1965, a few days before the Council ended, 40 bishops led by bishop Hélder Câmara met at night in the <a href="/wiki/Catacombs_of_Rome#Catacombs_of_Domitilla" title="Catacombs of Rome">Catacombs of Domitilla</a> outside Rome. They celebrated the Eucharist and signed a document under the title of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pact_of_the_Catacombs" title="Pact of the Catacombs">Pact of the Catacombs</a>. In 13 points, they challenged their brother bishops to live lives of evangelical poverty: without honorific titles, privileges, and worldly ostentation. They taught that "the collegiality of the bishops finds its supreme evangelical realization in jointly serving the two-thirds of humanity who live in physical, cultural, and moral misery". They called for openness "to all, no matter what their beliefs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Providencia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1847" data-file-width="2637" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Providencia.jpg/280px-Providencia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Providencia.jpg/420px-Providencia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Providencia.jpg/560px-Providencia.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Providencia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Entrance to the branch of the Banco da Providência located in the <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro_Cathedral" title="Rio de Janeiro Cathedral">Rio de Janeiro Cathedral</a></div>

</div>
<p>Under the guidance of archbishop Hélder Câmara, the Catholic church in Brazil became an outspoken critic of the 1964-85 military dictatorship and a powerful movement for social change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bellos_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bellos-7">[7]</a></sup> Câmara spoke out and wrote about the implications of using violence to repress rebellion resulting from poverty and injustice in other venues than Brazil. Traditionalist Catholics urged the military government to arrest Câmara for his support of land reform<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and Câmara's colleague, Father Antônio Henrique Pereira Neto, was murdered by unknown conservative forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>A proponent of <a href="/wiki/Liberation_theology" title="Liberation theology">liberation theology</a>, he was Archbishop of the Diocese of <a href="/wiki/Olinda" title="Olinda">Olinda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Recife" title="Recife">Recife</a> from 1964 to 1985, during a period when the country had a series of military rulers. Liberation theology politicised the church's charitable work and brought criticisms that it was encouraging the armed revolutionary struggles that swept Latin America during the 1970s and 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bellos_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bellos-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>He published <i>Spiral of Violence</i> (1971), a short tract written when the United States was immersed in a still escalating <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. It is distinctive for linking structural injustice (Level 1 violence) with escalating rebellion (Level 2 violence) and repressive reaction (Level 3 violence). In it, Câmara called on the youth of the world to take steps to break the spiral, saying their elders became addicted to those escalating steps. By the early 21st century, this book had been out of print in the United Kingdom for about 20 years. A scanned version in English is available on the web at the link given below.</p>
<p>He died, aged 90, in <a href="/wiki/Recife" title="Recife">Recife</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H%C3%A9lder_C%C3%A2mara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Câmara had some controversial views, endorsing the position of the Orthodox church that spouses who were abandoned should be allowed to remarry within the Church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CESNUR_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-CESNUR-13">[13]</a></sup> He criticized <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_VI" title="Pope Paul VI">Pope Paul VI</a>'s removal of artificial contraception from the purview of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vatican_II" title="Vatican II">Vatican II</a> as "a mistake" meant to "torture spouses, to disturb peace of many homes", "a new condemnation of Galileo",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> "the death of the Council" and "the practical denial of <a href="/wiki/Collegiality_(Catholic_Church)" title="Collegiality (Catholic Church)">collegiality</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CESNUR_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-CESNUR-13">[13]</a></sup> However, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humanae_Vitae" title="Humanae Vitae">Humanae Vitae</a>, he had changed his mind about contraception, being the first person to telegram the <a href="/wiki/Secretariat_of_State_(Holy_See)" title="Secretariat of State (Holy See)">Vatican's Secretariat of State</a> praising the controversial encyclical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his famous interview with Italian journalist <a href="/wiki/Oriana_Fallaci" title="Oriana Fallaci">Oriana Fallaci</a>, he also stated that, despite his support for non-violence, he didn't oppose violent tactics: "And I respect a lot priests with rifles on their shoulders; I never said that to use weapons against an oppressor is immoral or anti-Christian. But thats not my choice, not my road, not my way to apply the Gospels".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Câmara identified himself as a socialist and not as a marxist, and while disagreeing with <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">marxism</a>, had marxist sympathies. In the Oriana Fallaci interview he stated that "My socialism is special, its a socialism that respects the human person and goes back to the Gospels. My socialism it is justice." He said, concerning Marx, that while he disagreed with his conclusions, he agreed with his analysis of the capitalist society.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_honors">Legacy and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=H%C3%A9lder_C%C3%A2mara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In 1973, Câmara was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> by the <a href="/wiki/American_Friends_Service_Committee" title="American Friends Service Committee">American Friends Service Committee</a> (AFSC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 1975, he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Pacem_in_Terris_Award" title="Pacem in Terris Award">Pacem in Terris Award</a>, initiated by the Catholic Interracial Council of the Diocese of <a href="/wiki/Davenport,_Iowa" title="Davenport, Iowa">Davenport, Iowa</a>. It was named after a 1963 <a href="/wiki/Encyclical" title="Encyclical">encyclical</a> letter by <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a> that calls upon all people of good will to secure peace among all nations. <i><a href="/wiki/Pacem_in_terris" title="Pacem in terris">Pacem in terris</a></i> is <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "Peace on Earth".</li>
<li>In 2015, the Vatican's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congregation_for_the_Causes_of_the_Saints" title="Congregation for the Causes of the Saints">Congregation for the Causes of the Saints</a> granted its <i>nihil obstat</i>, authorizing the Archbishop of Olinda and Recife to open the <a href="/wiki/Beatification" title="Beatification">beatification</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canonization" title="Canonization">canonization</a> process for Câmara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O.27Connell_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-O.27Connell-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
