<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> <b>Europa</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ʊ/ short 'oo' in 'foot'">ʊ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/oʊ/ long 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>, <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/j/ 'y' in 'yes'">j</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/-/ affix">-</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Εὐρώπη</span> <i>Eurṓpē</i>) was the mother of <a href="/wiki/Basileus" title="Basileus">King</a> <a href="/wiki/Minos" title="Minos">Minos</a> of <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, a woman with <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenician</a> origin of high lineage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and for whom the <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a> <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> was named. The story of her abduction by <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> in the form of a white bull was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cretan" title="Cretan">Cretan</a> story; as classicist <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1roly_Ker%C3%A9nyi" title="Károly Kerényi">Károly Kerényi</a> points out, "most of the love-stories concerning <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> originated from more ancient tales describing his marriages with goddesses. This can especially be said of the story of Europa".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Europa's earliest literary reference is in the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>, which is commonly dated to the 8th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Another early reference to her is in a fragment of the <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiodic</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Catalogue_of_Women" title="Catalogue of Women">Catalogue of Women</a></i>, discovered at <a href="/wiki/Oxyrhynchus" title="Oxyrhynchus">Oxyrhynchus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The earliest vase-painting securely identifiable as Europa dates from mid-7th century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europa_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Terracotta_Europa_bull_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2014" data-file-width="2106" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Terracotta_Europa_bull_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen.jpg/220px-Terracotta_Europa_bull_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Terracotta_Europa_bull_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen.jpg/330px-Terracotta_Europa_bull_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Terracotta_Europa_bull_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen.jpg/440px-Terracotta_Europa_bull_Staatliche_Antikensammlungen.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Europe#Name" title="Europe">Europe § Name</a></div>
<p>Greek <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Εὐρώπη</span> <i>Eurṓpē</i> contains the elements εὐρύς (<i>eurus</i>), "wide, broad"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and ὤψ/ὠπ-/ὀπτ- (<i>ōps</i>/<i>ōp</i>-/<i>opt-</i>) "eye, face, countenance".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <i>Broad</i> has been an <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithet</a> of Earth herself in the reconstructed <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_religion" title="Proto-Indo-European religion">Proto-Indo-European religion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is common in ancient Greek mythology and geography to identify lands or rivers with female figures. Thus, <i>Europa</i> is first used in a geographic context in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeric_Hymn" title="Homeric Hymn">Homeric Hymn</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delian_Apollo" title="Delian Apollo">Delian Apollo</a>, in reference to the western shore of the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> As a name for a part of the known world, it is first used in the 6th century BC by <a href="/wiki/Anaximander" title="Anaximander">Anaximander</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hecataeus_of_Miletus" title="Hecataeus of Miletus">Hecataeus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>An alternative suggestion due to <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Klein" title="Ernest Klein">Ernest Klein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Semerano" title="Giovanni Semerano">Giovanni Semerano</a> (1966) attempted to connect a Semitic term for "west", <a href="/wiki/Akkadian" title="Akkadian">Akkadian</a> <i>erebu</i> meaning "to go down, set" (in reference to the sun), <a href="/wiki/Phoenician_language" title="Phoenician language">Phoenician</a> <i>'ereb</i> "evening; west", which would parallel <i>occident</i> (the resemblance to <i><a href="/wiki/Erebus" title="Erebus">Erebus</a></i>, from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">PIE</a> <i>*h<sub>1</sub>regʷos</i>, "darkness", is accidental, however). Barry (1999) adduces the word <i>Ereb</i> on an Assyrian stele with the meaning of "night", "[the country of] sunset", in opposition to <i>Asu</i> "[the country of] sunrise", i.e. Asia (Anatolia coming equally from Ἀνατολή, "(sun)rise", "east").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This proposal is mostly considered unlikely or untenable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Astarte_and_Europa">Astarte and Europa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europa_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Astarte and Europa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the territory of Phoenician <a href="/wiki/Sidon" title="Sidon">Sidon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucian_of_Samosata" title="Lucian of Samosata">Lucian of Samosata</a> (2nd century AD) was informed that the temple of <a href="/wiki/Astarte" title="Astarte">Astarte</a>, whom Lucian equated with the moon goddess, was sacred to Europa:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>There is likewise in Phœnicia a temple of great size owned by the Sidonians. They call it the temple of Astarte. I hold this Astarte to be no other than the moon-goddess. But according to the story of one of the priests this temple is sacred to Europa, the sister of Cadmus. She was the daughter of Agenor, and on her disappearance from Earth the Phœnicians honoured her with a temple and told a sacred legend about her; how that Zeus was enamoured of her for her beauty, and changing his form into that of a bull carried her off into Crete. This legend I heard from other Phœnicians as well; and the coinage current among the Sidonians bears upon it the effigy of Europa sitting upon a bull, none other than Zeus. Thus they do not agree that the temple in question is sacred to Europa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The paradox, as it seemed to Lucian, would be solved if Europa <i>is</i> Astarte in her guise as the full, "broad-faced" moon.</p>

<p>Sources differ in details regarding Europa's family, but agree that she is <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenician</a>, and from a lineage that descended from <a href="/wiki/Io_(mythology)" title="Io (mythology)">Io</a>, the mythical <a href="/wiki/Nymph" title="Nymph">nymph</a> beloved of Zeus, who was transformed into a heifer. She is generally said to be the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Agenor" title="Agenor">Agenor</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">Phoenician</a> King of <a href="/wiki/Tyre,_Lebanon" title="Tyre, Lebanon">Tyre</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encarta2008_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-encarta2008-16">[16]</a></sup> the Syracusan poet <a href="/wiki/Moschus" title="Moschus">Moschus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> makes her mother Queen <a href="/wiki/Telephassa" title="Telephassa">Telephassa</a> ("far-shining") but elsewhere her mother is <a href="/wiki/Telephassa" title="Telephassa">Argiope</a> ("white-faced").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Other sources, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>, claim that she is the daughter of Agenor's son, the "sun-red" <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(son_of_Agenor)" title="Phoenix (son of Agenor)">Phoenix</a>. It is generally agreed that she had two brothers, <a href="/wiki/Cadmus" title="Cadmus">Cadmus</a>, who brought the alphabet to mainland Greece, and <a href="/wiki/Cilix" title="Cilix">Cilix</a> who gave his name to <a href="/wiki/Cilicia" title="Cilicia">Cilicia</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>, with the author of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bibliotheke" title="Bibliotheke">Bibliotheke</a></i> including Phoenix as a third. So some interpret this as her brother Phoenix (when he is assumed to be son of Agenor) gave his siblings' name to his three children and this Europa (by this case, niece of former) is also loved by Zeus, but because of the same name, gave some confusions to others. After arriving in Crete, Europa had three sons fathered by Zeus: <a href="/wiki/Minos" title="Minos">Minos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhadamanthus" title="Rhadamanthus">Rhadamanthus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sarpedon" title="Sarpedon">Sarpedon</a>, the three of whom became the three judges of the Underworld when they died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encarta2008_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-encarta2008-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In Crete she married <a href="/wiki/Asterion" title="Asterion">Asterion</a> also rendered Asterius and became mother (or step-mother) of his daughter <a href="/wiki/Crete_(mythology)" title="Crete (mythology)">Crete</a>.</p>

<p>There were two competing myths<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> relating how Europa came into the Hellenic world, but they agreed that she came to <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> (Kríti), where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bull_(mythology)" title="Bull (mythology)">sacred bull</a> was paramount. In the more familiar telling she was <a href="/wiki/Seduction" title="Seduction">seduced</a> by the <a href="/wiki/God_(male_deity)" title="God (male deity)">god</a> <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> in the form of a bull, who breathed from his mouth a <a href="/wiki/Saffron" title="Saffron">saffron crocus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and carried her away to Crete on his back—to be welcomed by <a href="/wiki/Asterion" title="Asterion">Asterion</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> but according to the more literal, <a href="/wiki/Euhemerism" title="Euhemerism">euhemerist</a> version that begins the account of Persian-Hellene confrontations of <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> she was <a href="/wiki/Kidnapping" title="Kidnapping">kidnapped</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cretans" title="Cretans">Cretans</a>, who likewise were said to have taken her to Crete. The mythical Europa cannot be separated from the mythology of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bull_(mythology)" title="Bull (mythology)">sacred bull</a>, which had been worshipped in the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>. In 2012, an archaeological mission of the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Lebanese_people" title="Lebanese people">Lebanese</a> archaeologist, Claude Doumet Serhal, discovered at the site of the old American school in <a href="/wiki/Sidon" title="Sidon">Sidon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a> currency that depicts Europa riding the bull with her veil flying all over like a bow, further proof of Europa's Phoenician origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Europa does not seem to have been venerated directly in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cult_(religion)" title="Cult (religion)">cult</a> anywhere in classical Greece,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> but at Lebadaea in <a href="/wiki/Boeotia" title="Boeotia">Boeotia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a> noted in the 2nd century CE that <i>Europa</i> was the <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithet</a> of <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">Demeter</a>—"Demeter whom they surname Europa and say was the nurse of Trophonios"—among the Olympians who were addressed by seekers at the cave sanctuary of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trophonios" title="Trophonios">Trophonios</a> of <a href="/wiki/Orchomenus_(Boeotia)" title="Orchomenus (Boeotia)">Orchomenus</a>, to whom a <a href="/wiki/Chthonic" title="Chthonic">chthonic</a> cult and <a href="/wiki/Oracle" title="Oracle">oracle</a> were dedicated: "the <a href="/wiki/Sacred_grove" title="Sacred grove">grove</a> of Trophonios by the river Herkyna ... there is also a sanctuary of Demeter Europa ... the nurse of Trophonios."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abduction">Abduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europa_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Abduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Palazzo_Ferreria_statue_3.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="1936" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Palazzo_Ferreria_statue_3.jpeg/220px-Palazzo_Ferreria_statue_3.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Palazzo_Ferreria_statue_3.jpeg/330px-Palazzo_Ferreria_statue_3.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Palazzo_Ferreria_statue_3.jpeg/440px-Palazzo_Ferreria_statue_3.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Palazzo_Ferreria_statue_3.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Statue of Europa representing <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> at <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Ferreria" title="Palazzo Ferreria">Palazzo Ferreria</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythographers" title="Mythographers">mythographers</a> tell that Zeus was enamored of Europa and decided to seduce or ravish her, the two being near-equivalent in Greek myth. He transformed himself into a tame white bull and mixed in with her father's herds. While Europa and her helpers were gathering flowers, she saw the bull, caressed his flanks, and eventually got onto his back. Zeus took that opportunity and ran to the sea and swam, with her on his back, to the island of <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>. He then revealed his true identity, and Europa became the first queen of Crete. Zeus gave her a necklace made by <a href="/wiki/Hephaestus" title="Hephaestus">Hephaestus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and three additional gifts: <a href="/wiki/Talos" title="Talos">Talos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laelaps_(mythology)" title="Laelaps (mythology)">Laelaps</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Pilum" title="Pilum">javelin</a> that never missed. Zeus later re-created the shape of the white bull in the stars, which is now known as the constellation <a href="/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)" title="Taurus (constellation)">Taurus</a>. Some readers interpret as manifestations of this same bull the <a href="/wiki/Cretan_Bull" title="Cretan Bull">Cretan beast</a> that was encountered by <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marathonian_Bull" title="Marathonian Bull">Marathonian Bull</a> slain by <a href="/wiki/Theseus" title="Theseus">Theseus</a> (and that fathered the <a href="/wiki/Minotaur" title="Minotaur">Minotaur</a>). Roman mythology adopted the tale of the <i>Raptus</i>, also known as "The Abduction of Europa" and "The Seduction of Europa", substituting the god <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_(mythology)" title="Jupiter (mythology)">Jupiter</a> for <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>.</p>
<p>The myth of Europa and Zeus may have its origin in a sacred union between the Phoenician deities <a href="/wiki/Attar_(god)" title="Attar (god)">`Aštar</a> and `Aštart (Astarte), in bovine form. Having given birth to three sons by Zeus, Europa married a king <a href="/wiki/Asterion" title="Asterion">Asterios</a>, this being also the name of the Minotaur and an epithet of Zeus, likely derived from the name <i>`Aštar</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-West1997_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-West1997-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>' rationalizing approach, Europa was kidnapped by Greeks (probably Cretans) who were seeking to avenge the kidnapping of <a href="/wiki/Io_(mythology)" title="Io (mythology)">Io</a>, a princess from <a href="/wiki/Argos" title="Argos">Argos</a>. His variant story may have been an attempt to rationalize the earlier myth; or the present myth may be a garbled version of facts—the abduction of a Phoenician aristocrat—later enunciated without gloss by Herodotus.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_art_and_literature">In art and literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europa_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In art and literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Europa_copy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1081" data-file-width="1000" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Europa_copy.jpg/220px-Europa_copy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Europa_copy.jpg/330px-Europa_copy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Europa_copy.jpg/440px-Europa_copy.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Europa_copy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Europa and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bull_(mythology)" title="Bull (mythology)">bull</a> on a Greek vase. Tarquinia Museum, circa 480 BCE</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Europa</i> provided the substance of a brief <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic" title="Hellenistic">Hellenistic</a> epic written in the mid-2nd century BCE by <a href="/wiki/Moschus" title="Moschus">Moschus</a>, a bucolic poet and friend of the Alexandrian grammarian <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samothrace" title="Aristarchus of Samothrace">Aristarchus of Samothrace</a>, born at Syracuse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphoses_(poem)" title="Metamorphoses (poem)">Metamorphoses</a></i>, the poet <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a> wrote the following depiction of Jupiter's seduction:</p>
<dl>
<dd>And gradually she lost her fear, and he</dd>
<dd>Offered his breast for her virgin caresses,</dd>
<dd>His horns for her to wind with chains of flowers</dd>
<dd>Until the princess dared to mount his back</dd>
<dd>Her pet bull's back, unwitting whom she rode.</dd>
<dd>Then—slowly, slowly down the broad, dry beach—</dd>
<dd>First in the shallow waves the great god set</dd>
<dd>His spurious hooves, then sauntered further out</dd>
<dd>'til in the open sea he bore his prize</dd>
<dd>Fear filled her heart as, gazing back, she saw</dd>
<dd>The fast receding sands. Her right hand grasped</dd>
<dd>A horn, the other lent upon his back</dd>
<dd>Her fluttering tunic floated in the breeze.</dd>
</dl>
<p>His picturesque details belong to anecdote and fable: in all the depictions, whether she straddles the bull, as in archaic vase-paintings or the ruined metope fragment from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sikyon" title="Sikyon">Sikyon</a>, or sits gracefully sidesaddle as in a mosaic from North Africa, there is no trace of fear. Often Europa steadies herself by touching one of the bull's horns, acquiescing.</p>
<p>Her tale is also mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Hawthorne" title="Nathaniel Hawthorne">Nathaniel Hawthorne</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Tanglewood_Tales" title="Tanglewood Tales">Tanglewood Tales</a></i>. Though his story titled "Dragon's teeth" is largely about Cadmus, it begins with an elaborate albeit toned down version of Europa's abduction by the beautiful bull.</p>
<p>Contemporary artists continue to explore the ancient Greek subject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continent">Continent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europa_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Continent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_symbols#Europa" title="European symbols">European symbols § Europa</a></div>
<p>The name <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, as a geographical term, was used by <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greek</a> geographers such as <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> to refer to part of <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a> below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balkan_mountains" title="Balkan mountains">Balkan mountains</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Later, under the Roman Empire the name was given to a Thracian <a href="/wiki/Europa_(Roman_province)" title="Europa (Roman province)">province</a>.</p>
<p>It is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word <i>Eurōpē</i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Εὐρώπη</span>) in all <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltic_languages" title="Baltic languages">Baltic languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iranian_languages" title="Iranian languages">Iranian languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">Uralic languages</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a> Európa, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> Eurooppa, <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a> Euroopa).</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Imago_Europae_euronis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="405" data-file-width="255" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Imago_Europae_euronis.jpg/100px-Imago_Europae_euronis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Imago_Europae_euronis.jpg/150px-Imago_Europae_euronis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Imago_Europae_euronis.jpg/200px-Imago_Europae_euronis.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Imago_Europae_euronis.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Europa seen on the 2013 <i>Europa Series</i> of euro banknotes</div>

</div>
<p>Jürgen Fischer, in <i>Oriens-Occidens-Europa</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> summarized how the name came into use, supplanting the <i><a href="/wiki/Orient" title="Orient">oriens</a></i>-<i><a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">occidens</a></i> dichotomy of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Later_Roman_Empire" title="Later Roman Empire">later Roman Empire</a>, which was expressive of a divided empire, Latin in the West, Greek in the East.</p>
<p>In the 8th century, ecclesiastical uses of "Europa" for the imperium of <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> provide the source for the modern geographical term. The first use of the term <i>Europenses</i>, to describe peoples of the Christian, western portion of the continent, appeared in the Hispanic Latin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozarabic_Chronicle_of_754" title="Mozarabic Chronicle of 754">Chronicle of 754</a>, sometimes attributed to an author called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isidorus_Pacensis" title="Isidorus Pacensis">Isidore Pacensis</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> in reference to the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tours" title="Battle of Tours">Battle of Tours</a> fought against Muslim forces.</p>
<p>The European Union has also used Europa as a symbol of <a href="/wiki/Pan-European_identity" title="Pan-European identity">pan-Europeanism</a>, notably by naming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europa_(web_portal)" title="Europa (web portal)">its web portal</a> after her, and depicting her on the Greek €2 coin and on several <a href="/wiki/Euro_gold_and_silver_commemorative_coins" title="Euro gold and silver commemorative coins">gold and silver commemorative coins</a> (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Euro_gold_and_silver_commemorative_coins_(Belgium)#2004_coinage" title="Euro gold and silver commemorative coins (Belgium)">Belgian €10 European Expansion coin</a>). Her name appeared on <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamp" title="Postage stamp">postage stamps</a> celebrating the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a>, which were first issued in 1956. The second series of <a href="/wiki/Euro_banknotes" title="Euro banknotes">euro banknotes</a> is known as the Europa Series and bears her likeness in the watermark and hologram.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_element">Chemical element</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europa_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chemical element">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The metal <i><a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth element</a>, was named in 1901 after the continent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moon_of_Jupiter">Moon of Jupiter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europa_(mythology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Moon of Jupiter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Europa-moon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="913" data-file-width="913" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Europa-moon.jpg/170px-Europa-moon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Europa-moon.jpg/255px-Europa-moon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Europa-moon.jpg/340px-Europa-moon.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Europa-moon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a>, a moon of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa (moon)</a></div>
<p>The invention of the <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">telescope</a> revealed that the planet <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, clearly visible to the naked eye and known to humanity since prehistoric times, has an attendant family of moons. These were named for male and female lovers of the god and other mythological persons associated with him. The smallest of Jupiter's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galilean_moon" title="Galilean moon">Galilean moons</a> was named after Europa.</p>
