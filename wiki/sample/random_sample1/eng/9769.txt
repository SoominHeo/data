<p><i><b>Eurypterus</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/juː/ long 'u' in 'cute'">juː</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ər/ 'er' in 'finger'">ər</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i>ew-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">RIP</span></span></b>-tər-əs</i></a></span>) is an extinct <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurypterida" title="Eurypterida">sea scorpions</a>. They existed during the <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a> <a href="/wiki/Period_(geology)" title="Period (geology)">Period</a>, from around 432 to 418 million years ago.</p>
<p>There are fifteen species belonging to the genus <i>Eurypterus</i>, the most common of which is <i>Eurypterus remipes</i>, the first <a href="/wiki/Eurypterid" title="Eurypterid">eurypterid</a> <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> discovered and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._state_fossils" title="List of U.S. state fossils">state fossil</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.</p>
<p>Members of <i>Eurypterus</i> averaged at about 5 to 9 in (13 to 23 cm) in length, but the largest individual discovered was 1.3 m (4.3 ft) long. They all possessed spine-bearing appendages and a large paddle they used for swimming. They were <a href="/wiki/Generalist_and_specialist_species" title="Generalist and specialist species">generalist species</a>, equally likely to engage in <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scavenging" title="Scavenging">scavenging</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurypterus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> of <i>Eurypterus</i> was found in 1818 by S. L. Mitchill, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_collector" title="Fossil collector">fossil collector</a>. It was recovered from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bertie_Formation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bertie Formation (page does not exist)">Bertie Formation</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> (near <a href="/wiki/Westmoreland,_New_York" title="Westmoreland, New York">Westmoreland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oneida_County,_New_York" title="Oneida County, New York">Oneida County</a>). Mitchill interpreted the appendages on the carapace as <a href="/wiki/Barbel_(anatomy)" title="Barbel (anatomy)">barbels</a> arising from the mouth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nudds_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nudds-2">[2]</a></sup> He consequently identified the fossil as a <a href="/wiki/Catfish" title="Catfish">catfish</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Silurus" title="Silurus">Silurus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madrid_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-madrid-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ucmp2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ucmp2-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p>It was only after seven years, in 1825, that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_people" title="American people">American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoologist" title="Zoologist">zoologist</a> <a href="/wiki/James_Ellsworth_De_Kay" title="James Ellsworth De Kay">James Ellsworth De Kay</a> identified the fossil correctly as an <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropod</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waering_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-waering-5">[5]</a></sup> He named it <i>Eurypterus remipes</i> and established the genus <i>Eurypterus</i> in the process. The name means "wide wing" or "broad paddle", referring to the swimming legs, from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> εὐρύς (<i>eurús</i>, wide) and πτερόν (<i>pteron</i>, wing).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nudds_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-nudds-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>However, De Kay thought <i>Eurypterus</i> was a <a href="/wiki/Branchiopoda" title="Branchiopoda">branchiopod</a> (a group of <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> which include <a href="/wiki/Anostraca" title="Anostraca">fairy shrimps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cladocera" title="Cladocera">water fleas</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waering_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-waering-5">[5]</a></sup> Soon after, <i>Eurypterus lacustris</i> was also discovered in New York in 1835 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleontologist" title="Paleontologist">paleontologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Harlan" title="Richard Harlan">Richard Harlan</a>. Another species was discovered in <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> in 1858 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jan_Nieszkowski&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jan Nieszkowski (page does not exist)">Jan Nieszkowski</a>. He considered it to be of the same species as the first discovery (<i>E. remipes</i>); it is now known as <i>Eurypterus tetragonophthalmus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nudds_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-nudds-2">[2]</a></sup> These specimens from Estonia are often of extraordinary quality, retaining the actual <a href="/wiki/Cuticle" title="Cuticle">cuticle</a> of their exoskeletons. In 1898, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_people" title="Swedish people">Swedish</a> paleontologist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gerhard_Holm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gerhard Holm (page does not exist)">Gerhard Holm</a> separated these fossils from the bedrock with <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>. Holm was then able to examine the almost perfectly preserved fragments under a <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscope</a>. His remarkable study led to the modern breakthrough on eurypterid <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madrid_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-madrid-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>More fossils were recovered in great abundance in New York in the 19th century, and elsewhere in eastern <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. Today, <i>Eurypterus</i> remains one of the most commonly found and best known <a href="/wiki/Eurypterid" title="Eurypterid">eurypterid</a> genera, comprising more than 95% of all known eurypterid fossils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dist_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-dist-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurypterus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus <i>Eurypterus</i> belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Eurypteridae" title="Eurypteridae">Eurypteridae</a>. They are classified under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superfamily_(zoology)" title="Superfamily (zoology)">superfamily</a> <a href="/wiki/Eurypteroidea" title="Eurypteroidea">Eurypteroidea</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suborder" title="Suborder">suborder</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eurypterina&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eurypterina (page does not exist)">Eurypterina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurypterida" title="Eurypterida">Eurypterida</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Subphylum" title="Subphylum">subphylum</a> <a href="/wiki/Chelicerata" title="Chelicerata">Chelicerata</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunlop_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-dunlop-7">[7]</a></sup> Until recently, <a href="/wiki/Eurypterid" title="Eurypterid">eurypterids</a> were thought to belong to the <a href="/wiki/Class_(biology)" title="Class (biology)">class</a> <a href="/wiki/Merostomata" title="Merostomata">Merostomata</a> along with order <a href="/wiki/Xiphosura" title="Xiphosura">Xiphosura</a>. It is now believed that eurypterids are a <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister group</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arachnida" title="Arachnida">Arachnida</a>, closer to <a href="/wiki/Scorpion" title="Scorpion">scorpions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spiders</a> than to <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_crab" title="Horseshoe crab">horseshoe crabs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunlopwebster_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-dunlopwebster-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weygoldt_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-weygoldt-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berkeley_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Eurypterus</i> was the first recognized <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a> of eurypterids and is the most common. As a consequence, nearly every remotely similar eurypterid in the 19th century was classified under the genus (except for the distinctive members of the family <a href="/wiki/Pterygotidae" title="Pterygotidae">Pterygotidae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stylonuridae" title="Stylonuridae">Stylonuridae</a>). The genus was eventually split into several genera as the science of <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">taxonomy</a> developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waering_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-waering-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1958, several species distinguishable by closer placed eyes and spines on their swimming legs were split off into the separate genus <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eiropterus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eiropterus (page does not exist)">Eiropterus</a></i> by Erik Kjellesvig-Waering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waering_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-waering-5">[5]</a></sup> Another split was proposed by Lief Størmer in 1973 when he reclassified some <i>Eurypterus</i> to <i>Baltoeurypterus</i> based on the size of some of the last segments of their swimming legs. O. Erik Tetlie in 2006 deemed these differences too insignificant to justify a separate genus. He merged <i>Baltoeurypterus</i> back into <i>Eurypterus</i>. It is now believed that the minor variations described by Størmer are simply the differences found in adults and juveniles within a species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tollerton_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-tollerton-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurypterus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eurypterus_anatomy.png"><img alt="Eurypterus anatomy.png" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2442" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Eurypterus_anatomy.png/350px-Eurypterus_anatomy.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Eurypterus_anatomy.png/525px-Eurypterus_anatomy.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Eurypterus_anatomy.png/700px-Eurypterus_anatomy.png 2x" width="350"/></a></div>
<p>The largest <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> to have ever existed were eurypterids. The largest known species (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jaekelopterus_rhenaniae" title="Jaekelopterus rhenaniae">Jaekelopterus rhenaniae</a></i>) reached up to 2.5 m (8.2 ft) in length, about the size of a crocodile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naturenews_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-naturenews-12">[12]</a></sup> Species of <i>Eurypterus</i>, however, were much smaller.</p>
<p><i>E. remipes</i> are usually between 5 to 8 in (13 to 20 cm) in length. <i>E. lacustris</i> average at larger sizes at 6 to 9 in (15 to 23 cm) in length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-size_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-size-13">[13]</a></sup> The largest specimen of <i>E. remipes</i> ever found was 1.3 m (4.3 ft) long, currently on display at the <a href="/wiki/Paleontological_Research_Institution" title="Paleontological Research Institution">Paleontological Research Institution</a> of New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-utexas_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-utexas-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Eurypterus</i> fossils often occur in similar sizes in a given area. This may be a result of the fossils being 'sorted' into <a href="/wiki/Windrow" title="Windrow">windrows</a> as they were being deposited in shallow waters by storms and wave action.</p>

<p>The <i>Eurypterus</i> body is broadly divided into two parts: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prosoma" title="Prosoma">prosoma</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Opisthosoma" title="Opisthosoma">opisthosoma</a> (in turn divided into the <a href="/wiki/Mesosoma" title="Mesosoma">mesosoma</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Metasoma" title="Metasoma">metasoma</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nudds_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-nudds-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burk_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-burk-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The prosoma is the forward part of the body, it is actually composed of six segments fused together to form the head and the <a href="/wiki/Thorax" title="Thorax">thorax</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burk_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-burk-15">[15]</a></sup> It contains the semicircular to subrectangular platelike <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a>. On the dorsal side of the latter are two large crescent-shaped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_eyes" title="Compound eyes">compound eyes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rich_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-rich-16">[16]</a></sup> They also possessed two smaller light-sensitive <a href="/wiki/Simple_eye_in_invertebrates" title="Simple eye in invertebrates">simple eyes</a> (the median <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocelli" title="Ocelli">ocelli</a>) near the center of the carapace on a small elevation (known as the ocellar mound).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lamsdell_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-lamsdell-17">[17]</a></sup> Underneath the carapace is the mouth and six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appendages" title="Appendages">appendages</a>, usually referred to in <a href="/wiki/Roman_numerals" title="Roman numerals">Roman numerals</a> I-VI. Each appendage in turn is composed of nine segments (known as podomeres) labeled in <a href="/wiki/Arabic_numerals" title="Arabic numerals">Arabic numerals</a> 1-9. The first segments which connect the appendages to the body are known as the coxa (plural coxae).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lamsdell_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-lamsdell-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The first pair (Appendage I) are the <a href="/wiki/Chelicerae" title="Chelicerae">chelicerae</a>, small <a href="/wiki/Pincer_(biology)" title="Pincer (biology)">pincer</a>-like arms used for tearing food apart (<a href="/wiki/Mastication" title="Mastication">mastication</a>) during feeding. After the chelicerae are three pairs of short legs (Appendages II, III, and IV). They are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiniferous" title="Spiniferous">spiniferous</a>, with predominantly two spines on each podomere and with the tipmost segment having a single spine. The last two segments are often indistinguishable and give the appearance of a single segment having three spines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tollerton_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-tollerton-11">[11]</a></sup> They are used both for walking and for food capture. The next pair (Appendage V) is the most leg-like of all appendages, longer than the first three pairs and are mostly spineless except at the tipmost segments. The last pair (Appendage VI) are two broad paddle-like legs used for swimming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nudds_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-nudds-2">[2]</a></sup> The coxae of Appendage VI are broad and flat, resembling an 'ear'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lamsdell_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-lamsdell-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ophisthosoma (the <a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a>) is composed of 12 segments, each consisting of a fused upper plate (tergite) and bottom plate (sternite).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ea_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ea-18">[18]</a></sup> It is further subdivided in two ways.</p>
<p>Based on the width and structure of each segment, they can be divided into the broad preabdomen (segments 1 to 7) and the narrow postabdomen (segments 8 to 12).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paleopedia_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-paleopedia-19">[19]</a></sup> The preabdomen is the broader segments of the anterior portion of the ophisthosoma while the postabdomen are the last five segments of the <i>Eurypterus body</i>. Each of the segments of the postabdomen contain lateral flattened protrusions known as the epimera with the exception of the last needle-like (styliform) segment known as the <a href="/wiki/Telson" title="Telson">telson</a> (the 'tail'). The segment immediately preceding the telson (which also has the largest epimera of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postabdomen" title="Postabdomen">postabdomen</a>) is known as the pretelson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lamsdell_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-lamsdell-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>An alternative way to divide the ophisthosoma is by function. It can also be divided into the mesosoma (segments 1 to 6), and the metasoma (segments 7 to 12).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rich_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-rich-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lamsdell_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-lamsdell-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plotnick_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-plotnick-20">[20]</a></sup> The mesosoma contains the gills and reproductive organs of <i>Eurypterus</i>. Its ventral segments are overlaid by appendage-derived plates known as Blatfüsse (singular Blatfuss, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> for "sheet foot"). Protected within which are the branchial chambers which contain the respiratory organs of <i>Eurypterus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunlopwebster_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-dunlopwebster-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-resp_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-resp-21">[21]</a></sup> The metasoma, meanwhile, do not possess Blatfüsse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plotnick_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-plotnick-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some authors incorrectly use mesosoma and preabdomen interchangeably, as with metasoma and postabdomen.</p>
<p>The main respiratory organs of <i>Eurypterus</i> were what seems to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_gills" title="Book gills">book gills</a>, located in branchial chambers within the segments of the mesosoma. They may have been used for underwater respiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burk_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-burk-15">[15]</a></sup> They are composed of several layers of thin tissue stacked in such a way as to resemble the pages of a book, hence the name. In addition, they also possessed five pairs of oval-shaped areas covered with microscopic projections on the ceiling of the second branchial chambers within the mesosoma, immediately below the gill tracts. These areas are known as Kiemenplatten (or gill-tracts, though the former term is preferred). They are unique to eurypterids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-resp_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-resp-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-selden_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-selden-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Eurypterus</i> are <a href="/wiki/Sexual_dimorphism" title="Sexual dimorphism">sexually dimorphic</a>. On the bottom side of the first two segments of the mesosoma are central appendages used for reproduction. In females, they are long and narrow. In the males they are very short.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-St.C3.B8rmer_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-St.C3.B8rmer-23">[23]</a></sup> A minority of authors, however, assume the reverse: longer genital appendage for males, shorter for females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autec_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-autec-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a> of <i>Eurypterus</i> is often covered with small outgrowths known as ornamentation. They include pustules (small protrusions), <a href="/wiki/Scale_(anatomy)" title="Scale (anatomy)">scales</a>, and striations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ea_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-ea-18">[18]</a></sup> They vary by species and are used for identification. For more detailed diagnostic descriptions of each species under <i>Eurypterus</i>, see <a href="/wiki/Eurypterus#Species" title="Eurypterus">sections below</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

</div>
</div>
<p>Members of <i>Eurypterus</i> existed for a relatively short time, yet they are the most abundant eurypterids found today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dist_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-dist-6">[6]</a></sup> They flourished between the Late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Llandovery_epoch#Llandovery" title="Llandovery epoch">Llandovery</a> <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(geology)" title="Epoch (geology)">epoch</a> (around 432 million years ago) to sometime during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P%C5%99%C3%ADdol%C3%AD_epoch" title="Přídolí epoch">Přídolí</a> epoch (418.1 million years ago) of the <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a> <a href="/wiki/Period_(geology)" title="Period (geology)">period</a>. A span of only around 10 to 14 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paleodb_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-paleodb-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>During this period, the landmasses were mostly restricted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" title="Southern hemisphere">southern hemisphere</a> of the Earth, with the <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a> <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> straddling the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a>. The equator had three continents (<a href="/wiki/Avalonia" title="Avalonia">Avalonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltica" title="Baltica">Baltica</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a>) which slowly drifted together to form the second supercontinent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laurussia" title="Laurussia">Laurussia</a> (also known as <a href="/wiki/Euramerica" title="Euramerica">Euramerica</a>, not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasia</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madrid_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-madrid-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ancestors of <i>Eurypterus</i> were believed to have originated from Baltica (eastern Laurussia, modern western Eurasia) based on the earliest recorded fossils. During the Silurian, they spread to Laurentia (western Laurussia, modern North America) when the two continents began to collide. They rapidly colonized the continent as <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>, becoming the most dominant eurypterid in the region. This accounts for why they are the most commonly found genus of eurypterids today. <i>Eurypterus</i> (and other members of <a href="/wiki/Eurypteroidea" title="Eurypteroidea">Eurypteroidea</a>), however, were unable to cross vast expanses of <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> between the two supercontinents during the Silurian. Their <a href="/wiki/Range_(biology)" title="Range (biology)">range</a> were thus limited to the coastlines and the large, shallow, and <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">hypersaline</a> inland seas of Laurussia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madrid_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-madrid-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dist_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-dist-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>They are now only known from fossils from North America, Europe, and northwestern <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Craton" title="Craton">cratons</a> that were the former components of Laurussia. While three species of <i>Eurypterus</i> were purportedly discovered in China in 1957, the evidence of them belonging to the genus (or if they were even eurypterids at all) is nonexistent. No other traces of <i>Eurypterus</i> in modern continents from Gondwana are currently known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dist_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-dist-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Eurypterus</i> are very common fossils in their regions of occurrence, millions of specimens are possible in a given area, though access to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formation_(stratigraphy)" title="Formation (stratigraphy)">rock formations</a> may be difficult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lacustris_fauna_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-lacustris_fauna-26">[26]</a></sup> Most fossil eurypterids are the disjointed shed exoskeleton (known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exuvia" title="Exuvia">exuviae</a>) of individuals after molting (<a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">ecdysis</a>). Some are complete but are most probably exuviae as well. Fossils of the actual remains of eurypterids (i.e. their <a href="/wiki/Cadaver" title="Cadaver">carcasses</a>) are relatively rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-madrid_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-madrid-3">[3]</a></sup> Fossil eurypterids are often deposited in characteristic windrows, probably a result of wave and wind action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wave_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-wave-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleobiology_and_paleoecology">Paleobiology and paleoecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurypterus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Paleobiology and paleoecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rowing.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1893" data-file-width="998" height="417" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Rowing.png/220px-Rowing.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Rowing.png/330px-Rowing.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Rowing.png/440px-Rowing.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rowing.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rowing in <i>Eurypterus</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Eurypterus</i> belongs to the suborder Eurypterina, eurypterids in which the sixth appendage had developed a broad swimming paddle remarkably similar to that of the modern-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimming_crab" title="Swimming crab">swimming crab</a>. These appendages could only be moved in near-horizontal planes (i.e. they can not be bent much upwards or downwards).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autec_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-autec-24">[24]</a></sup> They are generally thought to utilize a rowing type of locomotion. The paddles are almost vertically oriented on the backward and down stroke, pushing the animal forward and lifting it up. They are then oriented horizontally on the recovery stroke to slash through the water without pushing the animal back. This type of swimming is exhibited by <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_beetle" title="Water beetle">water beetles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merck_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-merck-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Subaqueous_flight.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="980" data-file-width="1910" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Subaqueous_flight.png/350px-Subaqueous_flight.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Subaqueous_flight.png/525px-Subaqueous_flight.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Subaqueous_flight.png/700px-Subaqueous_flight.png 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Subaqueous_flight.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Subaqueous flight in <i>Eurypterus</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>However, larger individuals may have been capable of underwater flying (or <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_locomotion" title="Aquatic locomotion">subaqueous flight</a>), in which the sinuous motions and shape of the paddles themselves acting as <a href="/wiki/Hydrofoil" title="Hydrofoil">hydrofoils</a> are enough to generate <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a>. This type is similar to that found in <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtle" title="Sea turtle">sea turtles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_lion" title="Sea lion">sea lions</a>. It has a relatively slower acceleration rate than the rowing type, especially since adults have proportionally smaller paddles than juveniles. But since the larger sizes of adults mean a higher <a href="/wiki/Drag_coefficient" title="Drag coefficient">drag coefficient</a>, using this type of propulsion is more energy-efficient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autec_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-autec-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merck2_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-merck2-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Juveniles probably swam using the rowing type, the rapid acceleration afforded by this propulsion is more suited for quickly escaping predators. A small 16.5 cm (6.5 in) <i>Eurypterus</i> could achieve two and a half body lengths per second immediately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autec_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-autec-24">[24]</a></sup> Larger adults, meanwhile, probably swam with the subaqueous flight type. The maximum velocity of adults when cruising would have been 3 to 4 m (9.8 to 13.1 ft) per second, slightly faster than turtles and <a href="/wiki/Sea_otter" title="Sea otter">sea otters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merck2_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-merck2-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gknight_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-gknight-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Eurypterus</i> did not swim to hunt, rather they simply swam in order to move from one feeding site to another quickly. Most of the time they walked on the substrate with their legs (including their swimming leg). They were <a href="/wiki/Generalist_and_specialist_species" title="Generalist and specialist species">generalist species</a>, equally likely to engage in <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scavenging" title="Scavenging">scavenging</a>. They hunted small soft-bodied <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> like worms. They utilized the mass of spines on their front appendages to both kill and hold them while they used their chelicerae to rip off pieces small enough to swallow. Young individuals may also have fallen prey to <a href="/wiki/Cannibalism_(zoology)" title="Cannibalism (zoology)">cannibalism</a> by larger adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autec_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-autec-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Eurypterus</i> were most probably <a href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> animals, as their remains are mostly found in intertidal shallow environments. The concentrations of <i>Eurypterus</i> fossils in certain sites has been interpreted to be a result of mass mating and molting behavior. Juveniles were likely to have inhabited nearshore hypersaline environments, safer from predators, and moved to deeper waters as they grew older and larger. Adults that reach sexual maturity would then <a href="/wiki/Animal_migration" title="Animal migration">migrate</a> en masse to shore areas in order to mate, lay eggs, and molt. Activities that would have made them more vulnerable to predators. This could also explain why the vast majority of fossils found in such sites are molts and not of actual animals. The same behavior can be seen in modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horseshoe_crabs" title="Horseshoe crabs">horseshoe crabs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-burk_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-burk-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Respiration">Respiration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurypterus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Respiration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Examinations of the <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_system" title="Respiratory system">respiratory systems</a> of <i>Eurypterus</i> have led many paleontologists to conclude that it was capable of breathing air and walking on land for a short amount of time. <i>Eurypterus</i> had two types of respiratory systems. Its main organs for breathing were the book gills inside the segments of the mesosoma. These structures were supported by semicircular 'ribs' and were probably attached near the center of the body, similar to the gills of modern horseshoe crabs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-resp_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-resp-21">[21]</a></sup> They were protected under platelike appendages (which actually formed the apparent 'belly' of <i>Eurypterus</i>) known as Blatfüsse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seldenjeram_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-seldenjeram-31">[31]</a></sup> These gills may have also played a role in <a href="/wiki/Osmoregulation" title="Osmoregulation">osmoregulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autec_24-5"><a href="#cite_note-autec-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The second system are the Kiemenplatten, also referred to as gill-tracts. These oval-shaped areas within the body wall of the preabdomen. Their surfaces are covered with numerous small spines arranged into hexagonal 'rosettes'. These areas were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vascularized" title="Vascularized">vascularized</a>, hence the conclusion that they were secondary breathing organs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-selden_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-selden-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>The function of the book gills are usually interpreted to be for aquatic breathing, while the Kiemenplatten are supplementary for temporary breathing on land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-resp_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-resp-21">[21]</a></sup> However, some authors have argued that the two systems alone could not have supported an organism the size of <i>Eurypterus</i>. Both structures might actually have been for breathing air and the true gills (for underwater breathing) of <i>Eurypterus</i> have yet to be discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-selden_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-selden-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seldenjeram_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-seldenjeram-31">[31]</a></sup> <i>Eurypterus</i>, however, were undoubtedly primarily aquatic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seldenjeram_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-seldenjeram-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ontogeny">Ontogeny</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurypterus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ontogeny">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Juvenile <i>Eurypterus</i> differed from adults in several ways. Their carapaces were narrower and longer (<a href="/wiki/Parabola" title="Parabola">parabolic</a>) in contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Trapezoid" title="Trapezoid">trapezoidal</a> carapaces of adults. The eyes are aligned almost laterally but move to a more anterior location during growth. The preabdomen also lengthened, increasing the overall length of the ophisthosoma. The swimming legs also became narrower and the telsons shorter and broader (though in <i>E. tetragonophthalmus</i> and <i>E. henningsmoeni</i> the telsons changed from being angular in juveniles to larger and more rounded in adults). All these changes are believed to be a result of the respiratory and reproductive requirements of adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<ul>
<li><i><b>Eurypterus flintstonensis</b></i> <small>Swartz, 1923</small> – Silurian, USA</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Probably a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synonym_(biology)" title="Synonym (biology)">synonym</a> of <i>E. remipes</i> or <i>E. lacustris</i>. Probably named after <a href="/wiki/Flintstone,_Georgia" title="Flintstone, Georgia">Flintstone, Georgia</a> (?). Specimen recovered from eastern United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-5"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Eurypterus hankeni</b></i> <small>Tetlie, 2006</small> – Silurian, Norway</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Small <i>Eurypterus</i> species, averaging at 10 to 15 centimetres (3.9 to 5.9 in) long. The largest specimen found is about 20 to 25 centimetres (7.9 to 9.8 in) in length. They can be distinguished by pustules and six scales at the rear margin of their carapaces. Appendages I to IV has two spines on each podomere. The postabdomen have small epimera. The pretelson has long pointed epimera. Telson has striations near its attachment to the pretelson. The species is named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_people" title="Norwegian people">Norwegian</a> paleontologist Nils-Martin Hanken, of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Troms%C3%B8" title="University of Tromsø">University of Tromsø</a>. Found in the <a href="/wiki/Steinsfjorden_Formation" title="Steinsfjorden Formation">Steinsfjorden Formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ringerike_(traditional_district)" title="Ringerike (traditional district)">Ringerike</a>, Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunlop_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-dunlop-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-6"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Eurypterus henningsmoeni</b></i> <small>Tetlie, 2002</small> – Silurian, Norway</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><i>Eurypterus</i> with broad paddles and metastoma. Postabdomen has small epimera. Pretelson has large rounded epimera with imbricate scales (overlapping, similar to fish scales). It is very similar and closely related to <i>E. tetragonophthalmus</i>. The species was named after the Norwegian paleontologist <a href="/wiki/Gunnar_Henningsmoen" title="Gunnar Henningsmoen">Gunnar Henningsmoen</a>. Found in <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A6rum" title="Bærum">Bærum</a>, Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunlop_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-dunlop-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-7"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Eurypterus laculatus</b></i> <small>Kjellesvig-Waering, 1958</small> – Silurian, USA &amp; Canada</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>The visual area of the compound eyes of this species are surrounded by depressions. The ocelli and the ocellar mound are small. No pustules or raised scales on the carapace or the first tergite. It is probably closely related to <i>E. dekayi</i>. Its specific name means "four-cornered", from Latin <i>laculatus</i> (four-cornered or checkered). Found in New York and Ontario.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-8"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Eurypterus lacustris</b></i> <small>Harlan, 1834</small> – Silurian, USA &amp; Canada</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><small>= <i>Eurypterus pachycheirus</i> Hall, 1859 – Silurian, USA &amp; Canada</small></dd>
<dd><small>= <i>Eurypterus robustus</i> Hall, 1859 – Silurian, USA &amp; Canada</small></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd>One of the two most common <i>Eurypterus</i> fossils found. It is very similar to <i>E. remipes</i> and often found in the same localities, but the eyes are placed at a more posterior position on the carapace of <i>E. lacustris</i>. It is also slightly larger with a slightly narrower metastoma. Its status as a distinct species was once disputed before diagnostic analysis by Tollerton in 1993. Its specific name means "from a lake", from Latin <i>lacus</i> (lake). Found in New York and Ontario.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-9"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Eurypterus leopoldi</b></i> <small>Tetlie, 2006</small> – Silurian, Canada</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Frontmost tergite is reduced. Metasoma is rhombiovate in shape with tooth-like projections at the anterior part. The pretelson has serrated edges. the epimera are large, semi-angular with angular striations. The telson is styliform with large angular striations interspersed among smaller more numerous striations. The species is named after <a href="/wiki/Port_Leopold" title="Port Leopold">Port Leopold</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Formation" title="Leopold Formation">Leopold Formation</a> where they were collected. Found in the Leopold Formation of <a href="/wiki/Somerset_Island_(Nunavut)" title="Somerset Island (Nunavut)">Somerset Island</a>, Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-10"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Eurypterus megalops</b></i> <small>Clarke &amp; Ruedemann, 1912</small> – Silurian, USA</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Specific name means "large eye", from Greek <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BC%CE%AD%CE%B3%CE%B1%CF%82" title="wikt:μέγας">μέγας</a> (<i>megas</i>, big or large) and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BD%A4%CF%88" title="wikt:ὤψ">ὤψ</a> (<i>ōps</i>, eye). Discovered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunlop_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-dunlop-7">[7]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li>?<i><b>Eurypterus minor</b></i> <small>Laurie, 1899</small> – Silurian, Scotland</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Small <i>Eurypterus</i> with large pustules on the carapace and abdomen. Does not possess the scale ornamentation found in other species of <i>Eurypterus</i>. It is the earliest known species of <i>Eurypterus</i>. They have large palpebral lobes (part of "cheeks" of the carapace adjacent to the compound eyes), making it easy to mistake their eyes for being oval. This enlargement is more typical of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Dolichopterus" title="Dolichopterus">Dolichopterus</a></i> and it may actually belong to <a href="/wiki/Dolichopteridae" title="Dolichopteridae">Dolichopteridae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dist_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-dist-6">[6]</a></sup> The specific name means "smaller", from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>minor</i>. Found in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Reservoir_Formation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Reservoir Formation (page does not exist)">Reservoir Formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pentland_Hills" title="Pentland Hills">Pentland Hills</a>, Scotland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-11"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Eurypterus ornatus</b></i> <small>Leutze, 1958</small> – Silurian, USA</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Ornamentation of pustules on the entire surface of the carapace and at least the first tergite. Does not possess raised scales. Its specific name means "adorned", from Latin <i>ōrnātus</i> (adorned, ornate). Recovered from <a href="/wiki/Fayette,_Ohio" title="Fayette, Ohio">Fayette</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-12"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Eurypterus pittsfordensis</b></i> <small>Sarle, 1903</small> – Silurian, USA</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>The posterior margin of the carapace has three raised scales. Apendages II to IV has two spines per podomere. The metastoma is rhomboid in shape with a deep notch at the front part. The postabdomen has serrated fringes at the middle with small angular epimera at the sides. The pretelson has large, semiangular epimera with angular striations at the margins. The telson is styliform with sparse angular striations at the margins. The name of the species comes from its place of discovery – the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Salina_shale&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Salina shale (page does not exist)">Salina shale</a> formations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsford_(town),_New_York" title="Pittsford (town), New York">Pittsford</a>, New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-13"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Eurypterus quebecensis</b></i> <small>Kjellesvig-Waering, 1958</small> – Silurian, Canada</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Has six raised scales on the posterior margin of the carapace but does not possess pustule ornamentation. It is named after the location it was recovered from – <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, Canada.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Eurypterus remipes</b></i> <small>DeKay, 1825</small> – Silurian, USA, Canada</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><small>= <i>Carcinosoma trigona</i> (Ruedemann, 1916) – Silurian, USA</small></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd>The most common <i>Eurypterus</i> species. Has four raised scales at the posterior margin of the carapace. Appendages I to IV has two spines on each podomere. Postabdomen has small epimera. Pretelson has small, semiangular epimera with imbricate scale ornamentation at the margins. The telson has serrated margins along most of its length. It is very similar to <i>E. lacustris</i> and can often only be distinguished by the position of the eyes. The specific name means "oar-foot", from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/remo" title="wikt:remo">rēmus</a></i> (oar) and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pes" title="wikt:pes">pes</a></i> (foot). Found in New York and Ontario.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-14"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Eurypterus serratus</b></i> <small>(Jones &amp; Woodward, 1888)</small> – Silurian, Sweden</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Similar to <i>E. pittsfordensis</i> and <i>E. leopoldi</i> but can be distinguished by the dense angular striations on their styliform telson. The specific name means "serrated", from Latin <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/serratus" title="wikt:serratus">serrātus</a></i> (sawn [into pieces]). Originally discovered from <a href="/wiki/Gotland" title="Gotland">Gotland</a>, Sweden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-15"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>
<ul>
<li><i><b>Eurypterus tetragonophthalmus</b></i> <small>Fischer, 1839</small> – Silurian, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><small>= <i>Eurypterus fischeri</i> Eichwald, 1854 – Silurian, Ukraine</small></dd>
<dd><small>= <i>Eurypterus fischeri</i> var. <i>rectangularis</i> Schmidt, 1883 – Silurian, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a></small></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
<dd>Four raised scales on the posterior margin of the carapce. Appendages II to IV each have two spines on each podomere. Postabdomen has small epimera. The pretelson has large, rounded epimera with imbricate scale ornamentation at the margins. Telson has imbricate scale ornamentations at the margins of the base which become serrations towards the tip. The specific name means "four-edged eye", from Greek <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tetra-" title="wikt:tetra-">τέσσαρες</a> (<i>tessares</i>, four), <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%CF%89%CE%BD%CE%AF%CE%B1" title="wikt:γωνία">γωνία</a> (<i>gōnia</i>, angle), and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ophthalmo-" title="wikt:ophthalmo-">ὀφθαλμός</a> (<i>ophthalmos</i>, eye). Found in the <a href="/wiki/Rootsik%C3%BCla,_Saare_County" title="Rootsiküla, Saare County">Rootsiküla Formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saaremaa" title="Saaremaa">Saaremaa</a> (<a href="/wiki/Saaremaa" title="Saaremaa">Ösel</a>), <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> with additional discoveries in Ukraine, Norway, and possibly <a href="/wiki/Moldova" title="Moldova">Moldova</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-16"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The list does not include the large number of fossils previously classified under <i>Eurypterus</i>. Most of them are now reclassified to other genera, identified as other animals (like <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudofossils" title="Pseudofossils">pseudofossils</a>, or remains of doubtful placement. Classification is based on Dunlop <i>et al.</i>(2011).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dunlop_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-dunlop-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phylogeny">Phylogeny</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eurypterus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Phylogeny">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The genus <i>Eurypterus</i> derives from <i>E. minor</i>, the oldest known species from the Llandovery of Scotland. <i>E. minor</i> is believed to have diverged from <i><a href="/wiki/Dolichopterus" title="Dolichopterus">Dolichopterus macrocheirus</a></i> sometime in the Llandovery. The following is the <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_tree" title="Phylogenetic tree">phylogenetic tree</a> of <i>Eurypterus</i> based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogeny" title="Phylogeny">phylogenetic studies</a> by O. Erik Tetlie in 2006. Some species are not represented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eur_25-17"><a href="#cite_note-eur-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
