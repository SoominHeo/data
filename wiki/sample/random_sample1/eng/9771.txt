<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Einojuhani Rautavaara</h1>

<p><b>Einojuhani Rautavaara</b> (<small>Finnish: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Estonian_and_Finnish" title="Help:IPA for Estonian and Finnish">[ˈɛi̯nɔjuhɑni ˈrɑu̯tɑvɑːrɑ]</a></span><small class="nowrap metadata"> (<a href="/wiki/File:Fi-Einojuhani_Rautavaara.ogg" title="File:Fi-Einojuhani Rautavaara.ogg"><img alt="" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/13px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/20px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Speaker_Icon.svg/26px-Speaker_Icon.svg.png 2x" width="13"/></a> <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/14/Fi-Einojuhani_Rautavaara.ogg" title="Fi-Einojuhani Rautavaara.ogg">listen</a>)</small>; 9 October 1928 – 27 July 2016) was a Finnish <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composer</a> of <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a>. He is among the most notable Finnish composers since <a href="/wiki/Jean_Sibelius" title="Jean Sibelius">Jean Sibelius</a> (1865–1957).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-WP-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rautavaara wrote a great number of works spanning various styles. These include 8 <a href="/wiki/Symphony" title="Symphony">symphonies</a>, 9 <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">operas</a> and 12 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concerti" title="Concerti">concerti</a>, as well as numerous vocal and <a href="/wiki/Chamber_music" title="Chamber music">chamber</a> works.</p>
<p>Having written early works using <a href="/wiki/Serialism" title="Serialism">12-tone serial</a> techniques, his later music may be described as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-romantic" title="Neo-romantic">Neo-romantic</a> and mystical. His major works include <i><a href="/wiki/Cantus_Arcticus" title="Cantus Arcticus">Cantus Arcticus</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._7_(Rautavaara)" title="Symphony No. 7 (Rautavaara)">Symphony No. 7 "Angel of Light"</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Einojuhani_Rautavaara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rautavaara was born in <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a> in 1928. His father Eino was an opera singer and cantor, and his mother Elsa was a doctor. Both of his parents died before he reached his 16th birthday, and he went to live with his aunt Hilja Teräskeli in a Helsinki suburb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rautavaara studied at the <a href="/wiki/Sibelius_Academy" title="Sibelius Academy">Sibelius Academy</a> in Helsinki under <a href="/wiki/Aarre_Merikanto" title="Aarre Merikanto">Aarre Merikanto</a> from 1948 to 1952. He first came to international attention when he won the Thor Johnson Contest for his composition <i><a href="/wiki/A_Requiem_in_Our_Time" title="A Requiem in Our Time">A Requiem in Our Time</a></i> in 1954, and the work prompted <a href="/wiki/Jean_Sibelius" title="Jean Sibelius">Jean Sibelius</a> to recommend him for a scholarship to study at the <a href="/wiki/Juilliard_School" title="Juilliard School">Juilliard School</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. There he was taught by <a href="/wiki/Vincent_Persichetti" title="Vincent Persichetti">Vincent Persichetti</a>, and he also took lessons from <a href="/wiki/Roger_Sessions" title="Roger Sessions">Roger Sessions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Copland" title="Aaron Copland">Aaron Copland</a> at <a href="/wiki/Tanglewood" title="Tanglewood">Tanglewood</a>. He graduated from the Sibelius Academy in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rautavaara served as a non-tenured teacher at the Sibelius Academy from 1957 to 1959, music archivist of the Helsinki Philharmonic Orchestra from 1959 to 1961, rector of the Käpylä Music Institute in Helsinki from 1965 to 1966, tenured teacher at the Sibelius Academy from 1966 to 1976, artist professor (appointed by the <a href="/wiki/Arts_Council_of_Finland" title="Arts Council of Finland">Arts Council of Finland</a>) from 1971 to 1976, and professor of composition at the Sibelius Academy from 1976 to 1990.</p>
<p>He married Heidi Maria Suovanen, an actor, in 1959. They had two sons and a daughter. They separated in 1982 and divorced in 1984, after he fell in love with Sinikka Koivisto, who was 29 years his junior. In 1984 he married Sinikka, who survived him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HS_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-HS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>After an <a href="/wiki/Aortic_dissection" title="Aortic dissection">aortic dissection</a> in January 2004, Rautavaara spent almost half a year in <a href="/wiki/Intensive_care_unit" title="Intensive care unit">intensive care</a> before recovering and continuing his work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-WP-1">[1]</a></sup> He died on 27 July 2016 in Helsinki from complications of hip surgery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-WP-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Einojuhani_Rautavaara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rautavaara_sep_2003_helsinki.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="227" data-file-width="173" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Rautavaara_sep_2003_helsinki.JPG/170px-Rautavaara_sep_2003_helsinki.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d8/Rautavaara_sep_2003_helsinki.JPG 1.5x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rautavaara_sep_2003_helsinki.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rautavaara in 2003</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Rautavaara was a prolific composer and wrote in a variety of forms and styles. His opus can be broadly divided into four periods: early "<a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism_(music)" title="Neoclassicism (music)">neoclassical</a>" period of the 1950s, exhibiting close ties to tradition; avant-garde and <a href="/wiki/Constructivism_(art)" title="Constructivism (art)">constructivist</a> stage of the 1960s, when he experimented with <a href="/wiki/Serialism" title="Serialism">serial</a> techniques, but abandoned them later in the decade; "<a href="/wiki/Neoromanticism_(music)" title="Neoromanticism (music)">neo-romantic</a>" period of the late 1960s and the 1970s; and an eclectic, "<a href="/wiki/Postmodern_music" title="Postmodern music">post-modern</a>" compositional style in which he blended a wide spectrum of stylistic techniques and genres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-White-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FG_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FG-6">[6]</a></sup> A recurring label given to his opus is "mysticism", for his fascination with metaphysical and religious subjects and texts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-WP-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-White-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FG_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FG-6">[6]</a></sup> (Several of his works have titles which allude to <a href="/wiki/Angel" title="Angel">angels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>)</p>
<p>His compositions include eight <a href="/wiki/Symphony" title="Symphony">symphonies</a>, 14 <a href="/wiki/Concerto" title="Concerto">concertos</a>, choral works (several for unaccompanied choir, including <i>Vigilia</i> (1971–1972)), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonata_(music)" title="Sonata (music)">sonatas</a> for various instruments, <a href="/wiki/String_quartet" title="String quartet">string quartets</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Chamber_music" title="Chamber music">chamber music</a>, and a number of biographical <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">operas</a> including <i><a href="/wiki/Vincent_(opera)" title="Vincent (opera)">Vincent</a></i> (1986–1987, based on the life of <a href="/wiki/Vincent_van_Gogh" title="Vincent van Gogh">Vincent van Gogh</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Aleksis_Kivi_(opera)" title="Aleksis Kivi (opera)">Aleksis Kivi</a></i> (1995–1996) and <i>Rasputin</i> (2001–2003).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s">1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Einojuhani_Rautavaara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Along with <a href="/wiki/Erik_Bergman" title="Erik Bergman">Erik Bergman</a>, Rautavaara was one of pioneers of serial composing in Finland in the early 1950s, although in the end he completed only several serial works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HS_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-HS-3">[3]</a></sup> His most important works from the period are the <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._3_(Rautavaara)" title="Symphony No. 3 (Rautavaara)">Third</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Symphony_No._4_(Rautavaara)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Symphony No. 4 (Rautavaara) (page does not exist)">Fourth</a> Symphony, and the opera <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kaivos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kaivos (page does not exist)">Kaivos</a></i> (The Mine), which saw only a television production in 1963,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> but was a source of material for string orchestra pieces <i>Canto I</i> (1960) and <i>Canto II</i> (1961) and the Third String Quartet (1965).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-White-5">[5]</a></sup> Even his serial works from the period carry obvious romantic and post-expressionist notes stylistically closer to <a href="/wiki/Alban_Berg" title="Alban Berg">Alban Berg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anton_Bruckner" title="Anton Bruckner">Anton Bruckner</a> than more straightforward serialists such as <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Boulez" title="Pierre Boulez">Pierre Boulez</a>. Rautavaara himself referred to the Third as the "Bruckner's symphony".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HS_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-HS-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-White-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the mid-1960s, Rautavaara fell into a creative crisis with serialism, noticing that the composition method was immensely laborious and its distance from the outcome too large. He found a resolution towards the end of the decade, when he began to explore different styles as he had earlier done in the Third Symphony. The Bach-influenced Cello Concerto No. 1 and Debussy-influenced <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anadyomene_(Rautavaara)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anadyomene (Rautavaara) (page does not exist)">Anadyomene</a></i> opened his creative deadlock. More eclectic works started to emerge, whose style loans and compositional techniques over time became characteristic of his style. Its hallmarks included three-chord-based, often modal harmonies, ringing softly romantic orchestrals, modernism springing from the new play modes, and finally the return of the twelve-tone passages embedded into the musical texture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HS_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-HS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Einojuhani_Rautavaara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1970s were Rautavaara's most productive period. In a short time he composed an extensive body of music, most of which has withstood the test of time. The softly-sounding new style was noticed by choirs, who commissioned a number of choral songs from the composer. His major choral works <i>Vigilia</i> and <i>True and False Unicorn</i> were made during 1971. <i>Cantus Arcticus</i> followed immediately after, in the beginning of 1972, and in the summer he composed an extensive score for male choir, <i>A Book of Life</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HS_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-HS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1970s Rautavaara started working more extensively on operas. Comical opera-musical <i>Apollo contra Marsyas</i>, made in 1970 in cooperation with librettist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bengt_V._Wall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bengt V. Wall (page does not exist)">Bengt V. Wall</a> turned out to be a disappointment. After that, based on motifs from <a href="/wiki/Kalevala" title="Kalevala">Kalevala</a> he composed the <i>The Myth of Sampo</i> (1974/1983) and <i>Marjatta, lowly maiden</i> (1975). His mature style in operas was exhibited later, in <i>Thomas</i> (1985), while subsequent <i>Vincent</i> (1987), <i>The House of the Sun</i> (1991) earned him notable international success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FG_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-FG-6">[6]</a></sup> His later operas include <i>The Gift of the Magi</i> (1994), <i>Aleksis Kivi</i> (1997) and <i>Rasputin</i> (2003).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HS_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-HS-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A number of his works have parts for <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tape" title="Magnetic tape">magnetic tape</a>, including <i><a href="/wiki/Cantus_Arcticus" title="Cantus Arcticus">Cantus Arcticus</a></i> (1972, also known as <i>Concerto for Birds &amp; Orchestra</i>) for taped <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bird_song" title="Bird song">bird song</a> and orchestra,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2">[2]</a></sup> and <i>True and False Unicorn</i> (1971, second version 1974, revised 2001–02), the final version of which is for three reciters, choir, orchestra and tape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s_and_1990s">1980s and 1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Einojuhani_Rautavaara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1980s and 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the second part of the 1970s, Rautavaara gradually turned toward stylistic synthesis, evident in the Organ Concerto "Annunciations" (1977) and the Violin Concerto (1977), and especially in <i>Angels and Visitations</i> (1978) for orchestra, one of his most fascinating works, the first from the "Angel" series, which also includes the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Symphony_No._5_(Rautavaara)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Symphony No. 5 (Rautavaara) (page does not exist)">Fifth Symphony</a>, whose working title was "Monologue with Angels", double-bass concerto <i>Angel of Dusk</i> in 1980, and the <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._7_(Rautavaara)" title="Symphony No. 7 (Rautavaara)">Seventh Symphony "Angel of Light"</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-White-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>His opera <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_(opera)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas (opera) (page does not exist)">Thomas</a></i> (1985) marked the beginning of his mature operatic style, combining neo-romantic harmonies with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleatoric" title="Aleatoric">aleatoric</a> counterpoint, twelve-tone rows and different modal systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-White-5">[5]</a></sup> The libretto, written by Rautavaara himself, tells the story of 13th-century Bishop of Finland as experienced by the protagonist himself, again using Kalevala motifs. A similar <a href="/wiki/First-person_narrative" title="First-person narrative">first-person narrative</a> is used in the next opera <i>Vincent</i> (1987) devoted to <a href="/wiki/Vincent_van_Gogh" title="Vincent van Gogh">Vincent van Gogh</a>. Along with <i>The House of the Sun</i> (1991), the operas earned him notable international success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FG_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-FG-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>His most widely acclaimed Seventh Symphony earned a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cannes_Classical_Award" title="Cannes Classical Award">Cannes Classical Award</a> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy" title="Grammy">Grammy</a> nomination for the recording by the <a href="/wiki/Helsinki_Philharmonic_Orchestra" title="Helsinki Philharmonic Orchestra">Helsinki Philharmonic Orchestra</a> conducted by <a href="/wiki/Leif_Segerstam" title="Leif Segerstam">Leif Segerstam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Apart from the <i>Angel of Light</i>, his notable instrumental works from the period include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symphony_No._6_(Rautavaara)" title="Symphony No. 6 (Rautavaara)">Sixth Symphony "Vincentiana"</a> (1992), based on <i>Vincent</i>; the Third Piano Concerto "Gift of Dreams" (1998), commissioned by <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Ashkenazy" title="Vladimir Ashkenazy">Vladimir Ashkenazy</a>; orchestral work <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Autumn_Gardens&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Autumn Gardens (page does not exist)">Autumn Gardens</a></i> (1998), commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Chamber_Orchestra" title="Scottish Chamber Orchestra">Scottish Chamber Orchestra</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Symphony_No._8_(Rautavaara)" title="Symphony No. 8 (Rautavaara)">Eighth Symphony "The Journey"</a>, commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Orchestra" title="Philadelphia Orchestra">Philadelphia Orchestra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-White-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Einojuhani_Rautavaara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rautavaara's later works include orchestral works <i>Book of Visions</i> (2003–2005), <i>Manhattan Trilogy</i> (2003–2005) and <i>Before the Icons</i> (2005) which is an expanded version of his early piano work <i>Icons</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2005 he finished a work for violin and piano called <i>Lost Landscapes</i>, commissioned by the violinist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midori_Goto" title="Midori Goto">Midori Goto</a>. Orchestral work <i>A Tapestry of Life</i> was premiered by the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Symphony_Orchestra" title="New Zealand Symphony Orchestra">New Zealand Symphony Orchestra</a> in April 2008, directed by <a href="/wiki/Pietari_Inkinen" title="Pietari Inkinen">Pietari Inkinen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Rautavaara wrote a percussion concerto called <i><a href="/wiki/Incantations_(composition)" title="Incantations (composition)">Incantations</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Colin_Currie" title="Colin Currie">Colin Currie</a> in 2008 and a Second Cello Concerto for <a href="/wiki/Truls_M%C3%B8rk" title="Truls Mørk">Truls Mørk</a> in 2009. In 2010 Rautavaara's "Christmas Carol" was commissioned and performed by the men and boys choir of <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="King's College, Cambridge">King's College, Cambridge</a> (UK) for their annual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Festival_of_Nine_Lessons_and_Carols" title="Festival of Nine Lessons and Carols">Festival of Nine Lessons and Carols</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2011 Rautavaara completed two larger-scale compositions: <i>Missa a Capella</i> (premiered in the Netherlands, November 2011) and a work for string orchestra, <i>Into the Heart of Light</i>, which premiered in September 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rautavaara did not live to see the first opera stage première of <i>Kaivos</i>, the uncensored version, which took place 21 October 2016 in <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discography">Discography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Einojuhani_Rautavaara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Discography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Almost all of Rautavaara's works have been recorded by <a href="/wiki/Ondine_(record_label)" title="Ondine (record label)">Ondine</a>. Some of his major works have also been recorded by <a href="/wiki/Naxos_Records" title="Naxos Records">Naxos</a>. An album of vocal works called "Rautavaara Songs" was recorded by the Swedish label <a href="/wiki/BIS_Records" title="BIS Records">BIS Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
