<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Éire Nua</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p><b>Éire Nua</b>, or "New Ireland", was a proposal supported by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Provisional_IRA" title="Provisional IRA">Provisional IRA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a> during the 1970s and early 1980s for a <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federal</a> <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">United Ireland</a>. The proposal was particularly associated with the <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>-based leadership group centered on <a href="/wiki/Ruair%C3%AD_%C3%93_Br%C3%A1daigh" title="Ruairí Ó Brádaigh">Ruairí Ó Brádaigh</a> and <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1ith%C3%AD_%C3%93_Conaill" title="Dáithí Ó Conaill">Dáithí Ó Conaill</a>, who were the authors of the policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Éire Nua is still supported by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continuity_IRA" title="Continuity IRA">Continuity IRA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republican_Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Republican Sinn Féin">Republican Sinn Féin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Na_Fianna_%C3%89ireann" title="Na Fianna Éireann">Na Fianna Éireann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cumann_na_mBan" title="Cumann na mBan">Cumann na mBan</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideology">Ideology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%89ire_Nua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ideology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Éire Nua envisaged an all-<a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> republic that would be created when the British withdrew from <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. It also involved the dissolution of the existing <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, which many republicans considered an illegitimate entity imposed by the British in 1922. Under Éire Nua, Ireland would become a <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federal</a> state with parliaments for each of its four historic <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Ireland" title="Provinces of Ireland">provinces</a>, as well as a central parliament based in <a href="/wiki/Athlone" title="Athlone">Athlone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The purpose of the federal structure was twofold. Firstly, it was intended to show <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unionists_(Ireland)" title="Unionists (Ireland)">unionists</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> that they would have some kind of self-government in a <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">united Ireland</a>. This would be achieved by the provision of a parliament, <i>Dáil Uladh</i>, for <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a>. However, by including all of historic Ulster—nine counties instead of the six in Northern Ireland—it was intended that the unionist majority would be slim enough to prevent abuses against the Catholic/nationalist population in the province.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Secondly, the federal parliaments were intended to redress the perceived economic imbalance between the eastern and western parts of Ireland, and was hoped to enable prosperity in the poorer west of the country.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish_reactions_and_decline_in_popularity">Irish reactions and decline in popularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%89ire_Nua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Irish reactions and decline in popularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many members of Sinn Féin, particularly in the north, objected to Éire Nua on the grounds that it would perpetuate the dominance of Protestant unionists in the north of the country. Despite this, Éire Nua committees were established at least in Ulster and Connacht, largely due to the efforts of <a href="/wiki/Desmond_Fennell" title="Desmond Fennell">Desmond Fennell</a> and Emmett O'Connell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the scheme was dismissed as unworkable by some influential Republicans. When Northern Republicans grouped around <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Adams" title="Gerry Adams">Gerry Adams</a> gained control of the IRA and Sinn Féin in the late 1970s, they attacked the policy. In 1982, the Sinn Féin Ard Fheis voted to drop the policy, and the following year all reference to it in the Sinn Féin Constitution and rules was removed, and it was removed as the policy of the Republican movement in favour of the creation of a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary</a> Irish Republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ó Brádaigh and his supporters walked out of the 1986 Ard Fheis after a motion was passed that ended the Republican policy of <a href="/wiki/Abstentionism" title="Abstentionism">abstentionism</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a> and reconvened the Ard Fheis at the West County Hotel in the village of <a href="/wiki/Chapelizod" title="Chapelizod">Chapelizod</a> just west of Dublin. Henceforth referring to itself as <a href="/wiki/Republican_Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Republican Sinn Féin">Republican Sinn Féin</a> to distinguish itself from former associates, the party still advocates the Éire Nua agenda.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._reactions">U.S. reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%89ire_Nua&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: U.S. reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The reaction of the U.S. Administration to the Éire Nua program was mixed. Due in large part to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish-American" title="Irish-American">Irish-American</a> pressure at home, a synopsis of Éire Nua was entered into the <i><a href="/wiki/Congressional_Record" title="Congressional Record">Congressional Record</a></i> as a solution that "merits consideration" to the crisis in Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Officials in Ireland were less optimistic, placing more hope in the <a href="/wiki/Sunningdale_Agreement" title="Sunningdale Agreement">Sunningdale Agreement</a>. Ambassador John Moore did note, however: “In long term, some sort of federated Ireland seems a real possibility, but path to it seems more likely to be the pragmatic Sunningdale route than the dramatic one advocated by <a href="/wiki/Desmond_Boal" title="Desmond Boal">Boal</a> and the Provos.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1974DUBLIN00184_b_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-1974DUBLIN00184_b-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
