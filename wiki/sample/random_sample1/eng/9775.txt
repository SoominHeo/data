<p><b>Amos Aaron Strunk</b> (January 22, 1889 – July 22, 1979) was a <a href="/wiki/Center_fielder" title="Center fielder">center fielder</a> who played in <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> from <a href="/wiki/1908_in_baseball" title="1908 in baseball">1908</a> through <a href="/wiki/1924_in_baseball" title="1924 in baseball">1924</a>. A member of four <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a> champion teams, Strunk batted and threw left-handed. He was born in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>.</p>
<p>A dependable and speedy player, both on the basepaths and in the field, Strunk was <a href="/wiki/Scout_(sport)" title="Scout (sport)">scouted</a> and signed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Athletics" title="Philadelphia Athletics">Philadelphia Athletics</a>' <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">manager</a> <a href="/wiki/Connie_Mack" title="Connie Mack">Connie Mack</a>, who did not hesitate to call him "the most underrated outfielder in baseball".</p>
<p>Strunk reached the majors in 1908 with the Athletics, spending nine years with them before moving to the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> (1918–19), and played again for Philadelphia (1919–20) and in parts of four seasons with the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_White_Sox" title="Chicago White Sox">Chicago White Sox</a> (1920–23). Then, he returned with the Athletics in 1924, his last major league season. Five times he led <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American League</a> <a href="/wiki/Outfielder" title="Outfielder">outfielders</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fielding_percentage" title="Fielding percentage">fielding percentage</a>, and played in five World Series with the Athletics (<a href="/wiki/1910_World_Series" title="1910 World Series">1910</a>–<a href="/wiki/1911_World_Series" title="1911 World Series">11</a>, <a href="/wiki/1913_World_Series" title="1913 World Series">1913</a>–<a href="/wiki/1914_World_Series" title="1914 World Series">14</a>) and Red Sox (<a href="/wiki/1918_World_Series" title="1918 World Series">1918</a>).</p>
<p>In a 17-season career, Strunk was a .284 hitter (1418-for-4999) with 15 <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home runs</a> and 530 <a href="/wiki/Run_batted_in" title="Run batted in">RBI</a> in 1512 <a href="/wiki/Games_played" title="Games played">games played</a>, including 696 <a href="/wiki/Run_(baseball)" title="Run (baseball)">runs</a>, 213 <a href="/wiki/Double_(baseball)" title="Double (baseball)">doubles</a>, 96 <a href="/wiki/Triple_(baseball)" title="Triple (baseball)">triples</a> and 185 <a href="/wiki/Stolen_base" title="Stolen base">stolen bases</a>.</p>
<p>Following his baseball career, Strunk spent fifty years in the insurance business. He died in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_places_in_Pennsylvania:_La-Ll" title="List of places in Pennsylvania: La-Ll">Llanerch, Pennsylvania</a>, at the age of 90.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Highlights">Highlights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amos_Strunk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Highlights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>From 1911–18, he averaged 20 stolen bases per season, with a career-high 29 in <a href="/wiki/1912_in_baseball" title="1912 in baseball">1912</a>.</li>
<li>In three seasons, he hit .300 or more, with a career-high .332 in <a href="/wiki/1921_in_baseball" title="1921 in baseball">1921</a>.</li>
<li>In 1923, he led the AL with 12 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinch-hitter" title="Pinch-hitter">pinch-hits</a> in 39 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/At-bat" title="At-bat">at-bats</a> (.308).</li>
<li>He achieved a notable 1.73 <a href="/wiki/Walk-to-strikeout_ratio" title="Walk-to-strikeout ratio">walk-to-strikeout ratio</a> (573-to-331).</li>
</ul>
