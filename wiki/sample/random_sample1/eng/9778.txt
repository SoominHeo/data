<p><b>Amy Marie Madigan</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (born September 11, 1950) is an American actress, producer, and singer. She was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress">Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress</a> for the 1985 film <i><a href="/wiki/Twice_in_a_Lifetime_(film)" title="Twice in a Lifetime (film)">Twice in a Lifetime</a></i>. Her other film credits include <i><a href="/wiki/Love_Child_(1982_film)" title="Love Child (1982 film)">Love Child</a></i> (1982), <i><a href="/wiki/Places_in_the_Heart" title="Places in the Heart">Places in the Heart</a></i> (1984), <i><a href="/wiki/Field_of_Dreams" title="Field of Dreams">Field of Dreams</a></i> (1989), <i><a href="/wiki/Uncle_Buck" title="Uncle Buck">Uncle Buck</a></i> (1989), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Half_(film)" title="The Dark Half (film)">The Dark Half</a></i> (1993), <i><a href="/wiki/Pollock_(film)" title="Pollock (film)">Pollock</a></i> (2000), and <i><a href="/wiki/Gone_Baby_Gone" title="Gone Baby Gone">Gone Baby Gone</a></i> (2007). Madigan won the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actress_%E2%80%93_Series,_Miniseries_or_Television_Film" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actress – Series, Miniseries or Television Film">Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actress on Television</a> and was nominated for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Lead_Actress_in_a_Miniseries_or_a_Movie" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Miniseries or a Movie">Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Miniseries or a Movie</a> for her portrayal of <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Weddington" title="Sarah Weddington">Sarah Weddington</a> in the 1989 television film <i><a href="/wiki/Roe_vs._Wade_(film)" title="Roe vs. Wade (film)">Roe vs. Wade</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amy_Madigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Madigan was born in Southside, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, the daughter of John J. Madigan (1918–2012), a well-known journalist who gave political commentary on shows such as <i><a href="/wiki/Meet_the_Press" title="Meet the Press">Meet the Press</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Face_the_Nation" title="Face the Nation">Face the Nation</a></i>, and worked for <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i>. He interviewed a range of political figures, from <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr." title="Martin Luther King, Jr.">Martin Luther King, Jr.</a>, and hosted his own show with <a href="/wiki/WBBM_(AM)" title="WBBM (AM)">WBBM (AM)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_J._2-0"><a href="#cite_note-John_J.-2">[2]</a></sup> Her mother was Dolores (<a href="/wiki/Given_name#Name_at_birth" title="Given name">née</a> Hanlon; 1921–92), an administrative assistant and amateur actress who performed in community theatre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolores_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dolores-3">[3]</a></sup> She has two brothers: Jack and Jim.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dolores_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dolores-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1993, a year after her mother's death, her father was remarried to a woman named Elizabeth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_J._2-1"><a href="#cite_note-John_J.-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Madigan attended Catholic schools and performed in school plays. In the 1960s, she studied piano at the <a href="/wiki/The_Music_Conservatory_of_Chicago_College_of_Performing_Arts" title="The Music Conservatory of Chicago College of Performing Arts">Chicago Conservatory of Music</a>, and went on to graduate from <a href="/wiki/Marquette_University" title="Marquette University">Marquette University</a> with a degree in <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> She moved to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Madigan later studied acting at the <a href="/wiki/Lee_Strasberg_Theatre_and_Film_Institute" title="Lee Strasberg Theatre and Film Institute">Lee Strasberg Theatre and Film Institute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alamo_Bay_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alamo_Bay-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amy_Madigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amy_Madigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1970s, Madigan pursued a career as a musician, singing lead vocals for the band Jelly, whose only album, <i>A True Story</i> (1977), was released by <a href="/wiki/Asylum_Records" title="Asylum Records">Asylum Records</a>. Madigan appeared in <i><a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">Playboy</a></i> (June 1978) nude and covered in jelly, to promote her band. She toured across the United States performing for several bands throughout the late 1970s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting">Acting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amy_Madigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1980s, Madigan transitioned from a singing career to acting, and studied at the <a href="/wiki/Lee_Strasberg_Theatre_and_Film_Institute" title="Lee Strasberg Theatre and Film Institute">Lee Strasberg Theatre and Film Institute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alamo_Bay_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alamo_Bay-6">[6]</a></sup> Her first television role was as the character Adele on an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Hart_to_Hart" title="Hart to Hart">Hart to Hart</a></i> in 1981. She followed this guest starring role with a role in the <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Award" title="Emmy Award">Emmy</a>-nominated television film <i>Crazy Times</i>. The following year, Madigan made her film debut as Terry Jean Moore in <i><a href="/wiki/Love_Child_(1982_film)" title="Love Child (1982 film)">Love Child</a></i>, for which she was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_New_Star_of_the_Year_%E2%80%93_Actress" title="Golden Globe Award for New Star of the Year – Actress">Golden Globe Award for New Star of the Year – Actress</a>. In 1983, she starred as Alison Ransom in the Emmy-winning television film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Day_After" title="The Day After">The Day After</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1984, she portrayed McCoy in the cult film <i><a href="/wiki/Streets_of_Fire" title="Streets of Fire">Streets of Fire</a></i>, and also had a supporting role as Viola Kelsey in <i><a href="/wiki/Places_in_the_Heart" title="Places in the Heart">Places in the Heart</a></i>, which was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Picture" title="Academy Award for Best Picture">Academy Award for Best Picture</a>. In 1985, she starred in the television film <i>The Laundromat</i> written by <a href="/wiki/Marsha_Norman" title="Marsha Norman">Marsha Norman</a>, opposite <a href="/wiki/Carol_Burnett" title="Carol Burnett">Carol Burnett</a>. She won a <a href="/wiki/CableACE_Award" title="CableACE Award">CableACE Award</a> for her performance as Deedee Johnson. She then co-starred as Glory Scheer, alongside her husband <a href="/wiki/Ed_Harris" title="Ed Harris">Ed Harris</a>, in <i><a href="/wiki/Alamo_Bay" title="Alamo Bay">Alamo Bay</a></i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Malle" title="Louis Malle">Louis Malle</a>. Also in 1985, she portrayed Sunny Mackenzie-Sobel in <i><a href="/wiki/Twice_in_a_Lifetime_(film)" title="Twice in a Lifetime (film)">Twice in a Lifetime</a></i>, for which she was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actress_%E2%80%93_Motion_Picture" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actress – Motion Picture">Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actress – Motion Picture</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress">Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress</a>.</p>

<p>Madigan made her <a href="/wiki/Off-Broadway" title="Off-Broadway">Off-Broadway</a> debut in 1987, portraying Sue Jack Tiller in <i>The Lucky Spot</i> by <a href="/wiki/Beth_Henley" title="Beth Henley">Beth Henley</a>, for which she won a <a href="/wiki/Theatre_World_Award" title="Theatre World Award">Theatre World Award</a> and was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Drama_Desk_Award_for_Outstanding_Actress_in_a_Play" title="Drama Desk Award for Outstanding Actress in a Play">Drama Desk Award for Outstanding Actress in a Play</a>. In 1988, she was nominated for an <a href="/wiki/Independent_Spirit_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Female" title="Independent Spirit Award for Best Supporting Female">Independent Spirit Award for Best Supporting Female</a> for her performance in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prince_of_Pennsylvania" title="The Prince of Pennsylvania">The Prince of Pennsylvania</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> That same year, she performed in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Lie_of_the_Mind" title="A Lie of the Mind">A Lie of the Mind</a></i> at the <a href="/wiki/Mark_Taper_Forum" title="Mark Taper Forum">Mark Taper Forum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1989, Madigan played the wife of <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Costner" title="Kevin Costner">Kevin Costner</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Field_of_Dreams" title="Field of Dreams">Field of Dreams</a></i>, which was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Picture" title="Academy Award for Best Picture">Academy Award for Best Picture</a>. That same year, she played Chanice Kobolowski, the girlfriend of <a href="/wiki/John_Candy" title="John Candy">John Candy</a>'s character, in the <a href="/wiki/John_Hughes_(filmmaker)" title="John Hughes (filmmaker)">John Hughes</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Uncle_Buck" title="Uncle Buck">Uncle Buck</a></i>. Also in 1989, she won a <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Actress_%E2%80%93_Series,_Miniseries_or_Television_Film" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actress – Series, Miniseries or Television Film">Golden Globe Award for Best Supporting Actress – Series, Miniseries or Television Film</a> and was nominated for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy_Award_for_Outstanding_Lead_Actress_in_a_Miniseries_or_a_Movie" title="Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Miniseries or a Movie">Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Miniseries or a Movie</a> for her performance as <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Weddington" title="Sarah Weddington">Sarah Weddington</a> in the television film <i><a href="/wiki/Roe_vs._Wade_(film)" title="Roe vs. Wade (film)">Roe vs. Wade</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1990, Madigan starred opposite <a href="/wiki/Paula_Kelly_(actress)" title="Paula Kelly (actress)">Paula Kelly</a> in <i>Stevie Wants To Play The Blues</i> by <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Machado" title="Eduardo Machado">Eduardo Machado</a>, for which she won a <a href="/wiki/Drama-Logue_Award" title="Drama-Logue Award">Drama-Logue Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1991, she starred opposite <a href="/wiki/Olympia_Dukakis" title="Olympia Dukakis">Olympia Dukakis</a> in the Emmy-nominated television film <i>Lucky Day</i>. Madigan made her <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> debut in the role of Stella Kowalski in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_Streetcar_Named_Desire_(play)" title="A Streetcar Named Desire (play)">A Streetcar Named Desire</a></i> in 1992, opposite <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Lange" title="Jessica Lange">Jessica Lange</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alec_Baldwin" title="Alec Baldwin">Alec Baldwin</a>. She was nominated for an <a href="/wiki/Outer_Critics_Circle_Award" title="Outer Critics Circle Award">Outer Critics Circle Award</a> for Outstanding Debut Performance for her role in the play. In 1996, she and Harris produced and starred in the television film <i><a href="/wiki/Riders_of_the_Purple_Sage_(1996_film)" title="Riders of the Purple Sage (1996 film)">Riders of the Purple Sage</a></i>. Madigan then starred with <a href="/wiki/Tilda_Swinton" title="Tilda Swinton">Tilda Swinton</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Female_Perversions" title="Female Perversions">Female Perversions</a></i>. In 1997, she was nominated for an <a href="/wiki/Independent_Spirit_Award_for_Best_Supporting_Female" title="Independent Spirit Award for Best Supporting Female">Independent Spirit Award for Best Supporting Female</a> for her performance as Brett Armerson in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Loved_(film)" title="Loved (film)">Loved</a></i>. In 2000, she portrayed <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Guggenheim" title="Peggy Guggenheim">Peggy Guggenheim</a> in the Oscar-winning film <i><a href="/wiki/Pollock_(film)" title="Pollock (film)">Pollock</a></i>, which her husband directed, produced, and starred in.</p>
<p>In 2002, she had a supporting role as Reggie Fluty, the officer who first responded to the call for aid for a dying <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Shepard" title="Matthew Shepard">Matthew Shepard</a>, in the Emmy-nominated television film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Laramie_Project_(film)" title="The Laramie Project (film)">The Laramie Project</a></i>. In 2005, Madigan starred as Lori Lansky in <i><a href="/wiki/Winter_Passing" title="Winter Passing">Winter Passing</a></i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Adam_Rapp" title="Adam Rapp">Adam Rapp</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The following year, she had a supporting role as Patricia Carver, a CIA headquarters analyst, in the Emmy-winning television film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Path_to_9/11" title="The Path to 9/11">The Path to 9/11</a></i>. In 2007, Madigan played the sister-in-law of Helene (<a href="/wiki/Amy_Ryan" title="Amy Ryan">Amy Ryan</a>), Beatrice "Bea" McCready, in the Oscar-nominated film <i><a href="/wiki/Gone_Baby_Gone" title="Gone Baby Gone">Gone Baby Gone</a></i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Ben_Affleck" title="Ben Affleck">Ben Affleck</a>. In 2008, she played Dr. Katharine Wyatt on several episodes of <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a>'s medical drama series <i><a href="/wiki/Grey%27s_Anatomy" title="Grey's Anatomy">Grey's Anatomy</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> She then guest starred on <a href="/wiki/TNT_(TV_channel)" title="TNT (TV channel)">TNT</a>'s crime drama series <i><a href="/wiki/Saving_Grace_(TV_series)" title="Saving Grace (TV series)">Saving Grace</a></i> as Gretchen Lagardi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2011, she guest starred in the final episode of TNT's drama series <i><a href="/wiki/Memphis_Beat" title="Memphis Beat">Memphis Beat</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2016, Madigan starred in the revival of <a href="/wiki/Sam_Shepard" title="Sam Shepard">Sam Shepard</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/Buried_Child" title="Buried Child">Buried Child</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/The_New_Group" title="The New Group">The New Group</a> at the The Pershing Square Signature Center. The show transferred to the <a href="/wiki/West_End_theatre" title="West End theatre">West End</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Studios" title="Trafalgar Studios">Trafalgar Studios</a> in November 2016, where Madigan reprised her role as Halie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amy_Madigan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Madigan has been married to actor <a href="/wiki/Ed_Harris" title="Ed Harris">Ed Harris</a> since November 21, 1983. They have one daughter, Lily Dolores Harris (born May 3, 1993).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Madigan and Harris have been frequent collaborators over the course of their careers.</p>
