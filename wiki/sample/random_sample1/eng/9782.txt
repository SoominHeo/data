<p><b>Ava Marie DuVernay</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/juː/ long 'u' in 'cute'">juː</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ər/ 'er' in 'finger'">ər</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born August 24, 1972) is an American director, screenwriter, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Film_marketing" title="Film marketing">film marketer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Film_distributor" title="Film distributor">film distributor</a>. At the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Sundance_Film_Festival" title="Sundance Film Festival">Sundance Film Festival</a>, DuVernay won the Best Director Prize for her second feature film <i><a href="/wiki/Middle_of_Nowhere_(2012_film)" title="Middle of Nowhere (2012 film)">Middle of Nowhere</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HuffPo-MoN-2012_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-HuffPo-MoN-2012-1">[1]</a></sup> becoming the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African-American" title="African-American">African-American</a> woman to win the award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATimes-MoN-2012_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-LATimes-MoN-2012-2">[2]</a></sup> For her work in <i><a href="/wiki/Selma_(film)" title="Selma (film)">Selma</a></i>, DuVernay was the first black female director to be nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award" title="Golden Globe Award">Golden Globe Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HReporter-SelmaGG-2014_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-HReporter-SelmaGG-2014-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian-Selma-2014_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-Selma-2014-4">[4]</a></sup> With <i>Selma</i>, she was also the first black female director to have her film nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Picture" title="Academy Award for Best Picture">Academy Award for Best Picture</a>, though she was not nominated for Best Director.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ava_DuVernay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>DuVernay was born in <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_California" title="Long Beach, California">Long Beach, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio-5">[5]</a></sup> She grew up in <a href="/wiki/Lynwood,_California" title="Lynwood, California">Lynwood, California</a> (near <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrap15_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-wrap15-6">[6]</a></sup> and graduated in 1990 from <a href="/wiki/St._Joseph_High_School_(Lakewood,_California)" title="St. Joseph High School (Lakewood, California)">Saint Joseph High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lakewood,_California" title="Lakewood, California">Lakewood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StJosephsHigh_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-StJosephsHigh-7">[7]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">University of California, Los Angeles</a> (UCLA), she was a double major in English and African-American studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-bio-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCLAMag-MoN-2012_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-UCLAMag-MoN-2012-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conv_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-conv-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>During her summer vacations, she would travel to the childhood home of her father, Murray Maye (which was not far from <a href="/wiki/Selma,_Alabama" title="Selma, Alabama">Selma, Alabama</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60Minutes-Selma-2015_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-60Minutes-Selma-2015-10">[10]</a></sup> DuVernay said that these summers influenced the making of <i>Selma,</i> as her father saw the <a href="/wiki/Selma_to_Montgomery_marches" title="Selma to Montgomery marches">Selma to Montgomery marches</a> as a small child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RStone-Selma-2015_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-RStone-Selma-2015-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ava_DuVernay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Her first interest was in journalism, a choice influenced by an internship with <a href="/wiki/CBS_News" title="CBS News">CBS News</a>, where she was assigned to help cover the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People_of_the_State_of_California_vs._Orenthal_James_Simpson" title="People of the State of California vs. Orenthal James Simpson">O.J. Simpson murder trial</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCLAMag-MoN-2012_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-UCLAMag-MoN-2012-8">[8]</a></sup> She became disillusioned with journalism however, and decided to move into public relations, eventually opening her own public relations firm, <i>The DuVernay Agency.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-bio-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCLAMag-MoN-2012_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-UCLAMag-MoN-2012-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN-MoN-2012_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-MoN-2012-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt100512_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt100512-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ava_DuVernay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DuVernay made her feature directorial debut in 2008 with the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/This_Is_the_Life_(2008_film)" title="This Is the Life (2008 film)">This Is the Life</a>,</i> a history of LA's <a href="/wiki/Good_Life_Cafe" title="Good Life Cafe">Good Life Cafe</a>'s arts movement. DuVernay began with documentaries because they can be done on a smaller budget than a feature film, and she could learn the trade while doing so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Interview-MoN-2012_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Interview-MoN-2012-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2011, DuVernay's first narrative feature film, <i><a href="/wiki/I_Will_Follow_(film)" title="I Will Follow (film)">I Will Follow</a></i>, a drama starring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salli_Richardson-Whitfield" title="Salli Richardson-Whitfield">Salli Richardson-Whitfield</a>, was released theatrically. DuVernay's aunt Denise Sexton was the inspiration for the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Filmmaker-MoN-2012_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Filmmaker-MoN-2012-15">[15]</a></sup> The film cost DuVernay $50,000 and was made in 14 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt100512_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt100512-13">[13]</a></sup> Roger Ebert called it "one of the best films I've seen about coming to terms with the death of a loved one."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert-IWF-2012_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-IWF-2012-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChicagoSunTimes-Ebert-IWF-2011_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChicagoSunTimes-Ebert-IWF-2011-17">[17]</a></sup> <i>I Will Follow</i> was an official selection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AFI_Fest" title="AFI Fest">AFI Fest</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan-African_Film_Festival" title="Pan-African Film Festival">Pan-African Film Festival</a>, Urbanworld and <a href="/wiki/Chicago_International_Film_Festival" title="Chicago International Film Festival">Chicago International Film Festival</a>.</p>
<p>In the summer of 2011, DuVernay began production on her second feature film, <i><a href="/wiki/Middle_of_Nowhere_(2012_film)" title="Middle of Nowhere (2012 film)">Middle of Nowhere</a></i>. The film was acquired by AFFRM and <a href="/wiki/Participant_Media" title="Participant Media">Participant Media</a> at the 2012 Sundance Film Festival, where it played in U.S. Dramatic Competition and garnered the Best Director Award for DuVernay, the first African-American woman to ever win the prize. DuVernay also won the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Spirit_John_Cassavetes_Award" title="Independent Spirit John Cassavetes Award">Independent Spirit John Cassavetes Award</a> for her work on the film.</p>
<p>ESPN commissioned DuVernay to produce and direct <i><a href="/wiki/Nine_for_IX" title="Nine for IX">Venus Vs.</a></i>, a documentary on <a href="/wiki/Venus_Williams" title="Venus Williams">Venus Williams</a>' <a href="/wiki/Venus_Williams" title="Venus Williams">fight for equal prize money</a> for their film series <i><a href="/wiki/Nine_for_IX" title="Nine for IX">Nine for IX</a></i>, which aired on July 2, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ESPN-IX-2013_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ESPN-IX-2013-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, DuVernay was in consideration to direct and possibly co-write Marvel Studios' <i>Black Panther</i> film. According to reports it was between her and <i>Creed</i> director, <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Coogler" title="Ryan Coogler">Ryan Coogler</a>, with Coogler ultimately getting the job later in the year.</p>
<p>DuVernay was commissioned by the Smithsonian <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_African_American_History_and_Culture" title="National Museum of African American History and Culture">National Museum of African American History and Culture</a> to create a film for the museum's opening. <i>August 28: A Day In The Life Of A People</i> tells a story through events that happened on the same date over history, August 28th. The 22-minute film includes <a href="/wiki/Lupita_Nyong%27o" title="Lupita Nyong'o">Lupita Nyong'o</a>, <a href="/wiki/Don_Cheadle" title="Don Cheadle">Don Cheadle</a>, Regina King, David Oyelowo, <a href="/wiki/Angela_Bassett" title="Angela Bassett">Angela Bassett</a>, Michael Ealy, Gugu Mbatha-Raw, Andre Holland and Glynn Turnman. It focuses on events surrounding the murder of <a href="/wiki/Emmett_Till" title="Emmett Till">Emmett Till</a>, Dr. Martin Luther King's "I Have A Dream" speech, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> and the night then-senator Barack Obama accepted the Democratic nomination for president at the 2008 DNC. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Selma"><i>Selma</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ava_DuVernay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Selma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>DuVernay directed <i><a href="/wiki/Selma_(film)" title="Selma (film)">Selma</a></i>, a $20 million budget film produced by <a href="/wiki/Plan_B_Entertainment" title="Plan B Entertainment">Plan B Entertainment</a>, about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr." title="Martin Luther King, Jr.">Martin Luther King, Jr.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Selma_to_Montgomery_marches" title="Selma to Montgomery marches">1965 Selma to Montgomery march</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indiewire-Selma-2013_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indiewire-Selma-2013-20">[20]</a></sup> The movie was released on December 25, 2014 to critical acclaim and generally positive reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indiewire-Selma-2014_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indiewire-Selma-2014-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>There was significant <a href="/wiki/Selma_(film)#Historical_accuracy" title="Selma (film)">controversy</a> about <i>Selma</i> and its depiction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_Johnson" title="Lyndon Johnson">Lyndon Johnson</a>'s actions as portrayed in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheWrap-Sema-2015_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-TheWrap-Sema-2015-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes-Selma-LBJ-2014_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-Selma-LBJ-2014-23">[23]</a></sup> Former Johnson domestic policy aide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_A._Califano,_Jr." title="Joseph A. Califano, Jr.">Joseph A. Califano, Jr.</a> criticized DuVernay for ignoring and falsifying history, and particularly for suggesting that Johnson reluctantly supported King's efforts and that he sent the FBI to investigate King.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPost-Califano-2014_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-WashPost-Califano-2014-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>For the film she did uncredited re-writes of most of the original screenwriter Paul Webb's script with an increased emphasis on King and the people of Selma as central figures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPost-Selma-2014_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-WashPost-Selma-2014-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorker-Selma-2014_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorker-Selma-2014-26">[26]</a></sup> In response to the criticisms of historians and media sources that accused her of irresponsibly rewriting history to portray her own agenda, DuVernay pointed out that the film is "not a documentary. I'm not a historian. I'm a storyteller".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters-Selma-2015_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-Selma-2015-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film was nominated for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Picture" title="Academy Award for Best Picture">Best Picture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Song" title="Academy Award for Best Original Song">Best Original Song</a>, but not <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Best Director</a>, by the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Awards</a>. While the lack of diversity of the Oscar nominations for 2014 was the subject of much press,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grantland-Selma-2015_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grantland-Selma-2015-28">[28]</a></sup> especially on <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DemocracyNow-OscarSnub-2015_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-DemocracyNow-OscarSnub-2015-29">[29]</a></sup> the film of the only person of color that was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/87th_Academy_Awards" title="87th Academy Awards">87th Academy Awards</a>, Mexican director <a href="/wiki/Alejandro_Gonz%C3%A1lez_I%C3%B1%C3%A1rritu" title="Alejandro González Iñárritu">Alejandro G. Iñárritu</a>, ended up taking top honors in three categories at the ceremony – Best Picture, Best Director, and Best Original Screenplay. The award for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Song" title="Academy Award for Best Original Song">Best Original Song</a> went to "<a href="/wiki/Glory_(Common_and_John_Legend_song)" title="Glory (Common and John Legend song)">Glory</a>" from <i>Selma</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>DuVernay stated that she had not expected to be nominated so the omission didn't really bother her; rather she was hurt by actor <a href="/wiki/David_Oyelowo" title="David Oyelowo">David Oyelowo</a> not being nominated. As to the question of racial diversity of awards, she stated that the obstacles to people of color being represented in the Academy Awards were systemic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DemocracyNow-OscarSnub-2015_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-DemocracyNow-OscarSnub-2015-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="13TH"><i>13TH</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ava_DuVernay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 13TH">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In July 2016 the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Film_Festival" title="New York Film Festival">New York Film Festival</a> made the surprise announcement that <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/13TH_(film)" title="13TH (film)">13TH</a></i>, a documentary directed by DuVernay, would open the festival. Until the announcement no mention of the film had been made by either DuVernay or <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a>, the film's distributor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ava_DuVernay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DuVernay directed episode 3.08 of <a href="/wiki/Scandal_(TV_series)" title="Scandal (TV series)"><i>Scandal</i></a> which was called "<a href="/wiki/Vermont_is_For_Lovers,_Too" title="Vermont is For Lovers, Too">Vermont is For Lovers, Too</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indiewire-Scandal-2013_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indiewire-Scandal-2013-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, DuVernay executive produced and directed the <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> civil rights crime drama pilot <i>For Justice</i> starring <a href="/wiki/Anika_Noni_Rose" title="Anika Noni Rose">Anika Noni Rose</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HReporter-ForJustice-2015_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-HReporter-ForJustice-2015-34">[34]</a></sup> It was not picked up for distribution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>That same year DuVernay announced she would be creating and executive producing the upcoming drama series, <i><a href="/wiki/Queen_Sugar" title="Queen Sugar">Queen Sugar</a>,</i> alongside <a href="/wiki/Oprah_Winfrey" title="Oprah Winfrey">Oprah Winfrey</a>. The drama was set to air on <a href="/wiki/Oprah_Winfrey_Network_(U.S._TV_channel)" title="Oprah Winfrey Network (U.S. TV channel)">Oprah Winfrey Network</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deadline-QueenSugar-2015_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deadline-QueenSugar-2015-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HReporter-QueenSugar-Oprah-2015_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-HReporter-QueenSugar-Oprah-2015-36">[36]</a></sup> In 2016, DuVernay announced that actress <a href="/wiki/Rutina_Wesley" title="Rutina Wesley">Rutina Wesley</a> had been cast in the lead role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The series premiered in 2016 to critical acclaim and positive reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_distribution">Film distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ava_DuVernay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Film distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/ARRAY" title="ARRAY">ARRAY</a></div>
<p>In 2010 DuVernay began <a href="/wiki/ARRAY" title="ARRAY">AFFRM</a> (the African-American Film Festival Releasing Movement) her own company to distribute films made by or focusing on black people. DuVernay refers to AFFRM as "not so much a business, but a call to action" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes-AFFRM-2011_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-AFFRM-2011-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although building strong business foundations for films is a priority, DuVernay stresses that the driving force of the organization is activism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015 the company rebranded itself under the name <a href="/wiki/ARRAY" title="ARRAY">ARRAY</a>, promising a new focus on women filmmakers as well.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_projects">Future projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ava_DuVernay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Future projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2013, she announced development on a narrative feature film entitled <i>Part of the Sky</i> set in <a href="/wiki/Compton,_California" title="Compton, California">Compton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Affinity-PotS-2013_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Affinity-PotS-2013-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, it was announced that DuVernay would be writing, producing, and directing her next project, a fictional account which will focus on the "social and environmental" aspects of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> while including a love story and a murder mystery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashPost-Katrina-2015_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-WashPost-Katrina-2015-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Oyelowo" title="David Oyelowo">David Oyelowo</a>, from <i>Selma,</i> will be part of the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LATimes-Katrina-2015_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-LATimes-Katrina-2015-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, it was announced that Disney retained <a href="/wiki/A_Wrinkle_in_Time_(2018_film)" title="A Wrinkle in Time (2018 film)">film rights</a> to adapt the novel <i><a href="/wiki/A_Wrinkle_in_Time" title="A Wrinkle in Time">A Wrinkle in Time</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Following the success of <a href="/wiki/Tim_Burton" title="Tim Burton">Tim Burton</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(2010_film)" title="Alice in Wonderland (2010 film)">Alice in Wonderland</a></i>, Disney announced the hire of Jeff Stockwell to write the screenplay for <a href="/wiki/Cary_Granat" title="Cary Granat">Cary Granat</a> and his new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bedrock_Studios" title="Bedrock Studios">Bedrock Studios</a>. Cary Granat had previously worked with Disney on the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chronicles_of_Narnia_(film_series)" title="The Chronicles of Narnia (film series)">Chronicles of Narnia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Bridge_to_Terabithia_(2007_film)" title="Bridge to Terabithia (2007 film)">Bridge to Terabithia</a></i> films.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hollywood_Reporter_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hollywood_Reporter-45">[45]</a></sup> The project's budget is $35 million, which the company compares to <i><a href="/wiki/District_9" title="District 9">District 9</a></i> and <i>Bridge to Terabithia</i>, both of which had less than $30 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thewrap_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-thewrap-46">[46]</a></sup> On August 5, 2014, <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Lee_(filmmaker)" title="Jennifer Lee (filmmaker)">Jennifer Lee</a> was announced as the screenwriter taking over from Stockwell, who wrote the first draft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> On February 8, 2016, it was reported that DuVernay was offered to direct the film, and she was confirmed to direct later that same month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feb2016THR_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feb2016THR-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/A_Wrinkle_in_Time_(2018_film)" title="A Wrinkle in Time (2018 film)">A Wrinkle in Time</a></i> began filming in November 2016.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_work">Other work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ava_DuVernay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, DuVernay directed several network music documentaries, including <i>My Mic Sounds Nice</i> for BET Networks and the <a href="/wiki/Essence_Music_Festival" title="Essence Music Festival">Essence Music Festival</a> 2010 for TV One.</p>
<p>In 2013, DuVernay partnered with <a href="/wiki/Miu_Miu" title="Miu Miu">Miu Miu</a> as part of their <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Tales" title="Women's Tales">Women's Tales</a> film series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MiuMiu-Door-2014_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-MiuMiu-Door-2014-51">[51]</a></sup> Her short film <i>The Door</i> starred actress <a href="/wiki/Gabrielle_Union" title="Gabrielle Union">Gabrielle Union</a> and reunited DuVernay with her <i><a href="/wiki/Middle_of_Nowhere_(2012_film)" title="Middle of Nowhere (2012 film)">Middle of Nowhere</a></i> star <a href="/wiki/Emayatzy_Corinealdi" title="Emayatzy Corinealdi">Emayatzy Corinealdi</a>. The film was later presented at the <a href="/wiki/70th_Venice_International_Film_Festival" title="70th Venice International Film Festival">70th Venice International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HuffPo-Door-2013_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-HuffPo-Door-2013-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2013, DuVernay released a second short film through <a href="/wiki/Vimeo" title="Vimeo">Vimeo</a> entitled <i>Say Yes</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shine-FH-2013_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shine-FH-2013-53">[53]</a></sup> The film was sponsored by cosmetic brand <a href="/wiki/Johnson_Publishing_Company" title="Johnson Publishing Company">Fashion Fair</a> and starred <a href="/wiki/Kali_Hawk" title="Kali Hawk">Kali Hawk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lance_Gross" title="Lance Gross">Lance Gross</a>. <a href="/wiki/Julie_Dash" title="Julie Dash">Julie Dash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Mahoney" title="Victoria Mahoney">Victoria Mahoney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lorraine_Toussaint" title="Lorraine Toussaint">Lorraine Toussaint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Issa_Rae" title="Issa Rae">Issa Rae</a> appeared as extras in the film.</p>
<p>In September 2013, DuVernay started a podcast series called <i>The Call-In,</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Filmmaker-Podcast-2013_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Filmmaker-Podcast-2013-54">[54]</a></sup> a series of phone conversations recorded by African-American Film Festival Releasing Movement ("AFFRM") of Black filmmakers of feature narrative and documentary work</p>
<p>On March 14, 2015, DuVernay gave a keynote address<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Variety-SXSWKeynote-2015_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Variety-SXSWKeynote-2015-55">[55]</a></sup> at the 2015 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SXSW_Film_Festival" title="SXSW Film Festival">SXSW Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SXSW-FilmKeynote-Schedule-2015_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-SXSW-FilmKeynote-Schedule-2015-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indiewire-SXSWKeynote-2015_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indiewire-SXSWKeynote-2015-57">[57]</a></sup> In the speech, DuVernay shared that she was the seventh choice of people asked to direct <i>Selma</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indiewire-SXSW-7Things-2015_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indiewire-SXSW-7Things-2015-58">[58]</a></sup> and described her experience at the 2015 <a href="/wiki/87th_Academy_Awards" title="87th Academy Awards">Oscars</a>, while being an honor to be able to attend, it was just "a room in L.A."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HReporter-SXSW-Oscars-2015_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-HReporter-SXSW-Oscars-2015-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_and_themes">Style and themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ava_DuVernay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Style and themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Like others African American filmmakers, including pioneers of past generations, DuVernay subscribes to the ethos that art serves a social purpose, debunks demeaning and normative assumptions about black people, and renders black humanity in all manner of genres and complexity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">[40]</a></sup> Like many black art movements before her, she continues to advocate for black empowerment and representation in the media.</p>
<p>Two core themes embody her line of work. First, is sustained interrogation that engages with black women’s agency and subjectivity. Second, she refers to family as "site and source of resilience, memory, cultural transmission, generational continuity and dissonance, and as purveyor of all things affirming of black identity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some say DuVernay is part of the vanguard of a new generation of African American filmmakers who are gaining new levels of exposure and prominence. This is exemplified in her socially conscious work, but more importantly affirmed by DuVernay’s mission and “call to action“ which constitutes a strategy “to further and foster the black cinematic image in an organized and consistent way, and to not have to defer and ask permission to traffic our films: to be self-determining.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>One step towards this strategy has been her founding of the collective <a href="/wiki/ARRAY" title="ARRAY">AFFRM</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards.2C_nominations.2C_honors">Awards, nominations, honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ava_DuVernay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Awards, nominations, honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>In June 2013, she was invited to both the director's and writer's branches of <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Motion_Picture_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences">AMPAS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Variety-Academy-2013_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Variety-Academy-2013-60">[60]</a></sup> DuVernay was only the second black woman, following <a href="/wiki/Kasi_Lemmons" title="Kasi Lemmons">Kasi Lemmons</a>, to be invited to the director's branch.</li>
<li>Duvernay became the inaugural recipient of the Tribeca Film Institute's Heineken Affinity Award, receiving a $20,000 prize and industry support for future projects. DuVernay donated all the money to the black arthouse film collective she founded known as <a class="external text" href="http://www.affrm.com/" rel="nofollow">AFFRM</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indiewire-AffinityAward-2013_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indiewire-AffinityAward-2013-61">[61]</a></sup></li>
<li>In June 2015, Duvernay will be honored as part of <a href="/wiki/Women_in_Film_Crystal_%2B_Lucy_Awards" title="Women in Film Crystal + Lucy Awards">Women in Film Crystal + Lucy Awards</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Arzner" title="Dorothy Arzner">Dorothy Arzner</a> Directors Award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WiF-CrystalLucyAwards-2015_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-WiF-CrystalLucyAwards-2015-62">[62]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indiewire-WiFAwards-2015_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indiewire-WiFAwards-2015-63">[63]</a></sup></li>
<li>In April 2015 DuVernay was chosen as one of <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a>'s "Sheros" of 2015. As such a custom-made one-of-a-kind <a href="/wiki/Barbie" title="Barbie">Barbie</a> in DuVernay's likeness was produced. The doll was auctioned off with the proceeds given to charity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Due to high demand, a collectible version of the doll was produced and sold in December of that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2017, DuVernay became the first black woman nominated for an Oscar for Best Documentary Feature, which she was nominated for for her film <i><a href="/wiki/13th_(film)" title="13th (film)">13th</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
