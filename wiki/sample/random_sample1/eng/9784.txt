<p><b>Avery Brundage</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/eɪ/ long 'a' in 'base'">eɪ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ʌ/ short 'u' in 'bud'">ʌ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="/dʒ/ 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span></span>/</a></span></span>; September 28, 1887 – May 8, 1975) was the fifth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presidents_of_the_International_Olympic_Committee" title="Presidents of the International Olympic Committee">president</a> of the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> (IOC), serving from 1952 to 1972. The only American to attain that position, Brundage is remembered as a zealous advocate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amateurism" title="Amateurism">amateurism</a> and for his involvement with the 1936 and <a href="/wiki/1972_Summer_Olympics" title="1972 Summer Olympics">1972 Summer Olympics</a>, both held in Germany.</p>
<p>Brundage was born in <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a> in 1887 to a working class family; when he was five years old, his father moved his family to Chicago and subsequently abandoned his wife and children. Raised mostly by relatives, he attended the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois" title="University of Illinois">University of Illinois</a> to study engineering and became a track star. In 1912, he competed in <a href="/wiki/1912_Summer_Olympics" title="1912 Summer Olympics">the Summer Olympics</a>, contesting the <a href="/wiki/Pentathlon" title="Pentathlon">pentathlon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Decathlon" title="Decathlon">decathlon</a>, but did not win any medals; both events were won by <a href="/wiki/Jim_Thorpe" title="Jim Thorpe">Jim Thorpe</a>. He won national championships in track three times between 1914 and 1918, and founded his own construction business. He earned his wealth from this company and from investments, and never accepted pay for his sports involvement.</p>
<p>Following his retirement from athletics, Brundage became a sports administrator, rising rapidly through the ranks in United States sports groups. As leader of America's Olympic organizations, he fought zealously against a boycott of the <a href="/wiki/1936_Summer_Olympics" title="1936 Summer Olympics">1936 Summer Olympics</a>, which had been awarded to Germany before the rise of its <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi government</a> and its subsequent, escalating persecution of Jews. Although Brundage was successful in getting a team to the Games in Berlin, its participation was controversial and has remained so. Brundage was elected to the IOC that year, and quickly became a major figure in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympic_movement" title="Olympic movement">Olympic movement</a>. He was elected IOC president in 1952.</p>
<p>As president, Brundage fought strongly for amateurism and against commercialization of the Olympic Games, even as these stands came to be seen as incongruous with the realities of modern sports. His final Olympics as president, <a href="/wiki/1972_Summer_Olympics" title="1972 Summer Olympics">at Munich in 1972</a>, was marked by controversy: at the memorial service following <a href="/wiki/Munich_massacre" title="Munich massacre">the murder of 11 Israeli athletes by terrorists</a>, Brundage decried the politicization of sports and, refusing to cancel the remainder of the Olympics, declared "the Games must go on". Although Brundage's statement was applauded by those in attendance, his decision to continue the Games has since been harshly criticized, and his actions in 1936 and 1972 seen as evidence of <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">anti-Semitism</a>. In retirement, Brundage married a German princess; he died in 1975.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_athletic_career">Early life and athletic career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and athletic career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Avery Brundage was born in Detroit, Michigan, on September 28, 1887, the son of Charles and Minnie (Lloyd) Brundage. Charles Brundage was a stonecutter; the Brundages moved to Chicago when Avery was five, and Charles Brundage soon thereafter abandoned his family. Avery and his younger brother, Chester, were mostly raised by aunts and uncles. At age 13, in 1901, Brundage finished first in an essay competition, winning a trip to President <a href="/wiki/William_McKinley" title="William McKinley">William McKinley</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Second_inauguration_of_William_McKinley" title="Second inauguration of William McKinley">second inauguration</a>. Avery attended Sherwood Public School and then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crane_High_School_(Chicago,_Illinois)" title="Crane High School (Chicago, Illinois)">R. T. Crane Manual Training School</a>, both in Chicago. Crane Tech was a journey of 7 miles (11 km) by public transportation, which he undertook only after completing a newspaper delivery route. Even though the school had no athletic facilities, Brundage made his own equipment (including a shot and a hammer to throw) in the school's workshop and by his final year was written of in the newspapers as a schoolboy track star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann1.E2.80.934_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann1.E2.80.934-1">[1]</a></sup> According to sportswriter William Oscar Johnson in a 1980 article in <i><a href="/wiki/Sports_Illustrated" title="Sports Illustrated">Sports Illustrated</a></i>, Brundage was "the kind of man whom <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Alger" title="Horatio Alger">Horatio Alger</a> had canonized—the American urchin, tattered and deprived, who rose to thrive in the company of kings and millionaires".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sports_Illustrated.27.27_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sports_Illustrated.27.27-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After graduation from Crane Tech in 1905, Brundage enrolled at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois" title="University of Illinois">University of Illinois</a>, where he undertook an arduous schedule of <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a> courses. He received an honors degree in 1909.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann5.E2.80.936_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann5.E2.80.936-3">[3]</a></sup> He wrote for various campus publications and continued his involvement in sports. Brundage played basketball and ran track for Illinois, and also participated in several <a href="/wiki/Intramural_sports" title="Intramural sports">intramural sports</a>. In his senior year, he was a major contributor to Illinois' <a href="/wiki/Big_Ten_Conference" title="Big Ten Conference">Western Conference</a> championship track team, which defeated the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> (coached by <a href="/wiki/Amos_Alonzo_Stagg" title="Amos Alonzo Stagg">Amos Alonzo Stagg</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann6.E2.80.937_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann6.E2.80.937-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_1916.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="660" data-file-width="263" height="427" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Brundage_1916.png/170px-Brundage_1916.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Brundage_1916.png/255px-Brundage_1916.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cd/Brundage_1916.png 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_1916.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Brundage en route to victory in the 1916 all-around championship in Newark, New Jersey</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After graduation, Brundage began work as a construction superintendent for the leading architectural firm of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holabird_%26_Roche" title="Holabird &amp; Roche">Holabird &amp; Roche</a>. In the three years he worked for the firm, he supervised the construction of $7.5 million in buildings—3 percent of the total built in Chicago in that time-frame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22219_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22219-5">[5]</a></sup> He disliked the corruption of the Chicago building trades. Brundage's biographer, Allen Guttmann, points out that the young engineer was in a position to benefit from influence if he had wanted to, as his uncle, <a href="/wiki/Edward_J._Brundage" title="Edward J. Brundage">Edward J. Brundage</a>, was by then Republican leader of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Side,_Chicago" title="North Side, Chicago">Chicago's North Side</a> and would become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attorney_General_of_Illinois" title="Attorney General of Illinois">Attorney General of Illinois</a>. Brundage had been successful in a number of track and field events while at Illinois. In 1910, as a member of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chicago_Athletic_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chicago Athletic Association (page does not exist)">Chicago Athletic Association</a> (CAA), he finished third in the national all-around championships (an American predecessor of the <a href="/wiki/Decathlon" title="Decathlon">decathlon</a>), sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/Amateur_Athletic_Union" title="Amateur Athletic Union">Amateur Athletic Union</a> (AAU), and continued training, aiming at the <a href="/wiki/1912_Summer_Olympics" title="1912 Summer Olympics">1912 Olympics</a> in Stockholm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann10.E2.80.9311_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann10.E2.80.9311-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>, Brundage finished sixth in the <a href="/wiki/Pentathlon" title="Pentathlon">pentathlon</a> and 16th in the decathlon. Far behind on points, after eight events he dropped out of the decathlon, which he always regretted. He later moved up one spot in the standings in each event when his fellow American, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Thorpe" title="Jim Thorpe">Jim Thorpe</a>, who had won both events, was disqualified after it was shown that he had played baseball for money: this meant Thorpe was considered a professional, not an amateur as was required for Olympic participation. Throughout his tenure as president, Brundage refused to ask the IOC to restore Thorpe's medals despite advocacy by Thorpe supporters. The committee eventually did so in 1982, after the deaths of both men. Brundage's refusal led to charges that he held a grudge for being beaten in Stockholm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann26.E2.80.9327_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann26.E2.80.9327-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterfield118_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterfield118-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss53_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss53-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon his return to Chicago, Brundage accepted a position as construction superintendent for John Griffith and Sons Contractors. Among the structures he worked on for Griffith were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cook_County_Hospital" title="Cook County Hospital">Cook County Hospital</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Morrison_Hotel_(Chicago)" title="Morrison Hotel (Chicago)">Morrison Hotel</a>, the Monroe Building, and the National Biscuit Company warehouse. In 1915, he struck out on his own in construction, founding the Avery Brundage Company, of which his uncle Edward was a director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22219.E2.80.93220_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22219.E2.80.93220-10">[10]</a></sup> Brundage continued his athletic career as well. He was US all-around champion in 1914, 1916, and 1918. Once he had ceased to be a track star, he took up <a href="/wiki/American_handball" title="American handball">handball</a>. As a young man, he was ranked in the top ten in the country and even in 1934, at the age of 46, he won one game out of two against Angelo Trulio, who had recently been the US national champion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann30.E2.80.9331_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann30.E2.80.9331-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports_administrator">Sports administrator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sports administrator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_to_leadership">Rise to leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rise to leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As Brundage approached the end of his track career, he began to involve himself in sports administration, at first through the CAA, then through the Central Association of the Amateur Athletic Union (of which the CAA was a member) and then, beginning in 1919, in the AAU. That group was involved in an ongoing battle for dominance over US amateur sports with the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">National Collegiate Athletic Association</a> (NCAA). Athletes were often used as pawns in the battle, with one organization threatening to suspend those who participated in events sponsored by the other. Another venue for conflict was in the United States' <a href="/wiki/National_Olympic_Committee" title="National Olympic Committee">National Olympic Committee</a> (NOC), which was then called the American Olympic Committee (AOC),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[a]</a></sup> and which was AAU-dominated. In 1920, there was public outcry when the AOC chartered a disused troopship to carry home America's representatives in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1920_Olympic_Games" title="1920 Olympic Games">1920 Olympic Games</a> in Antwerp; much of the team instead booked passage by ocean liner. In response, the AAU founded an American Olympic Association as a separate group, although it was still initially dominated by AAU representatives—it then selected the AOC. In 1928, on the resignation of then-AOA president General <a href="/wiki/Douglas_MacArthur" title="Douglas MacArthur">Douglas MacArthur</a>, Brundage was elected president of the AOA;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann31.E2.80.9333_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann31.E2.80.9333-13">[12]</a></sup> he was also elected president of the AOC, a post he held for over 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterfield118_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterfield118-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1925 Brundage became vice-president of the AAU, and chairman of its Handball Committee. After a year as first vice-president, he became president in 1928 and kept the post (except for a one-year break in 1933) through 1935. In that capacity, he was able to secure peace between the NCAA and AAU, with the former gaining the right to certify college students as amateurs, and greater representation on the AOA's executive board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann35.E2.80.9337_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann35.E2.80.9337-14">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brundage quickly displayed what writer Roger Butterfield in a 1948 article for <a href="/wiki/Life_(magazine)" title="Life (magazine)"><i>Life</i> magazine</a> termed "a dictatorial temperament."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterfield118_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterfield118-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1929, American track star <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charlie_Paddock" title="Charlie Paddock">Charlie Paddock</a> stated that Brundage and other sports officials were making money for the AOC by using him as a gate attraction, while treating him badly; Brundage shot back accusing Paddock of "untruths" and "sensationalism of the rankest sort".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterfield118_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterfield118-8">[8]</a></sup> The runner turned professional, escaping Brundage's jurisdiction. In 1932, soon after winning three medals at the <a href="/wiki/1932_Summer_Olympics" title="1932 Summer Olympics">1932 Summer Olympics</a> in Los Angeles, track star <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babe_Didrikson" title="Babe Didrikson">Mildred "Babe" Didrikson</a> appeared in an automobile advertisement, and the Brundage-led AAU quickly suspended her amateur status. Didrikson objected that she had not been paid and that regardless, the rules for maintaining amateur status were overcomplex. In the first of several well-publicized run-ins he had with female athletes, Brundage replied that he had not had any problem with the rules when an Olympic athlete himself, and stated, "You know, the ancient Greeks kept women out of their athletic games. They wouldn't even let them on the sidelines. I'm not so sure but they were right."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterfield118.2C_120_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterfield118.2C_120-15">[14]</a></sup> According to Butterfield, Brundage was suspicious of female athletes, suspecting that some were actually men in disguise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterfield120_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterfield120-16">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1936_Olympics">1936 Olympics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1936 Olympics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1936_Summer_Olympics" title="1936 Summer Olympics">1936 Summer Olympics</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fighting_a_boycott">Fighting a boycott</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fighting a boycott">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_on_Bremen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="545" data-file-width="800" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Brundage_on_Bremen.jpg/220px-Brundage_on_Bremen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Brundage_on_Bremen.jpg/330px-Brundage_on_Bremen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Brundage_on_Bremen.jpg/440px-Brundage_on_Bremen.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_on_Bremen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Brundage (left) and other Olympic officials on board and with the captain of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS_Bremen_(1929)" title="SS Bremen (1929)">SS <i>Bremen</i></a>, en route to the <a href="/wiki/1936_Winter_Olympics" title="1936 Winter Olympics">1936 Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Garmisch-Partenkirchen" title="Garmisch-Partenkirchen">Garmisch-Partenkirchen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1931, the IOC had awarded the 1936 Olympics to Germany, with the Summer Games to be held in the capital, Berlin. Several IOC members indicated that by giving Germany the Olympics, they were showing support for <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">its democratic government</a>, under attack from extremists in the hard economic times of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Depression" title="The Depression">the Depression</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[b]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge51.E2.80.9352_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge51.E2.80.9352-18">[16]</a></sup> The Berlin Games were thrown in doubt, however, by the <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_July_1932" title="German federal election, July 1932">July 1932 elections</a> for the national legislature, the <i><a href="/wiki/Reichstag_(Weimar_Republic)" title="Reichstag (Weimar Republic)">Reichstag</a></i>. In an unexpected result, the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>, became the largest in the <i>Reichstag</i>. The Nazis had expressed little interest in international sport, preferring the idea of German games to show the Germans being a "<a href="/wiki/Master_race" title="Master race">master race</a>" at which they could promote Aryan racial superiority in competing against subhuman "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Untermenschen" title="Untermenschen">Untermenschen</a>", such as people of Jewish, Gypsy or African descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-games1936_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-games1936-19">[17]</a></sup> When the Nazis <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machtergreifung" title="Machtergreifung">attained power</a> in January 1933, the Olympics were thought likely to be moved elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge57.E2.80.9358_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge57.E2.80.9358-20">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the Nazis were suspicious of the chairman of the local Olympic organizing committee, <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_Lewald" title="de:Theodor Lewald">Theodor Lewald</a>, as he had a Jewish grandmother, they quickly saw the propaganda potential in hosting the Olympic Games. Lewald had intended to stage the Games on a shoestring budget; instead, the resources of the <i>Reich</i> were thrown behind the effort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHilton13.E2.80.9314_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHilton13.E2.80.9314-21">[19]</a></sup> As the Nazi hatred of the <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> manifested itself in persecution, there were calls to move the Olympics from Germany, or alternatively, to boycott the Games. As head of the Olympic movement in the US, Brundage received many letters and telegrams urging action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarvin83.E2.80.9385_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarvin83.E2.80.9385-22">[20]</a></sup> In 1933 and 1934, the IOC worked to assure that, consistent with Olympic ideals, the Games would be open to all, and that there would be no discrimination because of race or religion. IOC President Comte <a href="/wiki/Henri_de_Baillet-Latour" title="Henri de Baillet-Latour">Henri de Baillet-Latour</a> wrote to Brundage in 1933, "I am not personally fond of jews  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] and of the jewish  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] influence, but I will not have them molested in no way  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] whatsoever."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarvin85_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarvin85-23">[21]</a></sup> According to historical writer Christopher Hilton in his account of the 1936 Games, "Baillet-Latour, and the great and good around him, had no idea what was coming, and if the [IOC's] German delegates kept offering assurances, what else could they do but accept them?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHilton17_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHilton17-24">[22]</a></sup> Baillet-Latour opposed boycotting the Games, as did Brundage (who had learned in 1933 that he was being considered for IOC membership).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarvin85.E2.80.9386_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarvin85.E2.80.9386-25">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In her 1982 journal article on his role in the US participation in the 1936 Summer Games, Carolyn Marvin explained Brundage's political outlook:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The foundation of Brundage's political world view was the proposition that Communism was an evil before which all other evils were insignificant. A collection of lesser themes basked in the reflected glory of the major one. These included Brundage's admiration for Hitler's apparent restoration of prosperity and order to Germany, his conception that those who did not work for a living in the United States were an anarchic human tide, and a suspicious anti-Semitism which feared the dissolution of Anglo-Protestant culture in a sea of ethnic aspirations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarvin99_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarvin99-26">[24]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Nazi pledges of nondiscrimination in sports proved inconsistent with their actions, such as the expulsion of Jews from sports clubs, and in September 1934, Brundage sailed for Germany to see for himself. He met with government officials and others, although he was not allowed to meet with Jewish sports leaders alone. When he returned, he reported, "I was given positive assurance in writing ... that there will be no discrimination against Jews. You can't ask more than that and I think the guarantee will be fulfilled."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann69.E2.80.9370_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann69.E2.80.9370-27">[25]</a></sup> Brundage's trip only increased the controversy over the question of US participation, with New York Congressman <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Celler" title="Emanuel Celler">Emanuel Celler</a> stating that Brundage "had prejudged the situation before he sailed from America".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann70_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann70-28">[26]</a></sup> Nevertheless, on September 26, 1934, after hearing a report from Brundage on conditions in Germany, the AOC voted to send a US team to Berlin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarvin87_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarvin87-29">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brundage took the position that as the Germans had reported nondiscrimination to the IOC, and the IOC had accepted that report, US Olympic authorities were bound by that determination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarvin88_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarvin88-30">[28]</a></sup> Nevertheless, it became increasingly apparent that with the Nazi actions, no Jew was likely to secure a place on the German team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHilton38_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHilton38-31">[29]</a></sup> Brundage stated on this issue that as only 12 Jews had ever represented Germany in the Olympics, it would hardly be surprising if none did in 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[c]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarvin89_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarvin89-33">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Foiled at the AOC, boycott advocates turned to the AAU, hoping that the athletics organization, though also led by Brundage, would refuse to certify athletes for Berlin. Although no vote took place on a boycott at the AAU's December 1934 meeting, Brundage did not seek re-election, and delegates elected Judge <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jeremiah_T._Mahoney&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jeremiah T. Mahoney (page does not exist)">Jeremiah T. Mahoney</a> as the new president, to take office in 1935. Although pro-boycott activities briefly fell into a lull, renewed Nazi brutality against the Jews in June 1935 sparked a resurgence, and converted Mahoney to the pro-boycott cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge80.E2.80.9381.2C_93_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge80.E2.80.9381.2C_93-34">[31]</a></sup> In October, Baillet-Latour wrote to the three American IOC members: <a href="/wiki/William_May_Garland" title="William May Garland">William May Garland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_H._Sherrill_(ambassador)" title="Charles H. Sherrill (ambassador)">Charles Sherrill</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ernest_Lee_Jahncke" title="Ernest Lee Jahncke">Ernest Lee Jahncke</a>, asking them to do all they could to ensure a US team was sent to Germany. Garland and Sherrill agreed; Jahncke, however, refused, stating that he would be supporting the boycott.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge90.E2.80.9392_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge90.E2.80.9392-35">[32]</a></sup> Brundage, at Baillet-Latour's request, took the lead in the anti-boycott campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge93_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge93-36">[33]</a></sup> Matters came to a head at the AAU convention in December 1935. Brundage's forces won the key votes, and the AAU approved sending a team to Berlin, specifying that this did not mean it supported the Nazis. In victory, Brundage was not magnanimous, demanding the resignation of opponents. Although not all quit, Mahoney did.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge98.E2.80.9399_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge98.E2.80.9399-37">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brundage believed that the boycott controversy could be used effectively for fundraising, writing, "the fact that the Jews are against us will arouse interest among thousands of people who have never subscribed before, if they are properly approached."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarvin90_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarvin90-38">[35]</a></sup> In March 1936, he wrote to advertising mogul <a href="/wiki/Albert_Lasker" title="Albert Lasker">Albert Lasker</a>, a Jew, complaining that "a large number of misguided Jews still persist in attempting to hamper the activities of the American Olympic Committee. The result, of course, is increased support from the one hundred and twenty million non-Jews in the United States, for this is a patriotic enterprise."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarvin90_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarvin90-38">[35]</a></sup> In a letter which David Large, in his book on the 1936 Games, terms "heavy-handed", Brundage suggested that by helping to finance American participation in the Olympic Games, Jews could decrease anti-Semitism in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge100_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge100-39">[36]</a></sup> However, "Lasker, to his credit, refused to be blackmailed,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge100_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge100-39">[36]</a></sup> writing to Brundage that "You gratuitously insult not only Jews but the millions of patriotic Christians in America, for whom you venture to speak without warrant, and whom you so tragically misrepresent in your letter."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarvin90_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarvin90-38">[35]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Berlin">Berlin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Berlin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_at_Berlin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="595" data-file-width="420" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Brundage_at_Berlin.jpg/220px-Brundage_at_Berlin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Brundage_at_Berlin.jpg/330px-Brundage_at_Berlin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/59/Brundage_at_Berlin.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_at_Berlin.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julius_Lippert_(politician)" title="Julius Lippert (politician)">Julius Lippert</a>, Avery Brundage and <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Lewald" title="Theodor Lewald">Theodor Lewald</a>, organizer of the 1936 Olympics in Berlin</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The American contingent of athletes and officials, led by Brundage, embarked on the <i>S.S. Manhattan</i> at <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a> for <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> on July 15, 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHilton77.E2.80.9378_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHilton77.E2.80.9378-40">[37]</a></sup> Immediately upon arrival in Germany, Brundage became headline news when he and the AOC dismissed swimmer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eleanor_Holm_Jarrett" title="Eleanor Holm Jarrett">Eleanor Holm Jarrett</a>, a gold medalist in 1932 and expected to repeat, for activities on shipboard. Rumors and accounts of the married swimmer's pursuits on board vary, but include statements that she was at an "all-night party" with playwright <a href="/wiki/Charles_MacArthur" title="Charles MacArthur">Charles MacArthur</a>, who was traveling without his wife, actress <a href="/wiki/Helen_Hayes" title="Helen Hayes">Helen Hayes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge180_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge180-41">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHilton105_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHilton105-42">[39]</a></sup> Brundage discussed the matter with fellow AOC members, then met with Jarrett.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge180_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge180-41">[38]</a></sup> Although the AOC attempted to send her home, Jarrett, after pleading in vain for reinstatement, remained in Berlin as a journalist, "to the AOC's horror".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge180_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge180-41">[38]</a></sup> In later years, she claimed that Brundage had kicked her off the team because he had propositioned her, and she had turned him down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss415_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss415-43">[40]</a></sup> According to Guttmann, "Brundage has appeared, ever since [1936], in the guise of a killjoy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann77_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann77-44">[41]</a></sup> Butterfield noted that through the efforts of sportswriters who supported Jarrett, "Brundage became celebrated as a tyrant, snob, hypocrite, dictator and stuffed shirt, as well as just about the meanest man in the whole world of sports".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterfield115_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterfield115-45">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 30, 1936, six days after the American arrival in Germany, the IOC met in Berlin and unanimously expelled Jahncke. Two places for the United States were vacant, as Sherrill had died in June, but the minutes specifically note that Brundage was elected to the IOC in Jahncke's place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann81_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann81-46">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHilton104.E2.80.93105_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHilton104.E2.80.93105-47">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>One of the sensations of the Games was black American track star <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Owens" title="Jesse Owens">Jesse Owens</a>, who won four gold medals. According to some American press stories, Hitler left the stadium rather than shake hands with him. This was not the case; Hitler had been told by IOC president Baillet-Latour not to shake hands with the winners unless he was prepared to shake hands with all gold medalists, which he was not; this, however was not publicized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge230.E2.80.93231_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge230.E2.80.93231-48">[45]</a></sup> According to Butterfield, in later years retellings of what Brundage termed "a fairy tale" roused the American to "acute fury".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterfield124_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterfield124-49">[46]</a></sup> Hitler was, however, asked by his youth leader, <a href="/wiki/Baldur_von_Schirach" title="Baldur von Schirach">Baldur von Schirach</a>, to meet Owens, and he refused, "Do you really think that I'd allow myself to be photographed shaking hands with a Negro?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge233_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge233-50">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another controversy which may have involved Brundage was the question of the US <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4_%C3%97_100_meters_relay" title="4 × 100 meters relay">4 × 100 meters relay</a> squad. The expected team included <a href="/wiki/Sam_Stoller" title="Sam Stoller">Sam Stoller</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marty_Glickman" title="Marty Glickman">Marty Glickman</a>, both Jewish. After Owens won his third gold medal, both men were removed from the relay squad in favor of Owens and fellow black athlete <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Metcalfe" title="Ralph Metcalfe">Ralph Metcalfe</a>. The US track coach, <a href="/wiki/Lawson_Robertson" title="Lawson Robertson">Lawson Robertson</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>, told Stoller and Glickman that the Germans had upgraded their squad and it was important to have the fastest team possible. In the event, the US team turned in back-to-back world record times in the heats and final to take the gold medal; the Italians were a distant second, edging out the Germans for the silver medal. Neither Stoller nor Glickman (who were the only Jews on the US track team, and the only US athletes in any sport to be on the team in Berlin, and not compete) believed the stated reason for their replacement; Stoller recorded in his diary that he and Glickman had been left out of the relay because the two other participants, <a href="/wiki/Foy_Draper" title="Foy Draper">Foy Draper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Wykoff" title="Frank Wykoff">Frank Wykoff</a>, had been coached by one of Robertson's assistants at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge240.E2.80.93243_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge240.E2.80.93243-51">[48]</a></sup> Glickman conceded college favoritism as a possible reason, but thought anti-Semitism more likely, and hardened his position in the years to come: that he and Stoller had been replaced so as not to embarrass Hitler by having him see Jews, as well as blacks, win gold medals for the US track team. He believed Brundage was behind the replacement. Brundage denied any involvement in the decision, which remains controversial. Glickman went on to a lengthy career as a sports broadcaster, and was given the inaugural Douglas MacArthur Award (for lifetime achievement in the field of sports) in 1998, after Stoller's death, by the United States Olympic Committee (successor to the AOC). USOC chairman William Hybl stated that while he had seen no written proof that Brundage was responsible, "I was a prosecutor. I'm used to looking at evidence. The evidence was there"—though, as Large notes, "exactly <i>what</i> evidence, he didn't say."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge243_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge243-52">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_New_York_Times.27.27_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_New_York_Times.27.27-53">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEISAF_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEISAF-54">[51]</a></sup> In his report submitted after the Games, Brundage called the controversy "absurd": he noted that Glickman and Stoller had finished fifth and sixth at the Olympic trials at New York's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Randall%27s_Island" title="Randall's Island">Randall's Island</a> stadium and that the US victory had validated the decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHilton234.E2.80.93235_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHilton234.E2.80.93235-55">[52]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road_to_the_IOC_presidency">Road to the IOC presidency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Road to the IOC presidency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_1948.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2955" data-file-width="2842" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Brundage_1948.jpg/170px-Brundage_1948.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Brundage_1948.jpg/255px-Brundage_1948.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Brundage_1948.jpg/340px-Brundage_1948.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_1948.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Brundage addresses the media at the London Olympics, 1948</div>
</div>

<p>Brundage's first IOC session as an incumbent member was at Warsaw in June 1937. The vice president of the IOC, Baron <a href="/wiki/Godefroy_de_Blonay" title="Godefroy de Blonay">Godefroy de Blonay</a> of Switzerland, had died, and Sweden's <a href="/wiki/Sigfrid_Edstr%C3%B6m" title="Sigfrid Edström">Sigfrid Edström</a> was elected to replace him. Brundage was selected to fill Edström's place on the executive board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann83_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann83-56">[53]</a></sup> Edström had been a Brundage ally in the boycott fight, writing to the American that while he did not desire the persecution of the Jews, as an "intelligent and unscrupulous" people, "they had to be kept within certain limits".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHilton38.E2.80.9339_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHilton38.E2.80.9339-57">[54]</a></sup> Brundage wrote to a German correspondent regretting that <a href="/wiki/Leni_Riefenstahl" title="Leni Riefenstahl">Leni Riefenstahl</a>'s film about the Berlin Olympics, <i><a href="/wiki/Olympia_(1938_film)" title="Olympia (1938 film)">Olympia</a></i>, could not be commercially shown in the United States, as "unfortunately the theaters and moving picture companies are almost all owned by Jews".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann91_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann91-58">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Berlin Games had increased Brundage's admiration for Germany, and he spoke out at a speech before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German-American_Bund" title="German-American Bund">German-American Bund</a> at <a href="/wiki/Madison_Square_Garden_(1925)" title="Madison Square Garden (1925)">Madison Square Garden</a> in October 1936, stating that "five years ago they [Germans] were discouraged and demoralized—today they are united—sixty million people believing in themselves and in their country ..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarvin98_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarvin98-59">[56]</a></sup> In 1938, his construction company received the contract to build a new German embassy in Washington (this was not fulfilled as <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> intervened).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELarge99_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELarge99-60">[57]</a></sup> Brundage joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keep_America_Out_of_War_Committee" title="Keep America Out of War Committee">Keep America Out of War Committee</a> and became a member of <a href="/wiki/America_First_Committee" title="America First Committee">America First</a> (he resigned from both the day after <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">Pearl Harbor</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterfield124_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterfield124-49">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the 1940 Olympic Games could not be held due to war, Brundage sought to organize Western Hemisphere games that might be able to proceed despite the troubled international climate. Brundage was one of the leaders in the founding of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan-American_Games" title="Pan-American Games">Pan-American Games</a>, participating in the initial discussions in August 1940 in <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>. On his return, he arranged for the American Olympic Association to be renamed the United States of America Sports Federation (USASF), which would organize the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Olympic_Committee" title="United States Olympic Committee">United States Olympic Committee</a> (as the AOC would now be called) and another committee to see to American participation in the Pan-American Games. Brundage became an early member of the international Pan-American Games Commission, although the inaugural event in <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> was postponed because of the war and was eventually held in 1951, with Brundage present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann87.E2.80.9389_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann87.E2.80.9389-61">[58]</a></sup> Despite his role in founding them, Brundage viewed the Pan-American Games as imitative, with no true link with antiquity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller139_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller139-62">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>War postponed any future Olympics, and fractured the IOC geographically and politically. With Baillet-Latour in German-occupied Belgium, Brundage and IOC vice president Edström did their best to keep channels of communication open between IOC members; according to Guttmann, "He and Edström perceived themselves as keepers of the sacred flame, guardians of an ideal in whose name they were ready once again to act as soon as the madness ended."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann97_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann97-63">[60]</a></sup> Baillet-Latour died in 1942; Edström took on the duties of president, although he continued to style himself vice-president. Edström and Brundage did not await the end of war to rebuild the Olympic movement; Brundage even sent parcels to Europe in aid of IOC members and others in places where food was scarce. With Edström turning 74 in 1944, the Swede expressed concern as to who would lead the IOC if he should die, and suggested that Brundage become second vice-president, a newly created position. A mail ballot of IOC members who could be reached confirmed the choice the following year. When Edström was made president by the first postwar IOC session at Lausanne in September 1946, Brundage was elected first vice-president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann99.E2.80.93100_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann99.E2.80.93100-64">[61]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_St_Moritz.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="646" data-file-width="463" height="237" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Brundage_St_Moritz.png/170px-Brundage_St_Moritz.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Brundage_St_Moritz.png/255px-Brundage_St_Moritz.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Brundage_St_Moritz.png/340px-Brundage_St_Moritz.png 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_St_Moritz.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>As vice president, Brundage served on a commission appointed at the IOC's London session in 1948 to recommend whether the <a href="/wiki/1906_Intercalated_Games" title="1906 Intercalated Games">1906 Intercalated Games</a>, held in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>, should be considered a full Olympic Games. All three members of what came to be known as the Brundage Commission were from the Western Hemisphere and met in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a> in January 1949. The commission found that there was nothing to be gained by recognizing the 1906 games as Olympic, and it might set an embarrassing precedent. The full IOC endorsed the report when it met later that year in Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELennartz10_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELennartz10-65">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>Edström intended to retire following the <a href="/wiki/1952_Summer_Olympics" title="1952 Summer Olympics">1952 Summer Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, when a successor would be elected. Brundage's rival for the presidency was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">Great Britain's</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Cecil,_6th_Marquess_of_Exeter" title="David Cecil, 6th Marquess of Exeter">Lord Burghley</a>, an Olympic gold medalist in track in 1928 and president of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Amateur_Athletic_Federation" title="International Amateur Athletic Federation">International Amateur Athletic Federation</a> (IAAF). The balloting took place at the IOC session in the Finnish capital before the Games. Although Brundage was the executive board's candidate, he was disliked by some IOC members; others felt that the president should be a European. Private notes kept during the balloting reveal it to have been very close, but on the 25th and final ballot, Brundage received 30 votes to 17 for Burghley and was elected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann111.E2.80.93114_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann111.E2.80.93114-66">[63]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="IOC_president_.281952.E2.80.931972.29">IOC president (1952–1972)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: IOC president (1952–1972)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amateurism">Amateurism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Amateurism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout his career as a sports official, according to Guttmann, Brundage "was unquestionably an idealist."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann115_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann115-67">[64]</a></sup> He often concluded speeches by quoting from <a href="/wiki/John_Galsworthy" title="John Galsworthy">John Galsworthy</a>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Sport, which still keeps the flag of idealism flying, is perhaps the most saving grace in the world at the moment, with its spirit for rules kept, and regard for the adversary, whether the fight is going for or against. When, if ever, the spirit of sport, which is the spirit of fair play, reigns over international affairs, the cat force, which rules there now, will slink away, and human life emerge for the first time from the jungle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann115.E2.80.93116_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann115.E2.80.93116-68">[65]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This ideal was best realized, Brundage believed, in <a href="/wiki/Amateur_sports" title="Amateur sports">amateur sports</a>: the athlete, he stated, should compete "for the love of the game itself without thought of reward or payment of any kind", with professionals being part of the entertainment business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann116_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann116-69">[66]</a></sup> Amateurism, to Brundage, expressed the concept of the <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">Renaissance man</a>, with abilities in many fields, yet a specialist in none.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann117_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann117-70">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>As the definition of "amateur" varied by sport, many of the battles he engaged in concerned the question of what money or valuables could an athlete accept, while retaining his amateur status, with some sports more liberal than others—in 1948, tennis allowed expense payments of up to $600 per tournament, while boxing permitted valuable prizes as awards. Enforcement of these rules often fell to National Olympic Committees, and Brundage found them less than enthusiastic about rules which hampered their own athletes in the pursuit of medals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterfield123_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterfield123-71">[68]</a></sup></p>

<p>Both before and after becoming IOC president, Brundage was involved in a number of controversies involving threats to, or sometimes actual disqualification of, athletes for breach of amateur rules. In 1932, he was part of a special committee of the IAAF which disqualified Finnish runner <a href="/wiki/Paavo_Nurmi" title="Paavo Nurmi">Paavo Nurmi</a> from the Los Angeles Games for allegedly accepting monetary compensation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann60_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann60-72">[69]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/1948_Winter_Olympics" title="1948 Winter Olympics">1948 Winter Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/St._Moritz" title="St. Moritz">St. Moritz</a>, rival US ice hockey teams, sponsored by different accrediting organizations, came to the Games. The dispute proved difficult, and the IOC initially voted to cancel the tournament and eliminate ice hockey as an Olympic sport, but relented as thousands of tickets had been sold by the organizers. The team not favored by Brundage, whose organization was run by commercial rink operators, was allowed to compete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterfield116_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterfield116-73">[70]</a></sup> In 1972, Brundage banned Austrian skier <a href="/wiki/Karl_Schranz" title="Karl Schranz">Karl Schranz</a> from the <a href="/wiki/1972_Winter_Olympics" title="1972 Winter Olympics">Sapporo Winter Olympics</a> for commercial activities, calling him "a walking billboard".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sun-Times.27.27.2C_.22Avery.22_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sun-Times.27.27.2C_.22Avery.22-74">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>As IOC president, Brundage's views on amateurism came increasingly to be seen as outdated in the modern world, as the rules were tested by athletes who saw everyone making money but themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss53_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss53-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1962, against Brundage's opposition, the IOC amended the rules to allow sports federations to offer athletes "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broken_time" title="Broken time">broken time</a>" payments, compensating them for time missed from work, but only if they had dependents in need.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller139_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller139-62">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterfield123_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterfield123-71">[68]</a></sup> In 1972, Brundage called for the elimination of the Winter Olympics after 1976, finding them hopelessly polluted by rampant commercialism, especially in <a href="/wiki/Alpine_skiing" title="Alpine skiing">alpine skiing</a>. In his final speech to the IOC in Munich in 1972, Brundage maintained his position on amateurism: "There are only two kinds of competitors. Those free and independent individuals who are interested in sports for sport's sake, and those in sports for financial reasons. Olympic glory is for amateurs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sun-Times.27.27.2C_.22Purist.22_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sun-Times.27.27.2C_.22Purist.22-75">[72]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_participation_controversies">National participation controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: National participation controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>No German team was allowed at the <a href="/wiki/1948_Summer_Olympics" title="1948 Summer Olympics">1948 Summer Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> or the Winter Games in St. Moritz. Brundage was anxious to reintegrate Germany into the Olympic movement once the <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a> (West Germany, through Brundage's lifetime) was formed in 1949. Soon after the state's formation, its National Olympic Committee approached the IOC, seeking recognition, but there was still much animus towards Germany. Just prior to the IOC session in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> in 1951 (Brundage was still vice president), the <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">German Democratic Republic</a> (East Germany) also formed an NOC and requested recognition. This created controversy, as the Federal Republic and its NOC claimed to represent both West and East Germany, but did not control the latter. Despite lengthy discussions, no resolution was reached in 1951, and the matter was put over until February 1952, when a negotiating session was scheduled for <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>. Although the East Germans came to Copenhagen, they refused to attend the session, which was eventually cancelled by Edström after the IOC officials and West Germans waited for hours in vain. The German team which competed in Helsinki that summer was entirely West German.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann150.E2.80.93153_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann150.E2.80.93153-76">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1954, the East Germans resumed their attempts at recognition. The following year, after Brundage received assurances that the East German NOC was not government-run, the IOC voted to recognize it, but required that both East and West Germany compete as part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unified_Team_of_Germany" title="Unified Team of Germany">a single German team</a> in 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann154_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann154-77">[74]</a></sup> East Germany sent only 37 athletes to the <a href="/wiki/1956_Summer_Olympics" title="1956 Summer Olympics">1956 Summer Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, and they lived and trained separately from their West German counterparts. For <a href="/wiki/1960_Summer_Olympics" title="1960 Summer Olympics">the Summer Olympics</a> at <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> in 1960, under continuing IOC insistence that the two states send a single team, East Germany contributed 141 of the 321 athletes; competitors from both states lived in the same area of the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Village" title="Olympic Village">Olympic Village</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss43.E2.80.9344_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss43.E2.80.9344-78">[75]</a></sup> At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympic_opening_ceremony" title="Olympic opening ceremony">Opening Ceremony</a> at Rome, Italian President <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Gronchi" title="Giovanni Gronchi">Giovanni Gronchi</a> marveled, much to Brundage's delight, that the IOC had obtained the <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a> which politicians had been unable to secure; Brundage responded, "But in sport, we do such things."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann155_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann155-79">[76]</a></sup> Brundage saw the German participation as symbolic of the potential for the Olympic Games to overcome divisions to unite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann155_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann155-79">[76]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Wall" title="Berlin Wall">Berlin Wall</a> beginning in 1961, which increased tensions between East and West, Brundage was successful in securing a joint German team for the <a href="/wiki/1964_Summer_Olympics" title="1964 Summer Olympics">1964 Summer Olympics</a> in Tokyo. Nevertheless, the East Germans, supported by IOC members from <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> nations, aspired to have their own team. They made a major breakthrough when the IAAF (led by the Marquess of Exeter, the former Lord Burghley) recognized a separate East German team beginning with the <a href="/wiki/1966_European_Athletics_Championships" title="1966 European Athletics Championships">1966 European Athletics Championships</a>. The East Germans did their best to get Brundage's support, and, at the IOC session at Mexico City in 1968, they were granted full membership, with their own team under their own flag, which they displayed on West German soil four years later at the Opening Ceremony at Munich. Brundage, while finally supporting full membership for East Germany, considered the matter a defeat for Olympic ideals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann156.E2.80.93157_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann156.E2.80.93157-80">[77]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_Union">Soviet Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Soviet Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Czarist_Russia" title="Czarist Russia">Czarist Russia</a> had sent athletes to the Olympic Games, after the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> was formed, it declined to participate, considering the Olympics bourgeois. As early as 1923, the IOC attempted to lure the Soviets back into the fold; Brundage visited the USSR in 1934. He was impressed by the progress which had been made there since a visit he had made in 1912 after competing in Stockholm. Despite his anti-Communism, Brundage wanted the Russians to join the Olympic movement. According to Guttmann, "When Brundage had to choose between his hostility to Communism and his commitment to the ideal of Olympic universality, he chose the latter. He wanted the Russians in the Olympics, Communists or not."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann133.E2.80.93134_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann133.E2.80.93134-81">[78]</a></sup></p>
<p>During World War II, Brundage wrote to other IOC members that he had no objection to Soviet involvement in international sports, with representation on the IOC, if the USSR joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_international_sport_federations" title="List of international sport federations">international sports federations</a> (ISFs). The IOC required that an NOC be independent of the government of the territory which it represents; there were concerns a Soviet NOC would not be. This was a problem not unique to communist states; a number of <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin Americans</a> countries were starting to bring the local NOCs into the political structure, with an official naming the NOC chair—who might even be the country's political leader. This mixture of sports and politics worried Brundage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann134.E2.80.93135_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann134.E2.80.93135-82">[79]</a></sup></p>
<p>Beginning in 1946, the Soviets began to join international federations; in 1951 their NOC was recognized by the IOC, and they began Olympic competition the following year. As few Soviet sports officials were internationally known, the IOC had little alternative than to accept the nominees of the USSR's government if they wished to have Soviet IOC members. The Soviet members were believers in sport, and completely loyal to their nation and to communist ideals. They quickly became the leaders of the IOC members from behind the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Curtain" title="Iron Curtain">Iron Curtain</a>, who voted in accord with the Soviet members. Brundage visited the USSR at Soviet invitation (though at his own expense) in 1954. He deemed the nation's <a href="/wiki/Physical_education" title="Physical education">physical education</a> program as "creating the greatest army of athletes the world has ever seen," warning (as he would often through the 1950s) that Americans were by comparison soft and unfit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann140.E2.80.93141_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann140.E2.80.93141-83">[80]</a></sup> Brundage found his view, often expressed in the press, that physical education and competitive sports made for better citizens, especially in the event of war, more enthusiastically embraced in the Soviet Union than in the United States. According to David Maraniss in his account of the 1960 Rome Games, Brundage's admiration for the Soviet Union's sports programs "in some ways mirrored his response two decades earlier to his encounters with Nazi Germany".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss250.E2.80.93252_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss250.E2.80.93252-84">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>On his return, he related in an article for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Saturday_Evening_Post" title="The Saturday Evening Post">The Saturday Evening Post</a></i> that he had confronted Soviet officials with information from defectors stating that the USSR was running year-round training camps and giving athletes material inducements for success. He also repeated the Soviet response, which questioned the defectors' integrity: "These men are deserters, traitors. Would you attach any truth to their statements had they been Americans and had turned against your country?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss252.E2.80.93253_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss252.E2.80.93253-85">[82]</a></sup> Since Brundage did not comment on the response, there was a storm of controversy in the press, which accused Brundage of being a Soviet dupe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann141.E2.80.93142_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann141.E2.80.93142-86">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>Despite the evident conflicts between amateurism and the Soviet system in which athletes received stipends, jobs, and housing at state expense,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss250.E2.80.93252_84-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss250.E2.80.93252-84">[81]</a></sup> Brundage took no action against the USSR or Warsaw Pact nations with similar systems; when challenged on this point, he argued that Western nations had similar abuses, such as <a href="/wiki/Athletic_scholarship" title="Athletic scholarship">athletic scholarships</a>. The Soviet system remained in place, though Western nations reacted by giving their athletes greater state support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann142_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann142-87">[84]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="China_and_Taiwan">China and Taiwan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: China and Taiwan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China_(1912%E2%80%931949)" title="Republic of China (1912–1949)">Republic of China</a>, which then governed the mainland, had joined the Olympic movement in 1924, when the China National Amateur Athletic Federation was recognized by the IOC as the nation's NOC. China participated in the 1932 Olympics in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, as well as in Berlin four years later and the first post-war Olympics at London in 1948. When the communists were successful in the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War" title="Chinese Civil War">Chinese Civil War</a> and established the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> (PRC) in 1949, most NOC members fled the mainland for the island of <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. This left China with two rival NOCs, one on the mainland and one on Taiwan, each claiming to represent the whole of China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChan473.E2.80.93474_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChan473.E2.80.93474-88">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann143.E2.80.93144_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann143.E2.80.93144-89">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>Matters came to a head in 1952, when the mainland NOC (the All-China Athletic Federation), considering itself a continuation of the pre-1949 committee, wrote to the IOC stating that it desired to participate in the Helsinki Olympics to be held that year. As the Taiwanese also proposed to send a team, this conflicted with IOC rules stating that only one committee could represent a country, and both Chinese groups were unwilling to negotiate with the other, or to send a joint team. After considerable deliberation, the IOC decided that if either committee was recognized by the ISF for a sport, the committee could send athletes to participate in events in that discipline. In protest, Taiwan withdrew from the Games; the PRC sent a team to Helsinki, though it arrived ten days after the start of the Games. Brundage, president-elect when the decision was made to allow PRC athletes to compete, argued against the decision to allow mainland participation before its NOC was recognized, but he was overruled by his colleagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChan473.E2.80.93474_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChan473.E2.80.93474-88">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann143.E2.80.93144_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann143.E2.80.93144-89">[86]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1954, the Brundage-headed IOC, in a narrow vote, recognized both committees, thus allowing both states to participate at Melbourne. Only the PRC's committee initially accepted, but when the Taiwanese NOC changed its mind and decided to send a team to the Games, the mainlanders withdrew in protest. Brundage took the position that despite similar concerns about state sponsorship as with the USSR, once the PRC's committee was recognized and reported to the IOC that all eligibility rules were observed, the international committee had to accept that unless it had evidence to the contrary. He was frustrated by the continuing controversy, considering the squabble a distraction from the goal of advancing the Olympic movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann144.E2.80.93145_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann144.E2.80.93145-90">[87]</a></sup></p>

<p>When continuing efforts to exclude the Taiwanese failed, in 1958 the mainlanders withdrew from the IOC. The following year, the IOC ruled the Taiwanese could not compete under the name Republic of China Olympic Committee, but would have to compete under some other name which did not imply they governed sports in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEspy62.E2.80.9364_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEspy62.E2.80.9364-92">[89]</a></sup> Brundage and Exeter both advocated for the ruling, which they compared to having an Italian NOC represent only <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>. The press interpreted the ruling to mean that Nationalist China had been expelled from the Olympic movement, and for the next year, the anti-communist Brundage found himself under attack in the press as a communist sympathizer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss56.E2.80.9357_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss56.E2.80.9357-93">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann147_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann147-94">[91]</a></sup> Although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_State_Department" title="United States State Department">United States State Department</a> officials attempted to persuade them to stand on principle, Taiwanese officials decided to participate in the Rome Games, hoping to secure China's first medal, and believing their NOC's continued presence helped keep mainland China out of the Games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss59.E2.80.9360_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss59.E2.80.9360-95">[92]</a></sup> Taiwanese athletes competed under the designation Formosa (an alternate name for Taiwan), and caused a sensation by briefly displaying a sign reading "Under Protest" at the Opening Ceremony; when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yang_Chuan-Kwang" title="Yang Chuan-Kwang">Yang Chuan-Kwang</a> took the silver medal in the decathlon, he was not allowed to display the Nationalist Chinese flag at the medals ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss59.E2.80.9360_95-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss59.E2.80.9360-95">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESenn120_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESenn120-96">[93]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brundage, through his tenure, slowly came around to the position advocated by Iron Curtain IOC members, that the important thing was to recognize the mainland, with Taiwan of less importance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann130_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann130-97">[94]</a></sup> Although the mainland Chinese were invited by the Munich Olympic organizers to send an observer delegation to Munich (they declined due to the Taiwanese presence), it was not until 1975, after Brundage's departure as president, that the PRC applied to rejoin the Olympic movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChan475.E2.80.93476_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChan475.E2.80.93476-98">[95]</a></sup> The PRC again participated at the <a href="/wiki/1980_Winter_Olympics" title="1980 Winter Olympics">1980 Winter Games</a> at Lake Placid and then the <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics" title="1984 Summer Olympics">1984 Summer Olympics</a> in Los Angeles; the island NOC competed as the Republic of China in 1968 and 1972; when refused permission to compete under that name in 1976, after Brundage's death, it boycotted the 1976 and 1980 games, returning in 1984 as Chinese Taipei.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss410.E2.80.93412_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss410.E2.80.93412-99">[96]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa_and_Rhodesia">South Africa and Rhodesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: South Africa and Rhodesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the late 1950s, protest against <a href="/wiki/Union_of_South_Africa" title="Union of South Africa">South Africa's</a> <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a> regime reached the stage of seeking to exclude the nation from international sport. In 1956, government rules requiring separate events for whites and non-whites in South Africa were issued; non-whites received poorer facilities. Brundage initially opposed taking any action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELapchick58.E2.80.9359_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELapchick58.E2.80.9359-100">[97]</a></sup> The run-up to the 1960 Rome Olympics had seen tumult in South Africa, including the <a href="/wiki/Sharpeville_massacre" title="Sharpeville massacre">Sharpeville massacre</a> and a crackdown on the <a href="/wiki/African_National_Congress" title="African National Congress">African National Congress</a>. Activists attempted to persuade Brundage that South Africa should be excluded from the Games. Brundage initially took the word of South African sport leaders that all citizens were able to compete for a place on the Olympic team, and that non-white South Africans simply were not good enough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss53.E2.80.9354.2C_63.E2.80.9365_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss53.E2.80.9354.2C_63.E2.80.9365-101">[98]</a></sup></p>
<p>The drive towards a boycott was fueled by the large number of African nations which became independent in the late 1950s and early 1960s. To prevent the new nations from overwhelming the ISFs, Brundage proposed that the federations adopt weighted voting systems to allow earlier members to wield disproportionate influence, which some did.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEspy96.E2.80.9397_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEspy96.E2.80.9397-102">[99]</a></sup> By 1962, with the suspension of South Africa from <a href="/wiki/FIFA" title="FIFA">FIFA</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> governing body), Brundage had come around to the position that South Africa's racist policies were inconsistent with the ideals of the Olympic movement. At the 1963 IOC session in <a href="/wiki/Baden-Baden" title="Baden-Baden">Baden-Baden</a> (moved there from <a href="/wiki/Nairobi" title="Nairobi">Nairobi</a> when Kenyan officials refused to issue visas to South African representatives), the IOC voted to suspend South Africa from the Olympics unless its NOC and government adopted non-discrimination policies regarding Olympic selection. This did not come to pass, and South Africa did not participate in 1964. In 1968, Brundage and the IOC invited a South African team (supposedly to be multiracial) to the Mexico City Games, but under a threatened boycott and with evidence of minimal South African compliance, withdrew it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann231.E2.80.93240_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann231.E2.80.93240-103">[100]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1971, the IOC, at its <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> session, voted to strip the South African NOC of recognition. Although Brundage had hoped to keep South Africa within the Olympic movement, he believed that those who sought its expulsion had made the stronger case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann246.E2.80.93247_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann246.E2.80.93247-104">[101]</a></sup> South Africa did not return to the Olympics until the <a href="/wiki/1992_Summer_Olympics" title="1992 Summer Olympics">1992 Summer Olympics</a> in Barcelona, after the end of its apartheid government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Moscow-Pullman_Daily_News.27.27_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Moscow-Pullman_Daily_News.27.27-105">[102]</a></sup></p>
<p>A parallel problem was that of <a href="/wiki/Rhodesia" title="Rhodesia">Rhodesia</a>, the British colony which had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unilateral_Declaration_of_Independence" title="Unilateral Declaration of Independence">unilaterally declared its independence</a> from Great Britain in 1965. Rhodesia had a white minority government. In May 1968, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a> condemned its government and asked nations not to honor its passports, and the Mexican government, set to host the Olympics later that year, complied with the ban. The IOC initially believed that sports facilities in the breakaway colony were not segregated, despite its government's policies. The proposed 16-member Olympic team included two black athletes. Because of this, Brundage supported Rhodesian participation at <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, but he was overruled by the IOC; according to the head of the Rhodesian Olympic Committee, Douglas Downing, "His voice cries in a wilderness of spite."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann240.2C_303_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann240.2C_303-106">[103]</a></sup> For Munich in 1972, the IOC decided to allow the Rhodesians to compete as British subjects, which by international law they were. African nations again threatened to boycott if the Rhodesians were allowed to participate, and, at its <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> session in 1972 just before the Games, the IOC narrowly voted to exclude the Rhodesians. Brundage was livid at the decision, believing that the IOC had yielded to blackmail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann248.E2.80.93249_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann248.E2.80.93249-107">[104]</a></sup> In 1974, after Brundage left office, the IOC found evidence of segregated facilities in Rhodesia, and it subsequently withdrew recognition from its NOC. Rhodesia returned to the Olympics <a href="/wiki/1980_Summer_Olympics" title="1980 Summer Olympics">in 1980</a> as recognized independent <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPound119_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPound119-108">[105]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Olympic_administration.3B_challenges_to_leadership">Olympic administration; challenges to leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Olympic administration; challenges to leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Parc_Mon-Repos_Villa_Mon-Repos_et_fontaine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1353" data-file-width="2048" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Parc_Mon-Repos_Villa_Mon-Repos_et_fontaine.jpg/220px-Parc_Mon-Repos_Villa_Mon-Repos_et_fontaine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Parc_Mon-Repos_Villa_Mon-Repos_et_fontaine.jpg/330px-Parc_Mon-Repos_Villa_Mon-Repos_et_fontaine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Parc_Mon-Repos_Villa_Mon-Repos_et_fontaine.jpg/440px-Parc_Mon-Repos_Villa_Mon-Repos_et_fontaine.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

The <i>Maison de Mon-Repos</i>, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parc_de_Mon-Repos" title="Parc de Mon-Repos">Parc de Mon-Repos</a>, was the home of the IOC between 1922 and 1967.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Unpaid as IOC president, even for his expenses, Brundage sometimes spent $50,000 per year to finance his role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShaplen30_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShaplen30-109">[106]</a></sup> In 1960, the IOC had almost no funds. Brundage and the IOC had considered the potential of television revenue as early as the Melbourne Games of 1956, but had been slow to address the issue, with the result that television rights for the 1960 Games were in the hands of the Rome organizing committee; the IOC received only 5% of the $60,000 rights fee. Accounts submitted by the Rome organizers showed they lost money on the Olympics; the IOC would have received a portion of the profits, and had no money to offer the sports federations who wanted a percentage of the proceeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss132.E2.80.93133.2C_404.E2.80.93405_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss132.E2.80.93133.2C_404.E2.80.93405-110">[107]</a></sup> In future years, the sale of television rights became a major source of revenue for the IOC, rising to $10 million by the <a href="/wiki/1968_Summer_Olympics" title="1968 Summer Olympics">1968 Summer Olympics</a> in Mexico City, and $1.2 billion, long after Brundage's death, at Athens <a href="/wiki/2004_Summer_Olympics" title="2004 Summer Olympics">in 2004</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss409_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss409-111">[108]</a></sup> Brundage was concerned about the increasing revenue, warning IOC members in 1967, "The moment we handle money, even if we only distribute it, there will be trouble ..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESenn144_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESenn144-112">[109]</a></sup></p>
<p>NOC representatives had met with Brundage and the IOC executive board from time to time, but many NOC representatives felt that Brundage was taking no action in response to concerns expressed by the NOC attendees. In the early 1960s, many NOCs, led by Italian IOC member <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Giulio_Onesti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Giulio Onesti (page does not exist)">Giulio Onesti</a>, sought to bypass Brundage and the IOC by forming a Permanent General Assembly of National Olympic Committees (PGA-NOC), which Brundage strongly opposed and the IOC refused to recognize. The PGA-NOC from 1965 demanded a share of television revenue; it also desired that the ISFs, not the IOC, set policy on amateurism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann174.E2.80.93179_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann174.E2.80.93179-113">[110]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_at_Squaw_Valley.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="438" data-file-width="363" height="265" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Brundage_at_Squaw_Valley.png/220px-Brundage_at_Squaw_Valley.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Brundage_at_Squaw_Valley.png/330px-Brundage_at_Squaw_Valley.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/40/Brundage_at_Squaw_Valley.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_at_Squaw_Valley.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Brundage (left) examines the facilities at Squaw Valley, <a href="/wiki/1960_Winter_Olympics" title="1960 Winter Olympics">1960 Winter Olympics</a>.</div>
</div>

<p>Brundage had been initially elected in 1952 for an eight-year term;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Bend_Bulletin.27.271953-12-01_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Bend_Bulletin.27.271953-12-01-114">[111]</a></sup> he was re-elected unanimously in 1960 for an additional four years. Despite talk that he would be opposed by Exeter, Brundage's 1952 rival nominated him for the new term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sarasota_Herald-Tribune.27.27_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sarasota_Herald-Tribune.27.27-115">[112]</a></sup> Brundage was re-elected in 1964 by an announced unanimous vote, though Guttmann records that Brundage actually only narrowly turned back a challenge by Exeter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann175_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann175-116">[113]</a></sup> As Brundage's term as president neared its end in 1968, some IOC members, who saw him as hidebound, or just too old at 81 to effectively lead the organization, sought his ouster. Nevertheless, he was easily re-elected at the IOC session in Mexico City that year, though he pledged not to seek another four-year term, but to retire in 1972. Ireland's <a href="/wiki/Michael_Morris,_3rd_Baron_Killanin" title="Michael Morris, 3rd Baron Killanin">Lord Killanin</a> was elected first vice-president. Killanin, seen (correctly) as Brundage's likely successor, was more sympathetic to the concerns of the NOCs, and attended PGA-NOC meetings. Brundage did not recognize the PGA-NOC, but did establish joint IOC-NOC committees to address NOC concerns. Although the PGA-NOC did not gain Olympic recognition, it remained a significant outside organization through Brundage's presidency, and according to Guttmann, "Brundage won a less than total victory and Onesti suffered a far from complete defeat. The I.O.C. had become far more attractive to the national Olympic committees and to their interests, and that is what Onesti called for in the first place."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann180.E2.80.93187_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann180.E2.80.93187-117">[114]</a></sup></p>
<p>With Brundage in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> or at his <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> home, day to day IOC operations were overseen at "Mon Repos", the IOC headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Lausanne" title="Lausanne">Lausanne</a>, by Otto Meyer, the IOC's chancellor. Brundage came to consider Meyer too impetuous, and dismissed him in 1964, abolishing the office. Eventually, Brundage promoted Monique Berlioux to be IOC director in the last years of his tenure, and apparently found her services satisfactory. Mon Repos, the former home of the founder of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_Olympics" title="Modern Olympics">Modern Olympics</a>, Baron <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Coubertin" title="Pierre de Coubertin">Pierre de Coubertin</a>, proved too cramped for the IOC, which had to share space with de Coubertin's widow, who lived to be 101. In 1968, the IOC moved to new quarters at Lausanne's Château de Vidy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann187.E2.80.93189_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann187.E2.80.93189-118">[115]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_demonstration_at_Mexico_City">Political demonstration at Mexico City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Political demonstration at Mexico City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1968_Olympics_Black_Power_salute" title="1968 Olympics Black Power salute">1968 Olympics Black Power salute</a></div>
<p>The year 1968 had seen turmoil in the United States, including riots and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Martin_Luther_King" title="Assassination of Martin Luther King">assassination</a> of civil rights leader <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King" title="Martin Luther King">Martin Luther King</a>. Prior to the Olympics in Mexico City in October 1968, some African Americans, led by activist <a href="/wiki/Harry_Edwards_(sociologist)" title="Harry Edwards (sociologist)">Harry Edwards</a>, had urged a boycott of the Games, but found little enthusiasm among athletes, reluctant to waste years of effort. The atmosphere was made more tense by <a href="/wiki/Tlatelolco_massacre" title="Tlatelolco massacre">unrest</a> in Mexico City before the Games which left dozens dead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann241.E2.80.93243_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann241.E2.80.93243-119">[116]</a></sup></p>
<p>There were racial tensions between black US athletes and their white counterparts; in one incident, African Americans blocked whites from the track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[117]</a></sup> One black runner, <a href="/wiki/Tommie_Smith" title="Tommie Smith">Tommie Smith</a>, told writers on October 15, "I don't want Brundage presenting me any medals". The following day, Smith won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/200_meters" title="200 meters">200 meters</a>, and fellow African-American <a href="/wiki/John_Carlos" title="John Carlos">John Carlos</a> took the bronze medal. The two men, after receiving their medals from IAAF president Lord Exeter, and as "<a href="/wiki/The_Star-Spangled_Banner" title="The Star-Spangled Banner">The Star-Spangled Banner</a>" played, raised black-gloved fists, heads down, in salute of black power. Although the USOC initially only reprimanded the two men, under pressure from the IOC, two days after the race it suspended them from the team and expelled them from the Olympic Village. Other demonstrations by African-Americans also took place: the three African Americans who took the medals in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/400_meters" title="400 meters">400 meters</a> race, led by gold medalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lee_Evans_(athlete)" title="Lee Evans (athlete)">Lee Evans</a>, wore black berets during the anthem (they were not disciplined by the USOC) while African-American boxer <a href="/wiki/George_Foreman" title="George Foreman">George Foreman</a>, triumphant in the <a href="/wiki/Weight_class_(boxing)" title="Weight class (boxing)">heavyweight</a> division, waved an American flag around the boxing ring and with fellow American boxers invited Brundage to their victory celebration. Brundage's comment about the Smith-Carlos incident was "Warped mentalities and cracked personalities seem to be everywhere and impossible to eliminate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann243_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann243-121">[118]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Independent.27.27_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Independent.27.27-122">[119]</a></sup> The USOC's official report omits the iconic photograph of Smith and Carlos with their fists raised; the local organizing committee's official film showed footage of the ceremony. Brundage, who termed the incident "the nasty demonstration against the American flag by negroes", objected in vain to its inclusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann245_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann245-123">[120]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Munich_1972">Munich 1972</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Munich 1972">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/1972_Summer_Olympics" title="1972 Summer Olympics">1972 Summer Olympics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Munich_massacre" title="Munich massacre">Munich massacre</a></div>
<p>At the same IOC session in August 1972 in Munich at which the Rhodesians were excluded, the IOC elected Killanin as Brundage's successor, to take office after the Games. Brundage cast a blank ballot in the vote which selected the Irishman, considering him an intellectual lightweight without the force of character needed to hold the Olympic movement together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann247.E2.80.93248_124-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann247.E2.80.93248-124">[121]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Olympiastadion_Muenchen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="661" data-file-width="974" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Olympiastadion_Muenchen.jpg/220px-Olympiastadion_Muenchen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Olympiastadion_Muenchen.jpg/330px-Olympiastadion_Muenchen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Olympiastadion_Muenchen.jpg/440px-Olympiastadion_Muenchen.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Olympiastadion_Muenchen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Munich_Olympic_Stadium" title="Munich Olympic Stadium">Munich Olympic Stadium</a>, where Brundage gave his speech on September 6, 1972.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Brundage hoped that the Munich Games would take the sting out of his defeat over the Rhodesian issue. Munich was one of his favorite cities, and the <i>heitere Spiele</i> (cheerful Games) were designed to efface memories of 1936 and Berlin in the eyes of the world. They initially seemed to be doing so, as athletic feats, like those of gymnast <a href="/wiki/Olga_Korbut" title="Olga Korbut">Olga Korbut</a> and swimmer <a href="/wiki/Mark_Spitz" title="Mark Spitz">Mark Spitz</a> captivated viewers. In the early morning of September 5, 1972, Palestinian terrorists from the organization <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_September_(group)" title="Black September (group)">Black September</a> entered the Olympic Village and took 11 Israelis hostage, demanding freedom for hundreds of Palestinians held in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israeli</a> custody. Brundage, once informed, rushed to the Olympic Village, where he conferred with German and <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavarian</a> state officials through the day, playing what Guttmann describes as a modest role in the discussions. German officials moved the hostages and their captors to <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrstenfeldbruck_Air_Base" title="Fürstenfeldbruck Air Base">Fürstenfeldbruck Air Base</a>, where German police and troops tried a rescue late that evening. The attempt was bungled; the nine remaining hostages (two had been murdered earlier) and three of their captors were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann250.E2.80.93252_125-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann250.E2.80.93252-125">[122]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even before the ill-fated rescue attempt, IOC officials began conferring. Killanin and other officials were in Kiel for the yacht racing; they hurried back to Munich. Just before 4 pm, Brundage called off the remainder of the day's events, and announced a memorial service honoring those who had already died for the following morning. Many Olympic leaders were critical of Brundage for his participation in the discussions with the government, feeling that this should have been left for the authorities and the local organizing committee, but all supported the memorial service, which was held the following day in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympic_Stadium_(Munich)" title="Olympic Stadium (Munich)">Olympic Stadium</a>. There, before the audience in the stadium and the millions watching on television, Brundage offered what Guttmann called "the credo of his life":</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Every civilized person recoils in horror at the barbarous criminal intrusion of terrorists into the peaceful Olympic precincts. We mourn our Israeli friends, victims of this brutal assault. The Olympic flag and the flags of all the world fly at half mast. Sadly, in this imperfect world, the greater and more important the Olympic Games become, the more they are open to commercial, political and now criminal pressure. The Games of the 20th Olympiad have been subjected to two savage attacks. We lost the Rhodesian battle against naked political blackmail. We have only the strength of a great ideal. I am sure the public will agree that we cannot allow a handful of terrorists to destroy this nucleus of international cooperation and goodwill we have in the Olympic movement. The Games must go on and we must continue our efforts to keep them clear, pure and honest and try to extend sportsmanship of the athletic field to other areas. We declare today a day of mourning and will continue all the events one day later than scheduled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann250.E2.80.93252_125-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann250.E2.80.93252-125">[122]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The crowd in the stadium responded to Brundage's statement with loud applause; according to <i><a href="/wiki/Stars_and_Stripes_(newspaper)" title="Stars and Stripes (newspaper)">Stars &amp; Stripes</a></i>, "Brundage's statement that 'the games must go on' took much of the heavy gloom away which has permeated Munich since early Tuesday [September 5, the day of the attack]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Stars_.26_Stripes.27.27_126-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Stars_.26_Stripes.27.27-126">[123]</a></sup> Killanin, after his own retirement as IOC president, stated that "I believe Brundage was right to continue and that his stubborn determination saved the Olympic Movement one more time" but that Brundage's mention of the Rhodesian question was, while not inappropriate, at least better left for another time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESenn152.E2.80.93153_127-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESenn152.E2.80.93153-127">[124]</a></sup> According to future IOC vice president <a href="/wiki/Dick_Pound" title="Dick Pound">Dick Pound</a>, the insertion of the Rhodesian issue into the speech "was universally condemned, and Brundage left office under a cloud of criticism that effectively undermined a lifetime of well-intentioned work in the Olympic movement".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPound119_108-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPound119-108">[105]</a></sup> Brundage subsequently issued a statement that he did not mean to imply the decision to exclude the Rhodesians, which he stated was "purely a matter of sport", was comparable to the murder of the Israelis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESenn153.E2.80.93154_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESenn153.E2.80.93154-128">[125]</a></sup> According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Senn" title="Alfred Senn">Alfred Senn</a> in his history of the Olympics, the decision to continue the games "sat poorly with many observers";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESenn152_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESenn152-129">[126]</a></sup> sportswriter <a href="/wiki/Red_Smith_(sportswriter)" title="Red Smith (sportswriter)">Red Smith</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> was among the critics:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>This time surely, some thought, they would cover the sandbox and put the blocks aside. But, no. "The Games must go on," said Avery Brundage, and 80,000 listeners burst into applause. The occasion was yesterday's memorial service for eleven members of Israel's Olympic delegation murdered by Palestinian terrorists. It was more like a pep rally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann256_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann256-130">[127]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement_and_death">Retirement and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Retirement and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_Corbally.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1817" data-file-width="1280" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Brundage_Corbally.jpg/220px-Brundage_Corbally.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Brundage_Corbally.jpg/330px-Brundage_Corbally.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Brundage_Corbally.jpg/440px-Brundage_Corbally.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brundage_Corbally.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Brundage (left) with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois" title="University of Illinois">University of Illinois</a> president John Corbally, 1974, announcing the Avery Brundage Scholarships</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After the 1972 Summer Games, Brundage retired as IOC president. Accounts of Brundage's state of mind in his retirement differ. IOC director Berlioux stated that Brundage would come to the Château de Vidy and take telephone calls or look at correspondence, waiting for Lord Killanin to turn to him for help. According to Berlioux, Brundage sometimes called her from Geneva and asked her to go there. The two would spend hours wandering the streets, saying little. Brundage's longtime <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/factotum" title="wikt:factotum">factotum</a>, Frederick Ruegsegger, described a different, tranquil, Brundage, whom he compared to an abdicated <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> emperor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann257_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann257-131">[128]</a></sup></p>
<p>His wife of nearly half a century, Elizabeth, to whom he had not been faithful, died in 1971. Brundage had once jested that his ambition was to wed a German princess. In June 1973, this came to pass when he married Princess Mariann Charlotte Katharina Stefanie von Reuss (1936–2003), daughter of Heinrich XXXVII, Prince of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reuss-K%C3%B6stritz" title="Reuss-Köstritz">Reuss-Köstritz</a>. Von Reuss had worked as an interpreter during the Munich Games; she stated that she had met Brundage in 1955, when she was 19. When Brundage was asked by reporters about the 48-year difference in their ages, Brundage responded that he was young for his age and she mature for hers, and instead of 85 years to 37, it should be thought of as more like 55 to 46. Ruegsegger refused to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Best_man" title="Best man">best man</a> and stated after Brundage's death that the couple had dissipated much of Brundage's fortune through free spending, though Guttmann notes that some of those purchases were of real estate, which could be deemed investments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann257.E2.80.93259_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann257.E2.80.93259-132">[129]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 1974, Brundage underwent surgery for cataracts and glaucoma. The necessary arrangements had initially been made by Brundage's protégé, Spanish IOC member <a href="/wiki/Juan_Antonio_Samaranch" title="Juan Antonio Samaranch">Juan Antonio Samaranch</a>, who would become IOC president in 1980. At the last moment, Brundage cancelled the plans, choosing to have the surgery in Munich, near the home he had purchased in <a href="/wiki/Garmisch-Partenkirchen" title="Garmisch-Partenkirchen">Garmisch-Partenkirchen</a>, site of the <a href="/wiki/1936_Winter_Olympics" title="1936 Winter Olympics">1936 Winter Olympics</a>. After a month and a half, Brundage was discharged from the hospital, though whether the surgery had improved his vision was disputed, with Mariann Brundage stating that it did and Ruegsegger stating the contrary. Now frail, at age 87 he went with his wife on a final tour of the <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far East</a>. Despite the efforts of Olympic officials on his behalf, he was not given an invitation to mainland China, source of much of <a href="/wiki/Avery_Brundage#Art_collector_and_benefactor" title="Avery Brundage">the art he loved</a>. In April 1975, Brundage entered the hospital at <a href="/wiki/Garmisch-Partenkirchen" title="Garmisch-Partenkirchen">Garmisch-Partenkirchen</a> with flu and a severe cough. He died there on May 8, 1975, of heart failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann259.E2.80.93261_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann259.E2.80.93261-133">[130]</a></sup></p>
<p>In his will, Brundage provided for his wife and for Ruegsegger, as well as making several charitable bequests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sports_Illustrated.27.27_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sports_Illustrated.27.27-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann44_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann44-134">[131]</a></sup> He left his papers and memorabilia to the University of Illinois;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sports_Illustrated.27.27_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sports_Illustrated.27.27-2">[2]</a></sup> he had already given it $350,000 to fund scholarships for students interested in competing in sports who do not receive an athletic scholarship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Money_and_Olympic_Ideology.2265_135-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Money_and_Olympic_Ideology.2265-135">[132]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life_and_business_career">Personal life and business career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Personal life and business career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships">Relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Brundage married Elizabeth Dunlap, daughter of a Chicago banker, in 1927, when he was 40. Elizabeth Brundage, three years younger than her husband, was a trained <a href="/wiki/Soprano" title="Soprano">soprano</a>, a talent she exhibited for visitors to the Brundage home. She had a strong interest in classical music, one which may not have been fully shared by her husband, who said of a performance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wagner" title="Wagner">Wagner</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Walk%C3%BCre" title="Die Walküre">Die Walküre</a></i> that it "started at 7 o'clock, at 10:00 pm I looked at my watch and it registered exactly 8 o'clock".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann45.E2.80.9346_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann45.E2.80.9346-136">[133]</a></sup> She died at age 81 in 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann211_137-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann211-137">[134]</a></sup></p>
<p>Avery Brundage had no children with either of his wives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sports_Illustrated.27.27_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sports_Illustrated.27.27-2">[2]</a></sup> During his first marriage, however, Brundage fathered two sons out of wedlock with his Finnish mistress, Lilian Dresden. His affair with Dresden was one of many. The children were born in 1951 and 1952, at precisely the time that Brundage was being considered for the presidency of the IOC. Though he privately acknowledged paternity, Brundage took great pains to conceal the existence of these children, concerned that the truth about his extra-marital relationships might damage his chances of election: he requested that his name be kept off the birth certificates. Brundage visited his two sons periodically in the 1950s, visits that tailed off to telephone calls in the 1960s and nothing in his final years. He did establish a trust fund for the boys' education and start in life, but after his death, unnamed in his will, they sued and won a small settlement of $62,500 each out of his $19 million estate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sports_Illustrated.27.27_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27Sports_Illustrated.27.27-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_executive">Construction executive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Construction executive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:La_Salle_Hotel.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="517" data-file-width="327" height="348" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/La_Salle_Hotel.png/220px-La_Salle_Hotel.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e1/La_Salle_Hotel.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:La_Salle_Hotel.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Chicago's <a href="/wiki/La_Salle_Hotel" title="La Salle Hotel">La Salle Hotel</a> was owned by Brundage for many years.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After its founding in 1915, a large source of the Avery Brundage Company's business was wartime government contracts. Brundage, who applied for a commission in the <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Corps_(United_States_Army)" title="Ordnance Corps (United States Army)">Army Ordnance Corps</a> but was rejected, in the postwar period became a member of the Construction Division Association, composed of men who had built facilities for the military, and later became its president from 1926 to 1928.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22220_138-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22220-138">[135]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1920s, Brundage and his company became very active in constructing high-rise apartment buildings in Chicago. He used rapid construction methods, allowing clients to begin realizing income from their investments quickly—the Sheridan-Brompton Apartments (1924) overlooking <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Park" title="Lincoln Park">Lincoln Park</a>, were built in five months, allowing the start of $40,000 in monthly rental income, offsetting a monthly mortgage payment of $15,000. Often, the Brundage Company was involved in the ownership of the apartments: 3800 <a href="/wiki/Sheridan_Road" title="Sheridan Road">Sheridan Road</a> (1927), a 17-story building costing $3,180,000, was owned by a company which had as its president and treasurer Chester Brundage, Avery's younger brother. It was constructed in eight months, through the Chicago winter, using an onsite concrete mixing plant. This temporary structure also provided office space for the construction. Another source of income for Brundage and his company was hotel construction, for which he was often paid in part with stock in the new facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22220.E2.80.93221_139-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22220.E2.80.93221-139">[136]</a></sup> One president of an engineering firm specializing in large structures called Brundage's methods on the Shoreham Hotel "progressive, snappy, [and] up-to-date" and "straightforward and honest".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22223_140-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22223-140">[137]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1923, Brundage constructed <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Assembly" title="Chicago Assembly">a massive assembly plant</a> on Torrence Avenue on <a href="/wiki/South_Side,_Chicago" title="South Side, Chicago">Chicago's South Side</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a>. At $4 million in cost and bringing 16 acres (6.5 ha) under one roof, it was the largest industrial plant built by Brundage. Constructed in ten months, the new facility helped meet the national demand for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Model_T" title="Model T">Model T</a> cars in the 1920s, and in 1950, produced 154,244 vehicles. A plant for Hubbard &amp; Co. was erected in 125 days despite an unusually harsh Chicago winter. Despite later statements from Brundage that he avoided public works due to corruption, he built the 23rd Street viaduct as part of the South Shore Development project; Brundage's viaduct extended Chicago's shoreline into <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a> at a cost of two million dollars. By 1925, the Avery Brundage Company was acclaimed for speed, innovation and quality, and had a payroll of $50,000 a week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22222.E2.80.93223_141-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22222.E2.80.93223-141">[138]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although the start of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Depression" title="The Depression">the Depression</a> in 1929 was a major setback for Brundage, he rebuilt his wealth by investments in real estate, also accepting interests in buildings he had constructed in lieu of payments the owners were unable to make. He later stated that "you didn't have to be a wizard" in order to "buy stocks and bonds in depressed corporations for a few cents on the dollar—and then wait. I was just a little lucky."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22226.E2.80.93227_142-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22226.E2.80.93227-142">[139]</a></sup> According to historian and archivist Maynard Brichford, Brundage "emerged from the difficult depression years with a substantial annual income, a good reputation, and excellent investments".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Money_and_Olympic_Ideology.2262_143-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Money_and_Olympic_Ideology.2262-143">[140]</a></sup> His foresight resulted in a fortune which by 1960 was estimated at $25,000,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22226.E2.80.93227_142-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22226.E2.80.93227-142">[139]</a></sup></p>
<p>A major Brundage investment was Chicago's <a href="/wiki/La_Salle_Hotel" title="La Salle Hotel">La Salle Hotel</a>, which had been built in 1908. Located in the heart of <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Loop" title="Chicago Loop">The Loop</a> and the city's financial district, Brundage first leased it in 1940, later purchasing it. When the hotel was seriously damaged by fire in 1946, Brundage spent about $2.5 million remodeling and modernizing it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22229_144-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Chicago_Businessman.22229-144">[141]</a></sup> As Brundage made a home there during his time as IOC president, the hotel became famous in international sports as his residence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann42_145-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann42-145">[142]</a></sup> He sold the hotel in 1970, but later reclaimed it when the purchaser failed to make required payments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Money_and_Olympic_Ideology.2264_146-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Money_and_Olympic_Ideology.2264-146">[143]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_collector_and_benefactor">Art collector and benefactor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Art collector and benefactor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saturnism.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Saturnism.jpg/220px-Saturnism.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Saturnism.jpg/330px-Saturnism.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Saturnism.jpg/440px-Saturnism.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saturnism.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A seated Chinese Buddha, dated 338 (making it the earliest known dated Buddha figure), formerly owned by Brundage and now in the <a href="/wiki/Asian_Art_Museum_of_San_Francisco" title="Asian Art Museum of San Francisco">Asian Art Museum of San Francisco</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Brundage's interest in Asian art stemmed from a visit he made to an exhibition of Chinese art at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Academy" title="Royal Academy">Royal Academy</a> in London in early 1936, after the Winter Olympics in <a href="/wiki/Garmisch-Partenkirchen" title="Garmisch-Partenkirchen">Garmisch-Partenkirchen</a>. Brundage stated of the experience, "We [his first wife Elizabeth and himself] spent a week at the exhibition and I came away so enamored with Chinese art that I've been broke ever since."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann201.E2.80.93202_147-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann201.E2.80.93202-147">[144]</a></sup> He did not begin active collecting until after the Brundages' two-week visit to Japan in April 1939, where they visited <a href="/wiki/Yokohama" title="Yokohama">Yokohama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nara,_Nara" title="Nara, Nara">Nara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nikk%C5%8D,_Tochigi" title="Nikkō, Tochigi">Nikko</a>. They followed up Japan with visits to <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, but due to the war between Japan and China, were unable to explore further on Avery Brundage's only visit to mainland China—this disappointment bothered him his whole life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann202.E2.80.93203_148-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann202.E2.80.93203-148">[145]</a></sup></p>
<p>On his return to the United States after the June 1939 IOC session in London, Brundage systematically set about becoming a major collector of Asian art. The unsettled conditions caused wealthy Chinese to sell family heirlooms, and prices were depressed, making it an opportune moment to collect. He bought many books on Asian art, stating in an interview that a "major library is an indispensable tool".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann203_149-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann203-149">[146]</a></sup> After the US entered World War II, stock owned by Japanese dealers in the United States was impounded; Brundage was able to purchase the best items.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBusch5_150-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBusch5-150">[147]</a></sup> Dealers found him willing to spend money, but knowledgeable and a hard bargainer. Brundage rarely was fooled by forgeries, and was undeterred by the few he did buy, noting that in Asian art, fake pieces were often a thousand years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann203_149-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann203-149">[146]</a></sup> In his 1948 article on Brundage for <i>Life</i>, Butterfield noted that "his collection is regarded as one of the largest and most important in private hands in this country".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButterfield124_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButterfield124-49">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brundage engaged the French scholar René-Yvon Lefebvre d'Argencé, then teaching at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California" title="University of California">University of California</a>, as full-time curator of his collection and advisor on acquisitions. The two men made a deal—no piece would be purchased unless both men agreed. They built a collection of jade which ranged from the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">neolithic</a> period to the modern era; and hundreds of Chinese, Japanese and Korean bronzes, mostly Buddhas and <a href="/wiki/Bodhisattva" title="Bodhisattva">Bodhisattvas</a>. The painter who Brundage admired the most was <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Huizong_of_Song" title="Emperor Huizong of Song">Huizong</a>, 12th-century Chinese emperor of the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a>; the collector never was able to obtain any of his work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann203.E2.80.93205_151-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann203.E2.80.93205-151">[148]</a></sup> Brundage several times bought pieces smuggled out of their lands of origin to restore them there. When Brundage sold a piece, it was most likely because he no longer favored it artistically, rather than to realize a profit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann204.E2.80.93205_152-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann204.E2.80.93205-152">[149]</a></sup> In 1954, a financial statement prepared for Brundage listed the value of his collection as more than $1 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Money_and_Olympic_Ideology.2262_143-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrichford.2C_.22Money_and_Olympic_Ideology.2262-143">[140]</a></sup> In 1960, Robert Shaplen, in his article on Brundage for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i>, noted that Brundage, during his travels as IOC president, always found time to visit art dealers, and stated that the collection was valued at $15 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShaplen31_153-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShaplen31-153">[150]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the late 1950s, Brundage was increasingly concerned about what to do with his collection. His homes in Chicago and California were so overwhelmed with art that priceless artifacts were kept in shoeboxes under beds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBusch5.E2.80.936_154-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBusch5.E2.80.936-154">[151]</a></sup> In 1959, Brundage agreed to give part of his collection to the city of San Francisco. The following year city voters passed a bond issue of $2,725,000 to house the donation. The result was the <a href="/wiki/Asian_Art_Museum_of_San_Francisco" title="Asian Art Museum of San Francisco">Asian Art Museum of San Francisco</a>, which opened in 1966 in <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Park" title="Golden Gate Park">Golden Gate Park</a>, initially sharing space with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M._H._de_Young_Memorial_Museum" title="M. H. de Young Memorial Museum">M. H. de Young Memorial Museum</a> before moving to its own facility near the <a href="/wiki/Civic_Center,_San_Francisco" title="Civic Center, San Francisco">Civic Center</a> in 2003. Brundage made another major donation in 1969 (despite a fire which destroyed many pieces at his California home, "La Piñeta" near <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara,_California" title="Santa Barbara, California">Santa Barbara</a> in 1964), and left the remainder of his collection to the museum in his will. Today, the museum has 7,700 pieces from Brundage among the 17,000-plus objects which make up its collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAsian_Art_Museum_155-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAsian_Art_Museum-155">[152]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann210.E2.80.93211_156-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann210.E2.80.93211-156">[153]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brundage connected the world of art and that of amateur sports in his own mind. In a speech to the IOC session in Tokyo in 1958, he discussed <a href="/wiki/Netsuke" title="Netsuke">netsuke</a>, used at one time by Japanese to anchor items hung from <a href="/wiki/Kimono" title="Kimono">kimono</a> sashes, and of which he owned several thousand; he held two in his hands as he spoke. He told the members that a netsuke was at one time carefully carved by the man who wore it, building "something of himself into the design", and although a class of professional netsuke makers arose later, whose work might have been more technically adept, it was, "ordinarily cold, stiff, and without imagination. ... Missing was the element of the amateur carver, which causes these netsuke to be esteemed so much higher by the collector than the commercial product carved for money."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShaplen70.E2.80.9371_157-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShaplen70.E2.80.9371-157">[154]</a></sup> Brundage later commented about his speech, "Here was the difference between amateurism and professionalism spelled out in a netsuke."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShaplen71_158-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShaplen71-158">[155]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avery_Brundage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Retrospective news coverage has sometimes judged Brundage harshly: in May 2012, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> dubbed him "The ancient IOC emperor, anti-Semite and Nazi sympathiser bent on insulating the Games from the meddlesome tentacles of the real world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Independent.27.27_122-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Independent.27.27-122">[119]</a></sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Orange_County_Register" title="The Orange County Register">The Orange County Register</a></i> stated that Brundage's "racism and anti-Semitism are well documented",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Orange_County_Register.27.27_159-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27The_Orange_County_Register.27.27-159">[156]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Daily_News" title="New York Daily News">New York <i>Daily News</i></a> averred that Brundage "admired Hitler and infamously replaced two Jewish sprinters on the 4-by-100 relay team because it could have further embarrassed Hitler if they won".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE.27.27New_York_Daily_News.27.27_160-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE.27.27New_York_Daily_News.27.27-160">[157]</a></sup></p>
<p>Brundage leaves an uncertain legacy. On the question of amateurism, Maraniss suggests that Brundage was "caught in a vise" between communist and capitalist states pursuing dissimilar goals; nations following either system were not willing to accept Brundage's vision of pure amateurism, and broke those rules, each system in its own way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMaraniss407_161-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMaraniss407-161">[158]</a></sup> Guttmann notes that in the 1960s, Brundage may have been better-known as an art collector than for his sports activities, and "there are those who maintain that he will be remembered not for his career in sports but for his jades and bronzes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuttmann201_162-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuttmann201-162">[159]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Leigh" title="Andrew Leigh">Andrew Leigh</a>, a Member of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_House_of_Representatives" title="Australian House of Representatives">Australian House of Representatives</a>, criticizes Brundage for expelling the two athletes in Mexico City, calling him "a man who'd had no difficulty with the Nazi salute being used in the 1936 Olympics".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-163"><a href="#cite_note-163">[160]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dick_Pound" title="Dick Pound">Dick Pound</a> believes Brundage to have been one of the IOC's great presidents, along with <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Coubertin" title="Pierre de Coubertin">de Coubertin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samaranch" title="Samaranch">Samaranch</a>, but concedes that by the end of his term, Brundage was out of touch with the world of sports. While Pound credits Brundage with holding the Olympic movement together in a period when it was beset by many challenges, he notes that this might not be fully appreciated by those who remember Brundage for the final years of his term, and for Munich.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPound230.2C_234_164-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPound230.2C_234-164">[161]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Senn" title="Alfred Senn">Alfred Senn</a> suggests that Brundage remained too long as IOC president:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>After Munich, Brundage departed the Games, which had grown beyond his comprehension and his capacity to adjust. The NOCs and the [ISFs] were revolting against his arbitrary administration; violence had invaded his holy mountain and was giving every indication of returning; despite all his efforts to reach out to the world through athletics, he stood accused of bigotry and both race and class prejudice, not to mention the denunciations proclaiming him politically naive ... Few mourned his departure from the Olympic scene, and the International Olympic Committee turned to his successor, who, its members hoped, would be better suited to handle the new items on its agenda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESenn154_165-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESenn154-165">[162]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
