<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Agent-based model</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>An <b>agent-based model</b> (<b>ABM</b>) is one of a class of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computational_models" title="Computational models">computational models</a> for <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">simulating</a> the actions and interactions of autonomous agents (both individual or collective entities such as organizations or groups) with a view to assessing their effects on the system as a whole. It combines elements of <a href="/wiki/Game_theory" title="Game theory">game theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Complex_systems" title="Complex systems">complex systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emergence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computational_sociology" title="Computational sociology">computational sociology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multi-agent_system" title="Multi-agent system">multi-agent systems</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_programming" title="Evolutionary programming">evolutionary programming</a>. <a href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo_method" title="Monte Carlo method">Monte Carlo methods</a> are used to introduce randomness. Particularly within ecology, ABMs are also called <b>individual-based models</b> (<b>IBMs</b>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and individuals within IBMs may be simpler than fully autonomous agents within ABMs. A review of recent literature on individual-based models, agent-based models, and multiagent systems shows that ABMs are used on non-computing related scientific domains including <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, ecology and <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Niazi-Hussain_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Niazi-Hussain-2">[2]</a></sup> Agent-based modeling is related to, but distinct from, the concept of <b><a href="/wiki/Multi-agent_system" title="Multi-agent system">multi-agent systems</a></b> or <b>multi-agent simulation</b> in that the goal of ABM is to search for explanatory insight into the collective behavior of agents obeying simple rules, typically in natural systems, rather than in designing agents or solving specific practical or engineering problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Niazi-Hussain_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Niazi-Hussain-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Agent-based models are a kind of <a href="/wiki/Microscale_and_macroscale_models" title="Microscale and macroscale models">microscale model</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> that simulate the simultaneous operations and interactions of multiple agents in an attempt to re-create and predict the appearance of complex phenomena. The process is one of <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emergence</a> from the lower (micro) level of systems to a higher (macro) level. As such, a key notion is that simple behavioral rules generate complex behavior. This principle, known as <a href="/wiki/KISS_principle" title="KISS principle">K.I.S.S.</a> ("Keep it simple, stupid"), is extensively adopted in the modeling community. Another central tenet is that the whole is greater than the sum of the parts. Individual agents are typically characterized as boundedly rational, presumed to be acting in what they perceive as their own interests, such as reproduction, economic benefit, or social status,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> using heuristics or simple decision-making rules. ABM agents may experience "learning", adaptation, and reproduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most agent-based models are composed of: (1) numerous agents specified at various scales (typically referred to as agent-granularity); (2) decision-making heuristics; (3) learning rules or adaptive processes; (4) an interaction topology; and (5) an environment. ABMs are typically implemented as <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">computer simulations</a>, either as custom software, or via ABM toolkits, and this software can be then used to test how changes in individual behaviors will affect the system's emerging overall behavior.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The idea of agent-based modeling was developed as a relatively simple concept in the late 1940s. Since it requires computation-intensive procedures, it did not become widespread until the 1990s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_developments">Early developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The history of the agent-based model can be traced back to the <a href="/wiki/Von_Neumann_universal_constructor" title="Von Neumann universal constructor">Von Neumann machine</a>, a theoretical machine capable of reproduction. The device <a href="/wiki/John_von_Neumann" title="John von Neumann">von Neumann</a> proposed would follow precisely detailed instructions to fashion a copy of itself. The concept was then improved by von Neumann's friend <a href="/wiki/Stanislaw_Ulam" title="Stanislaw Ulam">Stanislaw Ulam</a>, also a mathematician; Ulam suggested that the machine be built on paper, as a collection of cells on a grid. The idea intrigued von Neumann, who drew it up—creating the first of the devices later termed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cellular_automata" title="Cellular automata">cellular automata</a>. Another advance was introduced by the mathematician <a href="/wiki/John_Horton_Conway" title="John Horton Conway">John Conway</a>. He constructed the well-known <a href="/wiki/Conway%27s_Game_of_Life" title="Conway's Game of Life">Game of Life</a>. Unlike von Neumann's machine, Conway's Game of Life operated by tremendously simple rules in a virtual world in the form of a 2-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Checkerboard" title="Checkerboard">checkerboard</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s_and_1980s:_the_first_models">1970s and 1980s: the first models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1970s and 1980s: the first models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the earliest agent-based models in concept was <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Schelling" title="Thomas Schelling">Thomas Schelling</a>'s segregation model,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas-6">[6]</a></sup> which was discussed in his paper "Dynamic Models of Segregation" in 1971. Though Schelling originally used coins and graph paper rather than computers, his models embodied the basic concept of agent-based models as autonomous agents interacting in a shared environment with an observed aggregate, emergent outcome.</p>
<p>In the early 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Axelrod" title="Robert Axelrod">Robert Axelrod</a> hosted a tournament of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisoner%27s_Dilemma" title="Prisoner's Dilemma">Prisoner's Dilemma</a> strategies and had them interact in an agent-based manner to determine a winner. Axelrod would go on to develop many other agent-based models in the field of political science that examine phenomena from <a href="/wiki/Ethnocentrism" title="Ethnocentrism">ethnocentrism</a> to the dissemination of culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Axelrod_1997_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Axelrod_1997-7">[7]</a></sup> By the late 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Craig_Reynolds_(computer_graphics)" title="Craig Reynolds (computer graphics)">Craig Reynolds</a>' work on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flocking_behavior" title="Flocking behavior">flocking</a> models contributed to the development of some of the first biological agent-based models that contained social characteristics. He tried to model the reality of lively biological agents, known as <a href="/wiki/Artificial_life" title="Artificial life">artificial life</a>, a term coined by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Langton" title="Christopher Langton">Christopher Langton</a>.</p>
<p>The first use of the word "agent" and a definition as it is currently used today is hard to track down. One candidate appears to be <a href="/wiki/John_Henry_Holland" title="John Henry Holland">John Holland</a> and John H. Miller's 1991 paper "Artificial Adaptive Agents in Economic Theory",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holland_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holland-8">[8]</a></sup> based on an earlier conference presentation of theirs.</p>
<p>At the same time, during the 1980s, social scientists, mathematicians, operations researchers, and a scattering of people from other disciplines developed Computational and Mathematical Organization Theory (CMOT). This field grew as a special interest group of The Institute of Management Sciences (TIMS) and its sister society, the Operations Research Society of America (ORSA).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s:_expansion">1990s: expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1990s: expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the appearance of StarLogo in 1990, <a href="/wiki/Swarm_(simulation)" title="Swarm (simulation)">Swarm</a> and <a href="/wiki/NetLogo" title="NetLogo">NetLogo</a> in the mid-1990s and <a href="/wiki/Repast_(modeling_toolkit)" title="Repast (modeling toolkit)">RePast</a> and <a href="/wiki/AnyLogic" title="AnyLogic">AnyLogic</a> in 2000, or GAMA<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> in 2007 as well as some custom-designed code, modelling software became widely available and the range of domains that ABM was applied to, grew. Bonabeau (2002) is a good survey of the potential of agent-based modeling as of the time<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 1990s were especially notable for the expansion of ABM within the social sciences, one notable effort was the large-scale ABM, <a href="/wiki/Sugarscape" title="Sugarscape">Sugarscape</a>, developed by <a href="/wiki/Joshua_M._Epstein" title="Joshua M. Epstein">Joshua M. Epstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Axtell" title="Robert Axtell">Robert Axtell</a> to simulate and explore the role of social phenomena such as seasonal migrations, pollution, sexual reproduction, combat, and transmission of disease and even culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GAS_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-GAS-11">[11]</a></sup> Other notable 1990s developments included <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Carley" title="Kathleen Carley">Kathleen Carley</a> ABM,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> to explore the co-evolution of social networks and culture. During this 1990s timeframe <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Gilbert" title="Nigel Gilbert">Nigel Gilbert</a> published the first textbook on Social Simulation: Simulation for the social scientist (1999) and established a journal from the perspective of social sciences: the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Artificial_Societies_and_Social_Simulation" title="Journal of Artificial Societies and Social Simulation">Journal of Artificial Societies and Social Simulation</a></i> (JASSS). Other than JASSS, agent-based models of any discipline are within scope of SpringerOpen journal <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Complex_Adaptive_Systems_Modeling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Complex Adaptive Systems Modeling (page does not exist)">Complex Adaptive Systems Modeling</a></i> (CASM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through the mid-1990s, the social sciences thread of ABM began to focus on such issues as designing effective teams, understanding the communication required for organizational effectiveness, and the behavior of social networks. CMOT—later renamed Computational Analysis of Social and Organizational Systems (CASOS)—incorporated more and more agent-based modeling. Samuelson (2000) is a good brief overview of the early history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and Samuelson (2005) and Samuelson and Macal (2006) trace the more recent developments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the late 1990s, the merger of TIMS and ORSA to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/INFORMS" title="INFORMS">INFORMS</a>, and the move by INFORMS from two meetings each year to one, helped to spur the CMOT group to form a separate society, the North American Association for Computational Social and Organizational Sciences (NAACSOS). Kathleen Carley was a major contributor, especially to models of social networks, obtaining <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> funding for the annual conference and serving as the first President of NAACSOS. She was succeeded by David Sallach of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argonne_National_Laboratory" title="Argonne National Laboratory">Argonne National Laboratory</a>, and then by Michael Prietula<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Emory_University" title="Emory University">Emory University</a>. At about the same time NAACSOS began, the <a href="/wiki/European_Social_Simulation_Association" title="European Social Simulation Association">European Social Simulation Association</a> (ESSA) and the Pacific Asian Association for Agent-Based Approach in Social Systems Science (PAAA), counterparts of NAACSOS, were organized. As of 2013, these three organizations collaborate internationally. The First World Congress on Social Simulation was held under their joint sponsorship in Kyoto, Japan, in August 2006.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Second World Congress was held in the northern Virginia suburbs of Washington, D.C., in July 2008, with <a href="/wiki/George_Mason_University" title="George Mason University">George Mason University</a> taking the lead role in local arrangements.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s_and_later">2000s and later</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2000s and later">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>More recently, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Sun" title="Ron Sun">Ron Sun</a> developed methods for basing agent-based simulation on models of human cognition, known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cognitive_social_simulation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cognitive social simulation (page does not exist)">cognitive social simulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Bill McKelvey, Suzanne Lohmann, Dario Nardi, Dwight Read and others at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UCLA" title="UCLA">UCLA</a> have also made significant contributions in organizational behavior and decision-making. Since 2001, UCLA has arranged a conference at Lake Arrowhead, California, that has become another major gathering point for practitioners in this field.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2014, Sadegh Asgari from <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> and his colleagues developed an agent-based model of the construction competitive bidding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> While his model were used to analyze the low-bid lump-sum construction bids, it could be applied to other bidding methods with little modifications to the model.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">Theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most computational modeling research describes systems in <a href="/wiki/Steady_state" title="Steady state">equilibrium</a> or as moving between equilibria. Agent-based modeling, however, using simple rules, can result in different sorts of complex and interesting behavior. The three ideas central to agent-based models are agents as objects, <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emergence</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Complexity" title="Complexity">complexity</a>.</p>
<p>Agent-based models consist of dynamically interacting rule-based agents. The systems within which they interact can create real-world-like complexity. Typically agents are <a href="/wiki/Situated" title="Situated">situated</a> in space and time and reside in networks or in lattice-like neighborhoods. The location of the agents and their responsive behavior are encoded in <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithmic</a> form in computer programs. In some cases, though not always, the agents may be considered as intelligent and purposeful. In ecological ABM (often referred to as "individual-based models" in ecology), agents may, for example, be trees in forest, and would not be considered intelligent, although they may be "purposeful" in the sense of optimizing access to a resource (such as water). The modeling process is best described as <a href="/wiki/Inductive_reasoning" title="Inductive reasoning">inductive</a>. The modeler makes those assumptions thought most relevant to the situation at hand and then watches phenomena emerge from the agents' interactions. Sometimes that result is an equilibrium. Sometimes it is an emergent pattern. Sometimes, however, it is an unintelligible mangle.</p>
<p>In some ways, agent-based models complement traditional analytic methods. Where analytic methods enable humans to characterize the equilibria of a system, agent-based models allow the possibility of generating those equilibria. This generative contribution may be the most mainstream of the potential benefits of agent-based modeling. Agent-based models can explain the emergence of higher-order patterns—network structures of terrorist organizations and the Internet, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power-law_distributions" title="Power-law distributions">power-law distributions</a> in the sizes of traffic jams, wars, and stock-market crashes, and social segregation that persists despite populations of tolerant people. Agent-based models also can be used to identify lever points, defined as moments in time in which interventions have extreme consequences, and to distinguish among types of path dependency.</p>
<p>Rather than focusing on stable states, many models consider a system's robustness—the ways that complex systems adapt to internal and external pressures so as to maintain their functionalities. The task of harnessing that complexity requires consideration of the agents themselves—their diversity, connectedness, and level of interactions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Framework">Framework</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Framework">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recent work on the Modeling and simulation of Complex Adaptive Systems has demonstrated the need for combining agent-based and complex network based models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> describe a framework consisting of four levels of developing models of complex adaptive systems described using several example multidisciplinary case studies:</p>
<ol>
<li>Complex Network Modeling Level for developing models using interaction data of various system components.</li>
<li>Exploratory Agent-based Modeling Level for developing agent-based models for assessing the feasibility of further research. This can e.g. be useful for developing proof-of-concept models such as for funding applications without requiring an extensive learning curve for the researchers.</li>
<li>Descriptive Agent-based Modeling (DREAM) for developing descriptions of agent-based models by means of using templates and complex network-based models. Building DREAM models allows model comparison across scientific disciplines.</li>
<li>Validated agent-based modeling using Virtual Overlay Multiagent system (VOMAS) for the development of verified and validated models in a formal manner.</li>
</ol>
<p>Other methods of describing agent-based models include code templates<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and text-based methods such as the ODD (Overview, Design concepts, and Design Details) protocol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>The role of the environment where agents live, both macro and micro,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> is also becoming an important factor in agent-based modelling and simulation work. Simple environment affords simple agents, but complex environments generates diversity of behaviour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_biology">In biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Agent-based_model_in_biology" title="Agent-based model in biology">Agent-based model in biology</a></div>
<p>Agent-based modeling has been used extensively in biology, including the analysis of the spread of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidemics" title="Epidemics">epidemics</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and the threat of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biowarfare" title="Biowarfare">biowarfare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agent-based_model_in_biology" title="Agent-based model in biology">biological applications</a> including <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics" title="Population dynamics">population dynamics</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> vegetation ecology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ch.27ng.2C_E._.282009.29_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ch.27ng.2C_E._.282009.29-29">[29]</a></sup> landscape diversity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> the growth and decline of ancient civilizations, evolution of ethnocentric behavior,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> forced displacement/migration,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> language choice dynamics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_model#Dynamical_systems" title="Cognitive model">cognitive modeling</a>, and biomedical applications including modeling 3D breast tissue formation/morphogenesis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> the effects of ionizing radiation on mammary stem cell subpopulation dynamics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> inflammation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and the human <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Agent-based models have also been used for developing decision support systems such as for breast cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Agent-based models are increasingly being used to model pharmacological systems in early stage and pre-clinical research to aid in drug development and gain insights into biological systems that would not be possible <i>a priori</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPT_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-CPT-40">[40]</a></sup> Military applications have also been evaluated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Moreover, agent-based models have been recently employed to study molecular-level biological systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_business.2C_technology_and_network_theory">In business, technology and network theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In business, technology and network theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Agent-based models have been used since the mid-1990s to solve a variety of business and technology problems. Examples of applications include the modeling of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organizational_behaviour" title="Organizational behaviour">organizational behaviour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognition</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes_et_al_.282012.29_JOOP_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes_et_al_.282012.29_JOOP-45">[45]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Teamwork" title="Teamwork">team working</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crowder_et_al_.282012.29_IEEE_TSMCA_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crowder_et_al_.282012.29_IEEE_TSMCA-46">[46]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain_optimization" title="Supply chain optimization">supply chain optimization</a> and logistics, modeling of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_behavior" title="Consumer behavior">consumer behavior</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Word_of_mouth" title="Word of mouth">word of mouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_network" title="Social network">social network</a> effects, <a href="/wiki/Distributed_computing" title="Distributed computing">distributed computing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Workforce_management" title="Workforce management">workforce management</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Investment_management" title="Investment management">portfolio management</a>. They have also been used to analyze <a href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion" title="Traffic congestion">traffic congestion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Recently, agent based modelling and simulation has been applied to various domains such as studying the impact of publication venues by researchers in the computer science domain (journals versus conferences).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In addition, ABMs have been used to simulate information delivery in ambient assisted environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> A November 2016 article in <a href="/wiki/ArXiv" title="ArXiv">arXiv</a> analyzed an agent based simulation of posts spread in the Facebook <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Online_social_network" title="Online social network">online social network</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In the domain of peer-to-peer, ad-hoc and other self-organizing and complex networks, the usefulness of agent based modeling and simulation has been shown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The use of a computer science-based formal specification framework coupled with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_sensor_networks" title="Wireless sensor networks">wireless sensor networks</a> and an agent-based simulation has recently been demonstrated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Agent based evolutionary search or algorithm is a new research topic for solving complex optimization problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_economics_and_social_sciences">In economics and social sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In economics and social sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Prior to, and in the wake of the financial crisis, interest has grown in ABMs as possible tools for economic analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> ABMs do not assume the economy can achieve equilibrium and "<a href="/wiki/Representative_agent" title="Representative agent">representative agents</a>" are replaced by agents with diverse, dynamic, and interdependent behavior including <a href="/wiki/Herding" title="Herding">herding</a>. ABMs take a "bottom-up" approach and can generate extremely complex and volatile simulated economies. ABMs can represent unstable systems with crashes and booms that develop out of non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear" title="Linear">linear</a> (disproportionate) responses to proportionally small changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agents_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-agents-56">[56]</a></sup> A July 2010 article in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> looked at ABMs as alternatives to <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_stochastic_general_equilibrium" title="Dynamic stochastic general equilibrium">DGSE</a> models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agents_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-agents-56">[56]</a></sup> The journal <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> also encouraged agent-based modeling with an editorial that suggested ABMs can do a better job of representing financial markets and other economic complexities than standard models<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> along with an essay by <a href="/wiki/J._Doyne_Farmer" title="J. Doyne Farmer">J. Doyne Farmer</a> and Duncan Foley that argued ABMs could fulfill both the desires of Keynes to represent a complex economy and of Robert Lucas to construct models based on microfoundations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarmerFoley2009685_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarmerFoley2009685-58">[58]</a></sup> Farmer and Foley pointed to progress that has been made using ABMs to model parts of an economy, but argued for the creation of a very large model that incorporates low level models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarmerFoley2009686_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarmerFoley2009686-59">[59]</a></sup> By modeling a complex system of analysts based on three distinct behavioral profiles – imitating, anti-imitating, and indifferent – financial markets were simulated to high accuracy. Results showed a correlation between network morphology and the stock market index.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
<p>Since the beginning of the 21st century ABMs have been deployed in architecture and urban planning to evaluate design and to simulate pedestrian flow in the urban environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> There is also a growing field of socio-economic analysis of infrastructure investment impact using ABM's ability to discern systemic impacts upon a socio-economic network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizational_ABM:_agent-directed_simulation">Organizational ABM: agent-directed simulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Organizational ABM: agent-directed simulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The agent-directed simulation (ADS) metaphor distinguishes between two categories, namely "Systems for Agents" and "Agents for Systems."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Systems for Agents (sometimes referred to as agents systems) are systems implementing agents for the use in engineering, human and social dynamics, military applications, and others. Agents for Systems are divided in two subcategories. Agent-supported systems deal with the use of agents as a support facility to enable computer assistance in problem solving or enhancing cognitive capabilities. Agent-based systems focus on the use of agents for the generation of model behavior in a system evaluation (system studies and analyses).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation">Implementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Implementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many agent-based modeling software are designed for serial <a href="/wiki/Von_Neumann_architecture" title="Von Neumann architecture">von-Neumann computer architectures</a>. This limits the speed and scalability of these systems. A recent development is the use of data-parallel algorithms on Graphics Processing Units <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPU" title="GPU">GPUs</a> for ABM simulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> The extreme memory bandwidth combined with the sheer number crunching power of multi-processor GPUs has enabled simulation of millions of agents at tens of frames per second.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Verification_and_validation">Verification and validation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Agent-based_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Verification and validation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Verification_and_validation" title="Verification and validation">Verification and validation</a> (V&amp;V) of simulation models is extremely important.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> Verification involves the model being debugged to ensure it works correctly, whereas validation ensures that the right model has been built. Face validation, sensitivity analysis, calibration and statistical validation have also been demonstrated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> A discrete-event simulation framework approach for the validation of agent-based systems has been proposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> A comprehensive resource on empirical validation of agent-based models can be found here.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>As an example of V&amp;V technique, consider VOMAS (virtual overlay multi-agent system),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> a software engineering based approach, where a virtual overlay multi-agent system is developed alongside the agent-based model. The agents in the multi-agent system are able to gather data by generation of logs as well as provide run-time validation and verification support by watch agents and also agents to check any violation of invariants at run-time. These are set by the Simulation Specialist with help from the SME (<a href="/wiki/Subject-matter_expert" title="Subject-matter expert">subject-matter expert</a>). Muazi et al. also provide an example of using VOMAS for verification and validation of a forest fire simulation model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>VOMAS provides a formal way of validation and verification. To develop a VOMAS, one must design VOMAS agents along with the agents in the actual simulation, preferably from the start. In essence, by the time the simulation model is complete, one can essentially consider it to be one model containing two models:</p>
<ol>
<li>An agent-based model of the intended system</li>
<li>An agent-based model of the VOMAS</li>
</ol>
<p>Unlike all previous work on verification and validation, VOMAS agents ensure that the simulations are validated in-simulation i.e. even during execution. In case of any exceptional situations, which are programmed on the directive of the Simulation Specialist (SS), the VOMAS agents can report them. In addition, the VOMAS agents can be used to log key events for the sake of debugging and subsequent analysis of simulations. In other words, VOMAS allows for a flexible use of any given technique for the sake of verification and validation of an agent-based model in any domain.</p>
<p>Details of validated agent-based modeling using VOMAS along with several case studies are given in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> This thesis also gives details of "exploratory agent-based modeling", "descriptive agent-based modeling" and "validated agent-based modeling", using several worked case study examples.</p>
