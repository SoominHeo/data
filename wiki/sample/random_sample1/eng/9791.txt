<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Eikþyrnir</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Eikþyrnir</b> (<a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> "oak-thorny"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ORCHARD36_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ORCHARD36-1">[1]</a></sup>), or <b>Eikthyrnir</b>, is a stag which stands upon <a href="/wiki/Valhalla" title="Valhalla">Valhalla</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>. The following is related in the <a href="/wiki/Gylfaginning" title="Gylfaginning">Gylfaginning</a> section of <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">Snorri Sturluson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Prose_Edda" title="Prose Edda">Prose Edda</a></i> after the description of <a href="/wiki/Hei%C3%B0r%C3%BAn" title="Heiðrún">Heiðrún</a>.</p>
<table>
<tr>
<td>
<p><i>Enn er meira mark at of hjörtinn Eirþyrni, [sic]<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> er stendr á Valhöll ok bítr af limum þess trés, en af hornum &lt;hans&gt; verðr svá mikill dropi at niðr kemr í Hvergelmi, en þaðan af falla ár þær er svá heita: Síð, Víð, Sekin, Ekin, Svöl, Gunnþró, Fjörm, Fimbulþul, Gipul, Göpul, Gömul, Geirvimul, þessar falla um ásabygðir. Þessar eru enn nefndar: Þyn, Vin, Þöll, Böll, Gráð, Gunnþráin, Nyt, Nöt, Nönn, Hrönn, Vína, Veg, Svinn, Þjóðnuma.</i> — (ch. 39; <a class="external text" href="http://www.hi.is/~eybjorn/gg/gg4par13.html" rel="nofollow">Normalized text of the R manuscript</a>)</p>
</td>
<td>
<p>Even more worthy of note is the hart Eikthyrni, which stands in Valhall and bites from the limbs of the tree; and from his horns distils such abundant exudation that it comes down into <a href="/wiki/Hvergelmir" title="Hvergelmir">Hvergelmir</a>, and from thence fall those rivers called thus: Síd, Víd, Søkin, Eikin, Svöl, Gunnthrá, Fjörm, Fimbulthul, Gípul, Göpul, Gömul, Geirvimul. Those fall about the abodes of the <a href="/wiki/%C3%86sir" title="Æsir">Æsir</a>; these also are recorded: Thyn, Vín, Thöll, Höll, Grád, Gunnthráin, Nyt, Nöt, Nönn, Hrönn, Vína, Vegsvinn, Thjódnuma. — <i><a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=_T1cAAAAMAAJ&amp;pg=PA268&amp;dq=Brodeur,+Prose+Edda&amp;hl=en&amp;sa=X&amp;ei=tGVGT96yEonZiAKK6bDbDQ&amp;ved=0CEkQ6AEwBA#v=onepage&amp;q=Even%20more%20worthy%20of%20note%20is%20the%20hart%20Eikthyrni%2C%20which%20stands%20in%20Valhall&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">Brodeur's translation</a></i></p>
</td>
<td></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arthur_Gilchrist_Brodeur" title="Arthur Gilchrist Brodeur">Brodeur</a> follows the text of the T manuscript of the <i>Prose Edda</i> in putting the stag <i>í Valhöll</i>, "in Valhall", rather than <i>á Valhöll</i>, "upon Valhall", as the other manuscripts do. The more recent translation by Anthony Faulkes puts the stag on top of the building, which seems much more natural from the context and weight of the evidence.</p>
<p>Snorri's source for this information was almost certainly <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%ADmnism%C3%A1l" title="Grímnismál">Grímnismál</a>, where the following strophes are found.</p>
<table>
<tr>
<td>
<dl>
<dd><i>Eikþyrnir heitir hiörtr,</i></dd>
<dd><i>er stendr á höllo Heriaföðrs</i></dd>
<dd><i>ok bítr af Læraðs limom;</i></dd>
<dd><i>en af hans hornom</i></dd>
<dd><i>drýpr i Hvergelmi,</i></dd>
<dd><i>þaðan eigo vötn öll vega:</i></dd>
</dl>
<p><br/></p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Síð ok Víð,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Sækin ok Eikin,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Svöl ok Gunnþró,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Fiörm ok Fimbulþul,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Rín ok Rennandi,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Gipul ok Göpul,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Gömul ok Geirvimul,</i></dd>
<dd><i>þær hverfa um hodd goða,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Þyn ok Vin,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Þöll ok Höll,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Gráð ok Gunnþorin.</i></dd>
</dl>
<p><br/></p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Vína heitir enn,</i></dd>
<dd><i>önnor Vegsvinn,</i></dd>
<dd><i>þriðia Þióðnuma,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Nyt ok Nöt,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Nönn ok Hrönn,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Slíð ok Hrið,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Sylgr ok Ylgr,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Víð ok Ván,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Vönd ok Strönd,</i></dd>
<dd><i>Giöll ok Leiptr,</i></dd>
<dd><i>þær falla gumnom nær,</i></dd>
<dd><i>en falla til heilar heðan. — <a class="external text" href="http://www.snerpa.is/net/kvaedi/grimnir.htm" rel="nofollow">Jón Helgason's edition</a></i></dd>
</dl>
</td>
<td>
<dl>
<dd>Eikthyrnir the hart is called,</dd>
<dd>that stands o’er <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a>’s hall,</dd>
<dd>and bits from Lærad’s branches;</dd>
<dd>from his horns fall</dd>
<dd>drops into Hvergelmir,</dd>
<dd>whence all waters rise:-</dd>
</dl>
<p><br/></p>
<dl>
<dd>Sid and Vid,</dd>
<dd>Soekin and Eikin,</dd>
<dd>Svöl and Gunntro,</dd>
<dd>Fiörm and Fimbulthul,</dd>
<dd>Rin and Rennandi,</dd>
<dd>Gipul and Göpul,</dd>
<dd>Gömul and Geirvimul:</dd>
<dd>they round the gods’ dwellings wind.</dd>
<dd>Thyn and Vin,</dd>
<dd>Thöll and Höll,</dd>
<dd>Grad and Gunnthorin.</dd>
</dl>
<p><br/></p>
<dl>
<dd>Vina one is called,</dd>
<dd>a second Vegsvin,</dd>
<dd>a third Thiodnuma;</dd>
<dd>Nyt and Nöt,</dd>
<dd>Nön and Hrön,</dd>
<dd>Slid and Hrid,</dd>
<dd>Sylg and Ylg,</dd>
<dd>Vid and Van,</dd>
<dd>Vönd and Strönd,</dd>
<dd>Giöll and Leipt;</dd>
<dd>these (two) fall near to men,</dd>
<dd>but fall hence to Hel. — <i><a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=s_kqAAAAMAAJ&amp;pg=PR8&amp;dq=Thorpe,+Poetic+Edda&amp;hl=en&amp;sa=X&amp;ei=7WxGT_nlKMGbiAKJt_HbDQ&amp;ved=0CEIQ6AEwAw#v=onepage&amp;q=Vina%20one%20is%20called%2C%20a%20second%20Vegsvin%2C%20a%20third%20Thiodnuma&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">Thorpe's translation</a></i></dd>
</dl>
</td>
<td></td>
</tr>
</table>
