<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eileithyia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to some authors, her name does not have an <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> etymology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> which for R.¬†F. Willets<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> strengthens her link to Minoan culture. "The links between Eileithyia, an earlier Minoan goddess, and a still earlier <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> prototype are, relatively, firm," he wrote. "The continuity of her cult depends upon the unchanging concept of her function. Eileithyia was the goddess of childbirth; and the divine helper of women in labour has an obvious origin in the human midwife." Additionally, for Willets, Cretan dialect 'Eleuthia' would connect Eileithyia (or perhaps the goddess "<a href="/wiki/Eleutheria" title="Eleutheria">Eleutheria</a>") to <a href="/wiki/Eleusis" title="Eleusis">Eleusis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>19th-century scholars suggested that the name is Greek, from the verb <i>eleutho</i> (·ºêŒªŒµœçŒ∏œâ), "to bring," the goddess thus being <i>The Bringer</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The variants <i>Eleuthia</i> (Cretan) and <i>Eleuth≈ç</i> (used by <a href="/wiki/Pindar" title="Pindar">Pindar</a>) suggest a possible connection with "<a href="/wiki/Eleutheria" title="Eleutheria">Eleutheria</a>" (freedom). The earliest form of the name is the <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greek" title="Mycenaean Greek">Mycenaean Greek</a> <span lang="gmy" xml:lang="gmy">êÄÅêÄ©êÄÑêÄ¥êÄä</span>, <i>e-re-u-ti-ja</i>, written in the <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a> syllabic script.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Ilithyia is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latinisation_(literature)" title="Latinisation (literature)">latinisation</a> of <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Œï·º∞ŒªŒµŒØŒ∏œÖŒπŒ±</span>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eileithyia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>, she is "the goddess of childbirth".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i> pictures Eileithyia alone, or sometimes multiplied, as the <b>Eileithyiai</b>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>And even as when the sharp dart striketh a woman in travail, [270] the piercing dart that the Eilithyiae, the goddesses of childbirth, send‚Äîeven the daughters of Hera that have in their keeping bitter pangs;</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>‚Äî<i>Iliad</i> 11.269‚Äì272</dd>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a> (c. 700 BC) described Eileithyia as a daughter of Hera by <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> (<i>Theogony</i> 921)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>‚Äîand the <i><a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_(Pseudo-Apollodorus)" title="Bibliotheca (Pseudo-Apollodorus)">Bibliotheca</a></i> (Roman-era) and <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a> (c.¬†90‚Äì27 BC) (5.72.5) agreed. But <a href="/wiki/Pausanias_(geographer)" title="Pausanias (geographer)">Pausanias</a>, writing in the 2nd century AD, reported another early source (now lost): "The <a href="/wiki/Lycia" title="Lycia">Lycian</a> <a href="/wiki/Olen_(poet)" title="Olen (poet)">Olen</a>, an earlier poet, who composed for the <a href="/wiki/Delos" title="Delos">Delians</a>, among other hymns, one to Eileithyia, styles her '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weaving_(mythology)" title="Weaving (mythology)">the clever spinner</a>', clearly identifying her with <a href="/wiki/Moirai" title="Moirai"><i>Fate</i></a>, and makes her older than <a href="/wiki/Cronus" title="Cronus">Cronus</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Being the youngest born to <a href="/wiki/Gaia_(mythology)" title="Gaia (mythology)">Gaia</a>, Cronus was a Titan of the first generation and he was identified as the father of Zeus. Likewise, the meticulously accurate mythographer <a href="/wiki/Pindar" title="Pindar">Pindar</a> (522‚Äì443 BC) also makes no mention of Zeus:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>Goddess of childbirth, Eileithyia, maid to the throne of the deep-thinking <a href="/wiki/Moirai" title="Moirai">Moirai</a>, child of all-powerful <a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">Hera</a>, hear my song.</i></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>‚ÄîSeventh Nemean Ode.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Later, for the Classical Greeks, "She is closely associated with <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">Artemis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">Hera</a>," Burkert asserts (1985, p 1761), "but develops no character of her own". In the <a href="/wiki/Orpheus" title="Orpheus">Orphic Hymn</a> to Prothyraeia, the association of a goddess of childbirth as an epithet of virginal Artemis, making the death-dealing huntress also "she who comes to the aid of women in childbirth," (Graves 1955 15.a.1), would be inexplicable in purely Olympian terms:</p>
<dl>
<dd><i>When racked with labour pangs, and sore distressed</i></dd>
<dd><i>the sex invoke thee, as the soul's sure rest;</i></dd>
<dd><i>for thou Eileithyia alone canst give relief to pain,</i></dd>
<dd><i>which art attempts to ease, but tries in vain.</i></dd>
<dd><i>Artemis Eileithyia, venerable power,</i></dd>
<dd><i>who bringest relief in labour's dreadful hour.</i></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>‚ÄîOrphic Hymn 2, to Prothyraeia, as translated by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Taylor_(neoplatonist)" title="Thomas Taylor (neoplatonist)">Thomas Taylor</a>, 1792.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Thus <a href="/wiki/Claudius_Aelianus" title="Claudius Aelianus">Aelian</a> in the 3rd century AD could refer to "Artemis of the child-bed" (<i>On Animals</i> 7.15).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Beauty_of_Durr%C3%ABs" title="The Beauty of Durr√´s">The Beauty of Durr√´s</a>, a large 4th-century BC mosaic showing the head figure of a woman, probably portrays the goddess Eileithyia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Vase-painters, when illustrating the birth of <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a> from Zeus' head, may show two assisting Eileithyiai, with their hands raised in the <a href="/wiki/Epiphany_(feeling)" title="Epiphany (feeling)">epiphany</a> gesture.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cult_at_Amnisos">Cult at Amnisos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eileithyia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cult at Amnisos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eileithyia_Cave" title="Eileithyia Cave">cave of Eileithyia</a> near <a href="/wiki/Amnisos" title="Amnisos">Amnisos</a>, the harbor of <a href="/wiki/Knossos" title="Knossos">Knossos</a>, mentioned in the <i><a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a></i> (xix.198) in connection with her cult, was accounted the birthplace of Eileithyia. The Cretan cave has stalactites suggestive of the goddess' double form (Kerenyi 1976 fig. 6), of bringing labor on and of delaying it, and votive offerings to her have been found establishing the continuity of her cult from Neolithic times, with a revival as late as the Roman period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Here she was probably being worshipped before Zeus arrived in the Aegean, but certainly in Minoan‚ÄìMycenaean times (Burkert 1985 p 171; Nilsson 1950:53). The goddess is mentioned as <b>Eleuthia</b> in a <a href="/wiki/Linear_B" title="Linear B">Linear B</a> fragment from Knossos. In classical times, there were shrines to Eileithyia in the Cretan cities of <a href="/wiki/Lato" title="Lato">Lato</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eleutherna" title="Eleutherna">Eleutherna</a> and a sacred cave at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inatos" title="Inatos">Inatos</a>.</p>
<p>On the Greek mainland, at <a href="/wiki/Olympia,_Greece" title="Olympia, Greece">Olympia</a>, an archaic shrine with an inner <a href="/wiki/Cella" title="Cella">cella</a> sacred to the serpent-savior of the city (<i>Sosipolis</i>) and to Eileithyia was seen by the traveller Pausanias in the 2nd century AD (<i>Greece</i> vi.20.1‚Äì3); in it, a virgin-priestess cared for a <a href="/wiki/Serpent_(symbolism)" title="Serpent (symbolism)">serpent</a> that was "fed" on honeyed barley-cakes and water‚Äîan offering suited to Demeter. The shrine memorialized the appearance of a crone with a babe in arms, at a crucial moment when <a href="/wiki/Elis" title="Elis">Elians</a> were threatened by forces from <a href="/wiki/Arcadia" title="Arcadia">Arcadia</a>. The child, placed on the ground between the contending forces, changed into a serpent, driving the Arcadians away in flight, before it disappeared into the hill.</p>
<p>There were ancient icons of Eileithyia at Athens, one said to have been brought from Crete, according to Pausanias, who mentioned shrines to Eileithyia in <a href="/wiki/Tegea" title="Tegea">Tegea</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Argos" title="Argos">Argos</a>, with an extremely important shrine in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aigion" title="Aigion">Aigion</a>. Eileithyia, along with Artemis and <a href="/wiki/Persephone" title="Persephone">Persephone</a>, is often shown carrying torches to bring children out of darkness and into light: in <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman mythology</a> her counterpart in easing labor is <a href="/wiki/Lucina_(goddess)" title="Lucina (goddess)">Lucina</a> ("of the light").</p>
<p>In Greek shrines, small terracotta votive figures (<i><a href="/wiki/Kourotrophos" title="Kourotrophos">kourotrophos</a></i>) depicted an immortal nurse who took care of divine infants, who may be connected with Eileithyia. According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeric_Hymn" title="Homeric Hymn">Homeric Hymn</a> III to Delian Apollo, Hera detained Eileithyia, who was coming from the <a href="/wiki/Hyperborea" title="Hyperborea">Hyperboreans</a> in the far north, to prevent <a href="/wiki/Leto" title="Leto">Leto</a> from going into labor with Artemis and Apollo, because the father was Zeus. Hera was very jealous of Zeus's relations with others and went out of her way to make the women suffer. The other goddesses present at the birthing on Delos sent <a href="/wiki/Iris_(mythology)" title="Iris (mythology)">Iris</a> to bring her. As she stepped upon the island, the birth began.</p>
<p>Eileithyia was especially worshipped in Crete, in the cities Lato and doubtless, from its etymological link, Eleutherna<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, though no archaeological find has identified her there. Caves were sacred to her: the inescapable association to the birth canal cannot be proved beyond a skeptic's doubt. Her Egyptian counterpart is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tawaret" title="Tawaret">Tawaret</a>.</p>
