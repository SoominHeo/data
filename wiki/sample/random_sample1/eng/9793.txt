<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Eilenberg–Steenrod axioms</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, specifically in <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_topology" title="Algebraic topology">algebraic topology</a>, the <b>Eilenberg–Steenrod axioms</b> are properties that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homology_theory" title="Homology theory">homology theories</a> of <a href="/wiki/Topological_space" title="Topological space">topological spaces</a> have in common. The quintessential example of a homology theory satisfying the axioms is <a href="/wiki/Singular_homology" title="Singular homology">singular homology</a>, developed by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Eilenberg" title="Samuel Eilenberg">Samuel Eilenberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norman_Steenrod" title="Norman Steenrod">Norman Steenrod</a>.</p>
<p>One can define a homology theory as a <a href="/wiki/Sequence" title="Sequence">sequence</a> of <a href="/wiki/Functor" title="Functor">functors</a> satisfying the Eilenberg–Steenrod axioms. The axiomatic approach, which was developed in 1945, allows one to prove results, such as the <a href="/wiki/Mayer%E2%80%93Vietoris_sequence" title="Mayer–Vietoris sequence">Mayer–Vietoris sequence</a>, that are common to all homology theories satisfying the axioms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>If one omits the dimension axiom (described below), then the remaining axioms define what is called an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraordinary_homology_theory" title="Extraordinary homology theory">extraordinary homology theory</a>. Extraordinary cohomology theories first arose in <a href="/wiki/K-theory" title="K-theory">K-theory</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobordism_theory" title="Cobordism theory">cobordism</a>.</p>

<p>If <i>P</i> is the one point space then <i>H</i><sub>0</sub>(<i>P</i>) is called the <b>coefficient group</b>. For example, singular homology (taken with integer coefficients, as is most common) has as coefficients the integers.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences">Consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eilenberg%E2%80%93Steenrod_axioms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some facts about homology groups can be derived directly from the axioms, such as the fact that homotopically equivalent spaces have isomorphic homology groups.</p>
<p>The homology of some relatively simple spaces, such as <i>n</i>-<a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">spheres</a>, can be calculated directly from the axioms. From this it can be easily shown that the (<i>n</i> − 1)-sphere is not a <a href="/wiki/Retract" title="Retract">retract</a> of the <i>n</i>-disk. This is used in a proof of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brouwer_fixed_point_theorem" title="Brouwer fixed point theorem">Brouwer fixed point theorem</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dimension_axiom">Dimension axiom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eilenberg%E2%80%93Steenrod_axioms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dimension axiom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A "homology-like" theory satisfying all of the Eilenberg–Steenrod axioms except the dimension axiom is called an <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraordinary_homology_theory" title="Extraordinary homology theory">extraordinary homology theory</a></b> (dually, <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraordinary_cohomology_theory" title="Extraordinary cohomology theory">extraordinary cohomology theory</a></b>). Important examples of these were found in the 1950s, such as <a href="/wiki/Topological_K-theory" title="Topological K-theory">topological K-theory</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobordism_theory" title="Cobordism theory">cobordism theory</a>, which are extraordinary <i>co</i>homology theories, and come with homology theories dual to them.</p>
