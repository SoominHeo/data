<p><b>Aileen Carol Wuornos</b> (February 29, 1956 – October 9, 2002) was an American <a href="/wiki/Serial_killer" title="Serial killer">serial killer</a> who killed seven men in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> between 1989 and 1990 by shooting them at point-blank range. Wuornos claimed that her victims had either <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">raped</a> or attempted to rape her while she was working as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prostitute" title="Prostitute">prostitute</a>, and that all of the homicides were committed in <a href="/wiki/Right_of_self-defense" title="Right of self-defense">self-defense</a>. She was convicted and <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">sentenced to death six times</a> for the murders and was executed by <a href="/wiki/Lethal_injection" title="Lethal injection">lethal injection</a> on October 9, 2002.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aileen_Wuornos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood">Childhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aileen_Wuornos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Childhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wuornos was born <b>Aileen Carol Pittman</b> in <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_Michigan" title="Rochester, Michigan">Rochester, Michigan</a>, on February 29, 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trutv.com_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-trutv.com-2">[2]</a></sup> Her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finnish-American" title="Finnish-American">Finnish-American</a> mother, Diane Wuornos (born 1939), was 14 years old when she married Aileen's father, Leo Dale Pittman (1937–1969), on June 3, 1954. Less than two years later, and two months before Aileen was born, Diane filed for divorce. Aileen's older brother Keith was born on March 14, 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wuornos never met her father; he was incarcerated at the time of her birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trutv.com_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-trutv.com-2">[2]</a></sup> Leo Dale Pittman was diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a>, later convicted of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex_crime" title="Sex crime">sex crimes</a> against children,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvio_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silvio-4">[4]</a></sup> and eventually hanged himself in prison on January 30, 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Howard-5">[5]</a></sup> In January 1960, when Wuornos was almost four years old, Diane abandoned her children, leaving them with their maternal grandparents, Lauri and Britta Wuornos, who legally adopted Keith and Aileen on March 18, 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Howard-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>By the age of 11, Wuornos began engaging in sexual activities in school in exchange for cigarettes, drugs, and food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> She had also engaged in sexual activities with her brother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvio_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Silvio-4">[4]</a></sup> Wuornos claimed that her alcoholic grandfather had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_assaulted" title="Sexually assaulted">sexually assaulted</a> and beaten her when she was a child; before beating her, he would force her to strip out of her clothes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvio_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Silvio-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1970, at age 14, she became pregnant,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> having been raped by an accomplice of her grandfather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvio_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Silvio-4">[4]</a></sup> Wuornos gave birth to a boy at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_for_unwed_mothers" title="Home for unwed mothers">home for unwed mothers</a> on March 23, 1971, and the child was placed for adoption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Howard-5">[5]</a></sup> A few months after her baby was born, she dropped out of school<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvio_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Silvio-4">[4]</a></sup> at about the time that her grandmother died of liver failure. When Wuornos was 15, her grandfather threw her out of the house, and she began supporting herself as a prostitute and living in the woods near her old home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Howard-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_criminal_activity">Early criminal activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aileen_Wuornos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early criminal activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 27, 1974, at age 18, Wuornos was arrested in <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_County,_Colorado" title="Jefferson County, Colorado">Jefferson County, Colorado</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Driving_under_the_influence" title="Driving under the influence">driving under the influence</a> (DUI), <a href="/wiki/Disorderly_conduct" title="Disorderly conduct">disorderly conduct</a>, and firing a .22-caliber pistol from a moving vehicle. She was later charged with <a href="/wiki/Failure_to_appear" title="Failure to appear">failure to appear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003116_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003116-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1976, Wuornos hitchhiked to <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, where she met 69-year-old yacht club president Lewis Gratz Fell. They married that same year, and the announcement of their nuptials was printed in the local newspaper's society pages. However, Wuornos continually involved herself in confrontations at their local bar and eventually went to jail for assault. She also hit Fell with his own cane, leading him to get a <a href="/wiki/Restraining_order" title="Restraining order">restraining order</a> against her. She returned to Michigan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> where, on July 14, 1976, she was arrested in <a href="/wiki/Antrim_County,_Michigan" title="Antrim County, Michigan">Antrim County, Michigan</a>, and charged with assault and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disturbing_the_peace" title="Disturbing the peace">disturbing the peace</a> for throwing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cue_ball" title="Cue ball">cue ball</a> at a bartender's head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003116.E2.80.93117_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003116.E2.80.93117-11">[11]</a></sup> On July 17, her brother Keith died of <a href="/wiki/Esophageal_cancer" title="Esophageal cancer">esophageal cancer</a> and Wuornos received $10,000 from his <a href="/wiki/Life_insurance" title="Life insurance">life insurance</a>. Wuornos and Fell annulled their marriage on July 21 after only nine weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In August 1976, Wuornos was given a $105 fine for drunk driving. She used her inheritance money that she had received from her late brother Keith the previous month to pay the fine and spent the rest of her inheritance money within two months by using it to buy luxuries including a new car, which she wrecked shortly afterwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 20, 1981, Wuornos was arrested in <a href="/wiki/Edgewater,_Volusia_County,_Florida" title="Edgewater, Volusia County, Florida">Edgewater, Florida</a>, for the armed robbery of a convenience store, where she stole $35 and two packs of cigarettes. She was sentenced to prison on May 4, 1982, and released on June 30, 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003117_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003117-14">[14]</a></sup> On May 1, 1984, Wuornos was arrested for attempting to pass forged checks at a bank in <a href="/wiki/Key_West,_Florida" title="Key West, Florida">Key West</a>. On November 30, 1985, she was named as a suspect in the theft of a revolver and ammunition in <a href="/wiki/Pasco_County,_Florida" title="Pasco County, Florida">Pasco County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003117_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003117-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 4, 1986, Wuornos was arrested in <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a> and charged with car theft, resisting arrest, and <a href="/wiki/Obstruction_of_justice" title="Obstruction of justice">obstruction of justice</a> for providing identification bearing her aunt's name. Miami police officers found a .38-caliber revolver and a box of ammunition in the stolen car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003117.E2.80.93118_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003117.E2.80.93118-15">[15]</a></sup> On June 2, 1986, <a href="/wiki/Volusia_County,_Florida" title="Volusia County, Florida">Volusia County, Florida</a>, deputy sheriffs detained her for questioning after a male companion accused her of pulling a gun in his car and demanding $200. Wuornos was found to be carrying spare ammunition, and police discovered a .22 pistol under the passenger seat she had occupied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003118_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003118-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>Around this time, Wuornos met Tyria Moore, a hotel maid, at a <a href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach,_Florida" title="Daytona Beach, Florida">Daytona</a> <a href="/wiki/Gay_bar" title="Gay bar">gay bar</a>. They moved in together, and Wuornos supported them with her prostitution earnings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> On July 4, 1987, <a href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach,_Florida" title="Daytona Beach, Florida">Daytona Beach</a> police detained Wuornos and Moore at a bar for questioning regarding an incident in which they were accused of assault and battery with a beer bottle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003119_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003119-18">[18]</a></sup> On March 12, 1988, Wuornos accused a Daytona Beach bus driver of assault. She claimed that he pushed her off the bus following a confrontation. Moore was listed as a witness to the incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003119_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds2003119-18">[18]</a></sup> Up until her execution, Wuornos claimed to still be in love with Moore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Murders">Murders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aileen_Wuornos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Murders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Richard Mallory, age 51, November 30, 1989—Electronics store owner in <a href="/wiki/Clearwater,_Florida" title="Clearwater, Florida">Clearwater, Florida</a>. Wuornos' first victim was a convicted rapist whom she claimed to have killed in <a href="/wiki/Self-defense" title="Self-defense">self-defense</a>. Two days later, a Volusia County, Florida, deputy sheriff found Mallory's abandoned vehicle. On December 13, Mallory's body was found several miles away in a wooded area; he had been shot several times, two bullets to the left lung were found to have been the cause of death. It was on this murder that Wuornos was initially condemned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>David Spears, age 43—Construction worker in <a href="/wiki/Winter_Garden,_Florida" title="Winter Garden, Florida">Winter Garden, Florida</a>. On June 1, 1990, his nude body was found along Highway 19 in <a href="/wiki/Citrus_County,_Florida" title="Citrus County, Florida">Citrus County, Florida</a>. He had been shot six times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>Charles Carskaddon, age 40, May 31, 1990—Part-time rodeo worker. On June 6, 1990, his body was found in <a href="/wiki/Pasco_County,_Florida" title="Pasco County, Florida">Pasco County, Florida</a>. He had been shot nine times with a small-caliber weapon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>Peter Siems, age 65—retired merchant seaman who devoted much of his time to a Christian outreach ministry. In June 1990, Siems left <a href="/wiki/Jupiter,_Florida" title="Jupiter, Florida">Jupiter, Florida</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>. On July 4, 1990, his car was found in <a href="/wiki/Orange_Springs,_Florida" title="Orange Springs, Florida">Orange Springs, Florida</a>. Moore and Wuornos were seen abandoning the car, and Wuornos' palm print was found on the interior door handle. His body was never found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>Troy Burress, age 50—Sausage salesman from <a href="/wiki/Ocala,_Florida" title="Ocala, Florida">Ocala, Florida</a>. On July 31, 1990, he was reported missing. On August 4, 1990, his body was found in a wooded area along State Road 19 in <a href="/wiki/Marion_County,_Florida" title="Marion County, Florida">Marion County, Florida</a>. He had been shot twice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>Charles "Dick" Humphreys, age 56, September 11, 1990—Retired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> <a href="/wiki/Major" title="Major">Major</a>, former State <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Child_Abuse" title="Child Abuse">Child Abuse</a> Investigator, and former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_of_Police" title="Chief of Police">Chief of Police</a>. On September 12, 1990, his body was found in Marion County, Florida. He was fully clothed and had been shot six times in the head and torso. His car was found in <a href="/wiki/Suwannee_County,_Florida" title="Suwannee County, Florida">Suwannee County, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>Walter Jeno Antonio, age 62—Trucker, security guard, and police reservist. On November 19, 1990, Antonio's nearly nude body was found near a remote logging road in <a href="/wiki/Dixie_County,_Florida" title="Dixie County, Florida">Dixie County, Florida</a>. He had been shot four times. Five days later, his car was found in <a href="/wiki/Brevard_County,_Florida" title="Brevard County, Florida">Brevard County, Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Justice_system">Justice system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aileen_Wuornos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Justice system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apprehension_and_sentencing">Apprehension and sentencing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aileen_Wuornos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Apprehension and sentencing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On July 4, 1990, Wuornos and Moore abandoned Peter Siems's car after they were involved in an accident. Witnesses who had seen the women driving the victims' cars provided police with their names and descriptions, resulting in a media campaign to locate them. Police also found some of the victims' belongings in pawn shops and retrieved fingerprints matching those found in the victims' cars. Wuornos had a criminal record in Florida, and her fingerprints were on file.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howard_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Howard-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Last_Resort_-_Port_Orange,_FL_(7113274795)_(cropped).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="598" data-file-width="873" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/The_Last_Resort_-_Port_Orange%2C_FL_%287113274795%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/270px-The_Last_Resort_-_Port_Orange%2C_FL_%287113274795%29_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/The_Last_Resort_-_Port_Orange%2C_FL_%287113274795%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/405px-The_Last_Resort_-_Port_Orange%2C_FL_%287113274795%29_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/The_Last_Resort_-_Port_Orange%2C_FL_%287113274795%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/540px-The_Last_Resort_-_Port_Orange%2C_FL_%287113274795%29_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="270"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Last_Resort_-_Port_Orange,_FL_(7113274795)_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>On January 9, 1991, Wuornos was arrested on an outstanding warrant at The Last Resort, a biker bar in Volusia County.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The police located Wuornos' former lover Tyria Moore the next day in <a href="/wiki/Scranton,_Pennsylvania" title="Scranton, Pennsylvania">Scranton, Pennsylvania</a>. She agreed to elicit a confession from Wuornos in exchange for <a href="/wiki/Legal_immunity" title="Legal immunity">immunity from prosecution</a>. Moore returned with the police to Florida, where she was put up in a motel. Under police guidance, she made numerous telephone calls to Wuornos, pleading for help in clearing her name. Three days later, on January 16, 1991, Wuornos confessed to the murders. She claimed the men had tried to rape her and she killed them in self-defense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeline_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 14, 1992, Wuornos went to trial for the murder of Richard Mallory; although previous convictions are normally inadmissible in criminal trials, under Florida's <a href="/wiki/Williams_Rule" title="Williams Rule">Williams Rule</a> the prosecution was allowed to introduce evidence related to her other crimes to show a pattern of illegal activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup> On January 27, 1992, Wuornos was convicted of Mallory's murder with help from Moore's testimony. At her sentencing, psychiatrists for the defense testified that Wuornos was mentally unstable and had been diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Borderline_personality_disorder" title="Borderline personality disorder">borderline personality disorder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antisocial_personality_disorder" title="Antisocial personality disorder">antisocial personality disorder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Four days later, she was sentenced to death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeline_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>On March 31, 1992, Wuornos pleaded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No_contest" title="No contest">no contest</a> to the murders of Dick Humphreys, Troy Burress, and David Spears, saying she wanted to "get right with God".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup> In her statement to the court, she said, in part: "I wanted to confess to you that Richard Mallory did violently rape me as I've told you; but these others did not. [They] only began to start to."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup> On May 15, 1992, Wuornos was given three more death sentences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In June 1992, Wuornos pleaded guilty to the murder of Charles Carskaddon; in November 1992, she received her fifth death sentence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup> The defense made efforts during the trial to introduce evidence that Mallory had been tried for intent to commit rape in Maryland and that he had been committed to a maximum security correctional facility in <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> that provided remediation to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_offender" title="Sexual offender">sexual offenders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-supremecourt_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-supremecourt-25">[25]</a></sup> Records obtained from that institution reflected that, from 1958 to 1962, Mallory was committed for treatment and observation resulting from a criminal charge of assault with intent to rape and received an over-all eight years of treatment from the facility. In 1961, "it was observed of Mr. Mallory that he possessed strong sociopathic trends".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-supremecourt_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-supremecourt-25">[25]</a></sup> The judge refused to allow this to be admitted in court as evidence and denied Wuornos' request for a retrial.</p>
<p>In February 1993, Wuornos pleaded guilty to the murder of Walter Jeno Antonio and was sentenced to death again. No charges were brought against her for the murder of Peter Siems, as his body was never found. In all, she received six death sentences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wuornos told several inconsistent stories about the killings. She claimed initially that all seven men had raped her while she was working as a prostitute but later recanted the claim of self-defense, citing robbery and a desire to leave no witnesses as the reason for murder. During an interview with filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Nick_Broomfield" title="Nick Broomfield">Nick Broomfield</a>, when she thought the cameras were off, she told him that it was, in fact, self-defense, but she could not stand being on <a href="/wiki/Death_row" title="Death row">death row</a>—where she had been for 10 years at that point—and wanted to die.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aileenfilm_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-aileenfilm-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Assessed using the <a href="/wiki/Psychopathy_Checklist" title="Psychopathy Checklist">Psychopathy Checklist</a>, Wuornos scored 32/40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The checklist evaluates individuals on a 20-item list of antisocial and interpersonal behaviors, with each item being scored at zero, 1 or 2 and thus a maximum score of 40. Depending on location and research perspective, scores above 25 or 30 are consistent with a diagnosis of <a href="/wiki/Psychopathy" title="Psychopathy">psychopathy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Semple_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Semple-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gap_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-gap-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Execution">Execution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aileen_Wuornos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Execution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wuornos was incarcerated at the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Department_of_Corrections" title="Florida Department of Corrections">Florida Department of Corrections</a> <a href="/wiki/Broward_Correctional_Institution" title="Broward Correctional Institution">Broward Correctional Institution</a> (BCI) death row for women, then transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Florida_State_Prison" title="Florida State Prison">Florida State Prison</a> for execution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trischittaetal_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trischittaetal-30">[30]</a></sup> Her appeal to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Supreme_Court" title="U.S. Supreme Court">U.S. Supreme Court</a> was denied in 1996. In a 2001 petition to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_Supreme_Court" title="Florida Supreme Court">Florida Supreme Court</a> she stated her intention to dismiss her legal counsel and terminate all pending appeals. "I killed those men," she wrote, "robbed them as cold as ice. And I'd do it again, too. There's no chance in keeping me alive or anything, because I'd kill again. I have hate crawling through my system...I am so sick of hearing this 'she's crazy' stuff. I've been evaluated so many times. I'm competent, sane, and I'm trying to tell the truth. I'm one who seriously hates human life and would kill again."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> While her attorneys argued that she was not mentally competent to make such a request, Wuornos insisted that she knew what she was doing, and a court-appointed panel of psychiatrists agreed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002, Wuornos began accusing prison matrons of tainting her food with saliva, dirt, and urine. She said she had overheard conversations among prison personnel "trying to get me so pushed over the brink by them I'd wind up committing suicide before the execution" and "wishing to rape me before execution". She also complained of strip searches, tight handcuffing, door kicking, frequent window checks, low water pressure, mildew on her mattress, and "cat calling ... in distaste and a pure hatred towards me". Wuornos threatened to boycott showers and food trays when certain officers were on duty. "In the meantime, my stomach's growling away and I'm taking showers through the sink of my cell." Her attorney stated that "Ms. Wuornos really just wants to have proper treatment, humane treatment until the day she's executed." He added, "She believes what she's written."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the weeks before her execution Wuornos gave a series of interviews to Broomfield. She depicts, "being taken away to meet God and Jesus and the angels and whatever is beyond the beyond".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Broomfield_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Broomfield-34">[34]</a></sup> In her final interview she once again charged that her mind was "tortured" at BCI, and her head crushed by "sonic pressure". Food poisonings and other abuses worsened, she said, each time she complained, with the goal of making her appear insane, or to drive her insane. She also turned on her interviewer: "You sabotaged my ass! Society, and the cops, and the system! A raped woman got executed, and was used for books and movies and shit!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Her final on-camera words were "Thanks a lot, society, for railroading my ass."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Dawn Botkins, a childhood friend of Wuornos, later told Broomfield that her verbal abuse was directed at society and the media in general, not at him specifically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>Wuornos's execution took place on October 9, 2002. She died at 9:47 a.m. EDT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> She declined her last meal which could have been anything under $20 and opted for a cup of coffee instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Her last words were, "Yes, I would just like to say I'm sailing with the rock, and I'll be back, like <i>Independence Day</i>, with Jesus. June 6, like the movie. Big mother ship and all, I'll be back, I'll be back."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clarkprosecutor_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-clarkprosecutor-1">[1]</a></sup> She was the tenth woman in the United States and the second in Florida to be executed since the 1976 Supreme Court decision restoring capital punishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_death">After death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aileen_Wuornos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: After death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wuornos's body was cremated, and her ashes were spread beneath a tree in her native Michigan by Dawn Botkins. Wuornos requested that <a href="/wiki/Natalie_Merchant" title="Natalie Merchant">Natalie Merchant</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Carnival_(Natalie_Merchant_song)" title="Carnival (Natalie Merchant song)">Carnival</a>" be played at her funeral. Merchant commented on this when asked why she permitted "Carnival" to be played during the credits of the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Aileen:_Life_and_Death_of_a_Serial_Killer" title="Aileen: Life and Death of a Serial Killer">Aileen: Life and Death of a Serial Killer</a></i>:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>When director Nick Broomfield sent a working edit of the film, I was so disturbed by the subject matter that I couldn't even watch it. Aileen Wuornos led a tortured, torturing life that is beyond my worst nightmares. It wasn't until I was told that Aileen spent many hours listening to my album <i>Tigerlily</i> while on death row and requested "Carnival" be played at her funeral that I gave permission for the use of the song. It's very odd to think of the places my music can go once it leaves my hands. If it gave her some solace, I have to be grateful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Broomfield later speculated on Wuornos' motive and state of mind:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I think this anger developed inside her. And she was working as a prostitute. I think she had a lot of awful encounters on the roads. And I think this anger just spilled out from inside her. And finally exploded. Into incredible violence. That was her way of surviving. I think Aileen really believed that she had killed in self-defense. I think someone who's deeply psychotic can't really tell the difference between something that is life threatening and something that is a minor disagreement, that you could say something that she didn't agree with. She would get into a screaming black temper about it. And I think that's what had caused these things to happen. And at the same time, when she wasn't in those extreme moods, there was an incredible humanity to her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aileen_Wuornos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Books">Books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aileen_Wuornos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">FBI</a> <a href="/wiki/Offender_profiling" title="Offender profiling">profiler</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_K._Ressler" title="Robert K. Ressler">Robert K. Ressler</a> mentioned Wuornos only briefly in his autobiographical history of his 20 years with the FBI. Writing in 1992, he said he often does not discuss female serial killers because they tend to kill in sprees instead of in a sequential fashion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ressler_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-ressler-43">[43]</a></sup> He noted Wuornos as the sole exception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ressler_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-ressler-43">[43]</a></sup> Ressler, who allegedly coined the phrase <a href="/wiki/Serial_killer" title="Serial killer">serial killer</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> to describe murderers seeking personal gratification, does not apply it to women killing in <a href="/wiki/Postpartum_psychosis" title="Postpartum psychosis">postpartum psychosis</a> or to any murderer acting solely for financial gain, such as women who have killed a series of boarders or spouses.</p>
<p>In 2002, journalist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sue_Russell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sue Russell (page does not exist)">Sue Russell</a> wrote a book about Wuornos called <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lethal_Intent&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lethal Intent (page does not exist)">Lethal Intent</a></i>. In 2012, Lisa Kester and Daphne Gottlieb edited and published a collection of letters written over a ten-year span from Wuornos to her childhood friend Dawn Botkins. The book is titled: <i>Dear Dawn: Aileen Wuornos in Her Own Words</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Documentaries">Documentaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aileen_Wuornos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Documentaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Nick_Broomfield" title="Nick Broomfield">Nick Broomfield</a> directed two documentaries about Wuornos:</p>
<ul>

<p>Wuornos was the subject of episodes of the documentary TV series <i><a href="/wiki/American_Justice" title="American Justice">American Justice</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Biography_(TV_series)" title="Biography (TV series)">Biography</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Deadly_Women" title="Deadly Women">Deadly Women</a></i>. She was also featured in an episode of the TV series, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Detectives" title="The New Detectives">The New Detectives</a></i> (season 3, episode 1: "Fatal Compulsion").</p>

<p>The TV movie <i>Overkill: The Aileen Wuornos Story</i> (1992) starred <a href="/wiki/Jean_Smart" title="Jean Smart">Jean Smart</a> as Aileen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>The theatrical film <i><a href="/wiki/Monster_(2003_film)" title="Monster (2003 film)">Monster</a></i> (2003) starred <a href="/wiki/Charlize_Theron" title="Charlize Theron">Charlize Theron</a> as Wuornos. It chronicles Wuornos' story from childhood until her first murder conviction. The film earned Theron an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Actress">Academy Award for Best Actress</a> for playing Wuornos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_media">Other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aileen_Wuornos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An operatic adaptation of Wuornos' life events premiered at <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Yerba_Buena_Center_for_the_Arts" title="Yerba Buena Center for the Arts">Yerba Buena Center for the Arts</a> on June 22, 2001. Entitled <i>Wuornos</i>, the opera was written by <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composer</a>/<a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">librettist</a> <a href="/wiki/Carla_Lucero" title="Carla Lucero">Carla Lucero</a>, conducted by Mary Chun, and produced by the <a href="/wiki/Jon_Sims_Center_for_the_Performing_Arts" title="Jon Sims Center for the Performing Arts">Jon Sims Center for the Performing Arts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several musicians have written songs about Wuornos, including <a href="/wiki/Jewel_(singer)" title="Jewel (singer)">Jewel</a> ("Nicotine Love") and the New York-based metalcore band <a href="/wiki/It_Dies_Today" title="It Dies Today">It Dies Today</a> ("Sixth of June"). The poet Doron Braunshtein dedicated a poem to her, called "Aileen Wuornos", that appears in his 2011 <a href="/wiki/Spoken_word" title="Spoken word">spoken word</a> CD <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Obsessive_Poet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Obsessive Poet (page does not exist)">The Obsessive Poet</a></i>.</p>
<p>The singer <a href="/wiki/Diamanda_Gal%C3%A1s" title="Diamanda Galás">Diamanda Galás</a> recorded a live cover of the <a href="/wiki/Phil_Ochs" title="Phil Ochs">Phil Ochs</a> song "Iron Lady", which she would often perform as a tribute to Wuornos, for her performance album <i><a href="/wiki/Malediction_and_Prayer" title="Malediction and Prayer">Malediction and Prayer</a></i>.</p>
<p>A song by "Dolly's circus" named "Aileen's song" was written and published in 2012.</p>
<p>The poem "Sugar Zero" by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rima_Banerji&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rima Banerji (page does not exist)">Rima Banerji</a> is dedicated to Wuornos and appears in the 2005 <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_Pulp_Press" title="Arsenal Pulp Press">Arsenal Pulp Press</a> publication, <i>Red Light: Superheroes, Saints, and Sluts</i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lily_Rabe" title="Lily Rabe">Lily Rabe</a> portrayed a fictionalised version of Wuornos as part of a Halloween storyline in <i><a href="/wiki/American_Horror_Story:_Hotel" title="American Horror Story: Hotel">American Horror Story: Hotel</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Devil%27s_Night_(American_Horror_Story)" title="Devil's Night (American Horror Story)">fourth episode</a> of the show's fifth season, and later in the season finale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychopathology_model">Psychopathology model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aileen_Wuornos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Psychopathology model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wuornos's crimes have been closely related to the psychopathology model of women who kill and has been related to having a psychopathic personality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Myers_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Myers-52">[52]</a></sup> Using the <a href="/wiki/Psychopathy_Checklist" title="Psychopathy Checklist">Psychopathy Checklist</a>, Wuornos was evidenced to have a psychopathic personality with a PCL-R score of 32<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Myers_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Myers-52">[52]</a></sup> with the cut off score for psychopathy being at 30 in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Semple_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Semple-28">[28]</a></sup> Aileen Wuornos was also known to meet the proper criteria for possessing both <a href="/wiki/Borderline_personality_disorder" title="Borderline personality disorder">borderline personality disorder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antisocial_personality_disorder" title="Antisocial personality disorder">antisocial personality disorder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Myers_52-2"><a href="#cite_note-Myers-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Much of Wuornos's childhood and early career in prostitution are said to have damaged her irrevocably<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arrigo_2004_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Arrigo_2004-53">[53]</a></sup> and it could be seen that traumatic experiences throughout most of her young life could play a part in Wuornos's psychological state, including her departure from her biological mother as well as her grandmother turning a blind eye to the abuse she endured from her grandfather thus leading to the lack of development of a "mother-daughter" bond for Wuornos as a young girl.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arrigo_2004_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Arrigo_2004-53">[53]</a></sup> The damage was then again made worse as both Wuornos and her brother believed that their grandparents were their actual parents, but at age eleven learned this was not the case which furthered the relationship between Wuornos and her adoptive parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arrigo_2004_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-Arrigo_2004-53">[53]</a></sup> Wuornos was also known to have early behavioral problems such as having an explosive temper which limited her ability to make friends as well as making it increasingly harder to maintain relationships.</p>
<p>All this including her traumatic upbringing and physical as well as sexual abuse which was inflicted upon her have been partially linked to her development of a borderline personality disorder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Myers_52-3"><a href="#cite_note-Myers-52">[52]</a></sup> Such severe trauma can also interrupt the structuralization of the mind at a developmental point and result in "primitive, dissociative, and splitting defenses to ward off the intensity of emotional and sexual stimulation that cannot be integrated as a child."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
