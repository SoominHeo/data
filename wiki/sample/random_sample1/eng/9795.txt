<p><b>Ames Research Center</b> (<b>ARC</b>), commonly known as <b>NASA Ames</b>, is a major <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> research center at <a href="/wiki/Moffett_Federal_Airfield" title="Moffett Federal Airfield">Moffett Federal Airfield</a> in California's <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a>. It was founded as the second <a href="/wiki/National_Advisory_Committee_for_Aeronautics" title="National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics">National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics</a> (NACA) laboratory. That agency was dissolved and its assets and personnel transferred to the newly created <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a> (NASA) on October 1, 1958. NASA Ames is named in honor of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Sweetman_Ames" title="Joseph Sweetman Ames">Joseph Sweetman Ames</a>, a physicist and one of the founding members of NACA. At last estimate NASA Ames has over US$3.0 billion in capital equipment, 2,300 research personnel and a US$860 million annual budget.</p>
<p>Ames was founded to conduct wind-tunnel research on the aerodynamics of propeller-driven aircraft; however, its role has expanded to encompass spaceflight and information technology. Ames plays a role in many NASA missions. It provides leadership in <a href="/wiki/Astrobiology" title="Astrobiology">astrobiology</a>; small satellites; robotic lunar exploration; the search for habitable planets; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supercomputing" title="Supercomputing">supercomputing</a>; intelligent/adaptive systems; advanced thermal protection; and airborne astronomy. Ames also develops tools for a safer, more efficient national airspace. The center's current director is Eugene Tu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The site is mission center for several key current missions (<a href="/wiki/Kepler_(spacecraft)" title="Kepler (spacecraft)">Kepler</a>, the <i>Lunar Crater Observation and Sensing Satellite</i> (<a href="/wiki/LCROSS" title="LCROSS">LCROSS</a>) mission, <a href="/wiki/Stratospheric_Observatory_for_Infrared_Astronomy" title="Stratospheric Observatory for Infrared Astronomy">Stratospheric Observatory for Infrared Astronomy</a> (SOFIA), <a href="/wiki/Interface_Region_Imaging_Spectrograph" title="Interface Region Imaging Spectrograph">Interface Region Imaging Spectrograph</a>) and a major contributor to the "new exploration focus"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-_2-0"><a href="#cite_note--2">[2]</a></sup> as a participant in the <a href="/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)" title="Orion (spacecraft)">Orion</a> crew exploration vehicle.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Missions">Missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although Ames is a NASA Research Center, and not a flight center, it has nevertheless been closely involved in a number of astronomy and space missions.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_program" title="Pioneer program">Pioneer program</a>'s eight successful space missions from 1965 to 1978 were managed by Charles Hall at Ames, initially aimed at the inner solar system. By 1972, it supported the bold flyby missions to Jupiter and Saturn with <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_10" title="Pioneer 10">Pioneer 10</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_11" title="Pioneer 11">Pioneer 11</a>. Those two missions were trail blazers (radiation environment, new moons, gravity-assist flybys) for the planners of the more complex <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a> missions, launched five years later. In 1978, the end of the program brought about a return to the inner solar system, with the Pioneer Venus Orbiter and Multiprobe, this time using orbital insertion rather than flyby missions.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">Lunar Prospector</a> was the third mission selected by NASA for full development and construction as part of the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Program" title="Discovery Program">Discovery Program</a>. At a cost of $62.8 million, the 19-month mission was put into a low polar orbit of the Moon, accomplishing mapping of surface composition and possible polar ice deposits, measurements of magnetic and gravity fields, and study of lunar outgassing events. Based on Lunar Prospector Neutron Spectrometer (NS) data, mission scientists have determined that there is indeed <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">water ice</a> in the polar craters of the Moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The mission ended July 31, 1999 when the orbiter was guided to an impact into a crater near the lunar south pole in an (unsuccessful) attempt to analyze lunar polar water by vaporizing it to allow spectroscopic characterization from Earth telescopes.</p>
<p>The 11-pound (5 kg) <a href="/wiki/GeneSat-1" title="GeneSat-1">GeneSat-1</a>, carrying bacteria inside a miniature laboratory, was launched on December 16, 2006. The very small NASA satellite has proven that scientists can quickly design and launch a new class of inexpensive spacecraft—and conduct significant science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Lunar Crater Observation and Sensing Satellite (LCROSS) mission to look for water on the moon was a 'secondary payload spacecraft.' LCROSS began its trip to the moon on the same rocket as the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter (<a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">LRO</a>), which continues to conduct a different lunar task. It launched in April 2009 on an Atlas V rocket from <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a>, Florida.</p>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler_mission" title="Kepler mission">Kepler mission</a> is NASA's first mission capable of finding Earth-size and smaller planets. The Kepler mission will monitor the brightness of stars to find planets that pass in front of them during the planets' orbits. During such passes or 'transits,' the planets will slightly decrease the star's brightness.</p>
<p>Stratospheric Observatory for Infrared Astronomy (SOFIA) is a joint venture of the U.S. and German aerospace agencies, NASA and the <a href="/wiki/German_Aerospace_Center" title="German Aerospace Center">German Aerospace Center</a> (DLR) to make an <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> telescope platform that can fly at altitudes high enough to be in the infrared-transparent regime above the water vapor in the Earth's atmosphere. The aircraft is supplied by the U.S., and the infrared telescope by Germany. Modifications of the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a>SP airframe to accommodate the telescope, mission-unique equipment and large external door were made by L-3 Communications Integrated Systems of <a href="/wiki/Waco,_Texas" title="Waco, Texas">Waco, Texas</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Interface_Region_Imaging_Spectrograph" title="Interface Region Imaging Spectrograph">Interface Region Imaging Spectrograph</a> mission is a partnership with the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory to understand the processes at the boundary between the Sun's <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">chromosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a>. This mission is sponsored by the NASA <a href="/wiki/Small_Explorer_program" title="Small Explorer program">Small Explorer program</a>.</p>
<p>The Lunar Atmosphere Dust Environment Explorer (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LADEE" title="LADEE">LADEE</a>) mission has been developed by NASA Ames. This successfully launched to the Moon on September 6, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, Ames has played a support role in a number of missions, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Pathfinder" title="Mars Pathfinder">Mars Pathfinder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars_Exploration_Rover" title="Mars Exploration Rover">Mars Exploration Rover</a> missions, where the Ames <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_Robotics_Group" title="Intelligent Robotics Group">Intelligent Robotics Laboratory</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> played a key role. NASA Ames was a partner on the Mars <i><a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(spacecraft)" title="Phoenix (spacecraft)">Phoenix</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Mars_Scout_Program" title="Mars Scout Program">Mars Scout Program</a> mission to send a high-latitude lander to Mars, deployed a robotic arm to dig trenches up to 1.6 feet (one half meter) into the layers of water ice and analyzing the soil composition. Ames is also a partner on the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory" title="Mars Science Laboratory">Mars Science Laboratory</a>, a next generation Mars rover to explore for signs of organics and complex molecules.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_traffic_control_automation_research">Air traffic control automation research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Air traffic control automation research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Aviation Systems Division conducts research and development in two primary areas: air traffic management, and high-fidelity flight simulation. For air traffic management, researchers are creating and testing concepts to allow for up to three times today's level of aircraft in the national airspace. Automation and its attendant safety consequences are key foundations of the concept development. Historically, the division has developed products that have been implemented for the flying public, such as the Traffic Management Advisor, which is being deployed nationwide. For high-fidelity flight simulation, the division operates the world's largest flight simulator (the Vertical Motion Simulator), a Level-D 747-400 simulator, and a panoramic air traffic control tower simulator. These simulators have been used for a variety of purposes including continued training for space shuttle pilots, development of future spacecraft handling qualities, helicopter control system testing, Joint Strike Fighter evaluations, and accident investigations. Personnel in the division have a variety of technical backgrounds, including guidance and control, flight mechanics, flight simulation, and computer science. Customers outside NASA have included the FAA, DOD, DHS, DOT, NTSB, Lockheed Martin, and Boeing.</p>
<p>The center's flight simulation and guidance laboratory was listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 2017.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ARC_Unitary_Plan_Wind_Tunnel">ARC Unitary Plan Wind Tunnel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: ARC Unitary Plan Wind Tunnel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unitary_Plan_Wind_Tunnel" title="Unitary Plan Wind Tunnel">Unitary Plan Wind Tunnel</a></div>
<p>The Unitary Plan Wind Tunnel (UPWT) was completed in 1956 at a cost of $27 million under the Unitary Plan Act of 1949. Since its completion, the UPWT facility has been the most heavily used <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> wind tunnel. Every major commercial transport and almost every military jet built in the United States over the last 40 years has been tested in this facility. Mercury, Gemini, and Apollo spacecraft space shuttle models were also tested in this tunnel complex.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Full-Scale_Aerodynamics_Complex_.28NFAC.29">National Full-Scale Aerodynamics Complex (NFAC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: National Full-Scale Aerodynamics Complex (NFAC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ames Research Center also houses the world's largest wind tunnel, part of the National Full-Scale Aerodynamic Complex (NFAC): it is large enough to test full-sized planes, rather than scale models. The complex of wind tunnels was listed on the National Register in 2017.</p>

<p>The 40 by 80 foot wind tunnel circuit was originally constructed in the 1940s and is now capable of providing test velocities up to 300 knots (560 km/h; 350 mph). It is used to support an active research program in aerodynamics, dynamics, model noise, and full-scale aircraft and their components. The aerodynamic characteristics of new configurations are investigated with an emphasis on estimating the accuracy of computational methods. Aeromechanical stability boundaries of advanced rotorcraft and rotor-fuselage interactions are explored. Stability and control derivatives are also determined, including the static and dynamic characteristics of new aircraft configurations. The acoustic characteristics of most of the full-scale vehicles are also determined, as well as acoustic research aimed at discovering and reducing aerodynamic sources of noise. In addition to the normal data gathering methods (e.g., balance system, pressure measuring transducers, and temperature sensing thermocouples), state-of-the-art, non-intrusive instrumentation (e.g., laser velocimeters and shadowgraphs) are available to help determine flow direction and velocity in and around the lifting surfaces of models or aircraft undergoing investigation. The 40 by 80 Foot Wind Tunnel is primarily used for determining the low- and medium-speed aerodynamic characteristics of high-performance aircraft, rotorcraft, and fixed wing, powered-lift V/STOL aircraft.</p>
<p>The 80 by 120 Foot Wind Tunnel at <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> Ames Research Center is the largest wind tunnel test section in the world. This open circuit leg was added and a new fan drive system was installed in the 1980s. The 80 by 120 Foot Wind Tunnel is used to support an active research program in aerodynamics, dynamics, model noise, and full-scale aircraft and their components. The aerodynamic characteristics of new configurations are investigated, with an emphasis on estimating the accuracy of computational methods. Aeromechanical stability boundaries of advanced rotorcraft and rotor-fuselage interactions are explored. The acoustic characteristics of most of the full-scale vehicles are also determined, as well as acoustic research aimed at discovering and reducing aerodynamic sources of noise. In addition to the normal data gathering methods (e.g., balance system, pressure measuring transducers, and temperature sensing thermocouples), state-of-the-art non-intrusive instrumentation (e.g., laser velocimeters and shadowgraphs) are available to help determine flow direction and velocity in and around the lifting surfaces of models or aircraft undergoing investigation. Some of the test programs that have come through the 80 by 120 Foot include: F-18 High Angle of Attack Vehicle, DARPA/Lockheed Common Affordable Lightweight Fighter, XV-15 Tilt Rotor, and Advance Recovery System Parafoil. The 80 by 120 foot test section is capable of testing a full size Boeing 737 at velocities up to 100 knots (190 km/h; 120 mph).</p>
<p>Although decommissioned by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> in 2003, the NFAC is now being operated by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> as a satellite facility of the <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Engineering_Development_Complex" title="Arnold Engineering Development Complex">Arnold Engineering Development Complex</a> (AEDC).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arc_Jet_Complex">Arc Jet Complex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Arc Jet Complex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ames Arc Jet Complex is an advanced thermophysics facility where sustained hypersonic- and hyperthermal testing of vehicular thermoprotective systems takes place under a variety of simulated flight- and re-entry conditions. Of its seven available test bays, four currently contain Arc Jet units of differing configurations, serviced by common facility support equipment. These are the Aerodynamic Heating Facility (AHF), the Turbulent Flow Duct (TFD), the Panel Test Facility (PTF), and the Interaction Heating Facility (IHF). The support equipment includes two D.C. power supplies, a steam ejector-driven vacuum system, a water-cooling system, high-pressure gas systems, data acquisition system, and other auxiliary systems.</p>
<p>The magnitude and capacity of these systems makes the Ames Arc Jet Complex unique in the world. The largest power supply can deliver 75 megawatts (MW) for a 30-minute duration or 150 MW for a 15-second duration. This power capacity, in combination with a high-volume 5-stage steam ejector vacuum-pumping system, enables facility operations to match high-altitude atmospheric flight conditions with samples of relatively large size. The Thermo-Physics Facilities Branch operates four arc jet facilities. The Interaction Heating Facility (IHF), with an available power of over 60-MW, is one of the highest-power arc jets available. It is a very flexible facility, capable of long run times of up to one hour, and able to test large samples in both a stagnation and flat plate configuration. The Panel Test Facility (PTF) uses a unique semielliptic nozzle for testing panel sections. Powered by a 20-MW arc heater, the PTF can perform tests on samples for up to 20 minutes. The Turbulent Flow Duct provides supersonic, turbulent high temperature air flows over flat surfaces. The TFD is powered by a 20-MW Hüls arc heater and can test samples 203 by 508 millimeters (8.0 by 20.0 in) in size. The Aerodynamic Heating Facility (AHF) has similar characteristics to the IHF arc heater, offering a wide range of operating conditions, samples sizes and extended test times. A cold-air-mixing plenum allows for simulations of ascent or high-speed flight conditions. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalycity" title="Catalycity">Catalycity</a> studies using air or nitrogen can be performed in this flexible rig. A 5-arm model support system allows the user to maximize testing efficiency. The AHF can be configured with either a Hüls or segmented arc heater, up to 20-MW. 1 MW is enough power to supply 750 homes.</p>
<p>The Arc Jet Complex was listed on the National Register in 2017.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Range_complex">Range complex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Range complex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ames_Vertical_Gun_Range">Ames Vertical Gun Range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ames Vertical Gun Range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ames_Vertical_Gun_Range" title="Ames Vertical Gun Range">Ames Vertical Gun Range</a> (AVGR) was designed to conduct scientific studies of lunar impact processes in support of the Apollo missions. In 1979, it was established as a National Facility, funded through the Planetary Geology and Geophysics Program. In 1995, increased scientific needs across various disciplines resulted in joint core funding by three different science programs at NASA Headquarters (Planetary Geology and Geophysics, Exobiology, and Solar System Origins). In addition, the AVGR provides programmatic support for various proposed and ongoing planetary missions (e.g. Stardust, Deep Impact).</p>
<p>Using its 0.30 <a href="/wiki/Caliber" title="Caliber">cal</a> light-gas gun and powder gun, the AVGR can launch projectiles to velocities ranging from 500 to 7,000 m/s (1,600 to 23,000 ft/s). By varying the gun’s angle of elevation with respect to the target vacuum chamber, impact angles from 0° to 90° relative to the gravitational vector are possible. This unique feature is extremely important in the study of crater formation processes.</p>
<p>The target chamber is approximately 2.5 meters (8 ft 2 in) in diameter and height and can accommodate a wide variety of targets and mounting fixtures. It can maintain vacuum levels below 0.03 torrs (4.0 Pa), or can be back filled with various gases to simulate different planetary atmospheres. Impact events are typically recorded with high-speed video/film, or Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypervelocity_Free-Flight_Range">Hypervelocity Free-Flight Range</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hypervelocity Free-Flight Range">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypervelocity_Free-Flight" title="Hypervelocity Free-Flight">Hypervelocity Free-Flight</a> (HFF) Range currently comprises two active facilities: the Aerodynamic Facility (HFFAF) and the Gun Development Facility (HFFGDF). The HFFAF is a combined Ballistic Range and Shock-tube Driven Wind Tunnel. Its primary purpose is to examine the aerodynamic characteristics and flow-field structural details of free-flying aeroballistic models.</p>
<p>The HFFAF has a test section equipped with 16 shadowgraph-imaging stations. Each station can be used to capture an orthogonal pair of images of a <a href="/wiki/Hypervelocity" title="Hypervelocity">hypervelocity</a> model in flight. These images, combined with the recorded flight time history, can be used to obtain critical aerodynamic parameters such as lift, drag, static and dynamic stability, flow characteristics, and pitching moment coefficients. For very high <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach number</a> (M &gt; 25) simulations, models can be launched into a counter-flowing gas stream generated by the shock tube. The facility can also be configured for hypervelocity impact testing and has an aerothermodynamic capability as well. The HFFAF is currently configured to operate the 1.5 inches (38 mm) <a href="/wiki/Light-gas_gun" title="Light-gas gun">light-gas gun</a> in support of continuing thermal imaging and transition research for NASA's hypersonics program.</p>
<p>The HFFGDF is used for gun performance enhancement studies, and occasional impact testing. The Facility uses the same arsenal of light-gas and powder guns as the HFFAF to accelerate particles that range in size from 3.2 to 25.4 millimeters (0.13 to 1.00 in) diameter to velocities ranging from 0.5 to 8.5 km/s (1,500 to 28,000 ft/s). Most of the research effort to date has centered on Earth atmosphere entry configurations (Mercury, Gemini, Apollo, and Shuttle), planetary entry designs (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Viking</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pioneer_Venus" title="Pioneer Venus">Pioneer Venus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo">Galileo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory" title="Mars Science Laboratory">MSL</a>), and aerobraking (AFE) configurations. The facility has also been used for scramjet propulsion studies (<a href="/wiki/Rockwell_X-30" title="Rockwell X-30">National Aerospace Plane (NASP)</a>) and meteoroid/orbital debris impact studies (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_Station" title="Space Station">Space Station</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RLV" title="RLV">RLV</a>). In 2004, the facility was utilized for foam-debris dynamics testing in support of the Return To Flight effort. As of March 2007, the GDF has been reconfigured to operate a cold gas gun for subsonic <a href="/wiki/Crew_Exploration_Vehicle" title="Crew Exploration Vehicle">CEV</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_capsule" title="Space capsule">capsule</a> aerodynamics.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_Arc_Shock_Tube">Electric Arc Shock Tube</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Electric Arc Shock Tube">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_Arc_Shock_Tube" title="Electric Arc Shock Tube">Electric Arc Shock Tube</a> (EAST) Facility is used to investigate the effects of radiation and ionization that occur during very high velocity atmospheric entries. In addition, the EAST can also provide air-blast simulations requiring the strongest possible shock generation in air at an initial pressure loading of 1 standard atmosphere (100 kPa) or greater. The facility has three separate driver configurations, to meet a range of test requirements: the driver can be connected to a diaphragm station of either a 102 millimeters (4.0 in) or a 610 millimeters (24 in) shock tube, and the high-pressure 102 millimeters (4.0 in) shock tube can also drive a 762 millimeters (30.0 in) shock tunnel. Energy for the drivers is supplied by a 1.25-MJ-capacitor storage system.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_Geological_Survey_.28USGS.29">United States Geological Survey (USGS)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: United States Geological Survey (USGS)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In September 2016, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a> (USGS) announced plans to relocate its West Coast science center from nearby <a href="/wiki/Menlo_Park,_California" title="Menlo Park, California">Menlo Park</a> to the Ames Research Center at Moffett Field. The relocation is expected to take five years and will begin in 2017 with 175 of the USGS employees moving to Moffett. The relocation is designed to save money on the $7.5 million annual rent the USGS pays for its Menlo Park campus. The land in Menlo Park is owned by the <a href="/wiki/General_Services_Administration" title="General Services Administration">General Services Administration</a>, which is required by federal law to charge market-rate rent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_Ames_Exploration_Center">NASA Ames Exploration Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: NASA Ames Exploration Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/NASA_Ames_Exploration_Center" title="NASA Ames Exploration Center">NASA Ames Exploration Center</a></div>
<p>The NASA Ames Exploration Center is a science museum and education center for NASA. There are displays and interactive exhibits about NASA technology, missions and space exploration. A moon rock, meteorite and other geologic samples are on display. The theater shows movies with footage from NASA's explorations of Mars and the planets, and about the contributions of the scientists at Ames. The facility is free and open to the public Tuesday through Friday from 10 a.m to 4 p.m and on Saturday and Sunday from noon to 4 p.m.<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Robotics_Alliance_Project">Robotics Alliance Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Robotics Alliance Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1999, Mark León developed NASA's Robotics Education Project — now called the Robotics Alliance Project — under his mentor Dave Lavery, which has reached over 100,000 students nationwide using <a href="/wiki/For_Inspiration_and_Recognition_of_Science_and_Technology" title="For Inspiration and Recognition of Science and Technology">FIRST robotics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Botball" title="Botball">BOTBALL</a> robotics competitions. The Project's FIRST branch originally comprised <a href="/wiki/FIRST_Robotics_Competition" title="FIRST Robotics Competition">FRC</a> <a class="external text" href="http://www.team254.com/" rel="nofollow">Team 254: "The Cheesy Poofs"</a>, an all-male team from <a href="/wiki/Bellarmine_College_Preparatory" title="Bellarmine College Preparatory">Bellarmine High School</a> in San José, California. In 2006, <a class="external text" href="http://spacecookies.org" rel="nofollow">Team 1868: "The Space Cookies"</a>, an all-female team, was founded in collaboration with the Girl Scouts. In 2012, <a class="external text" href="http://frc971.org/" rel="nofollow">Team 971: "Spartan Robotics"</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mountain_View_High_School_(Mountain_View,_California)" title="Mountain View High School (Mountain View, California)">Mountain View High School</a> joined the Project, though the team continues to operate at their school. All three teams are highly decorated. All three have won Regional competitions, two have won the <a href="/wiki/FIRST_Championship" title="FIRST Championship">FIRST Championship</a>, two of which have won the Regional Chairman's Award, and one is a Hall of Fame team. The three teams are collectively referred to as "House teams".</p>
<p>The mission of the project is "To create a human, technical, and programmatic resource of robotics capabilities to enable the implementation of future robotic space exploration missions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_events">Recent events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Recent events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_W._Bush_administration" title="George W. Bush administration">Bush administration</a> slightly increased funding for <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> overall, the substantial realignment in research priorities that followed the announcement of the <a href="/wiki/Vision_for_Space_Exploration" title="Vision for Space Exploration">Vision for Space Exploration</a> in 2004 led to a significant number of layoffs at Ames.</p>
<p>On October 22, 2006, NASA opened the Carl Sagan Center for the Study of Life in the Cosmos. The center continued work that <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Sagan</a> undertook, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Search_for_Extraterrestrial_Intelligence" title="Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence">Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence</a>.</p>
<p>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_Image_Recovery_Project" title="Lunar Orbiter Image Recovery Project">Lunar Orbiter Image Recovery Project</a> (LOIRP) was given space in the old <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> (the building was renamed McMoons) to digitize data tapes from the five 1966 and 1967 <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_program" title="Lunar Orbiter program">Lunar Orbiter</a> spacecraft that were sent to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>.</p>
<p>Also in 2008, it was announced that former Ames director <a href="/wiki/Henry_McDonald_(engineer)" title="Henry McDonald (engineer)">Henry McDonald</a> was a 60th Anniversary Class inductee of the Ames Hall of Fame for providing, "...exceptional leadership and keen technical insight to NASA Ames as the Center re-invented itself in the late 1990s."</p>
<p>In 2010, scientists at the Fluid Mechanics Laboratory at Ames studied the aerodynamics of the <a href="/wiki/Adidas_Jabulani" title="Adidas Jabulani">Jabulani</a> World Cup soccer ball, concluding that it tends to "<a href="/wiki/Knuckleball" title="Knuckleball">knuckle under</a>" at speeds of 45 to 50 miles per hour (72 to 80 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_soccer_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-nasa_soccer-16">[16]</a></sup> Aerospace engineer Rabi Mehta attributed this effect to asymmetric flow due to the ball's seam construction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In March 2015, scientists at Ames announced that they had synthesized "...<a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cytosine" title="Cytosine">cytosine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">thymine</a>, all three components of <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, non-biologically in a laboratory under conditions found in space." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public-private_partnerships">Public-private partnerships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Public-private partnerships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The federal government has re-tasked portions of the facility and human resources to <a class="external text" href="http://www.nasa.gov/centers/ames/business/index.html" rel="nofollow">support</a> private sector industry, research, and education.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">HP</a> became the first corporate affiliate of a new Bio-Info-Nano Research and Development Institute (BIN-RDI); a collaborative venture established by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_California_Santa_Cruz" title="University of California Santa Cruz">University of California Santa Cruz</a> and NASA, based at Ames. The Bio|Info|Nano R&amp;D Institute is dedicated to creating scientific breakthroughs by the convergence of biotechnology, information technology, and nanotechnology.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Singularity_University" title="Singularity University">Singularity University</a> hosts its leadership and educational program at the facility. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Organ_Preservation_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Organ Preservation Alliance (page does not exist)">Organ Preservation Alliance</a><a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.organpreservationalliance.org/" rel="nofollow">[2]</a> is also headquartered there; the Alliance is a nonprofit organization that works in partnership with the <a href="/wiki/Methuselah_Foundation" title="Methuselah Foundation">Methuselah Foundation</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Methuselah_Foundation#New_Organ_Prize" title="Methuselah Foundation">New Organ Prize</a> "to catalyze breakthroughs on the remaining obstacles towards the long-term storage of organs" to overcome the drastic unmet medical need for viable organs for transplantation. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kleenspeed_Technologies" title="Kleenspeed Technologies">Kleenspeed Technologies</a> is headquartered there.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Google">Google</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Google">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 28, 2005, <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> and Ames Research Center disclosed details to a long-term research partnership. In addition to pooling engineering talent, Google planned to build a 1,000,000-square-foot (9.3 ha) facility on the ARC campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> One of the projects between Ames, Google, and <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Gigapan" title="Gigapan">Gigapan</a> Project—a robotic platform for creating, sharing, and annotating terrestrial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigapixel" title="Gigapixel">gigapixel</a> images. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_Content_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Planetary Content Project (page does not exist)">Planetary Content Project</a> seeks to integrate and improve the data that Google uses for its <a href="/wiki/Google_Moon" title="Google Moon">Google Moon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Google_Mars" title="Google Mars">Google Mars</a> projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> On June 4, 2008, Google announced it had leased 42 acres (170,000 m<sup>2</sup>) from NASA, at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moffett_Field" title="Moffett Field">Moffett Field</a>, for use as office space and employee housing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Construction of the new Google project which is near Google's <a href="/wiki/Googleplex" title="Googleplex">Googleplex</a> headquarters began in 2013 and has a target opening date of 2015. It is called "Bay View" as it overlooks <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a>.</p>
<p>In May 2013, Google Inc announced that it was launching the Quantum Artificial Intelligence Lab, to be hosted by NASA’s Ames Research Center. The lab will house a 512-qubit quantum computer from D-Wave Systems, and the USRA (Universities Space Research Association) will invite researchers from around the world to share time on it. The goal being to study how quantum computing might advance machine learning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>Announced on 10 November 2014, Planetary Ventures LLC (a Google subsidiary) will lease the <a href="/wiki/Moffett_Federal_Airfield" title="Moffett Federal Airfield">Moffett Federal Airfield</a> from NASA Ames, a site of about 1,000 acres formerly costing the agency $6.3 million annually in maintenance and operation costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The lease includes the restoration of the site's historic landmark <a href="/wiki/Hangar_One_(Mountain_View,_California)" title="Hangar One (Mountain View, California)">Hangar One</a>, as well as hangars Two and Three. The lease went into effect in March 2015, and spans 60 years.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Living_and_working_at_Ames">Living and working at Ames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ames_Research_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Living and working at Ames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are a myriad of activities inside the research center and around for full-time workers and interns alike. The website new2nasa.wikispaces.com offers updated information. There was a Parcourse trail, also known as a <a href="/wiki/Fitness_trail" title="Fitness trail">fitness trail</a> inside the base, but sections of it are inaccessible due to changes in base layout since it was installed. An official NASA ID is required to enter Ames.</p>
