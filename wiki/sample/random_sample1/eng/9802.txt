<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Ecce homo</i></h1>

<p><i><b>Ecce homo</b></i> ("behold the man", <small>Ecclesiastical Latin: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Latin" title="Help:IPA for Latin">[ˈɛttʃɛ ˈɔmɔ]</a></span>, <small>Classical Latin: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Latin" title="Help:IPA for Latin">[ˈɛkkɛ ˈhɔmoː]</a></span>) are the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> words used by <a href="/wiki/Pontius_Pilate" title="Pontius Pilate">Pontius Pilate</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a> translation of <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">John</a> <span class="nourlexpansion nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+19%3A5&amp;version=4" rel="nofollow">19:5</a></span>, when he presents a <a href="/wiki/Scourge" title="Scourge">scourged</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a>, bound and <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_thorns" title="Crown of thorns">crowned with thorns</a>, to a hostile crowd shortly before his <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">Crucifixion</a>. The original <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> is Ίδε ό άνθρωπος (<i>Ide ho anthropos</i>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Douay-Rheims_Bible" title="Douay-Rheims Bible">Douay-Rheims Bible</a> translates the phrase into English as "Behold the man!"<sup class="nourlexpansion reference"><a class="external text" href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=John+19%3A5&amp;version=KJV" rel="nofollow">[John 19:5]</a></sup> The scene has been widely depicted in <a href="/wiki/Christian_art" title="Christian art">Christian art</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artistic_subject">Artistic subject</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecce_homo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Artistic subject">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ecce-homo_Mantegna.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1700" data-file-width="1299" height="288" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Ecce-homo_Mantegna.jpg/220px-Ecce-homo_Mantegna.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Ecce-homo_Mantegna.jpg/330px-Ecce-homo_Mantegna.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Ecce-homo_Mantegna.jpg/440px-Ecce-homo_Mantegna.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ecce-homo_Mantegna.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Andrea_Mantegna" title="Andrea Mantegna">Andrea Mantegna</a>, 1500</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A scene of the <i>Ecce Homo</i> is a standard component of cycles illustrating the <i>Passion</i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_of_Christ" title="Life of Christ">Life of Christ</a></i> in art. It follows the <i><a href="/wiki/Flagellation_of_Christ" title="Flagellation of Christ">Flagellation of Christ</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Crown_of_thorns" title="Crown of thorns">Crowning with thorns</a></i> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mocking_of_Christ" title="Mocking of Christ">Mocking of Christ</a></i>, the last two often being combined. The usual depiction shows Pilate and Christ, the mocking crowd and parts of the city of <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>.</p>
<p>But, from the 15th century, <a href="/wiki/Andachtsbilder" title="Andachtsbilder">devotional pictures</a> began to portray Jesus alone, in half or full figure with a purple robe, loincloth, crown of thorns and torture wounds, especially on his head. Similar subjects but with the wounds of the crucifixion visible (Nail wounds on the limbs, spear wounds on the sides), are termed a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Man_of_Sorrow" title="Man of Sorrow">Man of Sorrow</a>(s)</i> (also <i>Misericordia</i>). If the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Instruments_of_the_Passion" title="Instruments of the Passion">Instruments of the Passion</a>" are present, it may be called an <i><a href="/wiki/Arma_Christi" title="Arma Christi">Arma Christi</a></i>. If Christ is sitting down (usually supporting himself with his hand on his thigh), it may be referred to it as <i>Christ at rest</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Pensive_Christ" title="Pensive Christ">Pensive Christ</a></i>. It is not always possible to distinguish these subjects.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_depictions">Early depictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecce_homo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early depictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Titian_-_Christ_Shown_to_the_People_(Ecce_Homo).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="520" height="254" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Titian_-_Christ_Shown_to_the_People_%28Ecce_Homo%29.jpg/220px-Titian_-_Christ_Shown_to_the_People_%28Ecce_Homo%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Titian_-_Christ_Shown_to_the_People_%28Ecce_Homo%29.jpg/330px-Titian_-_Christ_Shown_to_the_People_%28Ecce_Homo%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Titian_-_Christ_Shown_to_the_People_%28Ecce_Homo%29.jpg/440px-Titian_-_Christ_Shown_to_the_People_%28Ecce_Homo%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Titian_-_Christ_Shown_to_the_People_(Ecce_Homo).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i>Ecce Homo</i> by <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>, between circa 1570 and circa 1576</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first depictions of the <i>ecce homo</i> scene in the arts appear in the 9th and 10th centuries in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syrian</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> culture. <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western</a> depictions in the Middle Ages that often seem to depict the <i>ecce homo</i> scene, (and are usually interpreted as such) more often than not only show the crowning of thorns and the mocking of Christ, (cf. the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Egbert_Codex&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Egbert Codex (page does not exist)">Egbert Codex</a></i> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Codex_Aureus_Epternacensis" title="Codex Aureus Epternacensis">Codex Aureus Epternacensis</a></i>) which precede the actual <i>ecce homo</i> scene in the Bible. The independent image only developed around 1400, probably in Burgundy, but then rapidly became extremely popular, especially in Northern Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The motif found increasing currency as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passion_(Christianity)" title="Passion (Christianity)">Passion</a> became a central theme in Western piety in the 15th and 16th centuries. The <i>ecce homo</i> theme was included not only in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passion_play" title="Passion play">passion plays</a> of <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> theatre, but also in cycles of illustrations of the story of the Passion, as in the Passions of <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a> or the prints of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Schongauer" title="Martin Schongauer">Martin Schongauer</a>. The scene was (especially in France) often depicted as a sculpture or group of sculptures; even altarpieces and other paintings with the motif were produced (by, for example, <a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Bosch" title="Hieronymus Bosch">Hieronymus Bosch</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hans_Holbein_the_Elder" title="Hans Holbein the Elder">Hans Holbein</a>). Like the passion plays, the visual depictions of the <i>ecce homo</i> scene, it has been argued, often, and increasingly, portray the people of Jerusalem in a highly critical light, bordering perhaps on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antisemitic" title="Antisemitic">antisemitic</a> caricatures. Equally, this style of art has been read as a kind of simplistic externalisation of the inner hatred of the angry crowd towards Jesus, not necessarily implying any racial judgment.</p>
<p>The motif of the lone figure of a suffering Christ who seems to be staring directly at the observer, enabling him/her to personally identify with the events of the Passion, arose in the late Middle Ages. A parallel development was that the similar motifs of the <i>Man of Sorrow</i> and <i>Christ at rest</i> increased in importance. The subject was used repeatedly in later <a href="/wiki/Old_master_print" title="Old master print">prints</a> (for example, by <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Callot" title="Jacques Callot">Jacques Callot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt" title="Rembrandt">Rembrandt</a>), the paintings of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a>, as well as in Baroque sculptures.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Bosch" title="Hieronymus Bosch">Hieronymus Bosch</a> painted his first <a href="/wiki/Ecce_Homo_(Bosch,_1470s)" title="Ecce Homo (Bosch, 1470s)">Ecce Homo</a> during the 1470s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WGoA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-WGoA-2">[2]</a></sup> He returned to the subject in 1490 to paint in a characteristically Netherlandish style, with deep perspective and a surreal ghostly image of praying monks in the lower left-hand corner.</p>
<p>In 1498, <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a> depicted the suffering of Christ in the <i>Ecce Homo</i> of his <i>Great Passion,</i> print series in unusually close relation with his self-portrait, leading to a reinterpretation of the motif as a metaphor for the suffering of the artist. <a href="/wiki/James_Ensor" title="James Ensor">James Ensor</a> used the <i>ecce homo</i> motif in his ironic print <i>Christ and the Critics</i> (1891), in which he portrayed himself as Christ.</p>

<p>One of the more famous modern versions of the Ecce Homo motif was that by the Polish artist, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adam_Chmielowski" title="Adam Chmielowski">Adam Chmielowski</a> who went on to found, as Brother Albert, The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Congregation_of_Albertine_Brothers_Serving_the_Poor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Congregation of Albertine Brothers Serving the Poor (page does not exist)">Congregation of Albertine Brothers Serving the Poor</a> (CSAPU) and a year later, <a href="/wiki/Albertine_Sisters" title="Albertine Sisters">Albertine Sisters</a>, eventually becoming proclaimed a saint on 12 November 1989 by Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Paul_II" title="John Paul II">John Paul II</a>, the author of a play about Chmielowski, written between 1944-1950 when the future Pontiff and later himself a saint was a young priest. Chmielowski's "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecce_Homo" title="Ecce Homo">Ecce Homo</a>" (146 cm x 96.5 cm, unsigned, painted between 1879 and 1881), was significant in Chmielowski's life, as it is in the I Act of Wojtyła's play. Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Paul_II" title="John Paul II">John Paul II</a> is said to have kept a copy of this painting in his apartment at the Vatican.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The original can be viewed in the Ecce Homo Sanctuary of the Albertine Sisters in Kraków. It was painted at a time when the painter was going through an inner struggle, trying to decide whether to remain an artist, or to give up painting to pursue the calling to minister to the poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Especially in the 19th and 20th centuries, the meaning of <i>ecce homo</i> motif has been extended to the portrayal of suffering and the degradation of humans through violence and war. A notable 20th-century depiction is <a href="/wiki/Lovis_Corinth" title="Lovis Corinth">Lovis Corinth</a>'s work <i>Ecce Homo</i> (1925), which shows, from the perspective of the crowd, Jesus, a soldier and Pilate dressed as a physician. Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocaust" title="Holocaust">Holocaust</a> of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otto_Dix" title="Otto Dix">Otto Dix</a> portrayed himself as the suffering Christ in a concentration camp, in <i>Ecce Homo with self-likeness behind barbed wire</i> (1948).</p>
<p>By contrast, Antonio Ciseri's 1871 <i>Ecce Homo</i> portrayal presents a semi-photographic view of a balcony seen from behind the central figures of a scourged Christ and Pilate (whose face is not visible). The crowd forms a distant mass, almost without individuality, and much of the detailed focus is on the normally secondary figures of Pilate's aides, guards, secretary and wife.</p>
<p>An <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecce_Homo_(El%C3%ADas_Garc%C3%ADa_Mart%C3%ADnez)" title="Ecce Homo (Elías García Martínez)">Ecce Homo</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> by 19th-century Spanish painter <a href="/wiki/El%C3%ADas_Garc%C3%ADa_Mart%C3%ADnez" title="Elías García Martínez">Elías García Martínez</a> gained notoriety in August 2012 when a woman named Cecilia Giménez took it upon herself to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Painting_restoration" title="Painting restoration">restore</a> it without any training or expertise, resulting in Jesus' looking like "a very hairy monkey".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The "monkey Christ" painting has become a tourist attraction and destination, as well as the basis of a popular <a href="/wiki/Internet_meme" title="Internet meme">internet meme</a>. So many people were flocking to see the painting that the town started charging an admission fee, which has raised more than 50,000 euros (£43,000) for charity as of mid August 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychology">Psychology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecce_homo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Psychology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term 'Ecce Homo' has been used by psychiatrist, <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Frankl" title="Viktor Frankl">Dr. Viktor Frankl</a> to describe a technique he uses in <a href="/wiki/Logotherapy" title="Logotherapy">logotherapy</a>. The 'Ecce Homo Technique' helps the client understand that he has strengths within that he may not have realized. Hence, "Behold the Man" and take note that he has shown true courage by his actions and continued search for meaning in his life despite the fact that he is also suffering.</p>
<p>&lt;&lt;Crumbaugh, J. C. &amp; Henrion R., (1994). The ecce homo technique: A special case of dereflection. The International Forum for Logotherapy, 17, 1-7.&gt;&gt;</p>
<p>&lt;&lt;Shantall, T., (2003). The Quest for Destiny, p. 113&gt;&gt;</p>
