<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Eteocles</h1>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, <b>Eteocles</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ɪ/ or /ə/ 'e' in 'roses'">ᵻ</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/iː/ long 'e' in 'seed'">iː</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἐτεοκλῆς</span>) was a king of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Thebes_(Boeotia)" title="Ancient Thebes (Boeotia)">Thebes</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Oedipus" title="Oedipus">Oedipus</a> and either <a href="/wiki/Jocasta" title="Jocasta">Jocasta</a> or <a href="/wiki/Euryganeia" title="Euryganeia">Euryganeia</a>. The name is from earlier <i>*Etewoklewes</i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">Ἐτεϝοκλέϝες</span>), meaning "truly glorious". <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tawagalawa" title="Tawagalawa">Tawagalawas</a></i> is thought to be the <a href="/wiki/Hittite_language" title="Hittite language">Hittite</a> rendition of the name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Oedipus killed his father <a href="/wiki/Laius" title="Laius">Laius</a> and married his mother without knowing his relationship to either. When the relationship was revealed, he was expelled from Thebes. The rule passed to his sons Eteocles and <a href="/wiki/Polynices" title="Polynices">Polynices</a>. However, because of a curse from their father, the two brothers did not share the rule peacefully. Eteocles was succeeded by his uncle, <a href="/wiki/Creon" title="Creon">Creon</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oedipus.27s_curse">Oedipus's curse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eteocles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Oedipus's curse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Thebaid_(Greek_poem)" title="Thebaid (Greek poem)">Thebaid</a></i>, the brothers were cursed by their father for their disrespect towards him on two occasions. The first of these occurred when they served him using the silver table of <a href="/wiki/Cadmus" title="Cadmus">Cadmus</a> and a golden cup, which he had forbidden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The brothers then sent him the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haunch" title="Haunch">haunch</a> of a sacrificed animal, rather than the shoulder, which he deserved. Enraged, Oedipus prayed to <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> that the brothers would die by each other's hands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, in <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Oedipus_at_Colonus" title="Oedipus at Colonus">Oedipus at Colonus</a></i>, Oedipus desired to stay in Thebes but was expelled by Creon. His sons argued over the throne, but Eteocles gained the support of the Thebans and expelled Polynices, who went to Oedipus to ask for his blessing to retake the city, but instead was cursed to die by his brother's hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quarrel_over_the_rule_of_Thebes">Quarrel over the rule of Thebes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eteocles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Quarrel over the rule of Thebes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several accounts of how Eteocles and Polynices shared the rule after Oedipus's departure from the city. In <a href="/wiki/Hellanicus_of_Lesbos" title="Hellanicus of Lesbos">Hellanicus</a>'s account, Eteocles offers his brother his choice of either the rule of the city or a share of the property. In <a href="/wiki/Pherecydes_of_Leros" title="Pherecydes of Leros">Pherecydes</a>, however, Eteocles expels Polynices by force, and keeps the rule of Thebes and the inheritance. The <i><a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_(Pseudo-Apollodorus)" title="Bibliotheca (Pseudo-Apollodorus)">Bibliotheca</a></i> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diodorus" title="Diodorus">Diodorus</a> state that the brothers agree to divide the kingship between them, switching each year. Eteocles, however, was allotted the first year, and refused to surrender the crown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In all of these versions, Polynices gathered the support of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argives" title="Argives">Argives</a> and attacked Thebes. This battle is the subject of <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a>' tragedy <i><a href="/wiki/Seven_Against_Thebes" title="Seven Against Thebes">Seven Against Thebes</a></i>. Although Eteocles's forces were victorious, the brothers killed each other.</p>
