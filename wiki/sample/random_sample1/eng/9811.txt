<p><b>Ettore Majorana</b> (<small>Italian: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[ˈɛttore majoˈraːna]</a></span>; born on 5 August 1906 – probably died after 1959<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cs_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-cs-1">[1]</a></sup>) was an Italian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theoretical_physicist" title="Theoretical physicist">theoretical physicist</a> who worked on <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrino</a> masses. On March 25, 1938, he disappeared under mysterious circumstances while going by ship from <a href="/wiki/Palermo" title="Palermo">Palermo</a> to <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Majorana_equation" title="Majorana equation">Majorana equation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Majorana_fermion" title="Majorana fermion">Majorana fermions</a> are named after him. In 2006, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Majorana_Prize" title="Majorana Prize">Majorana Prize</a> was established in his memory.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_work">Life and work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ettore_Majorana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>There are several categories of scientists in the world; those of second or third rank do their best but never get very far. Then there is the first rank, those who make important discoveries, fundamental to scientific progress. But then there are the geniuses, like <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galilei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a>. Majorana was one of these.</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>— (<a href="/wiki/Enrico_Fermi" title="Enrico Fermi">Enrico Fermi</a> about Majorana, Rome 1938)</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gifted_in_mathematics">Gifted in mathematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ettore_Majorana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gifted in mathematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Majorana was born in <a href="/wiki/Catania" title="Catania">Catania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>. Mathematically gifted, he was very young when he joined <a href="/wiki/Enrico_Fermi" title="Enrico Fermi">Enrico Fermi</a>'s team in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Rome_La_Sapienza" title="University of Rome La Sapienza">Rome</a> as one of the "<i><a href="/wiki/Via_Panisperna_boys" title="Via Panisperna boys">Via Panisperna boys</a></i>", who took their name from the street address of their laboratory.</p>
<p>His uncle <a href="/wiki/Quirino_Majorana" title="Quirino Majorana">Quirino Majorana</a> was also a physicist.</p>
<p>He began his university studies in <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> in 1923 but switched to <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> in 1928 at the urging of <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Segr%C3%A8" title="Emilio Segrè">Emilio Segrè</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digest69to72_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-digest69to72-2">[2]</a></sup> His first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academic_paper" title="Academic paper">papers</a> dealt with problems in <a href="/wiki/Atomic_spectroscopy" title="Atomic spectroscopy">atomic spectroscopy</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_published_academic_papers">First published academic papers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ettore_Majorana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First published academic papers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Majorana_Equazinfcomp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1861" data-file-width="1270" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Majorana_Equazinfcomp.jpg/200px-Majorana_Equazinfcomp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Majorana_Equazinfcomp.jpg/300px-Majorana_Equazinfcomp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Majorana_Equazinfcomp.jpg/400px-Majorana_Equazinfcomp.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Majorana_Equazinfcomp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Handwritten notes preparatory to the equation in infinite components</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>His first paper, published in 1928, was written when he was an undergraduate and coauthored by Giovanni Gentile, Jr., a junior professor in the Institute of Physics in Rome. This work was an early quantitative application to atomic spectroscopy of Fermi's statistical model of atomic structure (now known as the <a href="/wiki/Thomas%E2%80%93Fermi_model" title="Thomas–Fermi model">Thomas–Fermi model</a>, due to its contemporaneous description by <a href="/wiki/Llewellyn_Thomas" title="Llewellyn Thomas">Llewellyn Thomas</a>).</p>
<p>In this paper, Majorana and Gentile performed first-principles calculations within the context of this model that gave a good account of experimentally-observed core electron energies of <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, and of the fine structure splitting of <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a> lines observed in optical spectra. In 1931, Majorana published the first paper on the phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Autoionization" title="Autoionization">autoionization</a> in atomic spectra, designated by him as "spontaneous ionization"; an independent paper in the same year, published by <a href="/wiki/Allen_Shenstone" title="Allen Shenstone">Allen Shenstone</a> of <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>, designated the phenomenon as "auto-ionization", a name first used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pierre_Auger" title="Pierre Auger">Pierre Auger</a>. This name has since become conventional, without the hyphen.</p>
<p>Majorana earned his <a href="/wiki/Laurea" title="Laurea">Laurea</a> in physics at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Rome_La_Sapienza" title="University of Rome La Sapienza">University of Rome La Sapienza</a> in 1929.</p>
<p>In 1932, he published a paper in the field of atomic spectroscopy concerning the behaviour of aligned atoms in time-varying magnetic fields. This problem, also studied by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I.I._Rabi" title="I.I. Rabi">I.I. Rabi</a> and others, led to an important sub-branch of atomic physics, that of radio-frequency spectroscopy. In the same year, Majorana published his paper on a relativistic theory of particles with arbitrary intrinsic momentum, in which he developed and applied infinite dimensional representations of the <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_group" title="Lorentz group">Lorentz group</a>, and gave a theoretical basis for the mass spectrum of elementary particles. Like most of Majorana's papers in Italian, it languished in relative obscurity for several decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Experiments in 1932 by <a href="/wiki/Ir%C3%A8ne_Joliot-Curie" title="Irène Joliot-Curie">Irène Joliot-Curie</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Joliot" title="Frédéric Joliot">Frédéric Joliot</a> showed the existence of an unknown particle that they suggested was a <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma ray</a>. Majorana was the first to interpret correctly the experiment as requiring a new particle that had a neutral charge and a mass about the same as the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a>; this particle is the <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermi" title="Fermi">Fermi</a> told him to write an article, but Majorana didn't bother. <a href="/wiki/James_Chadwick" title="James Chadwick">James Chadwick</a> proved the existence of the neutron by experiment later that year, and he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> for this discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Solution of <a href="/wiki/Majorana_equation" title="Majorana equation">Majorana's equation</a> yields particles that are their own anti-particle, now referred to as <a href="/wiki/Majorana_fermion" title="Majorana fermion">Majorana Fermions</a>. In April 2012, some of what Majorana predicted may have been confirmed in experiments on hybrid semiconductor-superconductor wire devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These experiments may potentially lead to a better understanding of quantum mechanics and may help build a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_computer" title="Quantum computer">quantum computer</a>. There has also been speculation that at least some part of the "missing mass" in the universe, which cannot be detected except by inference of its gravitational influences, may be composed of <i>Majorana particles</i>.</p>
<p>Majorana was known for not seeking credit for his discoveries, considering his work to be banal. He wrote only nine papers in his lifetime.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_with_Heisenberg.2C_illness.2C_isolation">Work with Heisenberg, illness, isolation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ettore_Majorana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Work with Heisenberg, illness, isolation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"At Fermi's urging, Majorana left Italy early in 1933 on a grant from the National Research Council. In <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>, Germany, he met <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Werner Heisenberg</a>. In letters he subsequently wrote to Heisenberg, Majorana revealed that he had found in him, not only a scientific colleague, but a warm personal friend."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digest71_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-digest71-6">[6]</a></sup> Majorana also travelled to <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>, where he worked with <a href="/wiki/Niels_Bohr" title="Niels Bohr">Niels Bohr</a>, another <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> winner, and a friend and mentor of Heisenberg.</p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazis</a> had come to power in Germany as Majorana arrived there. He studied with <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Werner Heisenberg</a> in <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a>, and worked on a theory of the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nucleus</a> (published in German in 1933) which, in its treatment of exchange forces, represented a further development of <a href="/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg" title="Werner Heisenberg">Heisenberg's</a> theory of the nucleus. Majorana's last-published paper, in 1937, this time in Italian, was an elaboration of a symmetrical theory of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">positrons</a>.</p>
<p>"In the fall of 1933, Majorana returned to Rome in poor health, having developed acute <a href="/wiki/Gastritis" title="Gastritis">gastritis</a> in Germany and apparently suffering from nervous exhaustion. Put on a strict diet, he grew reclusive and became harsh in his dealings with his family. To his mother, with whom he had previously shared a warm relationship, he had written from Germany that he would not accompany her on their customary summer vacation by the sea. Appearing at the institute less frequently, he soon was scarcely leaving his home; the promising young physicist had become a hermit. For nearly four years he shut himself off from friends and stopped publishing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-digest71_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-digest71-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>During these years, in which he published few articles, Majorana wrote many small works on several topics, from <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysics</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> to <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">relativity</a>. These unpublished papers, preserved in Domus Galileiana in <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a>, recently have been edited by Erasmo Recami and Salvatore Esposito.</p>
<p>He became a full <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">theoretical physics</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Naples" title="University of Naples">University of Naples</a> in 1937, without needing to take an examination because of his "high fame of singular expertise reached in the field of theoretical physics",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holstein2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holstein2-7">[7]</a></sup> independently of the competition rules.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_on_neutrino_masses">Work on neutrino masses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ettore_Majorana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Work on neutrino masses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Majorana did prescient theoretical work on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutrino_mass" title="Neutrino mass">neutrino masses</a>, a currently active subject of research. He also worked on an idea that mass may exert a small shielding effect on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational_radiation" title="Gravitational radiation">gravitational waves</a>, which did not gain much traction.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disappearance_at_sea_and_suggested_explanations">Disappearance at sea and suggested explanations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ettore_Majorana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Disappearance at sea and suggested explanations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Majorana disappeared in unknown circumstances during a boat trip from <a href="/wiki/Palermo" title="Palermo">Palermo</a> to <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a> on March 25, 1938. Despite several investigations, his fate is still uncertain. His body has not been found. He had apparently withdrawn all of his money from his bank account, prior to making a trip to Palermo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holstein2_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holstein2-7">[7]</a></sup> He may have travelled to Palermo hoping to visit his friend <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Segr%C3%A8" title="Emilio Segrè">Emilio Segrè</a>, a professor at the university there, but Segrè was in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> at that time. On the day of his disappearance, Majorana sent the following note to Antonio Carrelli, Director of the Naples Physics Institute:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Dear Carrelli, I made a decision that has become unavoidable. There isn't a bit of selfishness in it, but I realize what trouble my sudden disappearance will cause you and the students. For this as well, I beg your forgiveness, but especially for betraying the trust, the sincere friendship and the sympathy you gave me over the past months. I ask you to remember me to all those I learned to know and appreciate in your Institute, especially Sciuti: I will keep a fond memory of them all at least until 11 pm tonight, possibly later too. E. Majorana</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This was followed rapidly by a telegram cancelling his earlier plans. He apparently bought a ticket from Palermo to Naples and was never seen again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holstein2_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Holstein2-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several possible explanations for his disappearance have been proposed, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Hypothesis of <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a>, by his colleagues <a href="/wiki/Edoardo_Amaldi" title="Edoardo Amaldi">Amaldi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Segr%C3%A8" title="Emilio Segrè">Segrè</a> and others</li>
<li>Hypothesis of escape to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, by Erasmo Recami and Carlo Artemi (who has developed a detailed hypothetical reconstruction of Majorana's possible escape and life in Argentina)</li>
<li>Hypothesis of escape to a <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/charterhouse" title="wikt:charterhouse">Charterhouse</a> of <a href="/wiki/Serra_San_Bruno" title="Serra San Bruno">Serra San Bruno</a>), by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Sciascia" title="Leonardo Sciascia">Sciascia</a></li>
<li>Hypothesis of kidnapping or killing, to avoid his participation in the construction of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weapon" title="Atomic weapon">atomic weapon</a>, by Bella, Bartocci, and others</li>
<li>Hypothesis of escape to become a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beggar" title="Beggar">beggar</a> ("omu cani" or "dog man" hypothesis), by Bascone, and Venturini.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Sciascia_hypothesis">The Sciascia hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ettore_Majorana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Sciascia hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Italian writer <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Sciascia" title="Leonardo Sciascia">Leonardo Sciascia</a> has summarized some of the results of these investigations and these hypotheses in his book <i>La Scomparsa di Majorana</i> (Einaudi, 1975 - English translation: <i>The Moro Affair and The Mystery of Majorana</i>, Carcanet, 1987, <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0856357006">ISBN 0-85635-700-6</a>). However, some of Sciascia's conclusions were refuted by some of Majorana's former colleagues, including E. Amaldi and E. Segrè. The various hypotheses on Majorana's disappearance have been extensively discussed by Erasmo Recami in his book "<i>Il caso Majorana: Lettere, testimonianze, documenti</i>" (Di Renzo Editore, Roma, 2000), and in a journal article (E. Recami, "I nuovi documenti sulla scomparsa del fisico Ettore Majorana", <i>Scientia</i>, vol. 110, pp. 577–588 (1975); English version titled "New Evidence on the Disappearance of the Physicist Ettore Majorana", <i>Scientia</i>, vol. 110, p. 589 ff. (1975)). In the above-mentioned book and article, Recami discusses critically the various rival explanations concerning Majorana's disappearance, including those advanced by Sciascia in his short book, and presents highly suggestive evidence to the effect that Majorana travelled to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, where he may have earned his living as an <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reopening_of_the_case">Reopening of the case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ettore_Majorana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reopening of the case">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March 2011, Italian media reported that the Rome Attorney's office<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> had announced an inquiry into the statement made by a witness about meeting with Majorana in Buenos Aires in the years after World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On June 7, 2011, Italian media reported that the <a href="/wiki/Carabinieri" title="Carabinieri">Carabinieri</a>'s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Reparto_Investigazioni_Scientifiche&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Reparto Investigazioni Scientifiche (page does not exist)">RIS</a> had analyzed a photograph of a man taken in Argentina in 1955, finding ten points of similarity with Majorana's face.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 4, 2015, the Rome Attorney's Office released a statement declaring that Majorana was alive between 1955 and 1959, living in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valencia,_Venezuela" title="Valencia, Venezuela">Valencia, Venezuela</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cs_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-cs-1">[1]</a></sup> These last findings, based on new evidence, has made the Office declare the case officially closed, having found no criminal evidence related to his disappearance which probably was a personal choice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cs_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-cs-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commemoration_of_centenary">Commemoration of centenary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ettore_Majorana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Commemoration of centenary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The year 2006 marked Majorana's centenary.</p>
<p>An international conference on "Ettore Majorana's legacy and the Physics of the XXI century" <a class="external text" href="http://pos.sissa.it/cgi-bin/reader/conf.cgi?confid=37" rel="nofollow">EMC2006</a> was held in commemoration of the centennial of Majorana's birth in Catania, October 5–6, 2006. The conference proceedings with articles of highly ranked international scientists A. Bianconi, D. Brink, N. Cabibbo, R. Casalbuoni, G. Dragoni, S. Esposito, E. Fiorini, M. Inguscio, R. W. Jackiw, L. Maiani, R. Mantegna, E. Migneco, R. Petronzio, B. Preziosi, R. Pucci, E. Recami, and <a href="/wiki/Antonino_Zichichi" title="Antonino Zichichi">Antonino Zichichi</a> have been published by <i>POS Proceedings of Science of SISSA</i>, edited by Andrea Rapisarda (chairman), Paolo Castorina, Francesco Catara, Salvatore Lo Nigro, Emilio Migneco, Francesco Porto, and Emanuele Rimini.</p>
<p>A commemorative book <a class="external text" href="http://www.sif.it/SIF/en/portal/libri/majorana" rel="nofollow">"Ettore Majorana Scientific Papers on the occasion of the centenary of the birth"</a> of his (nine) collected papers, with commentary and English translations, was published by the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Physical_Society" title="Italian Physical Society">Italian Physical Society</a>.</p>
<p>Also to commemorate the centenary <a class="external text" href="http://www.ejtp.com" rel="nofollow"><i>Electronic Journal of Theoretical Physics</i></a> (EJTP) published a special issue of twenty articles dedicated to the modern development of Majorana's legacy.</p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Electronic_Journal_of_Theoretical_Physics" title="Electronic Journal of Theoretical Physics">Electronic Journal of Theoretical Physics</a></i> also established a prize in his memory to mark the centenary. The "<a class="external text" href="http://www.majoranaprize.com/" rel="nofollow">Majorana Medal</a>" or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Majorana_Prize" title="Majorana Prize">Majorana Prize</a> is an annual prize for researchers who have shown peculiar creativity, critical sense, and mathematical rigour in theoretical physics—in its broadest sense. The recipients of the 2006 Majorana Prize were <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Erasmo_Recami&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Erasmo Recami (page does not exist)">Erasmo Recami</a> (<a href="/wiki/University_of_Bergamo" title="University of Bergamo">University of Bergamo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Istituto_Nazionale_di_Fisica_Nucleare" title="Istituto Nazionale di Fisica Nucleare">INFN</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Sudarshan" title="George Sudarshan">George Sudarshan</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Texas" title="University of Texas">University of Texas</a>); of the 2007 Majorana Prize: <a href="/wiki/Lee_Smolin" title="Lee Smolin">Lee Smolin</a> (<a href="/wiki/Perimeter_Institute_for_Theoretical_Physics" title="Perimeter Institute for Theoretical Physics">Perimeter Institute for Theoretical Physics</a>, Canada), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eliano_Pessa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eliano Pessa (page does not exist)">Eliano Pessa</a> (Centro Interdipartimentale di Scienze Cognitive, Università di Pavia and Dipartimento di Psicologia, Università di Pavia Piazza Botta, Italy) and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marcello_Cini&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marcello Cini (page does not exist)">Marcello Cini</a> (Dipartimento di Fisica, Università La Sapienza, Roma, Italy).</p>
