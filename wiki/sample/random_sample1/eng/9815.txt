<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Naming conventions in Ethiopia and Eritrea</h1>

<p>The <b>naming convention used in <a href="/wiki/Eritrea" title="Eritrea">Eritrea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a></b> does not have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_name" title="Family name">family names</a> and typically consists of an individual personal name and a separate <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a>. This is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Arabic_name" title="Arabic name">Arabic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">Icelandic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somali_name" title="Somali name">Somali</a> naming conventions. Traditionally for the habesha people the lineage is traced paternally, legislation has been passed in Eritrea that allows for this to be done on the maternal side as well. The word "Habesha" (English "Abyssinian") is an <a href="/wiki/Ethnonym" title="Ethnonym">ethnonym</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Abyssinian_people" title="Abyssinian people">Abyssinian people</a>.</p>
<p>In this convention, children are given a name at birth, by which name they will be known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eritrea_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eritrea-1">[1]</a></sup> To differentiate from others in the same generation with the same name, their father's first name and sometimes grandfather's first name is added. This may continue <i>ad infinitum</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bay_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-bay-2">[2]</a></sup> In the West, this is often mistaken for a <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> (family name)—but unlike <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> names, different generations do not have the same second or third names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hadiya_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hadiya-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In marriage, unlike in some Western countries, women do not change their name, as the second name is not a surname.</p>

<p>In the example above, the progenitors, <i>Feiven</i> and <i>Senai</i>, may be differentiated from others in their generation by their father's name. In this example, Feiven's and Senai's fathers' first names are <i>Tewolde</i> and <i>Abraham</i> respectively.</p>
<p>Feiven and Senai have a daughter and a son, each of whom is married and has a child. The first to have a child (a son) is their daughter, Yordanos Senai; she and her husband name the boy <i>Ammanuel</i>. The next sibling to have a child is Yordanos' brother, Zerezghi Senai; this child is also a son. As it is against custom to name a child after a living family member, his parents give him a different first name than his cousin: <i>Afwerki</i>. Ammanuel and Afwerki would each get their father's first name for their last.</p>
<p>In the diaspora, this method is sometimes dropped in favor of adopting the grandfather's name (the father's "last name") as a surname in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Personal_name#Naming_conventions" title="Personal name">Western naming conventions</a>. In contemporary Eritrea (post-Independence from Ethiopia), a person's legal name consists of their given name, followed by the given name of a parent and the given name a parent of that parent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Meanwhile in modern Ethiopia, a person's legal name includes both the father's and grandfather's names (the father's full name), so that the father's name becomes the child's "middle name".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hadiya_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hadiya-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
