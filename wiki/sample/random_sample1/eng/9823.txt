<p>An <b>epigram</b> is a brief, interesting, memorable, and sometimes surprising or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satirical" title="Satirical">satirical</a> statement. Derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">ἐπίγραμμα</span> <i>epigramma</i> "inscription" from ἐπιγράφειν <i>epigraphein</i> "to write on, to inscribe",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_device" title="Literary device">literary device</a> has been employed for over two millennia.</p>
<p>The presence of wit or sarcasm tends to distinguish non-poetic epigrams from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphorisms" title="Aphorisms">aphorisms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adage" title="Adage">adages</a>, which may lack them.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Greek">Ancient Greek</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epigram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ancient Greek">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Greek tradition of epigrams began as poems inscribed on votive offerings at sanctuaries – including statues of athletes – and on funerary monuments, for example <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Thermopylae#Epitaph_of_Simonides" title="Battle of Thermopylae">"Go tell it to the Spartans, passersby..."</a>. These original epigrams did the same job as a short prose text might have done, but in verse. Epigram became a literary genre in the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a>, probably developing out of scholarly collections of inscriptional epigrams.</p>
<p>Though modern epigrams are usually thought of as very short, Greek literary epigram was not always as short as later examples, and the divide between "epigram" and "<a href="/wiki/Elegy" title="Elegy">elegy</a>" is sometimes indistinct (they share a characteristic metre, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elegiac_couplets" title="Elegiac couplets">elegiac couplets</a>); all the same, the origin of the genre in inscription exerted a residual pressure to keep things concise. Many of the characteristic types of literary epigram look back to inscriptional contexts, particularly funerary epigram, which in the Hellenistic era becomes a literary exercise. Many "sympotic" epigrams combine sympotic and funerary elements – they tell their readers (or listeners) to drink and live for today because life is short.</p>
<p>Epigrams are also thought of as having a "point" – that is, the poem ends in a punchline or satirical twist. By no means do all Greek epigrams behave this way; many are simply descriptive. Epigram is associated with 'point' because the European epigram tradition takes the Latin poet <a href="/wiki/Martial" title="Martial">Martial</a> as its principal model; he copied and adapted Greek models (particularly the contemporary poets <a href="/wiki/Lucillius" title="Lucillius">Lucillius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicarchus" title="Nicarchus">Nicarchus</a>) selectively and in the process redefined the genre, aligning it with the indigenous Roman tradition of 'satura', hexameter <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satire</a>, as practised by (among others) his contemporary <a href="/wiki/Juvenal" title="Juvenal">Juvenal</a>. Greek epigram was actually much more diverse, as the <a href="/wiki/Milan_Papyrus" title="Milan Papyrus">Milan Papyrus</a> now indicates.</p>
<p>A major source for Greek literary epigram is the <i><a href="/wiki/Greek_Anthology" title="Greek Anthology">Greek Anthology</a></i>, a compilation from the 10th century AD based on older collections. It contains epigrams ranging from the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a> through the Imperial period and <a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a> into the compiler's own <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> era – a thousand years of short elegiac texts on every topic under the sun. The <i>Anthology</i> includes one book of Christian epigrams as well as one book of erotic and amorous epigrams called the Μουσα Παιδικη (Mousa Paidike, "The Boyish Muse").</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Roman">Ancient Roman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epigram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient Roman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Roman epigrams owe much to their Greek predecessors and contemporaries. Roman epigrams, however, were often more satirical than Greek ones, and at times used obscene language for effect. Latin epigrams could be composed as inscriptions or <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a>, such as this one from <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>, which exists in several versions and seems from its inexact meter to have been composed by a less educated person. Its content, of course, makes it clear how popular such poems were:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Admiror, O paries, te non cecidisse ruinis</dd>
<dd>qui tot scriptorum taedia sustineas.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>I'm astonished, wall, that you haven't collapsed into ruins,</dd>
<dd>since you're holding up the weary verse of so many poets.</dd>
</dl>
<p>However, in the literary world, epigrams were most often gifts to patrons or entertaining verse to be published, not inscriptions. Many Roman writers seem to have composed epigrams, including <a href="/wiki/Domitius_Marsus" title="Domitius Marsus">Domitius Marsus</a>, whose collection <i>Cicuta</i> (now lost) was named after the poisonous plant <i><a href="/wiki/Cicuta" title="Cicuta">Cicuta</a></i> for its biting wit, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucan_(poet)" title="Lucan (poet)">Lucan</a>, more famous for his epic <i><a href="/wiki/Pharsalia" title="Pharsalia">Pharsalia</a></i>. Authors whose epigrams survive include <a href="/wiki/Catullus" title="Catullus">Catullus</a>, who wrote both invectives and love epigrams – his poem 85 is one of the latter.</p>
<dl>
<dd>Odi et amo. Quare id faciam fortasse requires.</dd>
<dd>Nescio, sed fieri sentio, et excrucior.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>I hate and I love. Maybe you'd like to know why I do?</dd>
<dd>I don't know, but I feel it happening, and I'm crucified.</dd>
</dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Martial" title="Martial">Martial</a>, however, is considered to be the master of the Latin epigram.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> His technique relies heavily on the satirical poem with a joke in the last line, thus drawing him closer to the modern idea of epigram as a genre. Here he defines his genre against a (probably fictional) critic (in the latter half of 2.77):</p>
<dl>
<dd>Disce quod ignoras: Marsi doctique Pedonis</dd>
<dd>saepe duplex unum pagina tractat opus.</dd>
<dd>Non sunt longa quibus nihil est quod demere possis,</dd>
<dd>sed tu, Cosconi, disticha longa facis.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Learn what you don't know: one work of (Domitius) Marsus or learned Pedo</dd>
<dd>often stretches out over a doublesided page.</dd>
<dd>A work isn't long if you can't take anything out of it,</dd>
<dd>but you, Cosconius, write even a couplet too long.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Poets known for their epigrams whose work has been lost include <a href="/wiki/Cornificia" title="Cornificia">Cornificia</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="English">English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epigram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In early English literature the short couplet poem was dominated by the poetic epigram and proverb, especially in the translations of the Bible and the Greek and Roman poets. Since 1600, two successive lines of verse that rhyme with each other, known as a <a href="/wiki/Couplet" title="Couplet">couplet</a> featured as a part of the longer sonnet form, most notably in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare%27s" title="William Shakespeare's">William Shakespeare's</a> sonnets. <a href="/wiki/Sonnet_76" title="Sonnet 76">Sonnet 76</a> is an excellent example. The two line poetic form as a <a href="/wiki/Closed_couplet" title="Closed couplet">closed couplet</a> was also used by <a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">William Blake</a> in his poem <a href="/wiki/Auguries_of_Innocence" title="Auguries of Innocence">Auguries of Innocence</a> and also by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byron" title="Byron">Byron</a> (<a href="/wiki/Don_Juan_(Byron)" title="Don Juan (Byron)">Don Juan (Byron)</a> XIII); <a href="/wiki/John_Gay" title="John Gay">John Gay</a> (Fables); <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">Alexander Pope</a> (An Essay on Man).</p>
<p>The first work of English literature penned in North America was Robert Hayman's <i>Quodlibets, Lately Come Over from New Britaniola, Old Newfoundland,</i> which is a collection of over 300 epigrams, many of which do not conform to the two-line rule or trend. While the collection was written between 1618 and 1628 in what is now Harbour Grace, Newfoundland, it was published shortly after his return to Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In Victorian times the epigram couplet was often used by the prolific American poet <a href="/wiki/Emily_Dickinson" title="Emily Dickinson">Emily Dickinson</a>. Her poem No. 1534 is a typical example of her eleven poetic epigrams. The novelist <a href="/wiki/George_Eliot" title="George Eliot">George Eliot</a> also included couplets throughout her writings. Her best example is in her sequenced sonnet poem entitled <i>Brother and Sister</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in which each of the eleven sequenced sonnet ends with a couplet. In her sonnets, the preceding lead-in-line, to the couplet ending of each, could be thought of as a title for the couplet, as is shown in Sonnet VIII of the sequence.</p>
<p>During the early 20th century, the rhymed epigram couplet form developed into a fixed verse image form, with an integral title as the third line. <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_Crapsey" title="Adelaide Crapsey">Adelaide Crapsey</a> codified the couplet form into a two line rhymed verse of ten syllables per line with her image couplet poem <i>On Seeing Weather-Beaten Trees</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> first published in 1915.</p>
<p>By the 1930s, the five-line <a href="/wiki/Cinquain" title="Cinquain">cinquain</a> verse form became widely known in the poetry of the Scottish poet <a href="/wiki/William_Soutar" title="William Soutar">William Soutar</a>. These were originally labelled epigrams but later identified as image cinquains in the style of <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_Crapsey" title="Adelaide Crapsey">Adelaide Crapsey</a>. <a href="/wiki/J._V._Cunningham" title="J. V. Cunningham">J. V. Cunningham</a> was also a noted writer of epigrams, (a medium suited to a 'short-breathed' person).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poetic_epigrams">Poetic epigrams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epigram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Poetic epigrams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-poetic_epigrams">Non-poetic epigrams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epigram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Non-poetic epigrams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Occasionally, simple and witty statements, though not poetic per se, may also be considered epigrams. <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wilde</a>'s witticisms such as "I can resist everything except temptation" are considered epigrams. Wilde's statement, "the only thing worse than being talked about is not being talked about" is another example. This shows the epigram's tendency towards <a href="/wiki/Paradox" title="Paradox">paradox</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Parker" title="Dorothy Parker">Dorothy Parker</a>'s one-liners can be considered epigrams, as can <a href="/wiki/Macdonald_Carey" title="Macdonald Carey">Macdonald Carey</a>'s statement, "like sands through the hourglass, so are the days of our lives". <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Friedrich Nietzsche</a> considered that, "a witticism is an epigram on the death of a feeling," in <a href="/wiki/Human,_All_Too_Human" title="Human, All Too Human">Human, All Too Human</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (October 2014)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Wit or sarcasm help distinguish epigrams from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphorisms" title="Aphorisms">aphorisms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adage" title="Adage">adages</a>, which may lack them.</p>
<p>Epigrams are sometimes particularly pointed or much-quoted quotations taken from longer works.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
