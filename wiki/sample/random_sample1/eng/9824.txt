<p><b>Epirubicin</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Anthracycline" title="Anthracycline">anthracycline</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medication" title="Medication">drug</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a>. It can be used in combination with other medications to treat breast cancer in patients who have had surgery to remove the tumor. It is marketed by <a href="/wiki/Pfizer" title="Pfizer">Pfizer</a> under the trade name <b>Ellence</b> in the US and <b>Pharmorubicin</b> or <b>Epirubicin Ebewe</b> elsewhere.</p>
<p>Similarly to other anthracyclines, epirubicin acts by <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(chemistry)" title="Intercalation (chemistry)">intercalating</a> <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> strands. Intercalation results in complex formation which inhibits DNA and RNA synthesis. It also triggers DNA cleavage by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topoisomerase_II" title="Topoisomerase II">topoisomerase II</a>, resulting in mechanisms that lead to cell death. Binding to cell membranes and plasma proteins may be involved in the compound's cytotoxic effects. Epirubicin also generates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical">free radicals</a> that cause cell and DNA damage.</p>
<p>Epirubicin is favoured over <a href="/wiki/Doxorubicin" title="Doxorubicin">doxorubicin</a>, the most popular anthracycline, in some <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy_regimen" title="Chemotherapy regimen">chemotherapy regimens</a> as it appears to cause fewer side-effects. Epirubicin has a different spatial orientation of the hydroxyl group at the 4' carbon of the sugar - it has the opposite chirality - which may account for its faster elimination and reduced toxicity. Epirubicin is primarily used against breast and ovarian cancer, gastric cancer, lung cancer and lymphomas.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_history">Development history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Epirubicin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first trial of epirubicin in humans was published in 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Upjohn" title="Upjohn">Upjohn</a> applied for approval by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration_(United_States)" title="Food and Drug Administration (United States)">U.S. Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) in node-positive breast cancer in 1984, but was turned down because of lack of data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It appears to have been licensed for use in Europe from around this time however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1999 <a href="/wiki/Pharmacia" title="Pharmacia">Pharmacia</a> (who had by then merged with Upjohn) received FDA approval for the use of epirubicin as a component of adjuvant therapy in node-positive patients.</p>
<p>Patent protection for epirubicin expired in August 2007.</p>
