<p><i><b>X2</b></i> (sometimes promoted as <i><b>X2: X-Men United</b></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greg_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-greg-3">[3]</a></sup> or internationally as <i><b>X-Men 2</b></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>) is a 2003 American <a href="/wiki/Superhero_film" title="Superhero film">superhero film</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/X-Men" title="X-Men">X-Men</a> superhero team appearing in <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Comics" title="Marvel Comics">Marvel Comics</a>, distributed by <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">sequel</a> to 2000's <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men_(film)" title="X-Men (film)">X-Men</a></i> and the second installment in the <a href="/wiki/X-Men_(film_series)" title="X-Men (film series)"><i>X-Men</i> film series</a>. The film was directed by <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Singer" title="Bryan Singer">Bryan Singer</a>, written by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Dougherty" title="Michael Dougherty">Michael Dougherty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dan_Harris_(screenwriter)" title="Dan Harris (screenwriter)">Dan Harris</a>, and <a href="/wiki/David_Hayter" title="David Hayter">David Hayter</a>, and features an <a href="/wiki/Ensemble_cast" title="Ensemble cast">ensemble cast</a> including <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Stewart" title="Patrick Stewart">Patrick Stewart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Jackman" title="Hugh Jackman">Hugh Jackman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ian_McKellen" title="Ian McKellen">Ian McKellen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halle_Berry" title="Halle Berry">Halle Berry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Famke_Janssen" title="Famke Janssen">Famke Janssen</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Marsden" title="James Marsden">James Marsden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rebecca_Romijn" title="Rebecca Romijn">Rebecca Romijn-Stamos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Cox_(actor)" title="Brian Cox (actor)">Brian Cox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Cumming" title="Alan Cumming">Alan Cumming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Davison" title="Bruce Davison">Bruce Davison</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anna_Paquin" title="Anna Paquin">Anna Paquin</a>. The plot, inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Graphic_novel" title="Graphic novel">graphic novel</a> <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men:_God_Loves,_Man_Kills" title="X-Men: God Loves, Man Kills">God Loves, Man Kills</a></i>, pits the X-Men and their enemies, the <a href="/wiki/Brotherhood_of_Mutants" title="Brotherhood of Mutants">Brotherhood</a>, against the <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocidal</a> Colonel <a href="/wiki/William_Stryker" title="William Stryker">William Stryker</a> (<a href="/wiki/Brian_Cox_(actor)" title="Brian Cox (actor)">Brian Cox</a>). He leads an assault on <a href="/wiki/Professor_X" title="Professor X">Professor Xavier's</a> school to build his own version of Xavier's mutant-tracking computer <a href="/wiki/Cerebro" title="Cerebro">Cerebro</a>, in order to destroy every mutant on Earth and to save the human race from them.</p>
<p>Development began shortly after <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men_(film)" title="X-Men (film)">X-Men</a></i> (2000). <a href="/wiki/David_Hayter" title="David Hayter">David Hayter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zak_Penn" title="Zak Penn">Zak Penn</a> wrote separate scripts, combining what they felt to be the best elements of both scripts into one screenplay. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Dougherty" title="Michael Dougherty">Michael Dougherty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dan_Harris_(screenwriter)" title="Dan Harris (screenwriter)">Dan Harris</a> were eventually hired to rewrite the work, and changed the characterizations of <a href="/wiki/Beast_(comics)" title="Beast (comics)">Beast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warren_Worthington_III" title="Warren Worthington III">Angel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lady_Deathstrike" title="Lady Deathstrike">Lady Deathstrike</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sentinel_(comics)" title="Sentinel (comics)">Sentinels</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Danger_Room" title="Danger Room">Danger Room</a> were set to appear before being deleted because of budget concerns. The film's premise was influenced by the Marvel Comics storylines <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_Ultimate_X-Men_story_arcs#Return_to_Weapon_X" title="List of Ultimate X-Men story arcs">Return to Weapon X</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men:_God_Loves,_Man_Kills" title="X-Men: God Loves, Man Kills">God Loves, Man Kills</a></i>. Filming began in June 2002 and ended that November. Most of the filming took place at <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Film_Studios" title="Vancouver Film Studios">Vancouver Film Studios</a>, the largest North American production facility outside of Los Angeles. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Production_design" title="Production design">Production designer</a> <a href="/wiki/Guy_Hendrix_Dyas" title="Guy Hendrix Dyas">Guy Hendrix Dyas</a> adapted similar designs by <a href="/wiki/John_Myhre" title="John Myhre">John Myhre</a> from the previous film.</p>
<p><i>X2</i> was released in the United States on May 2, 2003. It received positive reviews from critics, who mostly praised the storyline, action sequences and acting of the film, and it is considered to be <a href="/wiki/List_of_films_considered_the_best" title="List of films considered the best">one of the best superhero films</a> ever made. With its critical success, it grossed over approximately $407 million and received eight <a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award" title="Saturn Award">Saturn Awards</a> nominations.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=X2_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a>, brainwashed <a href="/wiki/Teleportation" title="Teleportation">teleporting</a> <a href="/wiki/Mutant_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Mutant (Marvel Comics)">mutant</a> <a href="/wiki/Nightcrawler_(comics)" title="Nightcrawler (comics)">Nightcrawler</a> attempts to assassinate the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> but fails and escapes. Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/Wolverine" title="Wolverine">Wolverine</a> explores an abandoned military installation at Alkali Lake in <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> for clues to his past, but finds nothing. He returns to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professor_Xavier" title="Professor Xavier">Professor Xavier</a>'s school for mutants, and Xavier tracks Nightcrawler using <a href="/wiki/Cerebro" title="Cerebro">Cerebro</a>. Xavier and <a href="/wiki/Cyclops_(comics)" title="Cyclops (comics)">Cyclops</a> go to question the imprisoned <a href="/wiki/Magneto_(comics)" title="Magneto (comics)">Magneto</a> about the attack, while X-Men <a href="/wiki/Storm_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Storm (Marvel Comics)">Storm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Grey" title="Jean Grey">Jean Grey</a> retrieve Nightcrawler. Meanwhile, military scientist Colonel <a href="/wiki/William_Stryker" title="William Stryker">William Stryker</a> approaches the president and receives approval to investigate Xavier's mansion for their ties to mutants. Stryker's forces invade the school and abduct most of the students, with only Wolverine, <a href="/wiki/Rogue_(comics)" title="Rogue (comics)">Rogue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceman_(comics)" title="Iceman (comics)">Iceman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pyro_(comics)" title="Pyro (comics)">Pyro</a> escaping, and Stryker's assistant <a href="/wiki/Lady_Deathstrike" title="Lady Deathstrike">Yuriko Oyama</a> captures Cyclops and Xavier. During the attack Wolverine confronts Stryker, who knows him by name.</p>
<p>The shape-shifting <a href="/wiki/Mystique_(comics)" title="Mystique (comics)">Mystique</a> gains information about Magneto's prison and helps him escape while also discovering schematics for a second Cerebro. Wolverine, Rogue, Iceman, and Pyro visit Iceman's parents in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> and meet up with Storm, Jean, and Nightcrawler. The <a href="/wiki/Blackbird_(comics)" title="Blackbird (comics)">X-Jet</a> is attacked by fighter jets en route back to the mansion and is shot down, but Magneto saves them from crashing. Magneto explains to the group that Stryker has built the second Cerebro to use it, and Xavier, to telepathically kill every mutant on the planet. Stryker's son, Jason, is a mutant with mind-controlling powers, which Stryker will use to force Xavier to do this. Stryker had also previously used Jason's powers to orchestrate Nightcrawler's attack as a pretense to gain approval to invade Xavier's mansion. Magneto also tells Wolverine that Stryker was the man who grafted his <a href="/wiki/Adamantium" title="Adamantium">adamantium</a> skeleton onto his bones. Jean reads Nightcrawler's mind and determines that Stryker's base is underground in a dam at Alkali Lake.</p>
<p>The mutants infiltrate Stryker's base and Magneto and Mystique go to disable Cerebro before the brainwashed Xavier can activate it. Storm and Nightcrawler rescue the captured students, and Jean fights a mind-controlled Cyclops; their battle frees Cyclops but damages the dam, which begins to rupture. Wolverine finds Stryker in an adamantium smelting lab and remembers it as where he received his adamantium skeleton. Wolverine fights and kills Yuriko, then chases Stryker to a helicopter pad and chains him to the helicopter's wheel. Magneto stops Cerebro and, using Mystique impersonating Stryker to command Jason, has Xavier redirect its powers on normal humans. The two subsequently use Stryker's helicopter to escape, accompanied by Pyro who has been swayed to Magneto's views. Nightcrawler teleports Storm inside Cerebro, where she creates a snowstorm to break Jason's concentration and free Xavier from his control.</p>
<p>The X-Men flee the dam as water engulfs it, but the damaged X-Jet struggles to take flight. Jean sacrifices herself to hold back the flood of water while getting the jet into the air, and is then consumed by the flood and presumed dead. The X-Men give the president Stryker's files, and Xavier warns him that humans and mutants must work together to build peace. Back at the school, Xavier, Cyclops, and Wolverine remember Jean, and Xavier begins to hold a class.</p>
<p>The film ends with a voice-over narration from Jean accompanied by the flooded Akali Lake, a fiery <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Force_(comics)" title="Phoenix Force (comics)">Phoenix</a>-like shape rising from the water.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=X2_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=X2_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The financial and critical success of <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men_(film)" title="X-Men (film)">X-Men</a></i> persuaded <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> to immediately commission a sequel. Starting in November 2000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-second.2Funcanny_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-second.2Funcanny-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Singer" title="Bryan Singer">Bryan Singer</a> researched various storylines (one of them being the <a href="/wiki/Legacy_Virus" title="Legacy Virus">Legacy Virus</a>) of the <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men" title="X-Men">X-Men</a></i> comic book series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-next_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-next-18">[18]</a></sup> Singer wanted to study, "the human perspective, the kind of blind rage that feeds into warmongering and <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorism</a>,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> citing a need for a "human villain".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-second.2Funcanny_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-second.2Funcanny-17">[17]</a></sup> Bryan and producer <a href="/wiki/Tom_DeSanto" title="Tom DeSanto">Tom DeSanto</a> envisioned <i>X2</i> as <a href="/wiki/X-Men_(film_series)" title="X-Men (film series)">the film series</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/The_Empire_Strikes_Back" title="The Empire Strikes Back">The Empire Strikes Back</a></i>, in that the characters are "all split apart, and then dissected, and revelations occur that are significant... the romance comes to fruition and a lot of things happen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Producer <a href="/wiki/Avi_Arad" title="Avi Arad">Avi Arad</a> announced a planned November 2002 theatrical release date,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/David_Hayter" title="David Hayter">David Hayter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zak_Penn" title="Zak Penn">Zak Penn</a> were hired to write separate scripts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xcons_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-xcons-22">[22]</a></sup> Hayter and Penn combined what they felt to be the best elements of both scripts into one screenplay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Singer and Hayter worked on another script, finishing in October 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Dougherty" title="Michael Dougherty">Michael Dougherty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dan_Harris_(screenwriter)" title="Dan Harris (screenwriter)">Dan Harris</a> were hired to rewrite Hayter and Penn's script in February 2002,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> turning down the opportunity to write <i><a href="/wiki/Urban_Legends:_Bloody_Mary" title="Urban Legends: Bloody Mary">Urban Legends: Bloody Mary</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Warren_Worthington_III" title="Warren Worthington III">Angel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beast_(comics)" title="Beast (comics)">Beast</a> appeared in early drafts, but were deleted because there were too many characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legacy_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Legacy-9">[9]</a></sup> Dr. Hank McCoy, however, can be seen on a television interview in one scene. Beast's appearance was to resemble <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lee" title="Jim Lee">Jim Lee</a>'s 1991 artwork of the character in the series <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men:_Legacy" title="X-Men: Legacy">X-Men: Legacy</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legacy_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Legacy-9">[9]</a></sup> Angel was to have been a mutant experiment by William Stryker, transforming into Archangel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-writers.2Fproducers_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-writers.2Fproducers-13">[13]</a></sup> A reference to Dougherty's and Harris' efforts to include Angel remains in the form of an X-ray on display in one of Stryker's labs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-next_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-next-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tyler_Mane" title="Tyler Mane">Tyler Mane</a> was to reprise as <a href="/wiki/Sabretooth_(comics)" title="Sabretooth (comics)">Sabretooth</a> before the character was deleted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In Hayter's script, the role eventually filled by <a href="/wiki/Lady_Deathstrike" title="Lady Deathstrike">Lady Deathstrike</a> was Anne Reynolds, a character who appeared in <i>God Loves, Man Kills</i> as Stryker's personal assistant/assassin. Singer changed her to Deathstrike, citing a need for "another kick-ass mutant".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-second.2Funcanny_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-second.2Funcanny-17">[17]</a></sup> There was to be more development on <a href="/wiki/Cyclops_(comics)" title="Cyclops (comics)">Cyclops</a> and <a href="/wiki/Professor_X" title="Professor X">Professor X</a> being brainwashed by Stryker. The scenes were shot, but Fox cut them out because of time length and story complications. Hayter was disappointed, feeling that <a href="/wiki/James_Marsden" title="James Marsden">James Marsden</a> deserved more screentime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-writers.2Fproducers_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-writers.2Fproducers-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rewrites were commissioned once more, specifically to give <a href="/wiki/Halle_Berry" title="Halle Berry">Halle Berry</a> more screen time. This was because of her recent popularity in <i><a href="/wiki/Monster%27s_Ball" title="Monster's Ball">Monster's Ball</a></i>, earning her the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Actress">Academy Award for Best Actress</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> A budget cut meant that the <a href="/wiki/Sentinel_(comics)" title="Sentinel (comics)">Sentinels</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-next_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-next-18">[18]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Danger_Room" title="Danger Room">Danger Room</a> were dropped. <a href="/wiki/Guy_Hendrix_Dyas" title="Guy Hendrix Dyas">Guy Hendrix Dyas</a> and a production crew had already constructed the Danger Room set. In the words of Dyas, "The control room [of the danger room] was a large propeller that actually rotated around the room so that you can sit up [in that control room] and travel around the subject who is in the middle of the control room. The idea for the traveling is that if it's a mutant has some kind of mind control powers they can't connect."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-X2_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-X2-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=X2_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Producer <a href="/wiki/Lauren_Shuler_Donner" title="Lauren Shuler Donner">Lauren Shuler Donner</a> had hoped to start filming in March 2002,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xcons_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-xcons-22">[22]</a></sup> but production did not begin until June 17, 2002 in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a> and ended by November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greg_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-greg-3">[3]</a></sup> Over sixty-four sets were used in thirty-eight different locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-X2_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-X2-10">[10]</a></sup> The film crew encountered problems when there was insufficient snow in <a href="/wiki/Kananaskis_Improvement_District" title="Kananaskis Improvement District">Kananaskis, Alberta</a> for them to use for some scenes. A large amount of fake snow was then applied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The idea to have <a href="/wiki/Jean_Grey" title="Jean Grey">Jean Grey</a> sacrifice herself at the end and to be resurrected in a third installment was highly secretive. Singer did not tell <a href="/wiki/Famke_Janssen" title="Famke Janssen">Famke Janssen</a> until midway through filming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-update_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-update-30">[30]</a></sup> Cinematographer <a href="/wiki/Newton_Thomas_Sigel" title="Newton Thomas Sigel">Newton Thomas Sigel</a> and two stunt drivers nearly died when filming the scene in which <a href="/wiki/Pyro_(comics)" title="Pyro (comics)">Pyro</a> has a dispute with <a href="/wiki/Police_officer" title="Police officer">police officers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-singeraudio_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-singeraudio-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Ottman" title="John Ottman">John Ottman</a> composed the <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">score</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ottman_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-ottman-31">[31]</a></sup> Ottman established a new title theme, as well as themes specifically for <a href="/wiki/Magneto_(comics)" title="Magneto (comics)">Magneto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Grey" title="Jean Grey">Jean Grey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nightcrawler_(comics)" title="Nightcrawler (comics)">Nightcrawler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mystique_(comics)" title="Mystique (comics)">Mystique</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pyro_(comics)" title="Pyro (comics)">Pyro</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ottmusic_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-ottmusic-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_effects">Design and effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=X2_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Design and effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nightcrawler_from_X2.jpg"><img alt="Close up of a sitting man with hands like claws, a long, pointed tail, pointed ears, and an intimidating, ghoulish expression on his face." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="351" data-file-width="234" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c9/Nightcrawler_from_X2.jpg/200px-Nightcrawler_from_X2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c9/Nightcrawler_from_X2.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/Nightcrawler_(comics)" title="Nightcrawler (comics)">Nightcrawler</a>'s tail was mainly <a href="/wiki/Computer-generated_imagery" title="Computer-generated imagery">computer-generated</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Alan_Cumming" title="Alan Cumming">Alan Cumming</a> sometimes used one made of rubber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-singeraudio_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-singeraudio-12">[12]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Singer and Sigel credited <i><a href="/wiki/Road_to_Perdition" title="Road to Perdition">Road to Perdition</a></i> as a visual influence. Though Sigel shot the first <i>X-Men</i> in <a href="/wiki/Anamorphic_format" title="Anamorphic format">anamorphic format</a>, he opted to shoot <i>X2</i> in <a href="/wiki/Super_35" title="Super 35">Super 35</a>. Sigel felt the recent improvements in film stocks and optics increased the advantages of using spherical lenses, even if the blowup to anamorphic must be accomplished optically instead of digitally. Sigel noted, "If you think about it, every anamorphic lens is simply a spherical lens with an anamorphizer on it. They'll never be as good as the spherical lenses that they emulate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silberg_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silberg-33">[33]</a></sup> Cameras that were used during filming included two <a href="/wiki/Panavision_cameras#Panaflex_Millennium" title="Panavision cameras">Panaflex Millenniums</a> and a Millennium XL, as well as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aaton" title="Aaton">Aaton</a> 35mm. Singer also used <a href="/wiki/Zoom_lens" title="Zoom lens">zoom lenses</a> more often than he did in his previous films, while Sigel used a <a href="/wiki/Frazier_lens" title="Frazier lens">Frazier lens</a> specifically for dramatic moments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silberg_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Silberg-33">[33]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Blackbird_(comics)" title="Blackbird (comics)">Blackbird</a> was redesigned and increased in virtual size from 60 feet to 85 feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-X2_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-X2-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Myhre" title="John Myhre">John Myhre</a> served as the <a href="/wiki/Production_designer" title="Production designer">production designer</a> on the first film, but Singer hired <a href="/wiki/Guy_Hendrix_Dyas" title="Guy Hendrix Dyas">Guy Hendrix Dyas</a> for <i>X2</i>, which was his first film as a production designer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> For scenes involving Stryker's Alkali Base, <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Film_Studios" title="Vancouver Film Studios">Vancouver Film Studios</a>, the largest <a href="/wiki/Sound_stage" title="Sound stage">sound stage</a> in North America, was reserved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legacy_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Legacy-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Visual effects supervisor Mike Fink was not satisfied with his work on the previous film, despite the fact it nearly received an <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Visual_Effects" title="Academy Award for Best Visual Effects">Academy Award nomination</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Up to 520 shots were created for <i>X-Men</i>, while <i>X2</i> commissioned roughly 800.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> A new <a href="/wiki/Computer_program" title="Computer program">computer program</a> was created by <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_Hues_Studios" title="Rhythm and Hues Studios">Rhythm and Hues</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Dogfight" title="Dogfight">dogfight</a> <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> scene. <a href="/wiki/Cinesite" title="Cinesite">Cinesite</a> was in charge of scenes concerning <a href="/wiki/Cerebro" title="Cerebro">Cerebro</a>, enlisting a 20-man crew. The Alkali Lake Dam miniature was 25 ft (7.6 m) high and 28 ft (8.5 m) wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Cinesite created 300 <a href="/wiki/Visual_effects" title="Visual effects">visual effects</a> shots, focusing on character animation, while Rhythm and Hues created over 100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=X2_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=X2_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>X2</i> opened in North America on May 2, 2003, accumulating $85,558,731 on its opening weekend in 3,749 theaters. The film grossed $214,949,694 in North America, earning $192,761,855 worldwide, for a total of $407,711,549. <i>X2</i> was a financial success as it recouped its production budget three times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>X2</i> debuted simultaneously in 93 countries, the largest North American and international opening ever at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In addition, the film is the sixth highest grossing <a href="/wiki/List_of_films_based_on_Marvel_Comics" title="List of films based on Marvel Comics">film based on a Marvel Comic book</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and was the sixth highest-grossing film of 2003,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> also earning $107 million in its first five days of DVD release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-update_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-update-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=X2_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first cut of <i>X2</i> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_picture_rating_system#Restricted" title="Motion picture rating system">rated R</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Association_of_America" title="Motion Picture Association of America">Motion Picture Association of America</a>, due to violent shots with <a href="/wiki/Wolverine_(character)" title="Wolverine (character)">Wolverine</a> when Stryker's army storms the <a href="/wiki/X-Mansion" title="X-Mansion">X-Mansion</a>. A few seconds were cut to secure a PG-13 rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>X2</i> had a video game tie-in, <i><a href="/wiki/X2:_Wolverine%27s_Revenge" title="X2: Wolverine's Revenge">X2: Wolverine's Revenge</a></i>, which is unrelated to the events of the film, although <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Stewart" title="Patrick Stewart">Patrick Stewart</a> voiced <a href="/wiki/Professor_X" title="Professor X">Professor X</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Jackman" title="Hugh Jackman">Hugh Jackman</a> appears as Wolverine on the cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The game received mixed reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MCXB_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-MCXB-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MCPC_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-MCPC-45">[45]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men:_The_Official_Game" title="X-Men: The Official Game">X-Men: The Official Game</a></i> bridges the storyline between <i>X2</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men:_The_Last_Stand" title="X-Men: The Last Stand">X-Men: The Last Stand</a></i>. Specifically, it explains <a href="/wiki/Nightcrawler_(comics)" title="Nightcrawler (comics)">Nightcrawler</a>'s absence from <i>The Last Stand</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chris_Claremont" title="Chris Claremont">Chris Claremont</a> wrote a <a href="/wiki/Novelization" title="Novelization">novelization</a> of the film, which left out its secretive cliffhanger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>X2</i> received widespread critical acclaim, praise aimed at the acting, action, and story. The review-aggregation website <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> reports that 86% of critics viewed the film favorably, based on 222 reviews. The sites consensus states: "Tightly scripted, solidly acted, and impressively ambitious, <i>X2: X-Men United</i> is bigger and better than its predecessor – and a benchmark for comic sequels in general."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> calculated an average score of 68/100 (generally favorable reviews) from 37 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> was impressed by how Singer was able to handle so many characters in one film, but felt "the storyline did not live up to its potential". In addition, Ebert wrote that the film's closing was perfect for a future installment, giving <i>X2</i> three out of four stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Turan" title="Kenneth Turan">Kenneth Turan</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> wrote that it was rare for a sequel to be better than <a href="/wiki/X-Men_(film)" title="X-Men (film)">its predecessor</a>. Turan observed that the film carried emotional themes that are present in the world today and commented that "the acting was better than usual [for a <a href="/wiki/Superhero_film" title="Superhero film">superhero film</a>]".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Travers" title="Peter Travers">Peter Travers</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> wrote that <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Jackman" title="Hugh Jackman">Hugh Jackman</a> heavily improved his performance, concluding "<i>X2</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Blockbuster_(entertainment)" title="Blockbuster (entertainment)">summer firecracker</a>. It's also a tribute to outcasts, teens, <a href="/wiki/Gay" title="Gay">gays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minority_group" title="Minority group">minorities</a>, even <a href="/wiki/Dixie_Chicks" title="Dixie Chicks">Dixie Chicks</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_(film_magazine)" title="Empire (film magazine)">Empire</a></i> called <i>X2</i> the best comic book movie of all time in 2006,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> while <i><a href="/wiki/Wizard_(magazine)" title="Wizard (magazine)">Wizard</a></i> named the film's ending as the 22nd greatest <a href="/wiki/Cliffhanger" title="Cliffhanger">cliffhanger</a> of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In May 2007, Rotten Tomatoes listed <i>X2</i> as the fifth greatest <a href="/wiki/List_of_films_based_on_comics" title="List of films based on comics">comic book film</a> of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>The film won the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award_for_Best_Science_Fiction_Film" title="Saturn Award for Best Science Fiction Film">Saturn Award for Best Science Fiction Film</a>. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Singer" title="Bryan Singer">Bryan Singer</a> (<a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Saturn Award for Best Director">Direction</a>), <a href="/wiki/Dan_Harris_(screenwriter)" title="Dan Harris (screenwriter)">Dan Harris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Dougherty" title="Michael Dougherty">Michael Dougherty</a> (<a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award_for_Best_Writing" title="Saturn Award for Best Writing">Writing</a>), and <a href="/wiki/John_Ottman" title="John Ottman">John Ottman</a> (<a href="/wiki/Saturn_Award_for_Best_Music" title="Saturn Award for Best Music">Music</a>) all received nominations. It also received nominations for its costumes, makeup, special effects and DVD release, amounting to a total of eight nominations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Political_Film_Society" title="Political Film Society">Political Film Society</a> honored <i>X2</i> in the categories of <a href="/wiki/Political_Film_Society_Award_for_Human_Rights" title="Political Film Society Award for Human Rights">Human Rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_Film_Society_Award_for_Peace" title="Political Film Society Award for Peace">Peace</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> while the film was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award_for_Best_Dramatic_Presentation" title="Hugo Award for Best Dramatic Presentation">Hugo Award for Best Dramatic Presentation (Long Form)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mick_LaSalle" title="Mick LaSalle">Mick LaSalle</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> was critical of the storyline, special effects and action scenes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joe_Morgenstern" title="Joe Morgenstern">Joe Morgenstern</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> specifically referred to the film as "fast-paced, slow-witted".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hunter" title="Stephen Hunter">Stephen Hunter</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> wrote "Of the many comic book superhero movies, this is by far the lamest, the loudest, the longest".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Corliss" title="Richard Corliss">Richard Corliss</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> argued that Singer depended too much on seriousness and that he did not have enough sensibilities to communicate to an audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Film_score" title="Film score">film's score</a> was composed by <a href="/wiki/John_Ottman" title="John Ottman">John Ottman</a>, a regular collaborator with film director <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Singer" title="Bryan Singer">Bryan Singer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ottman_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-ottman-31">[31]</a></sup> The soundtrack album <i>X2: Original Motion Picture Score</i> was released on April 29, 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Ottman used a sample of <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_Amadeus_Mozart" title="Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart">Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Requiem_(Mozart)" title="Requiem (Mozart)">Requiem</a></i> as the basis for the music in scenes featuring Nightcrawler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ottmusic_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-ottmusic-32">[32]</a></sup> As well as the music on the album, tracks by <a href="/wiki/Conjure_One" title="Conjure One">Conjure One</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%27N_Sync" title="'N Sync">'N Sync</a> also featured in the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 19, 2012, <a href="/wiki/La-La_Land_Records" title="La-La Land Records">La-La Land Records</a> issued an expanded version of Ottman's score, including the specially recorded version of <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Newman_(composer)" title="Alfred Newman (composer)">Alfred Newman</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twentieth_Century_Fox" title="Twentieth Century Fox">Twentieth Century Fox</a> fanfare incorporating Ottman's film theme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ottmusic_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-ottmusic-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=X2_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>X2</i> was released on <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> in widescreen and full frame formats in 2003. The two-disc DVD includes over three hours of special features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>X2</i> was also released on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray_Disc" title="Blu-ray Disc">Blu-ray</a>, and additionally as a Blu-ray, DVD and digital-copy combination in 2011 with special features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-66">[66]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequel">Sequel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=X2_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sequel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the success of the second film in the franchise, a sequel titled, <i><a href="/wiki/X-Men:_The_Last_Stand" title="X-Men: The Last Stand">X-Men: The Last Stand</a></i> was released in 2006 albeit not directed by Singer.</p>
