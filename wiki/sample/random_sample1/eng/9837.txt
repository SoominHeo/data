<p><b>Endonuclease G, mitochondrial</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> that in humans is encoded by the <i>ENDOG</i> <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">gene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid7789991_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid7789991-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-entrez_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-entrez-4">[4]</a></sup> This protein primarily participates in caspase-independent apoptosis via DNA degradation when translocating from the mitochondrion to nucleus under oxidative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22160858_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22160858-5">[5]</a></sup> As a result, EndoG has been implicated in <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>, aging, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurodegenerative_disease" title="Neurodegenerative disease">neurodegenerative diseases</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parkinson%E2%80%99s_disease" title="Parkinson’s disease">Parkinson’s disease</a> (PD). Regulation of its expression levels thus holds potential to treat or ameliorate those conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22160858_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22160858-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid24129513_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid24129513-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ENDOG&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The enzyme encoded by this gene is a member of the conserved DNA/RNA non-specific ββα-Me-finger nuclease family and possesses a unique site selectivity of poly(dG).poly(dC) sequences in double-stranded <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>. The protein is initially synthesized as an inactive 33-kDa <a href="/wiki/Precursor_(chemistry)" title="Precursor (chemistry)">precursor</a>. This precursor is activated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteolytic_cleavage" title="Proteolytic cleavage">proteolytic cleavage</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Targeting_sequence" title="Targeting sequence">mitochondrial targeting sequence</a>, thus producing a mature 28-kDa enzyme that is translocated to the mitochondrial <a href="/wiki/Intermembrane_space" title="Intermembrane space">intermembrane space</a>, where it forms an active homodimer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid25401220_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid25401220-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20211237_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid20211237-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19272175_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19272175-9">[9]</a></sup> The H-N-N motif (His-141, Asn-163, Asn-172) is crucial for the protein's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalytic" title="Catalytic">catalytic</a> function and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substrate_specificity" title="Substrate specificity">substrate specificity</a>, and the His-141 <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acid</a> is necessary for <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> coordination. The amino acid Asn-251 also appears to be catalytic, and Glu-271 appears to be another magnesium <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligand</a>, but both are located far from the H-N-N motif and, thus, their interactions are unclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19272175_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19272175-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ENDOG&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The protein encoded by this gene is a nuclear encoded endonuclease that is localized in the mitochondrial intermembrane space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-entrez_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-entrez-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18309324_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18309324-10">[10]</a></sup> The encoded protein is widely distributed among animals and cleaves DNA at GC tracts. This protein is capable of generating the RNA primers required by DNA polymerase gamma to initiate replication of <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-entrez_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-entrez-4">[4]</a></sup> In some apoptotic pathways, EndoG is released from the mitochondrion and migrates to the nucleus, where it degrades <a href="/wiki/Chromatin" title="Chromatin">chromatin</a> with the help of other nuclear proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid22160858_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22160858-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid25401220_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid25401220-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19272175_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19272175-9">[9]</a></sup> In one such pathway, <a href="/wiki/Caspase" title="Caspase">caspase</a>-independent apoptosis, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E3_ligase" title="E3 ligase">E3 ligase</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-terminal" title="C-terminal">C-terminal</a> of Hsc-70 interacting protein (CHIP), a regulator of EndoG expression, functions as a protective mechanism against oxidative stress. Under normal conditions, EndoG remains bound to Hsp70 and CHIP; however, when undergoing oxidative stress, EndoG dissociates from Hsp70 and CHIP and translocates to the nucleus, where it degrades DNA to effect apoptosis. Therefore, maintaining low levels of EndoG could prevent cell death caused by stress conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid23764847_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid23764847-11">[11]</a></sup> In epithelial cells, the nuclear localization and proapoptotic function of EndoG leads it to play a role in cell senescence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20211237_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid20211237-8">[8]</a></sup> In addition to DNA degradation, EndoG also stimulates <a href="/wiki/Enzyme_inhibitor" title="Enzyme inhibitor">inhibitors</a> of apoptosis proteins (IAPs) to target proteins for <a href="/wiki/Proteasome#The_protein_degradation_process" title="Proteasome">proteasomal degradation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid25139236_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid25139236-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ENDOG&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Endonuclase G enzyme is an important constituent in apoptotic signaling and <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_stress" title="Oxidative stress">oxidative stress</a>, most notably as part of the mitochondrial death pathway and cardiac myocyte apoptosis signaling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Programmed cell death is a distinct genetic and biochemical pathway essential to metazoans. An intact death pathway is required for successful embryonic development and the maintenance of normal tissue homeostasis. Apoptosis has proven to be tightly interwoven with other essential cell pathways. The identification of critical control points in the cell death pathway has yielded fundamental insights for basic biology, as well as provided rational targets for new therapeutics a normal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embryologic" title="Embryologic">embryologic</a> processes, or during cell injury (such as ischemia-reperfusion injury during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_attacks" title="Heart attacks">heart attacks</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strokes" title="Strokes">strokes</a>) or during developments and processes in <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>, an apoptotic cell undergoes structural changes including cell shrinkage, plasma membrane blebbing, nuclear condensation, and fragmentation of the <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a>. This is followed by fragmentation into apoptotic bodies that are quickly removed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phagocytes" title="Phagocytes">phagocytes</a>, thereby preventing an <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammatory</a> response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It is a mode of cell death defined by characteristic morphological, biochemical and molecular changes. It was first described as a "shrinkage necrosis", and then this term was replaced by apoptosis to emphasize its role opposite <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a> in tissue kinetics. In later stages of apoptosis the entire cell becomes fragmented, forming a number of plasma membrane-bounded apoptotic bodies which contain nuclear and or cytoplasmic elements. The ultrastructural appearance of <a href="/wiki/Necrosis" title="Necrosis">necrosis</a> is quite different, the main features being mitochondrial swelling, plasma membrane breakdown and cellular disintegration. Apoptosis occurs in many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiological" title="Physiological">physiological</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pathological" title="Pathological">pathological</a> processes. It plays an important role during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embryonal" title="Embryonal">embryonal</a> development as programmed cell death and accompanies a variety of normal involutional processes in which it serves as a mechanism to remove "unwanted" cells.</p>
<p>The BNIP3 pathway involves mitochondrial release and nuclear translocation of the endonuclease G.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It is not clear, however, that how BNIP3 interacts with mitochondria. It has been shown that BNIP3 interacts with the <a href="/wiki/Voltage-dependent_anion_channel" title="Voltage-dependent anion channel">voltage-dependent anion channel</a> (VDAC) to directly induce mitochondrial release and nuclear translocation of EndonucleaseG. Data has identified VDAC as an interacting partner of BNIP3 and provide direct evidence to support that EndoG is a mediator of the BNIP3 cell death pathway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Most notably, Enodnuclease G is pivotal during oxidative stress by ischemia-reperfusion injury, specifically in the myocardium as part of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_attack" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a> (also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ischemic_heart_disease" title="Ischemic heart disease">ischemic heart disease</a>). Ischemic heart disease, which results from an <a href="/wiki/Vascular_occlusion" title="Vascular occlusion">occlusion</a> of one of the major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronary_arteries" title="Coronary arteries">coronary arteries</a>, is currently still the leading cause of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morbidity" title="Morbidity">morbidity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cause_of_death" title="Cause of death">mortality</a> in western society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> During ischemia reperfusion, ROS release substantially contribute to the cell damage and death via a direct effect on the cell as well as via apoptotic signals. More recently, Endonuclease G is considered a determinant of cardiac hypertrophy. A link has been established between Endonuclease G and mitochondrial function during cardiac hypertrophy, partly through the effects of Endo G on Mfn2 and Jp2, and revealed a role for Endonuclease G in the crosstalk between the processes controlled by Mfn2 and Jp2 in maladaptive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiac_hypertrophy" title="Cardiac hypertrophy">cardiac hypertrophy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>Previous studies reported greater efficacy of anticancer drugs when used in conjunction with high EndoG levels. Thus, regulators of EndoG, such as CHIP, could serve as therapeutic targets for oxidative stress-induced cell death in cancer and aging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid23764847_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid23764847-11">[11]</a></sup> Through its association with cell senescence in epithelial cells, EndoG may also contribute to age-related <a href="/wiki/Vascular_disease" title="Vascular disease">vascular diseases</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Arteriosclerosis" title="Arteriosclerosis">arteriosclerosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20211237_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-pmid20211237-8">[8]</a></sup> Similarly, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Myonuclear&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Myonuclear (page does not exist)">myonuclear</a> localization of EndoG is correlated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atrophied" title="Atrophied">atrophied</a> aging <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_muscle" title="Skeletal muscle">skeletal muscle</a>, leading to increased apoptotic signaling and muscle mass loss. EndoG has also been implicated in Parkinson’s disease (PD), as it induces DNA fragmentation in neurons when translocated from the mitochondria to nuclei. This mechanism involves the kynurenine pathway and the permeability transition pore; as such, targeting molecules in this pathway could prevent EndoG-mediated cell death and effectively help treat PD in patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid24129513_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid24129513-6">[6]</a></sup> Similarly, EndoG <a href="/wiki/Gene_knockdown" title="Gene knockdown">knockdown</a> in mice mitigated injurious insults; thus, therapeutic strategies to inhibit or silence EndoG could help protect tissues during injury and disease. So far, two EndoG inhibitors, PNR-3-80 (5-((1-(2-naphthoyl)-5-chloro-1H-indol-3-yl)methylene)-2-thioxodihydropyrimidine-4,6(1H,5H)-dione) and PNR-3-82 (5-((1-(2-naphthoyl)-5-methoxy-1H-indol-3-yl)methylene)-2-thioxodihydropyrimidine-4,6(1H,5H)-dione, have been tested and confirmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid25401220_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-pmid25401220-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
