<p><b>Nvidia Corporation</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'n' in 'no'">n</span><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ɪ/ short 'i' in 'bid'">ɪ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/i/ 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> <span title="English pronunciation respelling"><a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i>in-<b><span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">VID</span></span></b>-eeə</i></a></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (most commonly referred to as <b>Nvidia</b>, stylized as <b>NVIDIA</b>, <b>nVIDIA</b> or <b>nvidia</b>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Technology_company" title="Technology company">technology company</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California" title="Santa Clara, California">Santa Clara, California</a>. Nvidia designs <a href="/wiki/Graphics_processing_unit" title="Graphics processing unit">graphics processing units</a> (GPUs) for the gaming market, as well as <a href="/wiki/System_on_a_chip" title="System on a chip">system on a chip</a> units (SOCs) for the <a href="/wiki/Mobile_computing" title="Mobile computing">mobile computing</a> and automotive market. Nvidia's primary GPU product line, labeled "<a href="/wiki/GeForce" title="GeForce">GeForce</a>", is in direct competition with <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Micro_Devices" title="Advanced Micro Devices">Advanced Micro Devices</a>' (AMD) "<a href="/wiki/Radeon" title="Radeon">Radeon</a>" products. Nvidia expanded its presence in the gaming industry with its handheld <a href="/wiki/Shield_Portable" title="Shield Portable">SHIELD Portable,</a> <a href="/wiki/Shield_Tablet" title="Shield Tablet">SHIELD Tablet</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shield_Android_TV" title="Shield Android TV">SHIELD Android TV</a>.</p>
<p>Since 2014, Nvidia has shifted to become a platform company focused on four markets – gaming, professional visualization, data centers and auto.</p>
<p>In addition to GPU manufacturing, Nvidia provides <a href="/wiki/Parallel_computing" title="Parallel computing">parallel processing</a> capabilities to researchers and scientists that allow them to efficiently run high-performance applications. They are deployed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supercomputing" title="Supercomputing">supercomputing</a> sites around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> More recently, Nvidia has moved into the <a href="/wiki/Mobile_computing" title="Mobile computing">mobile computing</a> market, where it produces <a href="/wiki/Tegra" title="Tegra">Tegra</a> mobile processors for smartphones and tablets, as well as vehicle navigation and entertainment systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In addition to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AMD" title="AMD">AMD</a>, its competitors include <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qualcomm" title="Qualcomm">Qualcomm</a> and <a href="/wiki/ARM_Holdings" title="ARM Holdings">ARM</a> (e.g. because of Denver; while Nvidia also licenses ARM's designs).</p>
<p>Nvidia is now focused on artificial intelligence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> From the company's roots in computer graphics, Nvidia now provides GPU-accelerated computing to the gamers, designers and scientists, allowing them to create experiences in VR, deep learning, professional visualization and autonomous cars.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Company_history">Company history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Company history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name of the company comes from <a href="/wiki/Invidia" title="Invidia">Invidia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman mythology</a> who corresponds to <a href="/wiki/Nemesis_(mythology)" title="Nemesis (mythology)">Nemesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roman_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-roman-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founders_and_initial_investment">Founders and initial investment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founders and initial investment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Three people co-founded Nvidia in April 1993:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jen-Hsun_Huang" title="Jen-Hsun Huang">Jen-Hsun Huang</a> (CEO as of 2013), a Taiwanese-born American, previously Director of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CoreWare&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CoreWare (page does not exist)">CoreWare</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LSI_Logic" title="LSI Logic">LSI Logic</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Microprocessor" title="Microprocessor">microprocessor</a> designer at <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Micro_Devices" title="Advanced Micro Devices">Advanced Micro Devices</a> (AMD).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chris_Malachowsky" title="Chris Malachowsky">Chris Malachowsky</a>, an electrical engineer who worked at <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun Microsystems</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Curtis_Priem" title="Curtis Priem">Curtis Priem</a>, previously a senior staff engineer and graphics chip designer at Sun Microsystems.</li>
</ul>
<p>The founders received venture capital funding from <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_Capital" title="Sequoia Capital">Sequoia Capital</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_releases_and_acquisitions">Major releases and acquisitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Major releases and acquisitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Autumn 1999 saw the release of the <a href="/wiki/GeForce" title="GeForce">GeForce</a> (NV10), most notably introducing on-board <a href="/wiki/Transform,_clipping,_and_lighting" title="Transform, clipping, and lighting">transformation and lighting</a> (T&amp;L) to consumer-level 3D hardware. Running at 120 MHz and featuring four pixel pipelines, it implemented advanced video acceleration, motion compensation, and hardware sub-picture alpha blending. The GeForce outperformed existing products by a wide margin.</p>
<p>Due to the success of its products, Nvidia won the contract to develop the graphics hardware for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Xbox_(console)" title="Xbox (console)">Xbox</a> game console, which earned Nvidia a $200 million advance. However, the project drew the time of many of Nvidia's best engineers away from other projects. In the short term this did not matter, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GeForce_2_Series" title="GeForce 2 Series">GeForce2 GTS</a> shipped in the summer of 2000.</p>
<p>In December 2000, Nvidia reached an agreement to acquire the intellectual assets of its one-time rival <a href="/wiki/3dfx_Interactive" title="3dfx Interactive">3dfx</a>, a pioneer in consumer 3D graphics technology leading the field from mid 1990s until 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The acquisition process was finalized in April 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2002, Nvidia acquired Exluna for an undisclosed sum. Exluna made software rendering tools and the personnel were merged into the Cg project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>In August 2003, Nvidia acquired MediaQ for approximately US$70 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>On April 22, 2004, Nvidia acquired iReady, also a provider of high performance TCP/IP and iSCSI offload <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solutions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2004, saw the announcement that Nvidia would assist <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> with the design of the graphics processor (<a href="/wiki/RSX_%27Reality_Synthesizer%27" title="RSX 'Reality Synthesizer'">RSX</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/PlayStation_3" title="PlayStation 3">PlayStation 3</a> game console. In March 2006, it emerged that Nvidia would deliver <a href="/wiki/RSX_%27Reality_Synthesizer%27" title="RSX 'Reality Synthesizer'">RSX</a> to Sony as an <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_intellectual_property_core" title="Semiconductor intellectual property core">IP core</a>, and that Sony alone would organize the manufacture of the RSX. Under the agreement, Nvidia would provide ongoing support to port the RSX to Sony's fabs of choice (Sony and Toshiba), as well as <a href="/wiki/Die_shrink" title="Die shrink">die shrinks</a> to 65 nm. This practice contrasted with Nvidia's business arrangement with Microsoft, in which Nvidia managed production and delivery of the Xbox GPU through Nvidia's usual third-party foundry contracts. Meanwhile, in May 2005 Microsoft chose to license a design by ATI and to make its own manufacturing arrangements for the <a href="/wiki/Xbox_360" title="Xbox 360">Xbox 360</a> graphics hardware, as had <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a> console (which succeeded the ATI-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_GameCube" title="Nintendo GameCube">Nintendo GameCube</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>On December 14, 2005, Nvidia acquired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acer_Laboratories_Incorporated" title="Acer Laboratories Incorporated">ULI Electronics</a>, which at the time supplied third-party <a href="/wiki/Southbridge_(computing)" title="Southbridge (computing)">southbridge</a> parts for <a href="/wiki/Chipset" title="Chipset">chipsets</a> to <a href="/wiki/ATI_Technologies" title="ATI Technologies">ATI</a>, Nvidia's competitor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In March 2006, Nvidia acquired <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_Graphics" title="Hybrid Graphics">Hybrid Graphics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2006, Nvidia, along with its main rival in the graphics industry AMD (which had acquired ATI), received subpoenas from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">U.S. Department of Justice</a> regarding possible <a href="/wiki/United_States_antitrust_law" title="United States antitrust law">antitrust</a> violations in the graphics card industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> magazine named Nvidia its <i>Company of the Year</i> for 2007, citing the accomplishments it made during the said period as well as during the previous 5 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 5, 2007, Nvidia announced that it had completed the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/PortalPlayer" title="PortalPlayer">PortalPlayer</a>, Inc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2008, Nvidia acquired <a href="/wiki/Ageia" title="Ageia">Ageia</a> Technologies for an undisclosed sum. Ageia developed the <a href="/wiki/PhysX" title="PhysX">PhysX</a> physics engine hardware and SDK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2009, a court consolidated multiple class action suits into one case, titled The NVIDIA GPU Litigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Nvidia agreed to replace faulty chips in or reimburse purchasers who already spent to get their laptop repaired. Nvidia also gave replacement laptops to many users in lieu of making a repair. The replacements and payments were not made until the settlement was finalized in 2011. Users were required to show proof of purchase and mail in their original faulty laptop. The chips were present in a number of Dell and HP laptops, as well as two Apple MacBook Pro models. Although the settlement cost Nvidia millions of dollars, many of the individuals were unhappy with the settlement, and multiple websites and blogs reflected this. The website entitled Fair Nvidia Settlement<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> was one such site.</p>
<p>On January 10, 2011, Nvidia signed a six-year cross-licensing agreement with Intel, marking the end of all outstanding legal disputes between these two companies. According to the agreement, Intel agreed to pay Nvidia $1.5 billion in licensing fees in five annual installments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 15, 2011, Nvidia announced and demonstrated the first quad-core processor for mobile devices at the Mobile World Congress in Barcelona. It was announced that the chip was expected to ship with many <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablets</a> to be released in the second half of 2011,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and the chip, dubbed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tegra_3" title="Tegra 3">Tegra 3</a>, was released on November 9, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2011, it was announced that Nvidia had agreed to acquire <a href="/wiki/Icera" title="Icera">Icera</a>, a baseband chip making company in the UK, for $367 million in cash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>On July 29, 2013, Nvidia announced that they acquired <a href="/wiki/The_Portland_Group" title="The Portland Group">PGI</a> from STMicroelectronics.</p>
<p>On January 6, 2013, Nvidia introduced at CES 2013, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tegra_4" title="Tegra 4">Tegra 4</a> mobile processor (codename "Wayne"), containing 72 GPU cores, a Quad-core ARM Cortex-A15 CPU core, and LTE capability among its features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>On February 19, 2013, Nvidia announced the Tegra 4i (codename "Project Grey"), its first fully integrated 4G LTE mobile processor, featuring 5 times more GPU cores than Tegra 3, 1080p HD support, and Nvidia Chimera Computational Photography Architecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 6, 2014, Nvidia introduced at CES 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Tegra#Tegra_K1" title="Tegra">Tegra K1</a> mobile processor (codename "Logan"), containing 192 GPU cores and a quad-core ARM Cortex-A15 MPCore R3 + low power companion core (32-bit) or dual-core <a href="/wiki/Project_Denver" title="Project Denver">Project Denver</a> (64-bit). As of February 2014, Nvidia claims that the Tegra K1 outperforms both the Xbox 360 and the PS3 hardware.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>On October 1, 2015, Nvidia launched its cloud-based game-streaming service, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GeForce_NOW" title="GeForce NOW">GeForce NOW</a>. Owners of the SHIELD family of gaming devices can join the service to stream more than 80 PC games at up to 1080p resolution and 60fps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>On May 6, 2016, Nvidia introduced at Dreamhack in Austin, Texas, the GeForce GTX 1080 and GTX 1070 built on Nvidia's Pascal-architecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The company also unveiled other features, including Ansel, a new software for in-game photography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2016, court finally decided that Nvidia had to reimburse each owner of GeForce GTX 970 USD 30 for false advertising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="GPU_Technology_Conference">GPU Technology Conference</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: GPU Technology Conference">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>GPU Technology Conference</b> is an annual <a href="/wiki/Convention_(meeting)" title="Convention (meeting)">technical conference</a> started by Nvidia in 2009 which focuses on using the GPU to solve computing challenges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In 2015, the conference attracted over 4000 attendees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>

<p>Nvidia's family includes primarily graphics, wireless communication, PC processors and automotive hardware/software. Some families are listed below:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/GeForce" title="GeForce">GeForce</a>, consumer-oriented graphics processing products.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadro" title="Quadro">Quadro</a> computer-aided design and digital content creation workstation graphics processing products.</li>
<li>NVS, multi-display business graphics solution.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tegra" title="Tegra">Tegra</a>, a <a href="/wiki/System_on_a_chip" title="System on a chip">system on a chip</a> series for mobile devices.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nvidia_Tesla" title="Nvidia Tesla">Tesla</a>, dedicated general purpose GPU for high-end image generation applications in professional and scientific fields.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/NForce" title="NForce">nForce</a>, a motherboard chipset created by Nvidia for Intel (Celeron, Pentium and Core 2) and AMD (Athlon and Duron) microprocessors.</li>
<li>Nvidia Grid, a set of hardware and services by Nvidia for graphics virtualization.</li>
<li>Nvidia Shield, a range of gaming hardware including the <a href="/wiki/Shield_Portable" title="Shield Portable">Shield Portable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shield_Tablet" title="Shield Tablet">Shield Tablet</a> and most recently, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shield_Android_TV" title="Shield Android TV">Shield Android TV</a>.</li>
<li>Nvidia Drive automotive solutions, a range of hardware and software products for assisting car drivers. The <a href="/wiki/Drive_PX-series" title="Drive PX-series">Drive PX-series</a> is a high performance computer platform aimed at autonomous driving through deep learning,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> while Driveworks is an operating system for driverless cars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Open-source_software_support">Open-source software support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nvidia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Open-source software support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_and_open-source_device_drivers:_graphics" title="Free and open-source device drivers: graphics">Free and open-source device drivers: graphics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesa_3D" title="Mesa 3D">Mesa 3D</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nouveau_(software)" title="Nouveau (software)">nouveau (software)</a></div>
<p>Until September 23, 2013, Nvidia had not published any documentation for its hardware,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> meaning that programmers could not write appropriate and effective <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_and_open-source" title="Free and open-source">free and open-source</a> <a href="/wiki/Device_driver" title="Device driver">device driver</a> for Nvidia's products without resorting to (<a href="/wiki/Clean_room_design" title="Clean room design">clean room</a>) <a href="/wiki/Reverse_engineering" title="Reverse engineering">reverse engineering</a>.</p>
<p>Instead, Nvidia provides its own <a href="/wiki/Binary_file" title="Binary file">binary</a> GeForce graphics drivers for <a href="/wiki/X.Org_Server" title="X.Org Server">X.Org</a> and a thin open-source library that interfaces with the <a href="/wiki/Linux_kernel" title="Linux kernel">Linux</a>, <a href="/wiki/FreeBSD" title="FreeBSD">FreeBSD</a> or <a href="/wiki/Solaris_(operating_system)" title="Solaris (operating system)">Solaris</a> kernels and the <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_software" title="Proprietary software">proprietary</a> graphics software. Nvidia also provided but stopped supporting an obfuscated open-source driver that only supports two-dimensional hardware acceleration and ships with the <a href="/wiki/X.Org_Server" title="X.Org Server">X.Org</a> distribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>The proprietary nature of Nvidia's drivers has generated dissatisfaction within <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free-software</a> <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">communities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Some Linux and BSD users insist on using only open-source drivers, and regard Nvidia's insistence on providing nothing more than a binary-only driver as wholly inadequate, given that competing manufacturers (like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intel_Corporation" title="Intel Corporation">Intel</a>) offer support and documentation for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_source" title="Open source">open-source</a> developers, and that others (like <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Micro_Devices" title="Advanced Micro Devices">AMD</a>) release partial documentation and provide some active development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ubuntugamer_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-ubuntugamer-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>Because of the closed nature of the drivers, Nvidia video cards cannot deliver adequate features on some platforms and architectures given that Nvidia only provides x86/x64 driver builds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> As a result, support for 3D graphics acceleration in Linux on <a href="/wiki/PowerPC" title="PowerPC">PowerPC</a> does not exist, nor does support for Linux on the <a href="/wiki/Hypervisor" title="Hypervisor">hypervisor</a>-restricted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PS3" title="PS3">PlayStation 3</a> console.</p>
<p>Some users claim that Nvidia's Linux drivers impose artificial restrictions, like limiting the number of monitors that can be used at the same time, but the company has not commented on these accusations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
