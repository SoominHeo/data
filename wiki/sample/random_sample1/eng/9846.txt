<p><b>Enteroviruses</b> are a genus of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Positive_sense#RNA_sense_in_viruses" title="Positive sense">positive-sense</a> single-stranded <a href="/wiki/RNA_virus" title="RNA virus">RNA viruses</a> associated with several human and mammalian diseases.</p>
<p>Enteroviruses are named by their transmission-route through the intestine (enteric meaning intestinal).</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serologic" title="Serologic">Serologic</a> studies have distinguished 71 human enterovirus <a href="/wiki/Serotype" title="Serotype">serotypes</a> on the basis of <a href="/wiki/Antibody" title="Antibody">antibody</a> neutralization tests. Additional <a href="/wiki/Antigen" title="Antigen">antigenic</a> variants have been defined within several of the serotypes on the basis of reduced or nonreciprocal cross-neutralization between variant strains. On the basis of their <a href="/wiki/Pathogenesis" title="Pathogenesis">pathogenesis</a> in humans and animals, the enteroviruses were originally classified into four groups, <a href="/wiki/Poliovirus" title="Poliovirus">polioviruses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coxsackie_A_virus" title="Coxsackie A virus">Coxsackie A viruses</a> (CA), <a href="/wiki/Coxsackie_B_virus" title="Coxsackie B virus">Coxsackie B viruses</a> (CB), and <a href="/wiki/Echovirus" title="Echovirus">echoviruses</a>, but it was quickly realized that there were significant overlaps in the biological properties of viruses in the different groups. Enteroviruses isolated more recently are named with a system of consecutive numbers: <a href="/wiki/Enterovirus_68" title="Enterovirus 68">EV68</a>, EV69, EV70, <a href="/wiki/Enterovirus_71" title="Enterovirus 71">EV71</a>, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Enteroviruses affect millions of people worldwide each year, and are often found in the respiratory secretions (e.g., saliva, sputum, or nasal mucus) and stool of an infected person. Historically, <a href="/wiki/Poliomyelitis" title="Poliomyelitis">poliomyelitis</a> was the most significant disease caused by an enterovirus, <a href="/wiki/Poliovirus" title="Poliovirus">poliovirus</a>. There are 64 non-polio enteroviruses that can cause disease in humans: 23 Coxsackie A viruses, 6 Coxsackie B viruses, 28 echoviruses, and 5 other enteroviruses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_article_Non-Polio_Enterovirus_Infections_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_article_Non-Polio_Enterovirus_Infections-2">[2]</a></sup> Poliovirus, as well as coxsackie and echovirus are spread through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fecal-oral_route" title="Fecal-oral route">fecal-oral route</a>. Infection can result in a wide variety of symptoms ranging from mild respiratory illness (<a href="/wiki/Common_cold" title="Common cold">common cold</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand,_foot_and_mouth_disease" title="Hand, foot and mouth disease">hand, foot and mouth disease</a>, acute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemorrhagic" title="Hemorrhagic">hemorrhagic</a> <a href="/wiki/Conjunctivitis" title="Conjunctivitis">conjunctivitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aseptic_meningitis" title="Aseptic meningitis">aseptic meningitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myocarditis" title="Myocarditis">myocarditis</a>, severe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neonatal" title="Neonatal">neonatal</a> <a href="/wiki/Sepsis" title="Sepsis">sepsis</a>-like disease, and acute <a href="/wiki/Flaccid_paralysis" title="Flaccid paralysis">flaccid paralysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_article_Non-Polio_Enterovirus_Infections_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_article_Non-Polio_Enterovirus_Infections-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species_and_genetics">Species and genetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Species and genetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Enteroviruses are members of the <a href="/wiki/Picornavirus" title="Picornavirus">picornavirus</a> family, a large and diverse group of small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RNA_viruses" title="RNA viruses">RNA viruses</a> characterized by a single positive-strand genomic RNA. All enteroviruses contain a <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> of approximately 7,500 bases and are known to have a high <a href="/wiki/Mutation_rate" title="Mutation rate">mutation rate</a> due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virus_replication_cycle" title="Virus replication cycle">low-fidelity replication</a> and frequent <a href="/wiki/Genetic_recombination" title="Genetic recombination">recombination</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-China_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-China-4">[4]</a></sup> After infection of the host cell, the genome is translated in a cap-independent manner into a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyprotein" title="Polyprotein">polyprotein</a>, which is subsequently processed by virus-encoded <a href="/wiki/Protease" title="Protease">proteases</a> into the structural <a href="/wiki/Capsid" title="Capsid">capsid</a> proteins and the nonstructural proteins, which are mainly involved in the replication of the virus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The enterovirus <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> includes the following twelve species:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ictvonline1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ictvonline1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Enterovirus A</i> (formerly <i>Human enterovirus A</i>)</li>
<li><i>Enterovirus B</i> (formerly <i>Human enterovirus B</i>)</li>
<li><i>Enterovirus C</i> (formerly <i>Human enterovirus C</i>)</li>
<li><i>Enterovirus D</i> (formerly <i>Human enterovirus D</i>)</li>
<li><i>Enterovirus E</i> (formerly <i>Bovine enterovirus</i> group A)</li>
<li><i>Enterovirus F</i> (formerly <i>Bovine enterovirus</i> group B)</li>
<li><i>Enterovirus G</i> (formerly <i>Porcine enterovirus B</i>)</li>
<li><i>Enterovirus H</i> (formerly <i>Simian enterovirus A</i>)</li>
<li><i>Enterovirus J</i></li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coxsackie_and_echovirus">Coxsackie and echovirus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Coxsackie and echovirus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Coxsackie_A_virus" title="Coxsackie A virus">Coxsackie A virus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coxsackie_B_virus" title="Coxsackie B virus">Coxsackie B virus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Echovirus" title="Echovirus">Echovirus</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coxsackie_virus" title="Coxsackie virus">Coxsackie viruses</a> are a non-<a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetic</a> group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Coxsackie A viruses are mainly associated with human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand,_foot_and_mouth_disease" title="Hand, foot and mouth disease">hand, foot and mouth disease</a>. Coxsackie B viruses can cause signs and symptoms, similar to a "cold," but these viruses also can lead to more serious diseases, including <a href="/wiki/Myocarditis" title="Myocarditis">myocarditis</a> (inflammation of the heart); <a href="/wiki/Pericarditis" title="Pericarditis">pericarditis</a> (inflammation of the sac lining the heart); <a href="/wiki/Meningitis" title="Meningitis">meningitis</a> (inflammation of the membranes that line the brain and spinal cord); and <a href="/wiki/Pancreatitis" title="Pancreatitis">pancreatitis</a> (inflammation of the pancreas).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Echovirus" title="Echovirus">Echoviruses</a> are a cause of many of the nonspecific viral infections. It is mainly found in the intestine, and can cause nervous disorders.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The usual symptoms of Coxsackie and echovirus are fever, mild rash, and mild upper respiratory tract (URT) illness.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enterovirus_68">Enterovirus 68</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Enterovirus 68">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Enterovirus_68" title="Enterovirus 68">Enterovirus 68</a></div>
<p>EV-D68 first was identified in California in 1962. Compared with other enteroviruses, it has been rarely reported in the U.S. in the past 40 years. Most people who get infected are infants, children and teens. EV-D68 usually causes mild to severe respiratory illness; however, the full spectrum of EV-D68 illness is not well-defined. Most start with common cold symptoms of runny nose and cough. Some, but not all, may also have fever. For more severe cases, difficulty breathing, wheezing or problems catching your breath may occur. As of October 4, 2014, there has been one death in New Jersey directly linked to EV-D68,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> as well as one death in Rhode Island<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> attributed to a combination of EV-D68 and sepsis caused by an infection of staphylococcus aureus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enterovirus_71">Enterovirus 71</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Enterovirus 71">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Enterovirus_71" title="Enterovirus 71">Enterovirus 71</a></div>
<p>Enterovirus 71 (EV-71) is notable as one of the major causative agents for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand,_foot_and_mouth_disease" title="Hand, foot and mouth disease">hand, foot and mouth disease</a> (HFMD), and is sometimes associated with severe central nervous system diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lac_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-lac-13">[13]</a></sup> EV71 was first isolated and characterized from cases of neurological disease in California in 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taiwan_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taiwan-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> To date, little is known about the molecular mechanisms of host response to EV71 infection, but increases in the level of mRNAs encoding chemokines, proteins involved in protein degradation, complement proteins, and proapoptotis proteins have been implicated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poliovirus">Poliovirus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Poliovirus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Poliovirus" title="Poliovirus">Poliovirus</a></div>
<p>There are three <a href="/wiki/Serotype" title="Serotype">serotypes</a> of poliovirus, <i>PV1</i>, <i>PV2</i>, and <i>PV3</i>; each with a slightly different <a href="/wiki/Capsid" title="Capsid">capsid</a> protein. Capsid proteins define cellular receptor specificity and virus antigenicity. <i>PV1</i> is the most common form encountered in nature; however, all three forms are extremely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paul_1971_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paul_1971-17">[17]</a></sup> Poliovirus can affect the <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a> and cause <a href="/wiki/Poliomyelitis" title="Poliomyelitis">poliomyelitis</a>.</p>
<p><i>Polioviruses</i> were formerly classified as a species belonging to the genus Enterovirus in the family Picornaviridae. The Poliovirus species has been eliminated from the genus Enterovirus. The following serotypes, Human poliovirus 1, Human poliovirus 2, and Human poliovirus 3, were assigned to the species Human enterovirus C, in the genus Enterovirus in the family Picornaviridae. The type species of the genus Enterovirus was changed from Poliovirus to Human enterovirus C. This has been ratified in April 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-springerlink_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-springerlink-18">[18]</a></sup> The 39th Executive Committee (EC39) of the International Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses (ICTV) met in Canada during June 2007 with new taxonomic proposals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><br/>
Two of the proposals with three changes were:</p>
<ul>
<li>Code 2005.261V.04: To remove the following species Poliovirus from the existing genus Enterovirus in the family Picornaviridae.</li>
<li>Code 2005.262V.04: To assign the viruses; PV-1, PV-2, PV-3 to the existing species Human enterovirus C in the genus Enterovirus in the family Picornaviridae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Code 2005.263V.04: To change the type species Poliovirus from the existing genus Enterovirus in the family Picornaviridae to the type species Human enterovirus C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Proposals approved at the (EC39) meeting of 2007, were sent to members of ICTV via email for ratification and have become official taxonomy. There have been a total of 215 taxonomic proposals, which have been approved and ratified since the 8th ICTV Report of 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><br/>
The ratification process was performed by email. The proposals were sent electronically via email on March 18, 2008 to ICTV members with a request to vote on whether to ratify the taxonomic proposals, with a 1-month deadline. The following are two of the taxonomic proposals with three changes that were ratified by ICTV members in April 2008:<br/>
Picornaviruses</p>
<ul>
<li>2005.261V.04: To remove the following species from the existing genus Enterovirus in the family Picornaviridae: Poliovirus.<br/>
(Note: Poliovirus hereby loses its status as a virus species).</li>
<li>2005.262V.04: To assign the following viruses to the species Human enterovirus C in the existing genus Enterovirus in the family Picornaviridae: Human poliovirus 1, Human poliovirus 2, Human poliovirus 3. (This is not strictly necessary as a taxonomic proposal because it concerns entities below the species level, but it is left in to clarify this reorganization of the Picornaviridae).</li>
<li>2005.263V.04: To change the type species of the genus Enterovirus in the family Picornaviridae, from Poliovirus to Human enterovirus C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-springerlink_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-springerlink-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>The ICTVdb, International Committee on Taxonomy of Viruses data base, based on the ICTV Master Species List, 8th Report, June 2005 is obsolete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ictvonline1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ictvonline1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diseases_caused_by_enterovirus_infection">Diseases caused by enterovirus infection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diseases caused by enterovirus infection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Poliomyelitis" title="Poliomyelitis">Poliomyelitis</a> primarily via the fecal-oral route</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Polio-like_syndrome" title="Polio-like syndrome">Polio-like syndrome</a> found in children who tested positive for <a href="/wiki/Enterovirus_68" title="Enterovirus 68">enterovirus 68</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mysterious_polio-like_illness_found_in_5_California_children_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mysterious_polio-like_illness_found_in_5_California_children-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mysterious_Polio-Like_Illness_Found_in_California_Children_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mysterious_Polio-Like_Illness_Found_in_California_Children-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li>Nonspecific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Febrile" title="Febrile">febrile</a> illness is the most common presentation of enterovirus infection. Other than fever, symptoms include muscle pain, sore throat, gastrointestinal distress/abdominal discomfort, and headache. In newborns the picture may be that of <a href="/wiki/Sepsis" title="Sepsis">sepsis</a> however, and can be severe and life-threatening.</li>
<li>Enteroviruses are by far the most common causes of <a href="/wiki/Aseptic_meningitis" title="Aseptic meningitis">aseptic meningitis</a> in children. In the United States, enteroviruses are responsible for 30,000 to 50,000 meningitis hospitalizations per year as a result of 30 million to 50 million infections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_article_Non-Polio_Enterovirus_Infections_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_article_Non-Polio_Enterovirus_Infections-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bornholm_disease" title="Bornholm disease">Bornholm disease</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidemic_pleurodynia" title="Epidemic pleurodynia">epidemic pleurodynia</a> is characterized by severe paroxysmal pain in the chest and abdomen, along with fever, and sometimes nausea, headache, and <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">emesis</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pericarditis" title="Pericarditis">Pericarditis</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Myocarditis" title="Myocarditis">myocarditis</a> are typically caused by enteroviruses; symptoms consist of fever with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyspnea" title="Dyspnea">dyspnea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chest_pain" title="Chest pain">chest pain</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrhythmias" title="Arrhythmias">Arrhythmias</a>, heart failure, and myocardial infarction have also been reported.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acute_hemorrhagic_conjunctivitis" title="Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis">Acute hemorrhagic conjunctivitis</a> can be caused by enteroviruses.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Herpangina" title="Herpangina">Herpangina</a> is caused by Coxsackie A virus, and causes a vesicular rash in the oral cavity and on the pharynx, along with high fever, <a href="/wiki/Sore_throat" title="Sore throat">sore throat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaise" title="Malaise">malaise</a>, and often <a href="/wiki/Dysphagia" title="Dysphagia">dysphagia</a>, loss of appetite, back pain, and headache. It is also self-limiting, with symptoms typically ending in 3–4 days.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hand,_foot_and_mouth_disease" title="Hand, foot and mouth disease">Hand, foot and mouth disease</a> is a childhood illness most commonly caused by infection by Coxsackie A virus or EV71.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Encephalitis" title="Encephalitis">Encephalitis</a> is rare manifestation of enterovirus infection; when it occurs, the most frequent enterovirus found to be causing it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Echovirus_9" title="Echovirus 9">echovirus 9</a>.</li>
<li>A 2007 study suggested that acute respiratory or gastrointestinal infections associated with enterovirus may be a factor in <a href="/wiki/Chronic_fatigue_syndrome" title="Chronic fatigue syndrome">chronic fatigue syndrome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus_type_1" title="Diabetes mellitus type 1">Diabetes mellitus type 1</a> It has been proposed that type 1 diabetes is a virus-triggered autoimmune response in which the immune system attacks virus-infected cells along with the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> A team working at University of Tampere, Finland has identified a type of enterovirus that has a possible link to type 1 diabetes (which is an autoimmune disease).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterovirus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Treatment for enteroviral infection is mainly supportive. In cases of pleurodynia, treatment consists of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analgesics" title="Analgesics">analgesics</a> to relieve the severe pain that occurs in patients with the disease; in some severe cases, opiates may be needed. Treatment for aseptic meningitis caused by enteroviruses is also mainly symptomatic. In patients with enteroviral carditis, treatment consists of the prevention and treatment of complications, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrhythmias" title="Arrhythmias">arrhythmias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pericardial_effusion" title="Pericardial effusion">pericardial effusion</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiac_failure" title="Cardiac failure">cardiac failure</a>. Other treatments that have been investigated for enteroviral carditis include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intravenous_immunoglobulin" title="Intravenous immunoglobulin">intravenous immunoglobulin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
