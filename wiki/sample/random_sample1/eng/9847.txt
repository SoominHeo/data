<p><i><b>El Mundo</b></i> (Spanish for "The World", full name <i>El Mundo del Siglo Veintiuno</i>, "The World of the 21st Century") is the second largest printed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daily_newspaper" title="Daily newspaper">daily newspaper</a> in Spain. The paper is considered one of the country's <a href="/wiki/Newspaper_of_record" title="Newspaper of record">newspapers of record</a> along with <i><a href="/wiki/El_Pa%C3%ADs" title="El País">El País</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/ABC_(newspaper)" title="ABC (newspaper)"><i>ABC</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_profile">History and profile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Mundo_(Spain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and profile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>El Mundo</i> was first published on 23 October 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-many_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-many-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Perhaps the best known of its founders was <a href="/wiki/Pedro_J._Ram%C3%ADrez" title="Pedro J. Ramírez">Pedro J. Ramírez</a>, who served as editor until 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-4">[4]</a></sup> Ramirez had risen to prominence as a journalist during the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_transition_to_democracy" title="Spanish transition to democracy">Spanish transition to democracy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pedro_J_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pedro_J-5">[5]</a></sup> The other founders, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alfonso_de_Salas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alfonso de Salas (page does not exist)">Alfonso de Salas</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Balbino_Fraga&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Balbino Fraga (page does not exist)">Balbino Fraga</a> and Juan González, shared with Ramírez a background in Grupo 16, the publishers of the newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Diario_16" title="Diario 16">Diario 16</a></i>. Alfonso de Salas, Juan Gonzales and Gregorio Pena also launched <i><a href="/wiki/El_Economista_(Spain)" title="El Economista (Spain)">El Economista</a></i> in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>El Mundo</i>, along with <i><a href="/wiki/Marca_(newspaper)" title="Marca (newspaper)">Marca</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expansi%C3%B3n_(Spain)" title="Expansión (Spain)">Expansión</a></i>, is controlled by the Italian publishing company <a href="/wiki/RCS_MediaGroup" title="RCS MediaGroup">RCS MediaGroup</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-many_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-many-2">[2]</a></sup> through its Spanish subsidiary company Unidad Editorial S.L.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Its former owner was Unedisa which merged with Grupo Recoletos in 2007 to form Unidad Editorial, current owner of the paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>The paper has its headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anmee_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-anmee-10">[10]</a></sup> but maintains several news bureaus in other cities. The daily has a national edition and ten different regional editions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> including those for <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valencia_(autonomous_community)" title="Valencia (autonomous community)">Valencia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castile_and_Le%C3%B3n" title="Castile and León">Castile and León</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bilbao" title="Bilbao">Bilbao</a>. It is published in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tabloid_format" title="Tabloid format">tabloid format</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005 <i>El Mundo</i> started a <a href="/wiki/Supplement_(publishing)" title="Supplement (publishing)">supplement</a> for women, <i>Yo Donna</i>, which was modelled on <i><a href="/wiki/IO_Donna" title="IO Donna">IO Donna</a></i>, a supplement of the <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> daily <i><a href="/wiki/Corriere_della_Sera" title="Corriere della Sera">Corriere della Sera</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2014 Pedro J. Ramírez, editor of the paper, was fired from his post.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc14_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-loc14-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kass_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-kass-15">[15]</a></sup> He argued that reporting on corruption scandals involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Prime_Minister" title="Spanish Prime Minister">Spanish Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Mariano_Rajoy" title="Mariano Rajoy">Mariano Rajoy</a> led to his sacking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc14_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-loc14-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kass_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-kass-15">[15]</a></sup> Casimiro García-Abadillo served as editor until April 2015, when he was replaced in turn by David Jiménez.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_impact">Political impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Mundo_(Spain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Editorially, <i>El Mundo</i> often expresses the mainstream views of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centre-right" title="Centre-right">centre-right</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc14_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-loc14-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> with independent and <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberal</a> overtones.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><i>El Mundo</i> has played a key role in uncovering a number of scandals, among them embezzlement by the commander of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guardia_Civil_(Spain)" title="Guardia Civil (Spain)">Guardia Civil</a>, accusations of <a href="/wiki/Insider_trading" title="Insider trading">insider trading</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">tax fraud</a> by the governor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banco_de_Espa%C3%B1a" title="Banco de España">Central Bank of Spain</a> and aspects of the <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1rcenas_affair" title="Bárcenas affair">Bárcenas affair</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Preston_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Preston-18">[18]</a></sup> Investigative reporting by the staff of <i>El Mundo</i> also revealed connections between the terrorist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grupos_Antiterroristas_de_Liberaci%C3%B3n" title="Grupos Antiterroristas de Liberación">Grupos Antiterroristas de Liberación</a> (GAL) and the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Socialist_Workers%27_Party" title="Spanish Socialist Workers' Party">Socialist</a> administration of <a href="/wiki/Felipe_Gonz%C3%A1lez" title="Felipe González">Felipe González</a>, revelations that contributed to his defeat in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_legislative_election,_1996" title="Spanish legislative election, 1996">1996 elections</a>.</p>
<p>In October 2005, <i>El Mundo</i> revealed that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ex-Nazi" title="Ex-Nazi">Nazi</a> <a href="/wiki/Aribert_Heim" title="Aribert Heim">Aribert Heim</a> (aka "Doctor Death") had been living in Spain for 20 years, probably with help from the <a href="/wiki/ODESSA" title="ODESSA">ODESSA</a> network, in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Otto_Skorzeny" title="Otto Skorzeny">Otto Skorzeny</a>, who had helped set up one of the most important ODESSA bases of operation in Spain, during the rule of the late dictator <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Francisco Franco</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/11_March_2004_Madrid_train_bombings" title="11 March 2004 Madrid train bombings">11 March 2004 Madrid train bombings</a>, the newspapers <i>El Mundo</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/La_Raz%C3%B3n_(Madrid)" title="La Razón (Madrid)">La Razón</a></i>, the regional television channel <a href="/wiki/Telemadrid" title="Telemadrid">Telemadrid</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cadena_COPE" title="Cadena COPE">COPE</a> radio network alleged that there had been inconsistencies in the explanations given by the Spanish judiciary about the bombings. Other Spanish media, such as <i><a href="/wiki/El_Pa%C3%ADs" title="El País">El País</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/ABC_(newspaper)" title="ABC (newspaper)">ABC</a></i> and the <a href="/wiki/Cadena_SER" title="Cadena SER">Cadena SER</a> radio network, accused <i>El Mundo</i> and the other media of manipulation over this issue. The bombings and the results of the subsequent judicial inquiry are still debated in Spain today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Circulation">Circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Mundo_(Spain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The circulation of <i>El Mundo</i> rose in the 1990s. It was</p>
<ul>
<li>209,992 copies in 1993</li>
<li>268,748 copies in 1994<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In 2001 <i>El Mundo</i> had a circulation of 291,000 copies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and it was 312,366 copies next year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ward_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ward-23">[23]</a></sup> The paper had a circulation of 300,000 copies in 2003, making it the third best selling newspaper in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Based on the findings of the European Business Readership Survey <i>El Mundo</i> had 11,591 readers per issue in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll2010_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll2010-25">[25]</a></sup> Its circulation between June 2006 and July 2007 was 337,172 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anmee_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-anmee-10">[10]</a></sup> The 2007 circulation of the paper was 337,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-many_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-many-2">[2]</a></sup> It was 338,286 copies in 2008<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and had 200,000 readers for the printed edition in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-editor_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-editor-27">[27]</a></sup> The circulation of the paper was 266,294 copies in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_readership">Digital readership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Mundo_(Spain)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Digital readership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>El Mundo</i> (elmundo.es) is currently the second <a href="/wiki/Digital_newspaper" title="Digital newspaper">digital newspaper</a> in Spanish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> It was previously in the lead after <i>El País</i> introduced a payment system for access to the contents of its electronic version. It had 24 million unique web visitors per month in 2009.</p>
<p>Many online readers are in Latin America, and the website has an edition for the Americas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-editor_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-editor-27">[27]</a></sup> However, digital expansion has done little to offset the decline in revenues from Spanish advertisers since 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pedro_J_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pedro_J-5">[5]</a></sup> The newspaper aims to increase digital profits via a subscription model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orbyt_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-orbyt-30">[30]</a></sup> It launched a current affairs outlet only accessible to subscription customers, named ORBYT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orbyt_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-orbyt-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
