<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Eleusinian Mysteries</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>Eleusinian <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_mysteries" title="Greco-Roman mysteries">Mysteries</a></b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Ἐλευσίνια Μυστήρια</span>) were <a href="/wiki/Initiation" title="Initiation">initiations</a> held every year for the <a href="/wiki/Cult_(religious_practice)" title="Cult (religious practice)">cult</a> of <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">Demeter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Persephone" title="Persephone">Persephone</a> based at <a href="/wiki/Eleusis" title="Eleusis">Eleusis</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a>. They are the "most famous of the secret religious rites of ancient Greece".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is thought that their basis was an old <a href="/wiki/Agrarianism" title="Agrarianism">agrarian</a> cult which probably goes back to the <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greece" title="Mycenaean Greece">Mycenean period</a> (c. 1600 – 1100 BC)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nilsson42_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nilsson42-2">[2]</a></sup> and it is believed that the cult of Demeter was established in 1500 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The mysteries represented the myth of the abduction of Persephone from her mother Demeter by the king of the underworld <a href="/wiki/Hades" title="Hades">Hades</a>, in a cycle with three phases, the "descent" (loss), the "search" and the "ascent", with the main theme the "ascent" of Persephone and the reunion with her mother. It was a major festival during the Hellenic era, and later spread to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Rome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The name of the town, Eleusís, seems to be Pre-Greek and it is probably a counterpart with <a href="/wiki/Elysium" title="Elysium">Elysium</a> and the goddess <a href="/wiki/Eileithyia" title="Eileithyia">Eileithyia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rites, ceremonies, and beliefs were kept secret and consistently preserved from antiquity. The initiated believed that they would have a reward in the <a href="/wiki/Afterlife" title="Afterlife">afterlife</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> There are many paintings and pieces of pottery that depict various aspects of the Mysteries. Since the Mysteries involved visions and conjuring of an afterlife, some scholars believe that the power and longevity of the Eleusinian Mysteries came from <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_drug" title="Psychedelic drug">psychedelic drugs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wasson.2C_R_1978_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wasson.2C_R_1978-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleusinian_Mysteries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Eleusinian Mysteries</i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">Ἐλευσίνια Μυστήρια</span>) was the name of the <i>mysteries</i> of the city <i>Eleusis</i>. The name of the city <a href="/wiki/Eleusis" title="Eleusis">Eleusis</a> is Pre-Greek, and may be related with the name of the goddess <a href="/wiki/Eileithyia" title="Eileithyia">Eileithyia</a>, but this is debated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The ancient Greek word "mystery" (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">μυστήριον</span>) means "mystery or secret rite" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and is related with the verb <i>mueō</i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">μυέω</span>), which means initiation into the mysteries,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and the noun <i>mustēs</i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">μύστης</span>), which means one initiated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The word <i>mustikós</i> (<span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">μυστικός</span>) means "connected with the mysteries", or "private, secret" (as in Modern Greek).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demeter_and_Persephone">Demeter and Persephone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleusinian_Mysteries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Demeter and Persephone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">Demeter</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NAMA_Triade_%C3%A9leusinienne.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1653" data-file-width="1200" height="303" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/NAMA_Triade_%C3%A9leusinienne.jpg/220px-NAMA_Triade_%C3%A9leusinienne.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/NAMA_Triade_%C3%A9leusinienne.jpg/330px-NAMA_Triade_%C3%A9leusinienne.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/NAMA_Triade_%C3%A9leusinienne.jpg/440px-NAMA_Triade_%C3%A9leusinienne.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NAMA_Triade_%C3%A9leusinienne.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Triptolemus" title="Triptolemus">Triptolemus</a> receiving wheat sheaves from <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">Demeter</a> and blessings from <a href="/wiki/Persephone" title="Persephone">Persephone</a>, 5th-century BC relief, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Archaeological_Museum_of_Athens" title="National Archaeological Museum of Athens">National Archaeological Museum of Athens</a></div>

<p>The Mysteries are related to a myth concerning <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">Demeter</a>, the goddess of agriculture and fertility as recounted in one of the <a href="/wiki/Homeric_Hymns" title="Homeric Hymns">Homeric Hymns</a> (c. 650 BC). According to the hymn, Demeter's daughter <a href="/wiki/Persephone" title="Persephone">Persephone</a> (also referred to as <i>Kore</i>, "maiden") was gathering flowers with friends, when she was seized by <a href="/wiki/Hades" title="Hades">Hades</a>, the god of death and the <a href="/wiki/Greek_underworld" title="Greek underworld">underworld</a>. He took her to his underworld kingdom. Distraught, Demeter searched high and low for her daughter. Because of her distress, and in an effort to coerce <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> to allow the return of her daughter, she caused a terrible <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> in which the people suffered and starved. This would have deprived the gods of sacrifice and worship. As a result, Zeus relented and allowed Persephone to return to her mother.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to the myth, during her search Demeter traveled long distances and had many minor adventures along the way. In one she taught the secrets of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> to <a href="/wiki/Triptolemus" title="Triptolemus">Triptolemus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Finally, by consulting Zeus, Demeter reunites with her daughter and the earth returns to its former verdure and prosperity: the first spring.</p>
<p>Zeus, pressed by the cries of the hungry people and by the other deities who also heard their anguish, forced Hades to return Persephone. However, it was a rule of the <a href="/wiki/Moirai" title="Moirai">Fates</a> that whoever consumed food or drink in the Underworld was doomed to spend eternity there. Before Persephone was released to Hermes, who had been sent to retrieve her, Hades tricked her into eating pomegranate seeds, (either six or four according to the telling) which forced her to return to the underworld for some months each year. She was obliged to remain with Hades for six or four months (one month per seed) and lived above ground with her mother for the rest of the year. This left a long period of time when Demeter was unhappy due to Persephone's absence, neglecting to cultivate the earth. When Persephone returned to the surface, Demeter became joyful and cared for the earth again.</p>
<p>If we suppose that Persephone stayed with Hades for four months and Demeter eight months, corresponding to eight months of growth and abundance to be followed by four months of no productivity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> we see the parallel with the Mediterranean climate of ancient Greece. The four months during which Persephone is with Hades correspond to the dry Greek summer, a period during which plants are threatened with drought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> At the beginning of autumn when the seeds are planted, Persephone returns from the underworld and is reunited with her mother, and the cycle of growth begins anew.</p>
<p>This reading of the ritual, however, does not square with the central foundation document of the mystery, the Homeric <i>Hymn to Demeter</i> line 415, where Persephone is explicitly said to return in the spring of the year, not the fall: "This was the day [of Persephone's return], at the very beginning of bountiful springtime."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Her rebirth is symbolic of the rebirth of all plant life and the symbol of eternity of life that flows from the generations that spring from each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mysteries">Mysteries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleusinian_Mysteries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mysteries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Eleusinian Mysteries are believed to be of considerable antiquity, deriving from the religious practices of the Mycenaean period and thus predating the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Dark_Ages" title="Greek Dark Ages">Greek Dark Ages</a>. One line of thought by modern scholars has been that the Mysteries were intended "to elevate man above the human sphere into the divine and to assure his redemption by making him a god and so conferring immortality upon him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Comparative study shows parallels between these Greek rituals and similar systems—some of them older—<a href="/wiki/Religions_of_the_ancient_Near_East" title="Religions of the ancient Near East">in the Near East</a>. Such cults include the mysteries of <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a> in Egypt, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Adoniac&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Adoniac (page does not exist)">Adoniac</a> of Syrian cults, the Persian mysteries, and the Phrygian <a href="/wiki/Cabeiri" title="Cabeiri">Cabeirian</a> mysteries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Some scholars argued that the Eleusinian cult was a continuation of a <a href="/wiki/Minoan_civilization" title="Minoan civilization">Minoan</a> cult,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> probably affected by the Near East.</p>
<p>According to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mylonas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mylonas (page does not exist)">Mylonas</a>, the lesser mysteries were held "as a rule once a year in the early spring in the month of flowers, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthesterion" title="Anthesterion">Anthesterion</a>," while "the Greater Mysteries were held once a year and every fourth year they were celebrated with special splendor in what was known as the <i>penteteris</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Kerenyi concurs with this assessment: "The Lesser Mysteries were held at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Agrai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Agrai (page does not exist)">Agrai</a> in the month of Anthesterion, our February... The initiates were not even admitted to the <i>epopteia</i> [Greater Mysteries] in the same year, but only in September of the following year." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This cycle continued for about two millennia. In the <a href="/wiki/Homeric_Hymns" title="Homeric Hymns">Homeric Hymn</a> to Demeter, King <a href="/wiki/Celeus" title="Celeus">Celeus</a> is said to have been one of the first people to learn the secret rites and mysteries of her cult. He was also one of her original priests, along with <a href="/wiki/Diocles_(mythology)" title="Diocles (mythology)">Diocles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eumolpos" title="Eumolpos">Eumolpos</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyxeinus" title="Polyxeinus">Polyxeinus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triptolemus" title="Triptolemus">Triptolemus</a>, Celeus' son, who had supposedly learned agriculture from Demeter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/Peisistratos" title="Peisistratos">Peisistratos</a> of Athens, the Eleusinian Mysteries became <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pan-Hellenic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pan-Hellenic (page does not exist)">pan-Hellenic</a>, and pilgrims flocked from Greece and beyond to participate. Around 300 BC, the state took over control of the Mysteries; they were controlled by two families, the <a href="/wiki/Eumolpidae" title="Eumolpidae">Eumolpidae</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kerykes" title="Kerykes">Kerykes</a>. This led to a vast increase in the number of initiates. The only requirements for membership were freedom from "blood guilt"<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title='"reference'>citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, meaning never having committed murder, and not being a "barbarian" (being unable to speak Greek). Men, women and even slaves were allowed initiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Participants">Participants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleusinian_Mysteries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Participants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To participate in these mysteries one had to swear a vow of secrecy.</p>
<p>Four categories of people participated in the Eleusinian Mysteries:</p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">Priests</a>, priestesses, and <a href="/wiki/Hierophant" title="Hierophant">hierophants</a>.</li>
<li>Initiates, undergoing the ceremony for the first time.</li>
<li>Others who had already participated at least once. They were eligible for the fourth category.</li>
<li>Those who had attained <i>épopteia</i> (Greek: ἐποπτεία) (English: "contemplation"), who had learned the secrets of the greatest mysteries of Demeter.</li>
</ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secrets">Secrets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleusinian_Mysteries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Secrets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The outline below is only a capsule summary; much of the concrete information about the Eleusinian Mysteries was never written down. For example, only initiates knew what the <i>kiste</i>, a sacred chest, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Kalathos" title="Kalathos">kalathos</a></i>, a lidded basket, contained.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hippolytus_of_Rome" title="Hippolytus of Rome">Hippolytus of Rome</a>, one of the <a href="/wiki/Church_Fathers" title="Church Fathers">Church Fathers</a> writing in the early 3rd century CE, discloses in <i><a href="/wiki/Refutation_of_All_Heresies" title="Refutation of All Heresies">Refutation of All Heresies</a></i> that "the Athenians, while initiating people into the Eleusinian rites, likewise display to those who are being admitted to the highest grade at these mysteries, the mighty, and marvellous, and most perfect secret suitable for one initiated into the highest mystic truths: <i>an ear of grain in silence reaped</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lesser_Mysteries">Lesser Mysteries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleusinian_Mysteries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lesser Mysteries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There were two Eleusinian Mysteries, the Greater and the Lesser. According to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Taylor_(neoplatonist)" title="Thomas Taylor (neoplatonist)">Thomas Taylor</a>, "the dramatic shows of the Lesser Mysteries occultly signified the miseries of the soul while in subjection to the body, so those of the Greater obscurely intimated, by mystic and splendid visions, the felicity of the soul both here and hereafter, when purified from the defilements of a material nature and constantly elevated to the realities of intellectual [spiritual] vision." According to Plato, "the ultimate design of the Mysteries … was to lead us back to the principles from which we descended, … a perfect enjoyment of intellectual [spiritual] good."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Lesser Mysteries took place in the month of Anthesteria under the direction of Athens' <i><a href="/wiki/Archon_basileus" title="Archon basileus">archon basileus</a></i>. In order to qualify for initiation, participants would sacrifice a piglet to Demeter and Persephone, and then ritually purify themselves in the river Illisos. Upon completion of the Lesser Mysteries, participants were deemed <i>mystai</i> ("initiates") worthy of witnessing the Greater Mysteries.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greater_Mysteries">Greater Mysteries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleusinian_Mysteries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Greater Mysteries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30em; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>For among the many excellent and indeed divine institutions which your Athens has brought forth and contributed to human life, none, in my opinion, is better than those mysteries. For by their means we have been brought out of our barbarous and savage mode of life and educated and refined to a state of civilization; and as the rites are called "initiations," so in very truth we have learned from them the beginnings of life, and have gained the power not only to live happily, but also to die with a better hope.</p>
</div>
</div>
<cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">Cicero, Laws II, xiv, 36</cite></div>
<p>The Greater Mysteries took place in Boedromion – the third month of the <a href="/wiki/Attic_calendar" title="Attic calendar">Attic calendar</a>, falling in late summer – and lasted ten days.</p>
<p>The first act (on the 14th of Boedromion) was the bringing of the sacred objects from Eleusis to the <a href="/wiki/Eleusinion" title="Eleusinion">Eleusinion</a>, a temple at the base of the <a href="/wiki/Acropolis_of_Athens" title="Acropolis of Athens">Acropolis of Athens</a>.</p>
<p>On the 15th of Boedromion, called Agyrmos "the Gathering", the hierophants (priests, or "those who show the sacred ones") declared <a href="/wiki/Prorrhesis" title="Prorrhesis">prorrhesis</a>, the start of the rites, and carried out the "Hither the victims" sacrifice (<i>hiereía deúro</i>). The "Seawards initiates" (halade mystai) began in Athens on 16th Boedromion with the celebrants washing themselves in the sea at <a href="/wiki/Phalerum" title="Phalerum">Phaleron</a>.</p>
<p>On the 17th, the participants began the Epidauria, a festival for <a href="/wiki/Asclepius" title="Asclepius">Asklepios</a> named after his main sanctuary at <a href="/wiki/Epidaurus" title="Epidaurus">Epidauros</a>. This "festival within a festival" celebrated the hero's arrival at Athens with his daughter <a href="/wiki/Hygieia" title="Hygieia">Hygieia</a>, and consisted of a procession leading to the Eleusinion, during which the mystai apparently stayed at home, a great sacrifice, and an all-night feast (<i>pannykhís</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clinton_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clinton-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The procession to Eleusis began at <a href="/wiki/Kerameikos" title="Kerameikos">Kerameikos</a> (the Athenian cemetery) on the 19th, and from there the people walked to Eleusis, along what was called the "<a href="/wiki/Sacred_Way" title="Sacred Way">Sacred Way</a>" (Ἱερὰ Ὁδός, <i>Hierá Hodós</i>), swinging branches called <a href="/wiki/Bacchoi" title="Bacchoi">bacchoi</a>. At a certain spot along the way, they shouted obscenities in commemoration of <a href="/wiki/Iambe" title="Iambe">Iambe</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Baubo" title="Baubo">Baubo</a>), an old woman who, by cracking dirty jokes, had made Demeter smile as she mourned the loss of her daughter. The procession also shouted "Íakch', O Íakche!" referring to <a href="/wiki/Iacchus" title="Iacchus">Iacchus</a>, possibly an epithet for <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Dionysus</a>, or a separate deity, son of Persephone or Demeter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>Upon reaching Eleusis, there was an all-night vigil (<i>pannychis</i>) according to Mylonas<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and Kerenyi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> perhaps commemorating Demeter's search for Persephone. At some point, initiates had a special drink of barley and <a href="/wiki/Mentha_pulegium" title="Mentha pulegium">pennyroyal</a>, called <a href="/wiki/Kykeon" title="Kykeon">kykeon</a>, which has led to speculation about its chemicals perhaps having psychotropic effects.</p>
<p>Then, on the 20th and 21st of Boedromion, the initiates entered a great hall called <a href="/wiki/Telesterion" title="Telesterion">Telesterion</a>; in the center stood the Anaktoron ("palace"), which only the hierophants could enter, where sacred objects were stored. Before mystai could enter the Telesterion, they would recite, "I have fasted, I have drunk the <i>kykeon</i>, I have taken from the <i>kiste</i> ("box") and after working it have put it back in the <i>kalathos</i> ("open basket").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> It is widely supposed that the rites inside the Telesterion comprised three elements: <i>dromena</i> ("things done"), a dramatic reenactment of the Demeter/Persephone myth; <i>deiknumena</i> ("things shown"), displayed sacred objects, in which the hierophant played an essential role; and finally <i>legomena</i> ("things said"), commentaries that accompanied the <i>deiknumena</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Combined these three elements were known as the <i>aporrheta</i> ("unrepeatables"); the penalty for divulging them was death. <a href="/wiki/Athenagoras_of_Athens" title="Athenagoras of Athens">Athenagoras of Athens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>, and other ancient writers cite that it was for this crime (among others) that <a href="/wiki/Diagoras_of_Melos" title="Diagoras of Melos">Diagoras</a> was condemned to death in Athens;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> the tragic playwright <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a> was allegedly tried for revealing secrets of the Mysteries in some of his plays, but was acquitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The ban on divulging the core ritual of the Mysteries was thus absolute, which is probably why we know almost nothing about what transpired there.</p>
<p>As to the climax of the Mysteries, there are two modern theories. Some hold that the priests were the ones to reveal the visions of the holy night, consisting of a fire that represented the possibility of life after death, and various sacred objects. Others hold this explanation to be insufficient to account for the power and longevity of the Mysteries, and that the experiences must have been internal and mediated by a powerful psychoactive ingredient contained in the kykeon drink. (See "<a href="/wiki/Eleusinian_Mysteries#Entheogenic_theories" title="Eleusinian Mysteries">entheogenic theories</a>" below.)</p>
<p>Following this section of the Mysteries was the Pannychis, an all-night feast<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> accompanied by dancing and merriment. The dances took place in the <a href="/wiki/Rharian_Field" title="Rharian Field">Rharian Field</a>, rumored to be the first spot where grain grew. A bull sacrifice also took place late that night or early the next morning. That day (22nd Boedromion), the initiates honored the dead by pouring <a href="/wiki/Libation" title="Libation">libations</a> from special vessels.</p>
<p>On the 23rd 0f Boedromion, the Mysteries ended and everyone returned home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demise">Demise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleusinian_Mysteries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Demise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 170 AD, the Temple of Demeter was sacked by the <a href="/wiki/Sarmatians" title="Sarmatians">Sarmatians</a> but was rebuilt by <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aurelius" title="Marcus Aurelius">Marcus Aurelius</a>. Aurelius was then allowed to become the only lay person ever to enter the anaktoron. As Christianity gained in popularity in the 4th and 5th centuries, Eleusis's prestige began to fade. The last pagan emperor of Rome, <a href="/wiki/Julian_(emperor)" title="Julian (emperor)">Julian</a>, reigned from 361 to 363 after about fifty years of Christian rule. Julian attempted to restore the Eleusinian Mysteries and was the last emperor to be initiated into them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> emperor <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a> closed the sanctuaries by decree about 30 years later, in 392 AD. The last remnants of the Mysteries were wiped out in 396 AD, when <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">Arian</a> Christians under <a href="/wiki/Alaric_I" title="Alaric I">Alaric</a>, King of the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a>, destroyed and desecrated the old sacred sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKloft201025_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKloft201025-41">[41]</a></sup> The closing of the Eleusinian Mysteries in the 4th century is reported by <a href="/wiki/Eunapius" title="Eunapius">Eunapius</a>, a historian and biographer of the Greek philosophers. Eunapius had been initiated by the last legitimate <a href="/wiki/Hierophant" title="Hierophant">Hierophant</a>, who had been commissioned by the emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julian_the_Apostate" title="Julian the Apostate">Julian</a> to restore the Mysteries, which had by then fallen into decay. According to Eunapius, the very last Hierophant was a usurper, "the man from <a href="/wiki/Thespiae" title="Thespiae">Thespiae</a> who held the rank of Father in the mysteries of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mithras" title="Mithras">Mithras</a>."</p>
<p>According to historian Hans Kloft, despite the destruction of the Eleusinian Mysteries, elements of the cult survived in the Greek countryside. There, Demeter's rites and religious duties were partially transferred by peasants and shepherds onto <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">Saint</a> <a href="/wiki/Demetrius_of_Thessaloniki" title="Demetrius of Thessaloniki">Demetrius of Thessaloniki</a>, who gradually became the local patron of agriculture and "heir" to the pagan mother goddess.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKloft201025_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKloft201025-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_art.2C_literature_and_culture">In art, literature and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleusinian_Mysteries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In art, literature and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>There are many paintings and pieces of pottery that depict various aspects of the Mysteries. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eleusinian_Relief&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eleusinian Relief (page does not exist)">Eleusinian Relief</a>, from the late 5th century BC, displayed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Archaeological_Museum_of_Athens" title="National Archaeological Museum of Athens">National Archaeological Museum of Athens</a> is a representative example. Triptolemus is depicted receiving seeds from Demeter and teaching mankind how to work the fields to grow crops, with Persephone holding her hand over his head to protect him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Vases and other works of relief sculpture, from the 4th, 5th and 6th centuries BC, depict Triptolemus holding an ear of corn, sitting on a winged throne or chariot, surrounded by Persephone and Demeter with pine torches. The monumental Protoattic amphora from the middle of the 7th century BC, with the depiction of Medusa's beheading by <a href="/wiki/Perseus" title="Perseus">Perseus</a> and the blinding of Polyphemos by <a href="/wiki/Odysseus" title="Odysseus">Odysseus</a> and his companions on its neck, is kept in the <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_Museum_of_Eleusis" title="Archaeological Museum of Eleusis">Archaeological Museum of Eleusis</a> which is located inside the archaeological site of <a href="/wiki/Eleusis" title="Eleusis">Eleusis</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ninnion_Tablet" title="Ninnion Tablet">Ninnion Tablet</a>, found in the same museum, depicts Demeter, followed by Persephone and Iacchus, and then the procession of initiates. Then, Demeter is sitting on the kiste inside the Telesterion, with Persephone holding a torch and introducing the initiates. The initiates each hold a bacchoi. The second row of initiates were led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iakchos" title="Iakchos">Iakchos</a>, a priest who held torches for the ceremonies. He is standing near the <a href="/wiki/Omphalos" title="Omphalos">omphalos</a> while an unknown female (probably a priestess of Demeter) sat nearby on the kiste, holding a scepter and a vessel filled with kykeon. Pannychis is also represented.</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tempest" title="The Tempest">The Tempest</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Masque" title="Masque">masque</a> that Prospero conjures to celebrate the troth-pledging of Miranda and Ferdinand echoes the Eleusinian Mysteries, although it uses the Roman names for the deities involved – Ceres, Iris, Dis and others – instead of the Greek. It is interesting that a play which is so steeped in esoteric imagery from <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hermeticism" title="Hermeticism">hermeticism</a> should draw on the Mysteries for its central masque sequence.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="source please (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Carl Gustav Jung (1875-1961) borrowed terms and interpretations from the late 19th and early-20th century classical scholarship in German and French as a source of metaphors for his reframing of psychoanalytic treatment into a spiritualistic ritual of initiation and rebirth. The Eleusinian mysteries, particularly the qualities of the Kore, figured prominently in his writings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dimitris_Lyacos" title="Dimitris Lyacos">Dimitris Lyacos</a> in the second book of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Poena_Damni&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Poena Damni (page does not exist)">Poena Damni</a> trilogy <a href="/wiki/With_the_People_from_the_Bridge" title="With the People from the Bridge">With the People from the Bridge</a>, a contemporary, avant-garde play focusing on the return of the dead and the myth of the <a href="/wiki/Revenant" title="Revenant">revenant</a> combines elements from the Eleusinian mysteries as well as early Christian tradition in order to convey a view of <a href="/wiki/Collective_salvation" title="Collective salvation">collective salvation</a>. The text uses the pomegranate symbol in order to hint at the residence of the dead in the underworld and their periodical return to the world of the living.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entheogenic_theories">Entheogenic theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eleusinian_Mysteries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Entheogenic theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Numerous scholars have proposed that the power of the Eleusinian Mysteries came from the <a href="/wiki/Kykeon" title="Kykeon">kykeon</a>'s functioning as an <a href="/wiki/Entheogen" title="Entheogen">entheogen</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychedelic_agent" title="Psychedelic agent">psychedelic agent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wasson.2C_R_1978_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wasson.2C_R_1978-7">[7]</a></sup> The use of potions or philtres for magical or religious purposes was relatively common in Greece and the ancient world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The initiates, sensitized by their fast and prepared by preceding ceremonies (see <a href="/wiki/Set_and_setting" title="Set and setting">set and setting</a>), may have been propelled by the effects of a powerful psychoactive potion into revelatory mind states with profound spiritual and intellectual ramifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wasson.2C_et_al._46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wasson.2C_et_al.-46">[46]</a></sup> In opposition to this idea, other pointedly skeptical scholars note the lack of any solid evidence and stress the collective rather than individual character of initiation into the Mysteries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Indirect evidence in support of the entheogenic theory is that in 415 BC Athenian aristocrat <a href="/wiki/Alcibiades" title="Alcibiades">Alcibiades</a> was condemned partly because he took part in an "Eleusinian mystery" in a private house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>
<p>Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychoactive" title="Psychoactive">psychoactive</a> agents have been proposed as the significant element of kykeon, though without consensus or conclusive evidence. These include the <a href="/wiki/Ergot" title="Ergot">ergot</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasite</a> of the barley or rye grain, which contains the alkaloids <a href="/wiki/Ergine" title="Ergine">lysergic acid amide (LSA)</a>, a precursor to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LSD" title="LSD">LSD</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergonovine" title="Ergonovine">ergonovine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wasson.2C_et_al._46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wasson.2C_et_al.-46">[46]</a></sup> However, modern attempts to prepare a kykeon using ergot-parasitized barley have yielded inconclusive results, though <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Shulgin" title="Alexander Shulgin">Alexander Shulgin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ann_Shulgin" title="Ann Shulgin">Ann Shulgin</a> describe both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergonovine" title="Ergonovine">ergonovine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ergine" title="Ergine">LSA</a> to be known to produce LSD-like effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychoactive_mushrooms" title="Psychoactive mushrooms">Psychoactive mushrooms</a> are another candidate. <a href="/wiki/Terence_McKenna" title="Terence McKenna">Terence McKenna</a> speculated that the mysteries were focused around a variety of <i><a href="/wiki/Psilocybe" title="Psilocybe">Psilocybe</a></i>. Other <a href="/wiki/Entheogen" title="Entheogen">entheogenic</a> fungi, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Amanita_muscaria" title="Amanita muscaria">Amanita muscaria</a></i>, have also been suggested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> A recent hypothesis suggests that the ancient Egyptians cultivated <a href="/wiki/Psilocybe_cubensis" title="Psilocybe cubensis">Psilocybe cubensis</a> on barley and associated it with the deity <a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another candidate for the psychoactive drug is an opioid derived from the poppy. The cult of the goddess Demeter may have brought the poppy from Crete to Eleusis; it is certain that opium was produced in Crete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Another theory is that the psychoactive agent in kykeon is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimethyltryptamine" title="Dimethyltryptamine">DMT</a>, which occurs in many wild plants of the Mediterranean, including <i><a href="/wiki/Phalaris_(plant)" title="Phalaris (plant)">Phalaris</a></i> and/or <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">Acacia</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> To be active orally it must be combined with a <a href="/wiki/Monoamine_oxidase_inhibitor" title="Monoamine oxidase inhibitor">monoamine oxidase inhibitor</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syrian_Rue" title="Syrian Rue">Syrian Rue</a> (<i>Peganum harmala</i>), which grows throughout the Mediterranean.</p>
