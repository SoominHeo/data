<p><b>Ellen Jane Langer</b> (born March 25, 1947) is a professor of <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, having in 1981 become the first woman ever to be tenured in psychology at Harvard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NY_Times_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NY_Times-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheMindfulnessChronicles_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-TheMindfulnessChronicles-2">[2]</a></sup> Langer studies the <a href="/wiki/Illusion_of_control" title="Illusion of control">illusion of control</a>, decision-making, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aging" title="Aging">aging</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mindfulness_(psychology)" title="Mindfulness (psychology)">mindfulness</a> theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheMindfulnessChronicles_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-TheMindfulnessChronicles-2">[2]</a></sup> Her most influential work is <i>Counterclockwise</i>, published in 2009, which answers the questions of aging from her extensive research, and increased interest in the ins and outs of aging across the nation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Langer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Langer was born in <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">The Bronx</a>, New York. She received her bachelor's degree in Psychology from <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a>, and her PhD in Social and Clinical Psychology from <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NY_Times_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NY_Times-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Langer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Langer has had a significant influence on the <a href="/wiki/Positive_psychology" title="Positive psychology">positive psychology</a> movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boston_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boston-3">[3]</a></sup> Langer has published over 200 articles and academic texts. Langer was published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, and discussed her works on <i><a href="/wiki/Good_Morning_America" title="Good Morning America">Good Morning America</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NY_Times_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NY_Times-1">[1]</a></sup> Additionally, in many introductory psychology courses at universities across the United States, her studies are required reading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boston_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boston-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aging">Aging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Langer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Langer and colleagues conducted multiple forms of research to promote the flexibility of aging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boston_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Boston-3">[3]</a></sup> One study showed that rewarding behaviors and following completion of memory tasks improves memory. Another study showed that simply taking care of a plant improves mental and physical health, as well as life expectancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> These studies were the primitive steps to creating the Langer Mindfulness Scale.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Her research provided for improved methods in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nursing_home" title="Nursing home">nursing homes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assisted_living_facility" title="Assisted living facility">assisted living facilities</a>. Additionally, in one of her most famous studies, Langer found it was possible to lower obesity and diabetes in chambermaids by calling their everyday activity âexerciseâ rather than âlabor.â <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-On_Being_with_Krista_Tippett_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-On_Being_with_Krista_Tippett-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mindfulness">Mindfulness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Langer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mindfulness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Langer is well known for her contributions to the study of <a href="/wiki/Mindfulness" title="Mindfulness">mindfulness</a> and of mindless behaviour, with these contributions having provided the basis for many studies focused on individual differences in unconscious behavior and decision making processes in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvard_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1989, she published <i>Mindfulness</i>, her first book, and some have referred to her as the "mother of mindfulness".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PsychCentral_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-PsychCentral-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langer.com_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langer.com-8">[8]</a></sup> In an interview with Krista Tippett on the National Public Radio program "On Being," broadcast on Sept. 13, 2015, Langer defined mindfulness as "the simple act of noticing new things." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-On_Being_with_Krista_Tippett_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-On_Being_with_Krista_Tippett-5">[5]</a></sup> The Langer Mindfulness Scale is still used in modern research.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Langer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1980, she was the recipient of a <a href="/wiki/Guggenheim_Fellowship" title="Guggenheim Fellowship">Guggenheim Fellowship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langer.com_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Langer.com-8">[8]</a></sup> Other honors include the Award for Distinguished Contributions to Psychology in the Public Interest of the <a href="/wiki/American_Psychological_Association" title="American Psychological Association">American Psychological Association</a>, the Distinguished Contributions of Basic Science to Applied Psychology award from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Association_of_Applied_and_Preventive_Psychology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Association of Applied and Preventive Psychology (page does not exist)">American Association of Applied and Preventive Psychology</a>, the James McKeen Cattel Award, and the Gordon Allport Intergroup Relations Prize.</p>
