<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ellen Key</h1>

<p><b>Ellen Karolina Sofia Key</b> (<small>Swedish: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Swedish" title="Help:IPA for Swedish">[kej]</a></span>; 11 December 1849 – 25 April 1926) was a <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Difference_feminist" title="Difference feminist">difference feminist</a> writer on many subjects in the fields of family life, <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethics</a> and education and was an important figure in the <a href="/wiki/Modern_Breakthrough" title="Modern Breakthrough">Modern Breakthrough</a> movement. She was an early advocate of a child-centered approach to education and parenting, and was also a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suffragist" title="Suffragist">suffragist</a>.</p>
<p>She is best known for her book on education, <i>Barnets århundrade</i> (1900), which was translated in English in 1909 as <i>The Century of the Child</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Key&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Key&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ellen Key was born at Sundsholm mansion in <a href="/wiki/Sm%C3%A5land" title="Småland">Småland</a>, Sweden, on 11 December 1849.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Her father was Emil Key, the founder of the Swedish Agrarian Party and a frequent contributor to the Swedish newspaper <i>Aftonposten</i>. Her mother was Sophie Posse Key, who was born into an aristocratic family from the southernmost part of <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A5ne_County" title="Skåne County">Skåne County</a>. Emil bought Sundsholm at the time of his wedding; twenty years later he sold it for financial reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ellen was mostly educated at home, where her mother taught her grammar and arithmetic and her foreign-born governess taught her foreign languages. She cited reading <i>Amtmandens Døtre</i> (<i>The Official's Daughters</i>, 1855) by <a href="/wiki/Camilla_Collett" title="Camilla Collett">Camilla Collett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henrik_Ibsen" title="Henrik Ibsen">Henrik Ibsen</a>'s plays <i>Kjærlighedens komedie</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Love%27s_Comedy" title="Love's Comedy">Love's Comedy</a></i>, 1862), <i><a href="/wiki/Brand_(play)" title="Brand (play)">Brand</a></i> (1865), and <i><a href="/wiki/Peer_Gynt" title="Peer Gynt">Peer Gynt</a></i> (1867) as her childhood influences. When she was twenty years old, her father was elected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Sweden" title="Parliament of Sweden">Riksdag</a> and they moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stockholm,_Sweden" title="Stockholm, Sweden">Stockholm</a>, where she would capitalize on the access to libraries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup> Ellen Key also studied at the progressive <a href="/wiki/Rossander_Course" title="Rossander Course">Rossander Course</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1870s">1870s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Key&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1870s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After a correspondence with Urban von Feilitzen, who wrote <i>Protestantismens Maria-kult</i> (<i>The Protestant Cult of Mary</i>, 1874), she had written a review of the book for a periodical, under the pseudonym Robinson. His book gave her thoughts structure, helping to define her beliefs concerning the role of women as mothers and nurturers. Key hoped Feilitzen would leave his wife, as they did not share similar interests, but he refused.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the summer of 1874, Key traveled to Denmark and studied their folk colleges. Folk colleges were institutions of higher learning for young people from the countryside. One of her early ambitions was to found a Swedish folk high school, but instead she decided, in 1880, to become a teacher at <a href="/wiki/Anna_Whitlock" title="Anna Whitlock">Anna Whitlock</a>'s school for girls in Stockholm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly after she moved to Stockholm, she befriended <a href="/wiki/Sophie_Adlersparre" title="Sophie Adlersparre">Sophie Adlersparre</a>, who was the editor of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidskrift_f%C3%B6r_Hemmet" title="Tidskrift för Hemmet">Tidskrift för Hemmet</a></i> (<i>Journal for the Home</i>), founded in 1859 by Adlersparre and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosalie_Roos" title="Rosalie Roos">Rosalie Olivecrona</a>. In 1874 <i>Tidskrift för Hemmet</i> published her first article. It was about <a href="/wiki/Camilla_Collett" title="Camilla Collett">Camilla Collett</a>, and other articles soon followed. She would also do some biographical studies on <a href="/wiki/George_Eliot" title="George Eliot">George Eliot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Barrett_Browning" title="Elizabeth Barrett Browning">Elizabeth Barrett Browning</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fredrika-Bremer-f%C3%B6rbundet" title="Fredrika-Bremer-förbundet">Fredrika-Bremer-förbundet</a> (the Fredrika Bremer Society), the liberal women's organization, was founded in 1884. Many of the writers for <i>Tidskrift för Hemmet</i> were members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1880s">1880s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Key&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1880s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1883, Key began teaching at Anton Nyström new school, the People's Institute, which was founded in 1880. She also helped organize "The Twelves", a group of twelve upper class ladies who sponsored and organized social functions to help improve working class ladies' manners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Even though Key did share a lot of similar beliefs with the members of Fredrika-Bremer-förbundet, two main issues made her oppose the group in the mid-1880s: the importance of sexuality and the social significance of the biological differences between women and men. 1886 saw Key publishing <i>Om reaktionen mot kvinnofrågan</i> (<i>On the Reaction against the Woman Question</i>) which was highly critical and argued against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egalitarian" title="Egalitarian">egalitarian</a> tendencies of the Swedish women's movement. The piece was published in <a href="/wiki/Gustaf_af_Geijerstam" title="Gustaf af Geijerstam">Gustaf af Geijerstam</a>'s journal <i>Revy i litterära och sociala frågor</i> (<i>Review of Literary and Social Issues</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in 1886, she wrote a review of <i>En sommarsaga</i> (<i>A Summer Story</i>, 1886) by <a href="/wiki/Anne_Charlotte_Leffler" title="Anne Charlotte Leffler">Anne Charlotte Leffler</a> in the short-lived journal <i>Framåt</i> (<i>Forward</i>). She was critical of the piece for having one woman's attempt to combine marriage, motherhood, and a career as an artist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Key contributed to three journals all with different views on women's rights: <i>Tidskrift för Hemmet</i>, <i>Dagny</i>, and <i>Framåt</i>. The latter was edited by <a href="/wiki/Gothenburg" title="Gothenburg">Göteborg</a> Alma Åkermark and tended to have taboo information, including publishing texts on <a href="/wiki/Syphilis" title="Syphilis">syphilis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sexual_repression" title="Sexual repression">sexual repression</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mathilda_Malling" title="Mathilda Malling">Mathilda Malling</a>'s <i>Pyrrhus-segrar</i> (<i>Pyrrhic Victories</i>), published in 1886 under the pseudonym Stella Kleve, was very controversial among Scandinavian intellectuals. The story dealt with a dying young woman, who laments that if she had done the things she wanted to do, she may not be dying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in <i>Naturenliga arbetsområden för kvinnan</i> (<i>Natural Lines of Work for Women</i>) and <i>Kvinnopsykologi och kvinnlig logik</i> (<i>Female Psychology and Logic</i>, 1896) Key said a "monogamous heterosexual relationship aimed toward procreation formed the crux of a woman's happiness and fulfillment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1889, she published <i>Några tankar om huru reaktioner uppstå, jämte ett genmäle till d:r Carl v. Bergen, samt om yttrande och tryckfrihet</i> (<i>Some Thoughts about How Reactions Begin</i>), which marked her a social radical, which she would never deny.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changing_views">Changing views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Key&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Changing views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Key grew up in an atmosphere of <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberalism</a>, and throughout the 1870s her political beliefs were radically liberal. She was republican-minded, with the idea of freedom holding vast importance for her. As the 1880s advanced, her thinking became even more radical, affecting first her religious beliefs and then her views on life in society in general. This was the outcome of extensive reading. During the latter part of the 1880s and particularly in the 1890s, she began to read socialist literature and turned increasingly towards <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>.</p>
<p>Key was raised in a rigid Christian household, but while growing up she started questioning her views. From 1879 she studied <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Spencer" title="Herbert Spencer">Herbert Spencer</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T._H._Huxley" title="T. H. Huxley">T. H. Huxley</a>. In the autumn of that year she met both Huxley and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haeckel" title="Haeckel">Haeckel</a>, the German biologist and philosopher, in London. The principle of <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>, in which Ellen Key had come to believe, was also to have an influence on her educational views.</p>
<p>She is quoted as having said:</p>
<dl>
<dd>"Side by side with the class war, the culture war must ceaselessly be waged by the young and among the young upon whom rests the responsibility of making the new society better for all than the old could be."</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Key&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the late 1880s/early 1890s, Key decided to write biographies of women who had prominent roles in Swedish intellectual life; they were: <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Benedictsson" title="Victoria Benedictsson">Victoria Benedictsson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anne_Charlotte_Leffler" title="Anne Charlotte Leffler">Anne Charlotte Leffler</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonia_Kovalevsky" title="Sonia Kovalevsky">Sonia Kovalevsky</a>. She would also write about <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">Johann Wolfgang von Goethe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jonas_Love_Almqvist" title="Carl Jonas Love Almqvist">Carl Jonas Love Almqvist</a>.</p>
<p>After she retired from teaching, she met and helped the young poet <a href="/wiki/Rainer_Maria_Rilke" title="Rainer Maria Rilke">Rainer Maria Rilke</a>. She was later painted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanna_Pauli" title="Hanna Pauli">Hanna Pauli</a>. In <i>Die Antifeministen</i> (<i>The Antifeminists</i>, 1902) by <a href="/wiki/Hedwig_Dohm" title="Hedwig Dohm">Hedwig Dohm</a> cited both Key and <a href="/wiki/Lou_Andreas-Salom%C3%A9" title="Lou Andreas-Salomé">Lou Andreas-Salomé</a> as anti-feminists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-11"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>She died on 25 April 1926 at the age of seventy-six.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-12"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Selected_works">Selected works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Key&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Selected works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ellen Key started her career as a writer in the mid-1870s with literary essays. She became known to a large public through the pamphlet <i>On Freedom of Speech and Publishing</i> (1889). Her name and her books then became the topic of lively discussions. The following work focuses on her views on education, personal freedom, and the independent development of the individual. These works include:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>Individualism and Socialism</i> (1896)</li>
<li><i>Images of Thought</i> (1898)</li>
<li><i>Human-beings</i> (1899)</li>
<li><i>Lifelines</i>, volumes I-III (1903–06)</li>
<li><i>Neutrality of the Souls</i> (1916).</li>
</ul>
<p>On education, her earliest article may be <i>Teachers for Infants at Home and in School</i> in <i>Tidskrift för hemmet</i> (1876). Her first more widely read essay, <i>Books versus Coursebooks</i>, was published in the journal <i>Verdandi</i> (1884). Later, in the same journal, she published other articles <i>A Statement on Co-Education</i> (1888) and <i>Murdering the Soul in Schools</i> (1891). Later she published the works <i>Education</i> (1897) and <i>Beauty for All</i> (1899).</p>
<p>In 1906 came <i>Popular Education with Special Consideration for the Development of Aesthetic Sense</i>. In the last books Key views <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a>, as beauty and art, from the aspect of the elevation of humanity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Several of Key's writings were translated into English by <a href="/wiki/Mamah_Borthwick" title="Mamah Borthwick">Mamah Borthwick</a>, during the period of her affair with <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lloyd_Wright" title="Frank Lloyd Wright">Frank Lloyd Wright</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Among her best-known works published in English:</p>
<ul>
<li><i>The Morality of Woman</i> (1911)</li>
<li><i>Love and Marriage</i> (1911, repr. with critical and biographical notes by <a href="/wiki/Havelock_Ellis" title="Havelock Ellis">Havelock Ellis</a>, 1931)</li>
<li><i>The Century of the Child</i> (1909)</li>
<li><i>The Woman Movement</i> (1912)</li>
<li><i>The Younger Generation</i> (1914)</li>
<li><i>War, Peace, and the Future</i> (1916).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbia_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Columbia-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellen_Key&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She has inspired writers such as <a href="/wiki/Selma_Lagerl%C3%B6f" title="Selma Lagerlöf">Selma Lagerlöf</a>, Marika Stjernstedt, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waka_Yamada" title="Waka Yamada">Waka Yamada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elin_W%C3%A4gner" title="Elin Wägner">Elin Wägner</a>. <a href="/wiki/Maria_Montessori" title="Maria Montessori">Maria Montessori</a> wrote that she predicted the 20th Century would be <i>the century of the child</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Havelock_Ellis" title="Havelock Ellis">Havelock Ellis</a> wrote positively on her studies of human sexuality.</p>
<p>Key maintained that motherhood is so crucial to society that the government, rather than their husbands, should support mothers and their children. These ideas regarding <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=State_child_support&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="State child support (page does not exist)">state child support</a> influenced social legislation in several countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Columbia_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Columbia-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>A substantial collection of Key's papers is at the Royal Library in Stockholm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson_3-13"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1890s, Key commissioned the Strand house designed by architect Yngve Rasmussen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the 1890s, it was "a centre for the politically radical intellectual and artistic avant-garde of Stockholm".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Key's house has become a foundation and tourist spot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
