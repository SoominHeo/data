<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Elevator pitch</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Pitch_(disambiguation)" title="Pitch (disambiguation)">Pitch</a>.</div>
<p>An <b>elevator pitch</b>, <b>elevator speech</b> or <b>elevator statement</b> is a short <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sales_pitch" title="Sales pitch">sales pitch</a>, that is, a summary used to quickly and simply define a process, product, service, organization, or event and its <a href="/wiki/Value_proposition" title="Value proposition">value proposition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>The name 'elevator pitch' reflects the idea that it should be possible to deliver the summary in the time span of an <a href="/wiki/Elevator" title="Elevator">elevator</a> ride, or approximately thirty seconds to two minutes and is widely credited to Ilene Rosenzweig and Michael Caruso (while he was editor for <i>Vanity Fair</i>) for its origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The term itself comes from a scenario of an accidental meeting with someone important in the elevator. If the conversation inside the elevator in those few seconds is interesting and value adding, the conversation will either continue after the elevator ride, or end in exchange of <a href="/wiki/Business_card" title="Business card">business cards</a> or a scheduled meeting. A variety of people, including <a href="/wiki/Project_manager" title="Project manager">project managers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salesperson" title="Salesperson">salespeople</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evangelism_marketing" title="Evangelism marketing">evangelists</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Public_administration" title="Public administration">policy-makers</a>, commonly rehearse and use elevator pitches to get their points across quickly.</p>
