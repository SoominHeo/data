<p><b>Ellison Shoji Onizuka</b> (June 24, 1946Â â January 28, 1986) was an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a> from <a href="/wiki/Kealakekua,_Hawaii" title="Kealakekua, Hawaii">Kealakekua, Hawaii</a>, who successfully flew into space with the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery">Space Shuttle <i>Discovery</i></a> on <a href="/wiki/STS-51-C" title="STS-51-C">STS-51-C</a>. He died in the destruction of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Challenger" title="Space Shuttle Challenger">Space Shuttle <i>Challenger</i></a>, on which he was serving as <a href="/wiki/Mission_Specialist" title="Mission Specialist">Mission Specialist</a> for mission <a href="/wiki/STS-51-L" title="STS-51-L">STS-51-L</a>. He was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Asian_American_astronauts" title="List of Asian American astronauts">first Asian American</a> and the first person of Japanese ancestry to reach space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellison_Onizuka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born June 24, 1946, Onizuka was the oldest son and second youngest child of the late Masamitsu and Mitsue Onizuka. He was a Buddhist. He had two older sisters, Shirley and Norma, and a younger brother, Claude, who became the family spokesman after the <i>Challenger</i> disaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Growing up, Ellison was an active participant in <a href="/wiki/National_FFA_Organization" title="National FFA Organization">FFA</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/4-H" title="4-H">4-H</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Scouting_in_Hawaii" title="Scouting in Hawaii">Boy Scouts</a>, where he reached the level of <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Scout_(Boy_Scouts_of_America)" title="Eagle Scout (Boy Scouts of America)">Eagle Scout</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-honor_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-honor-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Onizuka graduated from <a href="/wiki/Konawaena_High_School" title="Konawaena High School">Konawaena High School</a> in 1964. He received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> degree in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerospace_Engineering" title="Aerospace Engineering">Aerospace Engineering</a> in June 1969, and a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a> degree in that field in December of the same year, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_at_Boulder" title="University of Colorado at Boulder">University of Colorado at Boulder</a>. He participated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROTC" title="ROTC">ROTC</a> during his time there and is an alumnus of <a href="/wiki/Triangle_Fraternity" title="Triangle Fraternity">Triangle Fraternity</a>.</p>
<p>Onizuka married Lorna Leiko Yoshida on June 7, 1969,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> while completing his studies at the University of Colorado. They had two daughters, Janelle Onizuka-Gillilan (b. 1969) and Darien Lei Shizue Onizuka-Morgan (b. 1975).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_Force_career">Air Force career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellison_Onizuka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Air Force career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On January 15, 1970, Onizuka entered active duty with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-graves_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-graves-8">[8]</a></sup> where he served as a <a href="/wiki/Flight_test_engineer" title="Flight test engineer">flight test engineer</a> and as a <a href="/wiki/Test_pilot" title="Test pilot">test pilot</a>. At the Sacramento Air Logistics Center at <a href="/wiki/McClellan_Air_Force_Base" title="McClellan Air Force Base">McClellan Air Force Base</a>. He worked in test flight programs and systems security engineering for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-84" title="F-84">F-84</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-100_Super_Sabre" title="F-100 Super Sabre">F-100</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-105" title="F-105">F-105</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-111" title="F-111">F-111</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EC-121" title="EC-121">EC-121</a>T, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-33_Shooting_Star" title="T-33 Shooting Star">T-33</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-39" title="T-39">T-39</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-28_Trojan" title="T-28 Trojan">T-28</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-1_Skyraider" title="A-1 Skyraider">A-1</a>.</p>
<p>From August 1974 to July 1975, Onizuka attended the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force_Test_Pilot_School" title="U.S. Air Force Test Pilot School">U.S. Air Force Test Pilot School</a>. In July 1975, he was assigned to the Flight Test Center at <a href="/wiki/Edwards_Air_Force_Base" title="Edwards Air Force Base">Edwards Air Force Base</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He became a squadron flight test engineer at the USAF Test Pilot School, and later worked as a manager for engineering support in the training resources division. His duties there consisted of course instruction and management of the airship fleet (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-7_Corsair_II" title="A-7 Corsair II">A-7</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-37_Dragonfly" title="A-37 Dragonfly">A-37</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-38_Talon" title="T-38 Talon">T-38</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-4_Phantom_II" title="F-4 Phantom II">F-4</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-33_Shooting_Star" title="T-33 Shooting Star">T-33</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KC-135" title="KC-135">NKC-135</a>) being used for the Test Pilot School and Flight Test Center. While at the school, he registered more than 1,700 flight hours.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_career">NASA career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellison_Onizuka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: NASA career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Onizuka was selected for the astronaut program in January 1978, and completed one year of evaluation and training in August 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Later, he worked in the experimentation team, Orbiter test team, and launch support crew at <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center" title="Kennedy Space Center">Kennedy Space Center</a> for the STS-1 and STS-2. At NASA, he worked on the <a href="/wiki/Shuttle_Avionics_Integration_Laboratory" title="Shuttle Avionics Integration Laboratory">Shuttle Avionics Integration Laboratory</a> (SAIL) test and revision software team.</p>
<p>Onizuka's first space mission took place on January 24, 1985, with the launch of mission <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/STS_51-C" title="STS 51-C">STS 51-C</a> on <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery">Space Shuttle <i>Discovery</i></a>, the first space shuttle mission for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He was accompanied by Commander <a href="/wiki/Ken_Mattingly" title="Ken Mattingly">Ken Mattingly</a>, Pilot <a href="/wiki/Loren_Shriver" title="Loren Shriver">Loren Shriver</a>, fellow <a href="/wiki/Mission_Specialist" title="Mission Specialist">Mission Specialist</a> <a href="/wiki/James_Buchli" title="James Buchli">James Buchli</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Payload_Specialist" title="Payload Specialist">Payload Specialist</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gary_E._Payton" title="Gary E. Payton">Gary E. Payton</a>. During the mission, he was responsible for the activities of the primary payloads, which included the unfolding of the Inertial Upper Stage (IUS) surface. After 48 orbits around the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, <i>Discovery</i> landed at Kennedy Space Center on January 27, 1985. He had completed a total of 74 hours in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a>.</p>

<p>Onizuka was assigned to the mission <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/STS_51-L" title="STS 51-L">STS 51-L</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Challenger" title="Space Shuttle Challenger">Space Shuttle <i>Challenger</i></a> that took off from Kennedy Space Center at 11:38:00 EST (16:38:00 UTC) on January 28, 1986. The other <i>Challenger</i> crew members were commander <a href="/wiki/Dick_Scobee" title="Dick Scobee">Dick Scobee</a>, pilot <a href="/wiki/Michael_J._Smith_(astronaut)" title="Michael J. Smith (astronaut)">Michael J. Smith</a>, mission specialists <a href="/wiki/Ronald_McNair" title="Ronald McNair">Ronald McNair</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judith_Resnik" title="Judith Resnik">Judith Resnik</a>, and payload specialists <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Jarvis" title="Gregory Jarvis">Gregory Jarvis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christa_McAuliffe" title="Christa McAuliffe">Christa McAuliffe</a>. It was destroyed when a leaking solid rocket booster ruptured the fuel tank 73 seconds after launch. All seven crew members were killed.</p>
<p>Following the <i>Challenger</i> disaster, examination of the recovered vehicle cockpit revealed that three of the crew members <a href="/wiki/Personal_Egress_Air_Pack" title="Personal Egress Air Pack">Personal Egress Air Packs</a> were activated: those of Onizuka, mission specialist <a href="/wiki/Judith_Resnik" title="Judith Resnik">Judith Resnik</a>, and pilot <a href="/wiki/Michael_J._Smith_(astronaut)" title="Michael J. Smith (astronaut)">Michael Smith</a>. The location of Smith's activation switch, on the back side of his seat, means that either Resnik or Onizuka could have activated it for him. This is the only evidence available from the disaster that shows Onizuka and Resnik were alive after the cockpit separated from the vehicle. However, if the cabin had lost pressure, the packs alone would not have sustained the crew during the two-minute descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Onizuka was buried at the <a href="/wiki/National_Memorial_Cemetery_of_the_Pacific" title="National Memorial Cemetery of the Pacific">National Memorial Cemetery of the Pacific</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honolulu,_Hawaii" title="Honolulu, Hawaii">Honolulu, Hawaii</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-graves_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-graves-8">[8]</a></sup> At the time of his death, he held the rank of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Colonel" title="Lieutenant Colonel">Lieutenant Colonel</a>. Posthumously, he was promoted to the rank of <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_States)" title="Colonel (United States)">Colonel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Memberships_and_distinctions">Memberships and distinctions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellison_Onizuka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Memberships and distinctions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Onizuka belonged to the following organizations: Society of Flight Test Engineers, the <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Association" title="Air Force Association">Air Force Association</a>, the <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Aeronautics_and_Astronautics" title="American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics">American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tau_Beta_Pi" title="Tau Beta Pi">Tau Beta Pi</a>, Sigma Tau, <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Air_Society" title="Arnold Air Society">Arnold Air Society</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Triangle_Fraternity" title="Triangle Fraternity">Triangle Fraternity</a>.</p>
<p>Among his distinctions are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Force_Commendation_Medal" title="Air Force Commendation Medal">Air Force Commendation Medal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meritorious_Service_Medal_(United_States)" title="Meritorious Service Medal (United States)">Air Force Meritorious Service Medal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Outstanding_Unit_Award" title="Air Force Outstanding Unit Award">Air Force Outstanding Unit Award</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Force_Organizational_Excellence_Award" title="Air Force Organizational Excellence Award">Air Force Organizational Excellence Award</a>, and <a href="/wiki/National_Defense_Service_Medal" title="National Defense Service Medal">National Defense Service Medal</a>. He was posthumously awarded the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Space_Medal_of_Honor" title="Congressional Space Medal of Honor">Congressional Space Medal of Honor</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellison_Onizuka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Onizuka Space Center at <a href="/wiki/Kona_International_Airport" title="Kona International Airport">Kona International Airport</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kona_District,_Hawaii" title="Kona District, Hawaii">Kona district</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(island)" title="Hawaii (island)">Hawai<span class="unicode">Ê»</span>i island</a> where he was born and raised, are dedicated to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomical</a> features were also named after him: an <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> discovered by <a href="/wiki/Edward_L._G._Bowell" title="Edward L. G. Bowell">Edward L. G. Bowell</a> on February 8, 1984, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3355_Onizuka" title="3355 Onizuka">3355 Onizuka</a> and a 29-km-diameter <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">crater</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Onizuka_(crater)" title="Onizuka (crater)">Onizuka</a>.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Little_Tokyo,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Little Tokyo, Los Angeles, California">Little Tokyo</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a> also has a street named after him, as does the street surrounding Whitcomb Elementary school in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clear_Lake_City,_Houston,_Texas" title="Clear Lake City, Houston, Texas">Clear Lake City, Houston, Texas</a>, where his daughters attended. It also named its library the Onizuka Memorial Library. (At the time of the <i>Challenger</i> disaster, his older daughter, Janelle, attended Clear Lake High School. His younger daughter, Darien Lei, was at Whitcomb.) In addition, Onizuka Street in Little Tokyo has a scale replica of the <i>Challenger</i> as a memorial, and a permanent memorial to Onizuka is located in the lobby of the Hompa Hongwanji Buddhist Temple. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Onizuka_Center_for_International_Astronomy" title="Onizuka Center for International Astronomy">Onizuka Center for International Astronomy</a>, named in Onizuka's honor, is the mid-level support and visitor complex for the <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Kea_Observatories" title="Mauna Kea Observatories">Mauna Kea Observatories</a> in Hawaii. It includes a Visitor Information Station as well as dining, lodging, office, and maintenance facilities for observatory staff and astronomers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A plaque of his face is mounted on a boulder by the entrance to the Visitor Information Station. <a href="/wiki/Triangle_Fraternity" title="Triangle Fraternity">Triangle Fraternity</a> has the Ellison Onizuka Young Alumnus Award in tribute to him.</p>
<p>The Engineering Center at the University of Colorado at Boulder has a conference room named after him.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Air_Society" title="Arnold Air Society">Arnold Air Society</a> Squadron attached to the 105th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_Force_ROTC" title="Air Force ROTC">Air Force ROTC</a> Detachment at the University of Colorado at Boulder bears Onizuka's name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Page 28 (Page X of additional page inserts) of every new U.S. passport contains this quotation: "Every generation has the obligation to free men's minds for a look at new worlds... to look out from a higher plateau than the last generation." - Ellison Onizuka</p>
<p>The Hawaii Space Grant Consortium holds an annual Astronaut Ellison Onizuka Science Day<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii-Hilo" title="University of Hawaii-Hilo">University of Hawai'i-Hilo</a> for students in grades 4-12, parents and teachers. <a href="/wiki/El_Camino_College" title="El Camino College">El Camino College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Torrance,_California" title="Torrance, California">Torrance, California</a> hosts an annual Onizuka Space Science Day,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> jointly organized by the Onizuka Memorial Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The students at the United States Air Force Test Pilot School present the <a href="/wiki/Onizuka_Prop_Wash_Award" title="Onizuka Prop Wash Award">Onizuka Prop Wash Award</a> to the classmate who contributed most to class spirit and morale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 1, 2017 the airport in his home district of Kona was renamed Ellison Onizuka <a href="/wiki/Kona_International_Airport" title="Kona International Airport">Kona International Airport</a> at KeÄhole.</p>
