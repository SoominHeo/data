<p><b>Elio Vittorini</b> (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:It-Elio_Vittorini.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4f/It-Elio_Vittorini.ogg" title="It-Elio Vittorini.ogg">pronunciation</a></span> <small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">(<a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help"><span style="cursor:help;">help</span></a>·<a href="/wiki/File:It-Elio_Vittorini.ogg" title="File:It-Elio Vittorini.ogg"><span style="cursor:help;">info</span></a>)</small></span>; 23 July 1908 – 12 February 1966) was an Italian writer and novelist. He was a contemporary of <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Pavese" title="Cesare Pavese">Cesare Pavese</a> and an influential voice in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modernist" title="Modernist">modernist</a> school of novel writing. His best-known work is the <a href="/wiki/Anti-fascism" title="Anti-fascism">anti-fascist</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/Conversations_in_Sicily" title="Conversations in Sicily">Conversations in Sicily</a></i>, for which he was jailed when it was published in 1941. The first U.S. edition of the novel, published in 1949, included an introduction from <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a>, whose style influenced Vittorini and that novel in particular.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elio_Vittorini&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vittorini was born in <a href="/wiki/Syracuse,_Sicily" title="Syracuse, Sicily">Syracuse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, and throughout his childhood moved around Sicily with his father, a railroad worker. Several times he ran away from home, culminating in his leaving Sicily for good in 1924. For a brief period, he found employment as a construction worker in the <a href="/wiki/Julian_March" title="Julian March">Julian March</a>, after which he moved to <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> to work as a type corrector (a line of work he abandoned in 1934 due to <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead poisoning</a>). Around 1927 his work began to be published in literary journals. In many cases, separate editions of his novels and short stories from this period, such as <i>The Red Carnation</i> were not published until after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, due to fascist censorship. In 1937, he was expelled from the <a href="/wiki/National_Fascist_Party" title="National Fascist Party">national Fascist Party</a> for writing in support of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Spanish_Republic" title="Second Spanish Republic">Republican</a> side in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>.</p>
<p>In 1939 he moved, this time to <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>. An anthology of <a href="/wiki/American_literature" title="American literature">American literature</a> which he edited was, once more, delayed by censorship. Remaining an outspoken critic of <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>'s regime, Vittorini was arrested and jailed in 1942. He joined the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Communist_Party" title="Italian Communist Party">Italian Communist Party</a> and began taking an active role in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_Resistance" title="Italian Resistance">Resistance</a>, which provided the basis for his 1945 novel <i>Men and not Men</i>. Also in 1945, he briefly became the editor of the Italian Communist daily <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%27Unita" title="L'Unita">L'Unita</a></i> and weekly <i><a href="/wiki/Il_Politecnico" title="Il Politecnico">Il Politecnico</a></i> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>After the war, Vittorini chiefly concentrated on his work as editor, helping publish work by young Italians such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvino" title="Calvino">Calvino</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beppe_Fenoglio" title="Beppe Fenoglio">Fenoglio</a>. His last major published work of fiction during his lifetime was 1956's <i>Erica and her Sisters</i>. The news of the events of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_Uprising_of_1956" title="Hungarian Uprising of 1956">Hungarian Uprising</a> deeply shook his convictions in Communism and made him decide to largely abandon writing,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> leaving unfinished work which was to be published in unedited form posthumously. For the remainder of his life, Vittorini continued in his post as an editor. In 1959, hero-founded with Calvino <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Il_Menab%C3%B2&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Il Menabò (page does not exist)">Il Menabò</a>, a cultural journal devoted to literature in the modern industrial age. He also ran as a candidate on an <a href="/wiki/Italian_Socialist_Party" title="Italian Socialist Party">Italian Socialist Party</a> list. He died in Milan in 1966. He was an atheist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
