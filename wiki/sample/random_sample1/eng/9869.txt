<p><b>Mary Elizabeth Alexander Hanford</b> "<b>Liddy</b>" <b>Dole</b> (born July 29, 1936)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genealogy_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-genealogy-1">[1]</a></sup> is an American politician who served in both the <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a> <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">presidential</a> administrations, as well as in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a>.</p>
<p>A graduate of <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard Law School</a>, Dole served as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Transportation" title="Secretary of Transportation">Secretary of Transportation</a> under <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Labor" title="Secretary of Labor">Secretary of Labor</a> under <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a> before becoming head of the <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a>. She next served as <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>'s first female <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">U.S. senator</a> (2003–09).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> She is a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Republican_Party" title="United States Republican Party">Republican Party</a> and former chair of the <a href="/wiki/National_Republican_Senatorial_Committee" title="National Republican Senatorial Committee">National Republican Senatorial Committee</a>. She is married to former U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senate_Majority_Leader" title="Senate Majority Leader">Senate majority leader</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1976" title="United States presidential election, 1976">1976 Republican vice-presidential nominee</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1996" title="United States presidential election, 1996">1996 Republican presidential nominee</a> <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dole" title="Bob Dole">Bob Dole</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_family">Early life and family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dole was born Mary Elizabeth Alexander Hanford in <a href="/wiki/Salisbury,_North_Carolina" title="Salisbury, North Carolina">Salisbury, North Carolina</a>, to Mary Ella (<i>née</i> Cathey; 1901–2004) and John Van Hanford (1893–1978).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genealogy_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-genealogy-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dole first met her future husband, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dole" title="Bob Dole">Bob Dole</a>, in the spring of 1972 at a meeting arranged by her boss and mentor, <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Knauer" title="Virginia Knauer">Virginia Knauer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The couple dated, and she became his second wife on December 6, 1975, in the <a href="/wiki/Washington_National_Cathedral" title="Washington National Cathedral">Washington National Cathedral</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-time-5">[5]</a></sup> They have no children, though she is stepmother to Bob's adult daughter Robin from his first marriage of 24 years, which ended in divorce in 1972.</p>
<p>She attended individually, and later with her husband, the Foundry <a href="/wiki/United_Methodist_Church" title="United Methodist Church">United Methodist Church</a> in Washington, D.C., before joining the <a href="/wiki/National_Presbyterian_Church" title="National Presbyterian Church">National Presbyterian Church</a> in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-time-5">[5]</a></sup> Articles at the time reported that the Doles stopped attending Foundry in 1995, finding the pastor at the time, <a href="/wiki/J._Philip_Wogaman" title="J. Philip Wogaman">J. Philip Wogaman</a>, too liberal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dole attended <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a> and graduated with distinction in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_Science" title="Political Science">political science</a>, on June 2, 1958. She was a finalist for an Angier B. Duke scholarship, a full-tuition award given to outstanding applicants who matriculate at Duke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> She was elected to Phi Beta Kappa, and was a recipient of the Algernon Sydney Sullivan award, a national prize given to those exemplifying the ideal of service to others.</p>
<p>Among her activities at Duke were the chapel choir, <i>Chanticleer</i> (yearbook) business staff, freshman advisory council, The Order of the White Duchy, a local honorary society for outstanding women student leaders, female counterpart of the <a href="/wiki/The_Order_of_The_Red_Friars" title="The Order of The Red Friars">The Order of The Red Friars</a>, Phi Kappa Delta, a local leadership honorary for senior women, and <a href="/wiki/Pi_Sigma_Alpha" title="Pi Sigma Alpha">Pi Sigma Alpha</a>, a national political science honorary society. Dole is a sister of <a href="/wiki/Delta_Delta_Delta" title="Delta Delta Delta">Delta Delta Delta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> She was also elected president of the woman's student government association, 1958 May queen, and "leader of the year" by the student newspaper, <a href="/wiki/The_Chronicle_(Duke_University)" title="The Chronicle (Duke University)">The Chronicle</a>.</p>
<p>Dole has remained involved with Duke University, serving at various points in time as president of the Duke University alumnae association, and a member of the board of trustees and board of visitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> She has also spoken at Duke several times, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>1982: Delivered commencement address for the <a href="/wiki/Fuqua_School_of_Business" title="Fuqua School of Business">Fuqua School of Business</a></li>
<li>1989: Delivered address on labor problems and education at the Founder's Day Convocation.</li>
<li>1997: Shared her experiences in humanitarian work in a Senior Week speech.</li>
<li>2000: Delivered the commencement address, on the theme of representative government. Awarded an honorary degree, Doctor of Humane Letters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Following her graduation from Duke, she did her post-graduate work at Oxford in 1959. After Oxford, she took a job as a student teacher at <a href="/wiki/Melrose_High_School_(Massachusetts)" title="Melrose High School (Massachusetts)">Melrose High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Melrose,_Massachusetts" title="Melrose, Massachusetts">Melrose, Massachusetts</a>, for the 1959–1960 school year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> While teaching, she also pursued her master's degree in education from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, which she earned in 1960, followed by a <a href="/wiki/Juris_Doctor" title="Juris Doctor">J.D.</a> from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard Law School</a> in 1965. At graduation, she was one of 24 women in a class of 550 students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> She is an alumna of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa" title="Phi Beta Kappa">Phi Beta Kappa</a> honor society.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="White_House_years">White House years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: White House years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Johnson_Administration">Johnson Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Johnson Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

Johnson">Johnson</a> presidential ticket in 1960, worked in the White House in the later years of the administration of <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nixon_and_Ford_Administrations">Nixon and Ford Administrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nixon and Ford Administrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When many <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrats</a> left the White House following <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>'s replacement of Johnson, Dole did not. From 1969 to 1973, she served as deputy assistant to President Nixon for consumer affairs. In 1973, Nixon appointed her to a seven-year term on the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a>. In 1975, she became a Republican. She took a leave from her post as a Federal Trade Commissioner for several months in 1976 to campaign for her husband for vice president of the United States, when he ran on the Republican ticket with <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a>. She later resigned from the FTC in 1979, to campaign for her husband's 1980 presidential run. During the 1970s, Dole was a self-described member of the <a href="/wiki/Second-wave_feminism" title="Second-wave feminism">Women's Liberation Movement</a> and helped reform laws to ensure equal credit for women. She was also a supporter of the <a href="/wiki/Equal_Rights_Amendment" title="Equal Rights Amendment">Equal Rights Amendment</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reagan_Administration_and_Secretary_of_Transportation">Reagan Administration and Secretary of Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reagan Administration and Secretary of Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>She served as director of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_House_Office_of_Public_Liaison" title="White House Office of Public Liaison">White House Office of Public Liaison</a>, from 1981 to 1983 and as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Transportation" title="United States Secretary of Transportation">United States Secretary of Transportation</a> from 1983 to 1987 under <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>. She was also appointed by Reagan to chair task forces that sought to reform federal and state laws to ensure equal rights for women. She was the <a href="/wiki/List_of_female_United_States_Cabinet_Secretaries" title="List of female United States Cabinet Secretaries">first woman</a> appointed Secretary of Transportation. In this role, she was the first woman to have served as the head of a branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Military" title="United States Military">United States Military</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">United States Coast Guard</a> was under the Department of Transportation at the time. Dole's appointment was "particularly irritating" to <a href="/wiki/Conservatism_in_the_United_States" title="Conservatism in the United States">conservative</a> activists, since "though at least nominally opposed to abortion, [she was] viewed by the right as [an] aggressive <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminist</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>During her tenure, the <a href="/wiki/National_Highway_Traffic_Safety_Administration" title="National Highway Traffic Safety Administration">National Highway Traffic Safety Administration</a>, mandated the installation of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Center_high-mounted_stop_lamp" title="Center high-mounted stop lamp">center high-mounted stop lamp</a> on new cars; these are sometimes called "Liddy Lights" in her recognition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> She worked with MADD (<a href="/wiki/Mothers_Against_Drunk_Driving" title="Mothers Against Drunk Driving">Mothers Against Drunk Driving</a>) to pass laws withholding federal highway funding from any state that had a drinking age below twenty-one. The state government of <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a> opposed the drinking age law and sued Dole in the case <i><a href="/wiki/South_Dakota_v._Dole" title="South Dakota v. Dole">South Dakota v. Dole</a></i>, but the Supreme Court ruled in favor of Dole. She oversaw the privatization of the national freight railroad, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consolidated_Rail_Corporation" title="Consolidated Rail Corporation">CONRAIL</a>. She initiated random <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drug_testing" title="Drug testing">drug testing</a> within the Department of Transportation.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bush_Administration_and_Secretary_of_Labor">Bush Administration and Secretary of Labor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bush Administration and Secretary of Labor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dole served as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Labor" title="United States Secretary of Labor">United States Secretary of Labor</a> from 1989 to 1990 under <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a>; she is the first woman to serve in two different Cabinet positions in the administrations of two Presidents. Her tenure as both U.S. Transportation Secretary and U.S. Labor Secretary focused heavily on improving public safety and workplace safety and health.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1990, Dole tapped former <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> Administration official and long-time public safety and health advocate, Roy Clason Jr. as Director of Policy of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="U.S. Occupational Safety and Health Administration">U.S. Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> to help her spearhead a number of <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">OSHA</a> reforms to better protect America's 91 million workers from workplace injuries and illnesses.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Red_Cross_presidency">American Red Cross presidency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: American Red Cross presidency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1991, Dole became the <a href="/wiki/President#Non-governmental_presidents" title="President">president</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a>. She served until 1999.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1996_Republican_National_Convention">1996 Republican National Convention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1996 Republican National Convention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dole's husband <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dole" title="Bob Dole">Bob Dole</a> was the Republican nominee in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1996" title="U.S. presidential election, 1996">US presidential election of 1996</a>. Elizabeth Dole, who would have become <a href="/wiki/First_Lady_of_the_United_States" title="First Lady of the United States">First Lady</a> had her husband won the election, received recognition for her speech at the <a href="/wiki/1996_Republican_National_Convention" title="1996 Republican National Convention">1996 Republican National Convention</a>, during which she walked out into the audience while talking conversationally about her husband's qualities.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_United_States_Presidential_candidacy">2000 United States Presidential candidacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2000 United States Presidential candidacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Elizabeth Dole ran for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Republican_Party" title="United States Republican Party">Republican nomination</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_2000" title="U.S. presidential election, 2000">US presidential election of 2000</a>, but pulled out of the race in October 1999 before any of the <a href="/wiki/Primary_election" title="Primary election">primaries</a>, largely due to inadequate <a href="/wiki/Campaign_finance" title="Campaign finance">fundraising</a> even though a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Gallup_Organization" title="The Gallup Organization">Gallup poll</a> had her in second place in the presidential race at 11% behind George W. Bush at 60% as late as October 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Dole placed third—behind <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Forbes" title="Steve Forbes">Steve Forbes</a>—in a large field in the <a href="/wiki/Iowa_Straw_Poll" title="Iowa Straw Poll">Iowa Straw Poll</a> (the first, non-binding, test of electability for the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a> nomination). The Iowa Straw Poll differed from the national polls where she was second only to Bush; Senator <a href="/wiki/John_McCain" title="John McCain">John McCain</a> was in third place.</p>
<p>In July 2000, shortly before the <a href="/wiki/Republican_National_Convention" title="Republican National Convention">Republican National Convention</a> in Philadelphia, Bush campaign sources said Elizabeth Dole was on the short list to be named the vice-presidential nominee, along with Michigan Governor <a href="/wiki/John_Engler" title="John Engler">John Engler</a>, New York Governor <a href="/wiki/George_Pataki" title="George Pataki">George Pataki</a>, Pennsylvania Governor <a href="/wiki/Tom_Ridge" title="Tom Ridge">Tom Ridge</a>, and former Missouri Senator <a href="/wiki/John_Danforth" title="John Danforth">John Danforth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Many pundits believed that Dole was the frontrunner for the Vice Presidential nomination. Bush then surprised most pundits by selecting former U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Defense" title="Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a> <a href="/wiki/Dick_Cheney" title="Dick Cheney">Dick Cheney</a>, who was actually in charge of leading Bush's search for a vice presidential nominee.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_Senate_career">United States Senate career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: United States Senate career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaigns">Campaigns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Campaigns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In late December 2001, Dole shifted her official residency from the Doles' condominium in the <a href="/wiki/Watergate_complex" title="Watergate complex">Watergate complex</a> to her mother's home in Salisbury to seek election to the U.S. Senate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-filing_deed_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-filing_deed-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leaving_KS_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-leaving_KS-18">[18]</a></sup> The seat was made available by the retirement of Republican <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Helms" title="Jesse Helms">Jesse Helms</a>. Although Dole had not lived regularly in North Carolina since 1959 and had been a resident of the Washington area for most of the time since the mid-1960s, the state and national Republican establishment quickly cleared the field for her. She handily won the Republican primary with 80 percent of the vote over a lesser-known candidate, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Ada_Fisher" title="Ada Fisher">Ada Fisher</a>. In the November general election, she defeated her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Democratic_Party" title="United States Democratic Party">Democratic</a> opponent <a href="/wiki/Erskine_Bowles" title="Erskine Bowles">Erskine Bowles</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/White_House_Chief_of_Staff" title="White House Chief of Staff">chief of staff</a> to former President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>, by an eight-point margin.</p>
<p>Her election to the Senate marked the first time a spouse of a former Senator was elected to the Senate from a different state from that of her spouse (although <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a> Senator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nancy_Landon_Kassebaum" title="Nancy Landon Kassebaum">Nancy Landon Kassebaum</a> married former <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> Senator <a href="/wiki/Howard_Baker" title="Howard Baker">Howard Baker</a>, the marriage occurred after Kassebaum and Baker both had finished their service in the Senate). Dole was criticized by Democrats during her first Senate campaign over the fact that for over 40 years prior to her nomination, she had not lived in North Carolina.</p>
<p>In November 2004, following Republican gains in the United States Senate, Dole narrowly edged out Senator <a href="/wiki/Norm_Coleman" title="Norm Coleman">Norm Coleman</a> of Minnesota for the post of chairman of the <a href="/wiki/National_Republican_Senatorial_Committee" title="National Republican Senatorial Committee">National Republican Senatorial Committee</a>. She is the first woman to become chair of the NRSC. During her election cycle as chairperson, her Democratic Party counterpart, Senator <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Schumer" title="Chuck Schumer">Chuck Schumer</a> raised significantly more money, and experienced more success in recruiting candidates. In the November election, Dole's party lost six U.S. Senate seats to the Democrats, thus losing control of the U.S. Senate. Dole was replaced as NRSC chair by Senator <a href="/wiki/John_Ensign" title="John Ensign">John Ensign</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a> following the 2006 midterms.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_Senate_re-election_campaign">2008 Senate re-election campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 2008 Senate re-election campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_North_Carolina,_2008" title="United States Senate election in North Carolina, 2008">United States Senate election in North Carolina, 2008</a></div>
<p>Dole was initially a heavy favorite for re-election, especially after several potential top-tier challengers such as Congressman <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brad_Miller_(congressman)" title="Brad Miller (congressman)">Brad Miller</a>, Governor <a href="/wiki/Mike_Easley" title="Mike Easley">Mike Easley</a> and former Governor <a href="/wiki/Jim_Hunt" title="Jim Hunt">Jim Hunt</a> all declined to compete against Dole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prospect_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-prospect-20">[20]</a></sup> Ultimately, <a href="/wiki/Kay_Hagan" title="Kay Hagan">Kay Hagan</a>, a <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Senate" title="North Carolina Senate">state senator</a> from <a href="/wiki/Greensboro,_North_Carolina" title="Greensboro, North Carolina">Greensboro</a>, won the Democratic primary election against Jim Neal and became Dole's general election opponent. Reports late in the campaign suggested that Dole suffered from <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>'s decision to aggressively contest North Carolina in the presidential election,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economist_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-economist-21">[21]</a></sup> while Hagan received substantial support from independent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/527_group" title="527 group">527 groups</a> lobbying/advertising against Dole,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prospect_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-prospect-20">[20]</a></sup> as well as the Democratic Senate Campaign Committee, which spent more money in North Carolina than in any other state during the 2008 election season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prospect_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-prospect-20">[20]</a></sup> Dole undertook an eight-day "ElizaBus" tour of the state in the days leading up to election day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-N.26O_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-N.26O-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Doleattackad.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="357" data-file-width="474" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/80/Doleattackad.JPG/200px-Doleattackad.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/80/Doleattackad.JPG/300px-Doleattackad.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/80/Doleattackad.JPG/400px-Doleattackad.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Doleattackad.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Dole's television "Godless" ad</div>

<p>In late October, Dole released a controversial television ad attacking Hagan for reportedly taking donations from individuals involved in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Godless_Americans_PAC" title="Godless Americans PAC">Godless Americans PAC</a>, a group which advocates for the rights of people who do not believe in God. The ad also included a female voice saying, "There is no God." Hagan's campaign said the ad sought to put inflammatory words in their candidate's mouth. Hagan, who is a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterian_Church" title="Presbyterian Church">Presbyterian Church</a> and a former <a href="/wiki/Sunday_school" title="Sunday school">Sunday school</a> teacher,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-23">[23]</a></sup> condemned the ad as "fabricated and pathetic," and, according to Hagan's campaign website, a cease-and-desist letter was "hand-delivered to Dole's Raleigh office and to her home at the Watergate in Washington, DC."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Hagan also filed a lawsuit in <a href="/wiki/Wake_County,_North_Carolina" title="Wake County, North Carolina">Wake County</a> Superior Court accusing Dole of <a href="/wiki/Defamation" title="Defamation">defamation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libel" title="Libel">libel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_2_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN_2-26">[26]</a></sup> The ad met significant criticism from some members of the public as well national media outlets. After the first ad Hagan received over 3,600 contributions, including major donors as well as individual support from a range of persons who believed in the right to participate in civil government free of religious orthodoxy requirements. Following the second ad Hagan's lead doubled according to some polls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miami_Herald_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miami_Herald-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the 2008 election, Dole lost by a wider-than-expected margin, taking 44 percent of the vote to Hagan's 53 percent – the largest margin of defeat for an incumbent Senator in the 2008 cycle. It has been speculated that the outcry over the "Godless" ad contributed to Dole's loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Hagan trounced Dole in the state's five largest counties – <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg_County,_North_Carolina" title="Mecklenburg County, North Carolina">Mecklenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wake_County,_North_Carolina" title="Wake County, North Carolina">Wake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guilford_County,_North_Carolina" title="Guilford County, North Carolina">Guilford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forsyth_County,_North_Carolina" title="Forsyth County, North Carolina">Forsyth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Durham_County,_North_Carolina" title="Durham County, North Carolina">Durham</a>. Hagan also dominated most of the eastern portion of the state, which had been the backbone of Helms' past Senate victories. While Dole dominated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charlotte" title="Charlotte">Charlotte</a> suburbs and most of the heavily Republican Foothills region, it was not enough to save her seat.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_positions">Political positions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Political positions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dole's voting record was somewhat more conservative than that of her husband, though slightly less conservative than that of Helms. She has a lifetime rating of 92 from the <a href="/wiki/American_Conservative_Union" title="American Conservative Union">American Conservative Union</a>.</p>
<p>Dole worked with other senators such as <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Hagel" title="Chuck Hagel">Chuck Hagel</a> to draft and attempt to pass legislation reforming housing financing regulation; the bill did not go up for a vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-davidson_dispatch1_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-davidson_dispatch1-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In September 2008, Dole joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gang_of_20" title="Gang of 20">Gang of 20</a>, a bipartisan group working towards comprehensive energy reform. The group is pushing for a bill that would encourage state-by-state decisions on <a href="/wiki/Offshore_drilling" title="Offshore drilling">offshore drilling</a> and authorize billions of dollars for conservation and alternative energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Senate_Committee_on_Armed_Services" title="U.S. Senate Committee on Armed Services">U.S. Senate Committee on Armed Services</a>, Dole is credited with helping to prevent any closures of North Carolina military bases despite threats from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indy_weekly_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-indy_weekly-31">[31]</a></sup> In 2007, she sponsored legislation which would have granted federal recognition of a North Carolina Native American tribe, the <a href="/wiki/Lumbee" title="Lumbee">Lumbee</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Robeson_County,_North_Carolina" title="Robeson County, North Carolina">Robeson County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lumbee_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-lumbee-32">[32]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_politics:_Elizabeth_Dole_Foundation">After politics: Elizabeth Dole Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: After politics: Elizabeth Dole Foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2012, Dole established the Elizabeth Dole Foundation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> dedicated to helping caregivers of "wounded warriors".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>
<p>Dole commissioned the RAND Corporation to develop the first nationwide comprehensive, evidence-based report on the needs of military and veteran caregivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The two-year study includes an environmental scan of available services, a gap analysis, and recommendations for meeting the enormous challenges of America's hidden heroes – the young spouses, mothers, fathers and other loved ones caring for those who cared for us. The study was generously supported by the Wounded Warrior Project, the Lilly Endowment, and the Cannon Foundation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Foundation selects military and veteran caregivers from each state to serve a two-year Fellowship with the Foundation. The Dole Fellows represent a vast array of military caregivers: spouses, parents, siblings and friends, and use their voice to help bring awareness on a national scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The Foundation also has a National Coalition Program to bring together private and public entities to create substantial change .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Actor <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hanks" title="Tom Hanks">Tom Hanks</a> joined the Foundation's Hidden Heroes Campaign to bring awareness to the over 5.5 million military caregivers across America who are facing enormous challenges every day caring for members of the military and gravely injured veterans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Charity_work">Charity work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Dole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Charity work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
