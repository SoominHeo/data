<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Elite</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Elite</b> (from late 18th century French <b>élite</b>), is a term that originates from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> eligere (“to choose, elect”). In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_theory" title="Political theory">political</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociological</a> theory for a small group of powerful people that controls a <a href="/wiki/Economic_inequality" title="Economic inequality">disproportionate amount of wealth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Privilege_(social_inequality)" title="Privilege (social inequality)">privilege</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_power" title="Political power">political power</a> in a society.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_and_social_structure">Identity and social structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Identity and social structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>American sociologist <a href="/wiki/C._Wright_Mills" title="C. Wright Mills">C. Wright Mills</a> wrote in his 1957 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Power_Elite" title="The Power Elite">The Power Elite</a></i> of the "elite" as: "those political, economic, and military circles, which as an intricate set of overlapping small but dominant groups share decisions having at least national consequences. Insofar as national events are decided, the power elite are those who decide them".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Mills states that the power elite members recognize other members' mutual exalted position in society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> "As a rule, '[t]hey accept one another, understand one another, marry one another, tend to work and to think, if not together at least alike'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> "It is a well-regulated existence where education plays a critical role. Youthful upper-class members attend prominent preparatory schools, which not only open doors to such elite universities as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard" title="Harvard">Harvard</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yale" title="Yale">Yale</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a> but also to the universities' highly exclusive clubs. These memberships in turn pave the way to the prominent social clubs located in all major cities and serving as sites for important business contacts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doob_2013_38_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doob_2013_38-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elitist_privilege">Elitist privilege</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Elitist privilege">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The men who receive the education necessary for <a href="/wiki/Elitism" title="Elitism">elitist</a> privilege obtain the background and contacts that allow them to enter three branches of the power elite, which are;</p>
<ul>
<li>The Political Leadership: Mills contended that since the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> corporate leaders had become more prominent in the political process, with a decline in central decision-making for professional politicians.</li>
<li>The Military Circle: In Mills' time a heightened concern about warfare existed, making top military leaders and such issues as defense funding and personnel recruitment very important. Most prominent corporate leaders and politicians were strong proponents of military spending.</li>
<li>The Corporate Elite: According to Mills, in the 1950s when the military emphasis was pronounced, it was corporate leaders working with prominent military officers who dominated the development of policies. These two groups tended to be mutually supportive".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>According to Mills, the governing elite in the United States primarily draws its members from political leaders, including the president, and a handful of key <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_United_States" title="Cabinet of the United States">cabinet</a> members and close advisers, major corporate owners and directors, and high-ranking military officers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Powell_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Powell-9">[9]</a></sup> These groups overlap, and elites tend to circulate from one sector to another, consolidating power in the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Unlike the <a href="/wiki/Ruling_class" title="Ruling class">ruling class</a>, a social formation based on heritage and social ties, the power elite is characterized by the organizational structures through which its wealth is acquired. According to Mills, the power elite rose from "the managerial reorganization of the propertied classes into the more or less unified stratum of the corporate rich".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Domhoff further clarified the differences in the two terms: "The upper class as a whole does not do the ruling. Instead, class rule is manifested through the activities of a wide variety of organizations and institutions...Leaders within the upper class join with high-level employees in the organizations they control to make up what will be called the power elite".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marxist" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> theoretician <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Bukharin" title="Nikolai Bukharin">Nikolai Bukharin</a> anticipated the power-elite theory in his 1929 work, <i>Imperialism and World Economy:</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> "present-day state power is nothing but an entrepreneurs' company of tremendous power, headed even by the same persons that occupy the leading positions in the banking and syndicate offices".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_.C3.A9lites">Power élites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Power élites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Power elite</i> is a term used by American sociologist <a href="/wiki/C._Wright_Mills" title="C. Wright Mills">C. Wright Mills</a> to describe a relatively small, loosely connected group of individuals who dominate American policymaking. This group includes bureaucratic, corporate, intellectual, military, media, and government elites who control the principal institutions in the United States and whose opinions and actions influence the decisions of the policymakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>The basis for membership of a power élite is institutional power, namely an influential position within a prominent private or public organization.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> One study (published in 2002) of power élites in the United States under President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> (in office 2001-2009) identified 7,314 institutional positions of power encompassing 5,778 individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> A later study of U.S. society noted demographic characteristics of this élite group as follows:</p>
<dl>
<dt><b>Age</b> </dt>
<dd>Corporate leaders aged about 60; heads of <a href="/wiki/Foundation_(nonprofit)" title="Foundation (nonprofit)">foundations</a>, law, education, and civic organizations aged around 62; government employees aged about 56</dd>
<dt><b>Gender</b> </dt>
<dd><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Men" title="Men">Men</a> contribute roughly 80% in the political realm whereas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Women" title="Women">women</a> contribute roughly only 20% in the political realm.</dd>
<dt><b>Ethnicity</b> </dt>
<dd>White <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a> dominate in the power élite, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestants" title="Protestants">Protestants</a> representing about 80 percent of the top business leaders and about 73% of members of Congress.</dd>
<dt><b>Education</b> </dt>
<dd>Nearly all the leaders have a college education, with almost half graduating with advanced degrees. About 54 percent of the big-business leaders and 42% of the government élite graduated from just 12 prestigious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universities" title="Universities">universities</a> with large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endowment_fund" title="Endowment fund">endowments</a>.</dd>
<dt><b>Social clubs</b> </dt>
<dd>Most holders of top positions in the power élite possess exclusive membership in one or more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_clubs" title="Social clubs">social clubs</a>. About a third belong to a small number of especially prestigious clubs in major cities like <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts_on_economy">Impacts on economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Impacts on economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1970s an organized set of policies promoted reduced <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxes</a>, especially for the wealthy, and a steady erosion of the welfare safety net.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Starting with legislation in the 1980s, the wealthy banking community successfully lobbied for reduced regulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The wide range of financial and social capital accessible to the power elite gives their members heavy influence in economic and political decision making, allowing them to move toward attaining desired outcomes. Sociologist Christopher Doob gives a hypothetical alternative stating that these elite individuals would consider themselves the overseers of the national economy, appreciating that it is not only a moral but a practical necessity to focus beyond their group interests. Doing so would hopefully alleviate various destructive conditions affecting large numbers of less affluent citizens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doob_2013_38_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Doob_2013_38-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_politics_and_hegemony">Global politics and hegemony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Global politics and hegemony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mills determined that there is an "inner core" of the power elite involving individuals that are able to move from one seat of institutional power to another. They therefore have a wide range of knowledge and interests in many influential organizations, and are, as Mills describes, "professional go-betweens of economic, political, and military affairs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Relentless expansion of <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> and the globalizing of economic and military power binds leaders of the power elite into complex relationships with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nation_states" title="Nation states">nation states</a> that generate global-scale class divisions. Sociologist <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Castells" title="Manuel Castells">Manuel Castells</a> writes in <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Rise_of_the_Network_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Rise of the Network Society (page does not exist)">The Rise of the Network Society</a></i> that contemporary globalization does not mean that "everything in the global economy is global".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> So, a global economy becomes characterized by fundamental social inequalities with respect to the "level of integration, competitive potential and share of the benefits from economic growth".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CastellsManuel_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-CastellsManuel-22">[22]</a></sup> Castells cites a kind of "double movement" where on one hand, "valuable segments of territories and people" become "linked in the global networks of value making and wealth appropriation", while, on the other, "everything and everyone" that is not valued by established networks gets "switched off...and ultimately discarded".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CastellsManuel_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-CastellsManuel-22">[22]</a></sup> The wide-ranging effects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_capitalism" title="Global capitalism">global capitalism</a> ultimately affect everyone on the planet as economies around the world come to depend on the functioning of global financial markets, technologies, trade and labor.</p>
