<p><b>Elihu Thomson</b> (March 29, 1853 – March 13, 1937) was an English-born American <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">engineer</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inventor" title="Inventor">inventor</a> who was instrumental in the founding of major <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electrical</a> companies in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.</p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elihu_Thomson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was born in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> (<a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>) on March 29, 1853, but his family moved to <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> in 1858.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrhpinv2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrhpinv2-2">[2]</a></sup> Thomson attended <a href="/wiki/Central_High_School_(Philadelphia)" title="Central High School (Philadelphia)">Central High School</a> in Philadelphia and graduated in 1870. Thomson took a teaching position at Central, and in 1876, at the age of twenty-three, held the Chair of Chemistry. In 1880, he left Central to pursue research in the emerging field of electrical engineering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrical_innovations">Electrical innovations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elihu_Thomson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Electrical innovations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With <a href="/wiki/Edwin_J._Houston" title="Edwin J. Houston">Edwin J. Houston</a>, a former teacher and later colleague of Thomson's at Central High School, Thomson founded the <a href="/wiki/Thomson-Houston_Electric_Company" title="Thomson-Houston Electric Company">Thomson-Houston Electric Company</a>. Notable inventions created by Thomson during this period include an arc-lighting system, an automatically regulated three-coil dynamo, a magnetic lightning arrester, and a local power transformer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ElihuThomson_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ElihuThomson-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1892 the <a href="/wiki/Thomson-Houston_Electric_Company" title="Thomson-Houston Electric Company">Thomson-Houston Electric Company</a> merged with the <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Edison</a> General Electric Company to become the <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric Company</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amphilsoc_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-amphilsoc-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The historian <a href="/wiki/Thomas_P._Hughes" title="Thomas P. Hughes">Thomas P. Hughes</a> writes that Thomson "displayed methodological characteristics in the workshop and the laboratory as [an] inventor and in the business world as [an] entrepreneur. He also chose to solve problems in the rapidly expanding field of electric light and power."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HughesThomas_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-HughesThomas-6">[6]</a></sup> Thomson's name is further commemorated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Thomson-Houston_Company" title="British Thomson-Houston Company">British Thomson-Houston Company</a> (BTH), and the French companies <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomson_SA" title="Thomson SA">Thomson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alstom" title="Alstom">Alstom</a>.</p>
<p>Thomson was notable both for his emphasis on models and for the singular focus with which he pursued his research, with Thomson referring to his workshop as a "model room" rather than a laboratory. Between 1880 and 1885, Thomson averaged twenty-one patent applications annually, doubling that average between 1885 and 1890.</p>
<p>Upon the merger of <a href="/wiki/Thomson-Houston_Electric_Company" title="Thomson-Houston Electric Company">Thomson-Houston Electric Company</a> (his namesake company) to form <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> in 1892, Thomson chose to keep his laboratory at <a href="/wiki/Lynn,_Massachusetts" title="Lynn, Massachusetts">Lynn, Massachusetts</a> near Boston away from GE's New York headquarters to ensure his control over his research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HughesThomas_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-HughesThomas-6">[6]</a></sup> At the Lynn GE plant, he worked with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edwin_Rice" title="Edwin Rice">Edwin Rice</a> (later President of GE in 1913) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanford_Moss" title="Sanford Moss">Sanford Moss</a> (developer of the <a href="/wiki/Turbocharger" title="Turbocharger">turbocharger</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Steinmetz" title="Charles Steinmetz">Charles Steinmetz</a> (who was located at GE headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Schenectady,_New_York" title="Schenectady, New York">Schenectady, New York</a>). After being asked to become a director of GE, Thomson rejected the offer preferring continued research to management.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors">Honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elihu_Thomson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thomson was the first recipient of the <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Electrical_Engineers" title="American Institute of Electrical Engineers">American Institute of Electrical Engineers</a> <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Electrical_Engineers" title="American Institute of Electrical Engineers">AIEE</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Electrical_and_Electronics_Engineers" title="Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers">Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE)</a>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edison_Medal" title="Edison Medal">Edison Medal</a>, bestowed upon him in 1909 "For meritorious achievement in electrical science, engineering and arts as exemplified in his contributions thereto during the past thirty years."; Thomson was also president of the organization from 1889-90.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Near the end of his life, Thomson's second wife Clarissa Hovey Thomson is reported to have said that she had to carry a basket with her to carry all of Thomson's awards and honors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amphilsoc_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-amphilsoc-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1889 he was decorated by the French Government for his electrical inventions, being made <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Honour" title="Legion of Honour">Chevalier et Officier de la Légion d'honneur</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEEEBIO_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-IEEEBIO-1">[1]</a></sup> He received the honorary degree of A.M. from Yale (1890). Tufts College in 1892 gave him the degree of Ph.D., and in 1899 he received a D.Sc. from Harvard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEEEBIO_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-IEEEBIO-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elihu_Thomson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was a founding member, as well as the second president, of the <a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">International Electrotechnical Commission</a>.</p>
<p>He served as acting president of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">MIT</a> from 1920-1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ElihuThomsonPapers_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ElihuThomsonPapers-8">[8]</a></sup> Thomson, overcoming his distaste for management accepted this role during a critical period for the university when it could not otherwise find a president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ElihuThomson_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ElihuThomson-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Thomson died at his estate in <a href="/wiki/Swampscott,_Massachusetts" title="Swampscott, Massachusetts">Swampscott, Massachusetts</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elihu_Thomson_House" title="Elihu Thomson House">Elihu Thomson House</a> in Swampscott was designated a U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a> in 1976 and serves as Swampscott's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Town_hall" title="Town hall">town hall</a>.</p>
