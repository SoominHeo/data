<p><b>Elmer Verner McCollum</b> (March 3, 1879 – November 15, 1967) was an American <a href="/wiki/Biochemist" title="Biochemist">biochemist</a> known for his work on the influence of <a href="/wiki/Diet_(nutrition)" title="Diet (nutrition)">diet</a> on <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kruse.2C_1961_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kruse.2C_1961-3">[3]</a></sup> McCollum is also remembered for starting the first <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_rat" title="Laboratory rat">rat</a> colony in the United States to be used for nutrition research. His reputation has suffered from posthumous <a href="/wiki/Elmer_McCollum#Posthumous_controversy" title="Elmer McCollum">controversy</a>. <i>Time</i> magazine called him Dr.<span class="nowrap"> </span>Vitamin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> His rule was, "Eat what you want after you have eaten what you should."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Living at a time when vitamins were unknown, he asked and tried to answer the questions, "How many dietary essentials are there, and what are they?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kruse.2C_1961_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kruse.2C_1961-3">[3]</a></sup> He and <a href="/wiki/Marguerite_Davis" title="Marguerite Davis">Marguerite Davis</a> discovered the first vitamin, named<span class="nowrap"> </span>A, in 1913. McCollum also helped to discover vitamin<span class="nowrap"> </span>B and vitamin<span class="nowrap"> </span>D and worked out the effect of trace elements in the diet.</p>
<p>As a worker in Wisconsin and later at <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins</a>, McCollum acted partly at the request of the <a href="/wiki/Dairy" title="Dairy">dairy</a> industry. When he said that <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a> was "the greatest of all protective foods", milk consumption in the United States doubled between 1918 and 1928.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> McCollum also promoted <a href="/wiki/Leaf_vegetable" title="Leaf vegetable">leafy greens</a>, which had no industry advocates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prengaman_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prengaman-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCollum wrote in his 1918 textbook that <a href="/wiki/Lacto_vegetarianism" title="Lacto vegetarianism">lacto vegetarianism</a> is, "when the diet is properly planned, the most highly satisfactory plan which can be adopted in the nutrition of man".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_and_education">Family and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McCollum's ancestors immigrated to the United States from <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in 1763.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chick.2C_1969_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chick.2C_1969-1">[1]</a></sup> McCollum was born in 1879 to Cornelius Armstrong McCollum and Martha Catherine Kidwell McCollum on a farm 3 miles (5 km) from <a href="/wiki/Redfield,_Kansas" title="Redfield, Kansas">Redfield, Kansas</a>, usually reported to have been <a href="/wiki/Fort_Scott,_Kansas" title="Fort Scott, Kansas">Fort Scott, Kansas</a>, which was 11 miles (18 km) away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup> His parents had little education but became relatively well-off by local standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chick.2C_1969_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chick.2C_1969-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>He spent his first seventeen years on this farm and attended a one-room school. He had one brother, Burton, and three sisters. At some point he had surgery for a <a href="/wiki/Retinal_detachment" title="Retinal detachment">detached retina</a>, which the doctors were unable to "glue back again".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rider.2C_1970_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rider.2C_1970-12">[12]</a></sup> His father suffered from <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>. His mother, who had only two winters of schooling but was devoted to her children's education, took McCollum and his brother to the 1893 <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World's Columbian Exposition">World's Columbian Exposition</a>. All three of his sisters graduated from <a href="/wiki/Lombard_College" title="Lombard College">Lombard University</a> and married <a href="/wiki/Universalist_Church_of_America" title="Universalist Church of America">Universalist</a> ministers. A lifelong close friend of his brother's, Burton became a geophysicist who helped to pioneer the use of sound waves in oil drilling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1896 his mother moved their family to <a href="/wiki/Lawrence,_Kansas" title="Lawrence, Kansas">Lawrence, Kansas</a>, where they hoped to profit from a 15 acres (6 ha) fruit farm adjoining the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kansas" title="University of Kansas">University of Kansas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup> They planted peach, apple, and plum trees and acres of raspberries and blackberries, all of which took some years to mature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCollum failed the general certification examination, but was allowed to enter high school provisionally based on his habit of extensive reading and his memorization of standard poetry. Though extremely shy around girls, he was elected class president in his junior and senior years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He fell in love with the school's <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a></i>, purchased a set for $25 (about two months of his earnings), and used it for twenty-five years until he bought a new edition. In high school he joined the Unitarian church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup> He worked his way through high school and college, including time spent as a gas <a href="/wiki/Lamplighter" title="Lamplighter">lamplighter</a>.</p>
<p>While a junior in college, McCollum was elected to <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Xi" title="Sigma Xi">Sigma Xi</a>, a non-profit honor society for those interested in science and engineering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gale_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-16">[16]</a></sup> As his mother had hoped, McCollum graduated from the University of Kansas in 1903. While there, he abandoned the dream of becoming a doctor, and during his sophomore year, he consumed <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>, earning a bachelor's degree in three years, followed by his master's in 1904.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rider.2C_1970_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rider.2C_1970-12">[12]</a></sup> He secured a scholarship to <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> in 1904, and wrote his thesis on <a href="/wiki/Pyrimidine" title="Pyrimidine">pyrimidines</a>. McCollum got his Ph.D. from Yale in two years. He remained for another year as a <a href="/wiki/Postdoctoral_researcher" title="Postdoctoral researcher">postdoctoral researcher</a> working with <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Burr_Osborne_(chemist)" title="Thomas Burr Osborne (chemist)">Thomas Osborne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lafayette_Mendel" title="Lafayette Mendel">Lafayette Mendel</a> on plant <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> and diet. Then Mendel found a position for McCollum at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin–Madison">University of Wisconsin–Madison</a>, not in his preferred organic chemistry, but as an instructor in <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_chemistry" title="Agricultural chemistry">agricultural chemistry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1907 he married Constance Carruth, whom he had known in Lawrence. They had five children (Donald, Jean, Margaret, Kathleen, and Elsbeth). The two divorced later in life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup> When he was living in Baltimore, in 1945,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup> he married J.<span class="nowrap"> </span>Ernestine Becker, a dietitian and co-author of one of his books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Graham-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Single-grain_experiment">Single-grain experiment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Single-grain experiment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>In Wisconsin, McCollum was assigned to analyze cow feed and the animal's <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>, blood, feces, and urine for the famous <a href="/wiki/Single-grain_experiment" title="Single-grain experiment">single-grain experiment</a>, directed by department chief <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Moulton_Babcock" title="Stephen Moulton Babcock">Stephen Babcock</a> and his successor <a href="/wiki/Edwin_B._Hart" title="Edwin B. Hart">Edwin B. Hart</a>. The experiment ran for four years until 1911.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIhistory_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-WIhistory-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Babcock had studied for his Ph.D. at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Gottingen" title="University of Gottingen">University of Gottingen</a> in Germany prior to working at the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Agricultural_Experiment_Station" title="New York State Agricultural Experiment Station">New York State Agricultural Experiment Station</a> and then joining the staff in Wisconsin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> During this era, German chemists were trying to create the perfect foods for farm animals. Due to discoveries by <a href="/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig" title="Justus von Liebig">Justus von Liebig</a> and others, they thought that any feed that contains a set amount of protein, carbohydrates, fats, salts, and water was equivalent to any other. Babcock did not believe them, and joked with his German colleagues that cows could survive on coal and ground-up leather according to their calculations. After Hart replaced him as department chief in 1906, Babcock and others created an experiment to test his hypothesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WIhistory_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-WIhistory-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<p>Sixteen calves were purchased on the market. One was part <a href="/wiki/Jersey_cattle" title="Jersey cattle">Jersey</a> and one was part <a href="/wiki/Guernsey_cattle" title="Guernsey cattle">Guernsey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-single_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-single-21">[21]</a></sup> Three out of four groups of calves were given feed prepared from a single grain and raised until they had had two pregnancies. One group received <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, the second received <a href="/wiki/Oat" title="Oat">oats</a>, while the third ate only <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>. A fourth group received a combination of all three grains. After the first year, only the corn group was thriving, and the others and their young were weak or dying. The wheat group was the worst off, and the oats and mixed groups were in between.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-single_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-single-21">[21]</a></sup> Then the weak cows were fed corn. They recovered and bore healthy calves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Madison_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Madison-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>Hart and his team published their findings in 1911 in <i>Physiological Effect on Growth and Reproduction of Rations Balanced from Restricted Sources</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-single_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-single-21">[21]</a></sup> The researchers and Babcock, the leader, could see the results but, they write, "At present we have no solution for the observations made."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-single_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-single-21">[21]</a></sup> McCollum summarized, "something fundamental remained to be discovered."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rat_colony">Rat colony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rat colony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Albino_Rat.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="173" data-file-width="248" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Albino_Rat.jpg/220px-Albino_Rat.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1d/Albino_Rat.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>

An <a href="/wiki/Albinism_in_biology" title="Albinism in biology">albino</a> <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_rat" title="Laboratory rat">laboratory rat</a></div>
</div>

<p>Searching for a breakthrough in human and animal nutrition, McCollum purchased all thirty-seven volumes of Richard Maly's <i>Jahresbericht Über Die Fortschritte Der Tier-Chemie</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> so he could read them at home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup> He read of about 13 experiments done between 1873 and 1906 on small animals, often mice, fed restricted diets. In all cases, the animals failed to thrive and died in a few weeks. McCollum determined that he must find out what was lacking in their purified diets, and that he needed to experiment on animals with a short life span, finally deciding on <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_rat" title="Laboratory rat">rats</a>. Rats are omnivorous, and cheaply fed, matured quickly, and "had little sentimental and no positive economic value". The dean of the college of agriculture would not support experiments with what he feared was vermin, but Babcock, who was retired, understood the rats' importance and told McCollum to proceed. Using skills he developed with his brother when they were children,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> McCollum captured some rats from a horse barn, but they were too vicious and wild. He replaced them with twelve young albino rats with some <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">domestication</a> that he bought for $6 from a Chicago pet store. His rat colony, started in January 1908, was the first in America used for nutrition research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_error">Early error</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early error">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McCollum decided that some diets failed because they lacked "palatability". He thought that if a diet tasted good, and the animals ate more food, then nutrition would be adequate. In 1911 Osborne and Mendel criticized McCollum's hypothesis and his data, when they found that plant protein diets needed to be <a href="/wiki/Dietary_supplement" title="Dietary supplement">supplemented</a> with protein-free milk. Rats would then eat purified diets without flavorings. Further, Mendel and Osborne suggested in their papers that McCollum had been less than careful in his experiments. Embarrassed, McCollum acknowledged his error.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-9"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_vitamin_A">Discovery of vitamin A</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Discovery of vitamin A">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was still responsible for the care of the heifers until 1911, and the care and feeding of his rats fell to a new volunteer, <a href="/wiki/Marguerite_Davis" title="Marguerite Davis">Marguerite Davis</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Home_economics" title="Home economics">home economics</a>-turned-<a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a> student who looked out for them daily, unpaid for five years. She earned $600 for her sixth and final year. Davis helped McCollum develop "the biological method for the analysis of food", and she co-authored a number of papers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-10"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup> Among them was "The Necessity Of Certain Lipins In The Diet During Growth" in 1913. They fed rats pure <a href="/wiki/Casein" title="Casein">casein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">lactose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dextrin" title="Dextrin">dextrine</a>, and/or <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>), with a little <a href="/wiki/Agar" title="Agar">agar-agar</a> and a mixture of six or seven <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a>. They substituted <a href="/wiki/Lard" title="Lard">lard</a> or <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">olive oil</a> for some of the carbohydrates for a group of rats. For 70 to 120 days, their rats grew, and then they stopped growing. They still appeared to be healthy, except the females did not have enough milk to nourish their young. They successfully restored about thirty rats to normality, after these rats had reached the stage of growth suspension, by adding a small amount of extracts of <a href="/wiki/Egg" title="Egg">egg</a> or <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">butter</a>. Their paper included charts for five rats, illustrating their weights over time compared to a normal growth curve, meant to show McCollum and Davis's "almost invariable success in inducing a resumption of growth after complete suspension for a time". One chart showed their results with a fat-free diet. They became convinced that without a substance in the egg or butter extract, rats could not grow, even though they appeared to be healthy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCollum-Davis-1913_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCollum-Davis-1913-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>They concluded that rats stop growing until they are fed certain "ether extracts of egg or of butter", and that "there are certain accessory articles in certain food-stuffs which are essential for normal growth for extended periods".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCollum-Davis-1913_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCollum-Davis-1913-25">[25]</a></sup> They also found this food factor in extracts of <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa leaves</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Offal" title="Offal">organ meats</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This substance that McCollum called "factor A," was later called <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A" title="Vitamin A">vitamin A</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Graham-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCollum and Davis, who ultimately received credit for the discovery, submitted their paper for publication three weeks before Osborne and Mendel. Both papers appeared in the same issue of the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Biological_Chemistry" title="Journal of Biological Chemistry">Journal of Biological Chemistry</a></i> in 1913.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenfeld_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenfeld-27">[27]</a></sup> This turned out to be a very close call given that learning about vitamin<span class="nowrap"> </span>A took scientists about 130 years, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Magendie" title="François Magendie">François Magendie</a> in 1816 through its synthesis in 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Davis published with McCollum from 1909 to 1916, and Nina Simmonds did the same from 1916 to 1929. In the space of six years, McCollum was promoted from instructor to assistant professor, associate professor, and then full professor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-11"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup> He thought that a professor succeeds with his students, "by his ability to make his conversations and lectures more interesting than song, dance, drink and fast driving".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_vitamin_B">Discovery of vitamin B</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Discovery of vitamin B">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Looking at <a href="/wiki/Beriberi" title="Beriberi">beriberi</a> and the problem of polished rice, <a href="/wiki/Christiaan_Eijkman" title="Christiaan Eijkman">Christiaan Eijkman</a> and Gerrit Grijns were trying to find the cause of polyneuritis (influenced by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a>. Eijkman thought <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> caused beriberi<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conlan_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conlan-30">[30]</a></sup>), but McCollum's approach differed in that he was seeking the reason for failure in growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kruse.2C_1961_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kruse.2C_1961-3">[3]</a></sup> McCollum and Davis's experiments with rat diets led to the discovery that Eijkman's and Grijns' anti-neuritic substance was the same as their water-soluble B<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kruse.2C_1961_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kruse.2C_1961-3">[3]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/B_vitamins" title="B vitamins">B vitamins</a>) in 1915.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rider.2C_1970_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rider.2C_1970-12">[12]</a></sup> The finding is number<span class="nowrap"> </span>8 in their list of conclusions, "The water-soluble accessory is not the same one as is furnished by butter fat. 20 percent addition of butter fat does not induce any growth unless the other accessory is supplied."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> He later found that B is actually combined from many different compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prengaman_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Prengaman-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1916 McCollum and Cornelia Kennedy named the factors with alphabetical letters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_to_the_new_word_.22vitamine.22">Opposition to the new word "vitamine"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title='Edit section: Opposition to the new word "vitamine"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McCollum opposed <a href="/wiki/Casimir_Funk" title="Casimir Funk">Casimir Funk</a>'s 1912 name <i>vitamines</i> (from <i>vital amines</i>). They thought the prefix "vita" gave the substance too much importance, and that the ending "<a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a>" means something specific in organic chemistry, but they only had scant evidence of one amino group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy-32">[32]</a></sup> McCollum and Kennedy write in 1916 in the <i>Journal of Biological Chemistry</i> in their paper "The Dietary Factors Operating in the Production of Polyneuritis" (summarizing what proved to be their incomplete understanding of the time):</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"We would, therefore, suggest the desirability of discontinuing the use of the term vitamine, and the substitution of the term fat-soluble A and water-soluble B for the two classes of unknown substances concerned in inducing growth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy-32">[32]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>In 1920 <a href="/wiki/Jack_Drummond" title="Jack Drummond">Jack Drummond</a> noted that the rules of nomenclature include "a neutral substance of undefined composition" ending in "in". Drummond also suggested that the "somewhat cumbrous nomenclature" of fat-soluble A, water-soluble B, etc. (Drummond himself used the expression water-soluble C<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>) stop in favor of vitamins A, B, C, etc., until their nature became known. The word vitamine survived, without its final "e".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenfeld_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenfeld-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Move_to_Johns_Hopkins">Move to Johns Hopkins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Move to Johns Hopkins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1917 the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Foundation" title="Rockefeller Foundation">Rockefeller Foundation</a> established a new <a href="/wiki/JHSPH_Department_of_Biochemistry_and_Molecular_Biology" title="JHSPH Department of Biochemistry and Molecular Biology">Department of Chemical Hygiene</a> at <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins University</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gale_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-16">[16]</a></sup> and the unconventional founder's first two appointments were to non-medical scientists working for <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_experiment_station" title="Agricultural experiment station">agricultural experiment stations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kruse.2C_1961_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kruse.2C_1961-3">[3]</a></sup> McCollum was offered the chairmanship and a professorship,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JHSPH_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-JHSPH-34">[34]</a></sup> although he almost didn't get the job. William H. Howell, assistant director of the new school, said, "We had just one misgiving about appointing you to a professorship, McCollum. You look so frail."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Indeed, McCollum was six feet tall but weighed only 127 pounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Graham-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCollum was elected a member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> in 1920.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> He became emeritus professor in 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gale_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>During his over twenty-five years at Johns Hopkins, McCollum published about 150 papers. His work was on fluorine and the prevention of tooth decay, vitamins D and E, and the effect of a slew of trace minerals in nutrition, including <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCollum worked with <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">Herbert Hoover</a>'s U.S. Food Administration to help those who were starving in Europe following <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. Hoover sent him on a lecture tour of all the major cities in the western U.S., where McCollum explained that the American diet was of poor quality, and that it would be better to eat organ meats (rather than muscle meats), fewer potatoes, and less sugar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_vitamin_D">Discovery of vitamin D</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Discovery of vitamin D">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In conversation with John Howland, a Johns Hopkins pediatrician, and later with Howland's staff doctors Edwards A. Park and Paul G. Shipley, they found that <a href="/wiki/Rickets" title="Rickets">rickets</a> could be induced through diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kruse.2C_1961_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Kruse.2C_1961-3">[3]</a></sup> McCollum's research during the early 1920s found that rats could develop rickets when fed a plain cereal diet. His group tested more than 300 diets on rats, finally finding that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cod-liver_oil" title="Cod-liver oil">cod-liver oil</a> could prevent rickets. Building on the work of <a href="/wiki/Edward_Mellanby" title="Edward Mellanby">Edward Mellanby</a>, who had been inspired by the discovery of vitamin<span class="nowrap"> </span>A by McCollum and Davis, McCollum fed animals with induced rickets cod liver oil that was heated and aerated so its vitamin A was destroyed. The oil could no longer cure night blindness but did cure rickets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conlan_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Conlan-30">[30]</a></sup> After his rats recovered, he named the substance for the next free letter of the alphabet, vitamin D.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Then they became convinced that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunshine" title="Sunshine">sunshine</a> and cod-liver oil both protected against rickets, and tested this by carrying rats outside to the sunshine. Soon after, a generation of children grew up on cod-liver oil, and rickets was virtually wiped out.</p>
<p>Harry Day, a colleague and McCollum biographer, writes that finding vitamin<span class="nowrap"> </span>D was the work of "a host of investigators" but that McCollum and his group did "much of the groundwork".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-12"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup> Kenneth Carpenter, professor emeritus at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, writes in his article "The Nobel Prize and the Discovery of Vitamins" that "strangely" McCollum was not nominated for a Nobel Prize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carpenter_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter-40">[40]</a></sup> According to Carpenter, during this era, nominations were made for Eijkman, <a href="/wiki/Casimir_Funk" title="Casimir Funk">Funk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Goldberger" title="Joseph Goldberger">Goldberger</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gerrit_Grijns" title="Gerrit Grijns">Grijns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Gowland_Hopkins" title="Frederick Gowland Hopkins">Hopkins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Umetaro_Suzuki" title="Umetaro Suzuki">Suzuki</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carpenter_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter-40">[40]</a></sup> Eventually, ten awards were made for various contributions to the discovery of vitamins, including to <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Windaus" title="Adolf Windaus">Adolf Windaus</a> for vitamin<span class="nowrap"> </span>D.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carpenter_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dairy_industry_ties">Dairy industry ties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dairy industry ties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like his predecessors at Wisconsin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Madison_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Madison-22">[22]</a></sup> McCollum was always mindful of the <a href="/wiki/Dairy" title="Dairy">dairy</a> industry. At his suggestion, the <a href="/wiki/Dairy_Management_Inc." title="Dairy Management Inc.">National Dairy Council</a> was formed in 1915.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-13"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup> While at Johns Hopkins, he was the leader of the National Dairy Products Corporation research laboratory and served as a consultant there, for one hour per day and one evening per week.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rider.2C_1970_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rider.2C_1970-12">[12]</a></sup> (National Dairy became <a href="/wiki/Kraft_Foods_Inc" title="Kraft Foods Inc">Kraft Foods Inc</a> and is known today as <a href="/wiki/Mondelez_International" title="Mondelez International">Mondelez International</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kraft_Heinz" title="Kraft Heinz">Kraft Heinz</a>.)</p>
<p>An ad for Formulac <a href="/wiki/Infant_formula" title="Infant formula">infant formula</a> appeared in the <i>Journal of the Indiana State Medical Association</i> in 1947. Developed by McCollum and marketed by Kraft Foods, Inc., the formula of concentrated milk contained all known vitamins necessary for "proper infant nutrition".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCollum wanted milk to be fortified with vitamin D. Today, the National Dairy Council's website welcomes researchers, saying that it was McCollum "who first made the scientific connection between dairy foods and good health".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1942 he wrote an article, "What Is the Right Diet?" for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Magazine" title="The New York Times Magazine">The New York Times Magazine</a></i>. He says there are about forty essential ingredients in the human diet: at least ten of the 22 amino acids, four vitamins (A, D, E, K) that are fat-soluble, nine water-soluble vitamins (C, and various <a href="/wiki/B_vitamins" title="B vitamins">B vitamins</a>), <a href="/wiki/Linoleic_acid" title="Linoleic acid">one fatty acid</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dextrose" title="Dextrose">dextrose</a>, at least thirteen minerals, water, and oxygen. In his article, he pictures five food groups. First among them is dairy, with the label, "Our best all around food...."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two years before he died, he and his wife attended the fiftieth anniversary of the founding of the National Dairy Council. He then traveled to <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_City,_New_Jersey" title="Atlantic City, New Jersey">Atlantic City, New Jersey</a>, where he attended the first awarding of the McCollum Award by what is today the American Society for Nutrition, sponsored by the National Dairy Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-14"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_health_advocacy">Public health advocacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Public health advocacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He gave the <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Society" title="Harvey Society">Harvey Lectures</a> in 1917,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rider.2C_1970_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Rider.2C_1970-12">[12]</a></sup> with the title, "The Supplementary Relations among Our Common Foodstuffs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCollum's textbook <i>The Newer Knowledge of Nutrition</i> (1918) influenced many dietitians and went through multiple editions coauthored by Nina Simmonds and Elsa Orent-Keiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swan_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Swan-46">[46]</a></sup> In this book he introduced his idea of "protective foods".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rider.2C_1970_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Rider.2C_1970-12">[12]</a></sup> He wrote that the American diet was of poor quality because it had too much "white flour or cornmeal, muscle meats, potatoes, and sugar".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> He encouraged a daily quart of milk and lots of green, leafy vegetables. Not surprisingly, McCollum also promoted lacto-vegetarianism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prengaman_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Prengaman-7">[7]</a></sup> The fifth and final edition of his textbook appeared in 1939.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rider.2C_1970_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-Rider.2C_1970-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Under the title "Our Daily Diet", between 1922 and 1946 McCollum wrote about 160 columns for <i><a href="/wiki/McCall%27s" title="McCall's">McCall's</a></i> magazine. He tackled topics like, "Are there such things as nerve foods?" and "Green vegetables are unbottled medicines."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Graham-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCollum was the nutrition editor of the magazines <i>McCall's</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Parents_(magazine)" title="Parents (magazine)">Parents</a></i>. He was on the editorial boards of the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Nutrition" title="Journal of Nutrition">Journal of Nutrition</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Biological_Chemistry" title="Journal of Biological Chemistry">Journal of Biological Chemistry</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Nutrition_Reviews" title="Nutrition Reviews">Nutrition Reviews</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rider.2C_1970_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-Rider.2C_1970-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Long interested in the effect of diet on the teeth, McCollum was awarded medals by the Connecticut and Ohio dental societies and was a fellow of the New York society as well as an honorary member of the American Academy of Dental Medicine. He published works on the subject, including "The Effect Of Additions Of Fluorine to the Diet Of The Rat On the Quality Of the Teeth" (1925).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In 1938 the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Public_Health_Service" title="United States Public Health Service">U.S. Public Health Service</a> reported that adding fluoride to drinking water resulted in fewer dental caries. McCollum was moderator of "The Cause and Prevention of Dental Caries", sponsored by the Good Teeth Council for Children, Inc., that same month. He was a supporter of <a href="/wiki/Water_fluoridation" title="Water fluoridation">water fluoridation</a> from the beginning of recorded evidence for this intervention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-15"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a new member of the Food and Nutrition Board, McCollum was part of the decision in 1941 to <a href="/wiki/Enriched_flour" title="Enriched flour">enrich bread and flour</a> with thiamine, niacin, and iron. McCollum was critical of the decision; while he agreed that white bread has nutritional deficiencies, he felt that this action did not compensate for all of the nutrients that are stripped away by milling. In the ensuing controversy, McCollum's status was changed from "board member" to "panel member" and he was no longer invited to board meetings. For years, he continued to think his own plan of adding "nonfat milk solids, brewer's yeast, and wheat and corn germs" was superior to the plan as enacted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>
<p>As he became a public figure, McCollum often gave lectures,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-16"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup> some of which were mentioned in the press. In 1932 he told the New York Academy of Medicine that, when mother rats were deprived of vitamin<span class="nowrap"> </span>B, their young were about "half as quick in mental alertness" as young rats whose mothers were not deprived of vitamin<span class="nowrap"> </span>B.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1934 he told <i>The New York Times</i>, "We have proved the necessity for [magnesium] in human food, but in very small amounts. Too much results in dopiness. I would say you can't have a sweet disposition without <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, but that does not prove that you will have one when you take plenty."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>According to <i>The New York Times</i>, in 1936 he asked an audience of four hundred doctors at the Kings County Medical Society in Brooklyn to help investigate the "extravagant claims" being made for some medicinal preparations. He thought that doctors could stop <a href="/wiki/False_advertising" title="False advertising">false advertising</a> by immediately establishing the facts of each new discovery. McCollum then gave a long list of dos and don'ts with vitamins<span class="nowrap"> </span>A and<span class="nowrap"> </span>B.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1937 he told an audience at the University of Buffalo that a lack of <a href="/wiki/Maternal_bond" title="Maternal bond">maternal instincts</a> is due to a deficiency in <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>. When the rat was fed a small amount of manganese chloride, the mothering instinct returned immediately. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At seven months of age, McCollum fell ill with what we know today as <a href="/wiki/Scurvy" title="Scurvy">scurvy</a> when his mother became pregnant. She weaned him on mashed potatoes and boiled milk. The boy was slowly dying, and had painful sores, bleeding gums, and swollen joints. One day while his mother was peeling apples, McCollum started to suck on the peels. When on the following day he felt a little better, she fed him more apple skins. When spring came, she gave him other fruits and vegetables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prengaman_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Prengaman-7">[7]</a></sup> McCollum completely recovered from scurvy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prengaman_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Prengaman-7">[7]</a></sup> but his teeth caused him trouble for the rest of his life. Finally, in 1926 he had all his teeth removed and got <a href="/wiki/Dentures" title="Dentures">dentures</a>. His health improved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCollum.2C_1964._pp._211.E2.80.93214_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCollum.2C_1964._pp._211.E2.80.93214-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCollum suffered from <a href="/wiki/Diverticulitis" title="Diverticulitis">diverticulitis</a>, and was often in pain. He had a number of operations, and finally his descending <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">colon</a> was removed. Again his health improved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCollum.2C_1964._pp._211.E2.80.93214_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCollum.2C_1964._pp._211.E2.80.93214-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCollum was blind in his left eye because of a <a href="/wiki/Retinal_detachment" title="Retinal detachment">detached retina</a> that doctors were unable to repair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCollum.2C_1964._pp._211.E2.80.93214_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-McCollum.2C_1964._pp._211.E2.80.93214-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCollum expressed the wish "that in my old age I want to keep my mind in a state of continual adventure". He got his wish by living twenty-three years in retirement, twenty-two of them in good health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-17"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement_and_death">Retirement and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Retirement and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gilman_Hall.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="600" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Gilman_Hall.jpg/140px-Gilman_Hall.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Gilman_Hall.jpg/210px-Gilman_Hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Gilman_Hall.jpg/280px-Gilman_Hall.jpg 2x" width="140"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gilman_Hall.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Homewood_Campus_of_Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Homewood Campus of Johns Hopkins University">Gilman Hall</a>, site of McCollum's office in retirement</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After his 1944 retirement as professor emeritus, he spent ten years writing <i>The History of Nutrition</i>; he also wrote his autobiography, <i>From Kansas Farm Boy to Scientist</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rider.2C_1970_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-Rider.2C_1970-12">[12]</a></sup> McCollum donated his honoraria from prizes and from lecturing to a student loan fund at the University of Kansas that was eventually worth $40,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rider.2C_1970_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-Rider.2C_1970-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1943 McCollum gave the American Dietetic Association, known today as the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_Nutrition_and_Dietetics" title="Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics">Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics</a>, a donation that became the foundation's scholarship fund. Now the largest provider of dietetic scholarships, the foundation awards $1,500,000 over three years to about one thousand students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCollum criticized drugstore vitamin supplements as being no better than old-time <a href="/wiki/Patent_medicine" title="Patent medicine">patent medicines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-Graham-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1947 <a href="/wiki/John_Lee_Pratt" title="John Lee Pratt">John Lee Pratt</a> gave $500,000 to Johns Hopkins for the study of trace inorganic elements in life. The McCollum-Pratt Institute formed, and Pratt donated another $1<span class="nowrap"> </span>million. McCollum did not participate beyond giving lectures, but he selected the first director, William McElroy. In 1951 Johns Hopkins held a two-day symposium called "The Physiological Role of Certain Vitamins and Trace Elements"; fifteen scientists delivered papers and many of his students and former colleagues returned to see him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-18"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>About two hundred people contributed several thousand dollars for the painting of an oil portrait of McCollum by Paul Trebilcock in 1954. The work hangs in the lobby of the Alan Mason Chesney Medical Archives at Johns Hopkins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-19"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rider.2C_1970_12-10"><a href="#cite_note-Rider.2C_1970-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<p>While working with Olaf S. Rask ten years before he retired, McCollum began to search for ways to separate specific <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> from protein <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysate" title="Hydrolysate">hydrolysates</a>. When he retired, he worked on this project exclusively. He had a small laboratory at <a href="/wiki/Homewood_Campus_of_Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Homewood Campus of Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins's Homewood Campus</a> and an assistant, Mrs.<span class="nowrap"> </span>Agatha Ann Rider. In retirement, he produced six papers and three patents, the last one issued to him and Rider on March<span class="nowrap"> </span>15, 1960, for the purification of <a href="/wiki/Glutamine" title="Glutamine">glutamine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-20"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1961 he was elected a foreign member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chick.2C_1969_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Chick.2C_1969-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1965 the University of Kansas named a ten-story dormitory, McCollum Hall, after McCollum and his brother Burton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-21"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup> The building housed about nine hundred students for fifty years until it was demolished in 2015. Each of them sat for a portrait painted by Kansas artist Daniel MacMorris. The portraits hung side by side in the foyer. After the demolition, McCollum's portrait was moved to the School of Pharmacy, and Burton's went to the Kansas Geological Survey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
<p>One issue became especially important to him, and he wrote dozens of his friends asking what could be done about it. He thought the country was losing its <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, and he wanted to find a way to recycle them instead of throwing them in our sewage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-22"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1965 he and his wife traveled so he could speak at the fiftieth anniversary of the appointment of <a href="/wiki/Agnes_Fay_Morgan" title="Agnes Fay Morgan">Agnes Fay Morgan</a> to the faculty of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-23"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup> The following year, he was unable to attend the 100th anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kansas" title="University of Kansas">University of Kansas</a> in 1966 because of physical disability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-24"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>His <a href="/wiki/Elmer_V._McCollum_House" title="Elmer V. McCollum House">house in Baltimore</a> was broken up into apartments but nevertheless became a National Historic Landmark. The <a href="/wiki/American_Society_for_Nutrition" title="American Society for Nutrition">American Society for Nutrition</a> offers a McCollum lectureship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>McCollum died on November 15, 1967, at the age of 88. Shortly before his death, he remarked: "I have had an exceptionally pleasant life and am thankful."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Day.2C_1974_11-25"><a href="#cite_note-Day.2C_1974-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Posthumous_controversy">Posthumous controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elmer_McCollum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Posthumous controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In her 1994 biography of Cornelia Kennedy for the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Nutrition" title="Journal of Nutrition">Journal of Nutrition</a></i>, former <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a> graduate dean Patricia B. Swan writes "forgotten...are the women who worked with Elmer V. McCollum throughout his outstanding research career." In his editor's note, Thomas Jukes adds, "I would hope this is not the case."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swan_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Swan-46">[46]</a></sup></p>
<p>Johns Hopkins professor <a href="/wiki/Richard_David_Semba" title="Richard David Semba">Richard David Semba</a> has written extensively on vitamin<span class="nowrap"> </span>A.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> His 2012 paper <i>The Discovery of the Vitamins</i> explicitly accuses McCollum of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_misconduct" title="Scientific misconduct">scientific misconduct</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Semba_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Semba-61">[61]</a></sup> a theme Semba explores again in his 2012 book <i>The Vitamin A Story</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> He believes that McCollum transferred from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin" title="University of Wisconsin">University of Wisconsin</a> to Johns Hopkins University under suspicion of possible <a href="/wiki/Academic_dishonesty" title="Academic dishonesty">academic dishonesty</a>. Semba cites an extraordinary 1918 letter written by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_B._Hart" title="Edwin B. Hart">Edwin B. Hart</a>, chair of the University of Wisconsin department of agricultural chemistry, to the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i>.</p>
<p>Hart states in this letter, which was published under the title "Professional Courtesy". that a paper co-authored by McCollum and Nina Simmonds, "A Study of the Dietary Essential, Water-Soluble<span class="nowrap"> </span>B, in Relation to its Solubility and Stability Towards Reagents",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> is actually the work of <a href="/wiki/Harry_Steenbock" title="Harry Steenbock">Harry Steenbock</a>, who is mentioned only in a footnote and is thus the victim of a "gross injustice". Further, Hart says that all the records of rat feeding at Wisconsin disappeared during the change of staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Semba accuses McCollum not only of <a href="/wiki/Theft" title="Theft">stealing</a> the Wisconsin research notebooks, but also of <a href="/wiki/Sabotage" title="Sabotage">sabotage</a>, by releasing all the rats from their cages in the rat colony when he left.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Semba_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-Semba-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<p>Semba disputes McCollum and Davis's claim to the discovery of vitamin<span class="nowrap"> </span>A, which was based on their observation that the unidentified substance was "fat soluble", for three reasons:</p>
<ul>
<li>Carl Socin had suggested that this unknown substance was fat-soluble in 1891.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></li>
<li>Wilhelm Stepp had shown that there was a growth factor and that it was fat-soluble in 1911.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></li>
<li>Fat-soluble extracts of butter and egg yolk contain three vitamins: vitamins A, D, and E (all of which were so far unknown).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Semba_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-Semba-61">[61]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
