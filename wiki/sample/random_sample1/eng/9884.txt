<p><b>Declan Patrick MacManus</b> (born 25 August 1954),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> better known by his stage name <b>Elvis Costello</b>, is an English musician, singer-songwriter, and record producer. He began his career as part of London's <a href="/wiki/Pub_rock_(United_Kingdom)" title="Pub rock (United Kingdom)">pub rock</a> scene in the early 1970s and later became associated with the first wave of the British <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> movement that emerged in the mid-to-late 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> His critically acclaimed debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/My_Aim_Is_True" title="My Aim Is True">My Aim Is True</a></i>, was released in 1977. Shortly after recording it, he formed <a href="/wiki/The_Attractions" title="The Attractions">the Attractions</a> as his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backing_band" title="Backing band">backing band</a>. His second album, <i><a href="/wiki/This_Year%27s_Model" title="This Year's Model">This Year's Model</a></i>, was released in 1978, and was ranked number 11 by <i>Rolling Stone</i> on its list of the best albums from 1967–1987. His third album, <i><a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_(album)" title="Armed Forces (album)">Armed Forces</a></i>, was released in 1979, and features his most successful single "<a href="/wiki/Oliver%27s_Army" title="Oliver's Army">Oliver's Army</a>". His first three albums all appeared on <i>Rolling Stone's</i> list of the <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone%27s_500_Greatest_Albums_of_All_Time" title="Rolling Stone's 500 Greatest Albums of All Time">500 Greatest Albums of All Time</a>.</p>
<p>Costello and the Attractions toured and recorded together for the better part of a decade, though differences between them caused a split by 1986. Much of Costello's work since has been as a solo artist, though reunions with members of the Attractions have been credited to the group over the years. Steeped in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wordplay" title="Wordplay">wordplay</a>, the vocabulary of Costello's lyrics is broad. His music has drawn on many diverse genres; one critic described him as a "pop encyclopaedia", able to "reinvent the past in his own image".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>He has won multiple awards in his career, including a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a>, and has twice been nominated for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brit_Award" title="Brit Award">Brit Award</a> for Best British Male Singer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2003, Costello and the Attractions were inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2004, <i>Rolling Stone</i> ranked Costello number 80 on their list of the 100 Greatest Artists of All Time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Costello has co-written several original songs for motion pictures, including "God Give Me Strength" from <i><a href="/wiki/Grace_of_My_Heart" title="Grace of My Heart">Grace of My Heart</a></i> (1996, with <a href="/wiki/Burt_Bacharach" title="Burt Bacharach">Burt Bacharach</a>) and "<a href="/wiki/The_Scarlet_Tide" title="The Scarlet Tide">The Scarlet Tide</a>" from <i><a href="/wiki/Cold_Mountain_(film)" title="Cold Mountain (film)">Cold Mountain</a></i> (2003, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-Bone_Burnett" title="T-Bone Burnett">T-Bone Burnett</a>). For the latter, Costello was nominated (along with Burnett) for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Song" title="Academy Award for Best Original Song">Academy Award for Best Original Song</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Song_Written_for_Visual_Media" title="Grammy Award for Best Song Written for Visual Media">Grammy Award for Best Song Written for Visual Media</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Costello was born in <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s_Hospital,_London" title="St Mary's Hospital, London">St Mary's Hospital, London</a>, the son of Lilian Alda (née Ablett, b. 1927, Liverpool)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ross_MacManus" title="Ross MacManus">Ross MacManus</a> (born in Birkenhead, 1927–2011), a musician and bandleader.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> He is of Irish descent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Costello lived in <a href="/wiki/Twickenham" title="Twickenham">Twickenham</a>, attending Archbishop Myers R.C. School, which is now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Marks_R.C_School" title="St. Marks R.C School">St Mark's Catholic Secondary School</a>, in neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Hounslow" title="Hounslow">Hounslow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> With a musically inclined father (who was a jazz trumpeter and sang with the <a href="/wiki/Joe_Loss" title="Joe Loss">Joe Loss Orchestra</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYer2010_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYer2010-18">[18]</a></sup> Costello's first broadcast recording was with his father in a television commercial for <a href="/wiki/R._White%27s_Lemonade" title="R. White's Lemonade">R. White's Lemonade</a> ("I'm a Secret Lemonade Drinker").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> His father wrote and sang the song; Costello provided backing vocals. The advertisement won a silver award at the 1974 International Advertising Festival.</p>
<p>Costello moved with his <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>-born mother to <a href="/wiki/Birkenhead" title="Birkenhead">Birkenhead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a>, in 1971. There, he formed his first band, a <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk</a> duo called Rusty, with Allan Mayes. After completing secondary school at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Francis_Xavier%27s_College_(Liverpool)" title="St. Francis Xavier's College (Liverpool)">St. Francis Xavier's College</a> he moved back to London where he next formed a band called Flip City,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> which had a style in the pub rock vein. They were active from 1974 through to early 1976. Around this time, Costello adopted the stage name <b>D.P. Costello</b>. His father had performed under the name Day Costello, and Elvis has said in interviews that he took this name as a tribute to his father.</p>
<p>Costello worked at a number of office jobs to support himself, most famously at <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Arden,_Inc." title="Elizabeth Arden, Inc.">Elizabeth Arden</a> – immortalised in the lyrics of "I'm Not Angry" as the "vanity factory" – where he worked as a data entry clerk. He worked for a short period as a computer operator at the <a href="/wiki/Midland_Bank" title="Midland Bank">Midland Bank</a> computer centre in <a href="/wiki/Bootle" title="Bootle">Bootle</a>. He continued to write songs and began looking for a solo recording contract. He was signed to independent label <a href="/wiki/Stiff_Records" title="Stiff Records">Stiff Records</a> on the basis of a demo tape. His manager at Stiff, <a href="/wiki/Jake_Riviera" title="Jake Riviera">Jake Riviera</a>, suggested a name change, combining <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>'s first name and Costello, his father's stage name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Costello's first single for Stiff was "<a href="/wiki/Less_Than_Zero_(song)" title="Less Than Zero (song)">Less Than Zero</a>", released on 25 March 1977. Four months later, his début album, <i><a href="/wiki/My_Aim_Is_True" title="My Aim Is True">My Aim Is True</a></i> (1977), was released to moderate commercial success (No. 14 in the UK and, later, Top 40 in the US) with Costello appearing on the cover in what became his trademark oversize glasses, bearing some resemblance to <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Holly" title="Buddy Holly">Buddy Holly</a>. Costello failed to chart with his early singles, which included the anti-fascist "Less Than Zero" and the ballad "Alison". Stiff's records were initially distributed only in the UK, which meant that Costello's first album and singles were available in the US as imports only. In an attempt to change this, Costello was arrested for <a href="/wiki/Street_performance" title="Street performance">busking</a> outside a London convention of <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">CBS Records</a> executives, protesting that no US record company had yet seen fit to release his records in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Costello signed to <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>, CBS in the U.S., a few months later.</p>
<p>The backing for Costello's debut album was provided by American West Coast band <a href="/wiki/Clover_(band)" title="Clover (band)">Clover</a>, a country outfit living in England whose members would later go on to join <a href="/wiki/Huey_Lewis_and_the_News" title="Huey Lewis and the News">Huey Lewis and the News</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Doobie_Brothers" title="The Doobie Brothers">the Doobie Brothers</a>. Costello released his first major hit single, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watching_The_Detectives_(song)" title="Watching The Detectives (song)">Watching the Detectives</a>", which was recorded with <a href="/wiki/Steve_Nieve" title="Steve Nieve">Steve Nieve</a> and the pair of Steve Goulding (drums) and Andrew Bodnar (bass), both members of <a href="/wiki/Graham_Parker" title="Graham Parker">Graham Parker</a>'s backing band <a href="/wiki/The_Rumour" title="The Rumour">the Rumour</a>. Added to the U.S. version of <i>My Aim Is True</i>, the song contained scathing verses about the vicarious enjoyment of TV violence over a reggae beat. Later in 1977, Costello formed his own permanent backing band, <a href="/wiki/The_Attractions" title="The Attractions">the Attractions</a>, consisting of Steve Nieve (piano), <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Thomas" title="Bruce Thomas">Bruce Thomas</a> (bass guitar), and <a href="/wiki/Pete_Thomas_(drummer)" title="Pete Thomas (drummer)">Pete Thomas</a> (drums; unrelated to Bruce Thomas).</p>

<p>On 17 December 1977, Costello and the Attractions, as a replacement act for the <a href="/wiki/Sex_Pistols" title="Sex Pistols">Sex Pistols</a>, were scheduled to play "Less Than Zero" on <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i>; however, in imitation of a rebellious act by Jimi Hendrix on a BBC show, Costello stopped the song mid-intro, yelling "Stop! Stop!" to his band, and played "<a href="/wiki/Radio_Radio" title="Radio Radio">Radio Radio</a>" instead – a song that criticizes the commercialization of the airwaves, which <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lorne_Michaels" title="Lorne Michaels">Lorne Michaels</a> had forbidden them to play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SNL_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-SNL-23">[23]</a></sup> Costello was subsequently banned from the show (the ban was lifted in 1989) and he received considerable attention as an angry young man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> His insistence on performing "Radio Radio" on <i>SNL</i> proved a boon to his debut album, and its popularity exploded in the U.S. after the performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SNL_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-SNL-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following a tour with other Stiff artists – captured on the <i><a href="/wiki/Live_Stiffs_Live" title="Live Stiffs Live">Live Stiffs Live</a></i> album, which includes Costello's version of the <a href="/wiki/Burt_Bacharach" title="Burt Bacharach">Burt Bacharach</a>/<a href="/wiki/Hal_David" title="Hal David">Hal David</a> standard "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Just_Don%27t_Know_What_to_Do_With_Myself" title="I Just Don't Know What to Do With Myself">I Just Don't Know What to Do With Myself</a>" – the band recorded <i><a href="/wiki/This_Year%27s_Model" title="This Year's Model">This Year's Model</a></i> (1978). Some of the more popular tracks include the British hit "<a href="/wiki/(I_Don%27t_Want_to_Go_to)_Chelsea" title="(I Don't Want to Go to) Chelsea">(I Don't Want to Go to) Chelsea</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Pump_It_Up_(Elvis_Costello_song)" title="Pump It Up (Elvis Costello song)">Pump It Up</a>." His U.S. record company saw Costello as such a priority that his last name replaced the word Columbia on the label of the disc's original pressing. The Attractions' first tour of Australia in December 1978 was notable for a controversial performance at Sydney's <a href="/wiki/Regent_Theatre_(Sydney)" title="Regent Theatre (Sydney)">Regent Theatre</a> when, angered by the group's failure to perform an encore after their brief 35-minute set, audience members destroyed some of the seating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> By the end of the 70s Costello was firmly established as both performer and songwriter, with Linda Ronstadt and Dave Edmunds having success with his compositions.</p>
<p>A tour of the U.S. and Canada also saw the release of the much-<a href="/wiki/Bootleg_recording" title="Bootleg recording">bootlegged</a> Canadian promo-only <i><a href="/wiki/Live_at_the_El_Mocambo_(Elvis_Costello_album)" title="Live at the El Mocambo (Elvis Costello album)">Live at the El Mocambo</a></i>, recorded at a Toronto rock club, which finally saw an official release as part of the <i><a href="/wiki/2%C2%BD_Years" title="2½ Years">2½ Years</a></i> box set in 1993.</p>
<p>In 1979, he released his third LP <i><a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_(album)" title="Armed Forces (album)">Armed Forces</a></i> (originally to have been titled <i>Emotional Fascism</i>, a phrase that appeared on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LP_album" title="LP album">LP</a>'s inner sleeve). American editions included a 45rpm EP recorded live at the Hollywood High School Gymnasium in Hollywood in 1978. Both the album and the single "<a href="/wiki/Oliver%27s_Army" title="Oliver's Army">Oliver's Army</a>" went to No. 2 in the UK, and the opening track "<a href="/wiki/Accidents_Will_Happen" title="Accidents Will Happen">Accidents Will Happen</a>" gained wide television exposure thanks to its innovative animated music video, directed by <a href="/wiki/Annabel_Jankel" title="Annabel Jankel">Annabel Jankel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Morton" title="Rocky Morton">Rocky Morton</a>. Costello also found time in 1979 to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specials_(album)" title="Specials (album)">the debut album</a> for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2_Tone" title="2 Tone">2 Tone</a> ska revival band <a href="/wiki/The_Specials" title="The Specials">the Specials</a> and worked as a backing vocalist on 'This Is Your Life', an album by New Wave band Twist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Costello's standing in the U.S. was bruised for a time when in March 1979, during a drunken argument with <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Stills" title="Stephen Stills">Stephen Stills</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bonnie_Bramlett" title="Bonnie Bramlett">Bonnie Bramlett</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Ohio" title="Columbus, Ohio">Columbus, Ohio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holiday_Inn" title="Holiday Inn">Holiday Inn</a> bar, the singer referred to <a href="/wiki/James_Brown" title="James Brown">James Brown</a> as a "jive-ass <a href="/wiki/Nigger" title="Nigger">nigger</a>", then upped the ante by pronouncing <a href="/wiki/Ray_Charles" title="Ray Charles">Ray Charles</a> a "blind, ignorant nigger".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Costello apologised at a New York City press conference a few days later, claiming that he had been drunk and had been attempting to be obnoxious in order to bring the conversation to a swift conclusion, not anticipating that Bramlett would bring his comments to the press. According to Costello, "it became necessary for me to outrage these people with about the most obnoxious and offensive remarks that I could muster." In his liner notes for the expanded version of <i><a href="/wiki/Get_Happy!!_(Elvis_Costello_album)" title="Get Happy!! (Elvis Costello album)">Get Happy!!</a></i> Costello writes that some time after the incident he had declined an offer to meet Charles out of guilt and embarrassment, though Charles himself had forgiven Costello saying "Drunken talk isn't meant to be printed in the paper." Costello worked extensively in Britain's <a href="/wiki/Rock_Against_Racism" title="Rock Against Racism">Rock Against Racism</a> campaign both before and after the incident. The incident inspired his <i>Get Happy!!</i> song "Riot Act."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In an interview with <a href="/wiki/Questlove" title="Questlove">Questlove</a> (drummer for <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">the Roots</a>, whom Costello collaborated with in 2013), he addressed the controversy, stating: "It’s upsetting because I can’t explain how I even got to think you could be funny about something like that," and further elaborating with, "I’m sorry. You know? It’s about time I said it out loud."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>
<p>Costello is also an avid country music fan and has cited <a href="/wiki/George_Jones" title="George Jones">George Jones</a> as his favourite country singer. In 1977, he appeared on Jones' duet album <i><a href="/wiki/My_Very_Special_Guests" title="My Very Special Guests">My Very Special Guests</a></i>, contributing "Stranger in the House", which they later performed together on an <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> special dedicated to Jones.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The soul-infused <i>Get Happy!!</i> was the first, and—along with <i><a href="/wiki/King_of_America" title="King of America">King of America</a></i>—possibly most successful, of Costello's many experiments with genres beyond those with which he is ordinarily associated. It also marked a distinct change in mood from the angry, frustrated tone of his first three albums to a more upbeat, happy manner. The single, "<a href="/wiki/I_Can%27t_Stand_Up_for_Falling_Down" title="I Can't Stand Up for Falling Down">I Can't Stand Up for Falling Down</a>" was an old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sam_and_Dave" title="Sam and Dave">Sam and Dave</a> song (though Costello increased the <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a> considerably). Lyrically, the songs are full of Costello's signature word play, to the point that he later felt he had become something of a self-parody and toned it down on later releases; he has mockingly described himself in interviews as "rock and roll's <a href="/wiki/Scrabble" title="Scrabble">Scrabble</a> champion." His only 1980 appearance in North America was at the <a href="/wiki/Heatwave_(festival)" title="Heatwave (festival)">Heatwave</a> festival in August near Toronto.</p>
<p>In January 1981, Costello released <i><a href="/wiki/Trust_(Elvis_Costello_album)" title="Trust (Elvis Costello album)">Trust</a></i> amidst growing tensions within the Attractions, particularly between Bruce and Pete Thomas. In the U.S., the single "Watch Your Step" was released and played live on <a href="/wiki/Tom_Snyder" title="Tom Snyder">Tom Snyder</a>'s <i>Tomorrow</i> show, and received airplay on FM rock radio. In the U.K., the single "<a href="/wiki/Clubland_(song)" title="Clubland (song)">Clubland</a>" scraped the lower reaches of the charts; follow-up single "From a Whisper to a Scream" (a duet with <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Tilbrook" title="Glenn Tilbrook">Glenn Tilbrook</a> of <a href="/wiki/Squeeze_(band)" title="Squeeze (band)">Squeeze</a>) became the first Costello single in over four years to completely miss the charts. Costello also co-produced Squeeze's popular 1981 album <i><a href="/wiki/East_Side_Story_(Squeeze_album)" title="East Side Story (Squeeze album)">East Side Story</a></i> (with <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bechirian" title="Roger Bechirian">Roger Bechirian</a>) and also performed backing vocals on the group's hit single "Tempted".</p>
<p>October saw the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Almost_Blue" title="Almost Blue">Almost Blue</a></i>, an album of country music cover songs written by the likes of <a href="/wiki/Hank_Williams" title="Hank Williams">Hank Williams</a> ("Why Don't You Love Me (Like You Used to Do?)"), <a href="/wiki/Merle_Haggard" title="Merle Haggard">Merle Haggard</a> ("Tonight the Bottle Let Me Down") and <a href="/wiki/Gram_Parsons" title="Gram Parsons">Gram Parsons</a> ("How Much I Lied"). The album, which received mixed reviews, was a tribute to the country music that Costello had grown up listening to, especially George Jones. The first pressings of the record in the UK bore a sticker with the message: "WARNING: This album contains country &amp; western music and may cause a radical reaction in narrow minded listeners." <i>Almost Blue</i> did spawn a surprise UK hit single in a version of George Jones' "<a href="/wiki/Good_Year_for_the_Roses" title="Good Year for the Roses">Good Year for the Roses</a>" (written by <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Chesnut" title="Jerry Chesnut">Jerry Chesnut</a>), which reached No. 6.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Imperial_Bedroom" title="Imperial Bedroom">Imperial Bedroom</a></i> (1982) had a much darker sound, due in part to the production of <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Emerick" title="Geoff Emerick">Geoff Emerick</a>, famed for engineering several <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">Beatles</a> records. <i>Imperial Bedroom</i> remains one of his most critically acclaimed records, but again failed to produce any hit singles. Costello has said he disliked the marketing pitch for the album. The album also featured Costello's song "<a href="/wiki/Almost_Blue_(song)" title="Almost Blue (song)">Almost Blue</a>", inspired by the music of jazz singer and trumpeter <a href="/wiki/Chet_Baker" title="Chet Baker">Chet Baker</a>, who would later perform and record a version of the song (<i>Chet Baker in Tokyo</i>).</p>
<p>In 1983, he released <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_the_Clock" title="Punch the Clock">Punch the Clock</a></i>, featuring female backing vocal duo (<a href="/wiki/Afrodiziak" title="Afrodiziak">Afrodiziak</a>) and a four-piece horn section (<a href="/wiki/The_TKO_Horns" title="The TKO Horns">the TKO Horns</a>), alongside the Attractions. <a href="/wiki/Clive_Langer" title="Clive Langer">Clive Langer</a> (who co-produced with Alan Winstanley), provided Costello with a melody which eventually became "<a href="/wiki/Shipbuilding_(song)" title="Shipbuilding (song)">Shipbuilding</a>", which featured a trumpet solo by <a href="/wiki/Chet_Baker" title="Chet Baker">Chet Baker</a>. Prior to the release of Costello's own version, a version of the song was a minor UK hit for former <a href="/wiki/Soft_Machine" title="Soft Machine">Soft Machine</a> founder <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wyatt" title="Robert Wyatt">Robert Wyatt</a>.</p>
<p>Under the pseudonym The Imposter, Costello released "Pills and Soap", an attack on the changes in British society brought on by <a href="/wiki/Thatcherism" title="Thatcherism">Thatcherism</a>, released to coincide with the run-up to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1983" title="United Kingdom general election, 1983">1983 U.K. general election</a>. <i>Punch the Clock</i> also generated an international hit in the single "<a href="/wiki/Everyday_I_Write_the_Book" title="Everyday I Write the Book">Everyday I Write the Book</a>", aided by a music video featuring lookalikes of the <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Prince Charles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diana,_Princess_of_Wales" title="Diana, Princess of Wales">Princess Diana</a> undergoing domestic strife in a suburban home. The song became Costello's first <a href="/wiki/Top_40" title="Top 40">Top 40</a> hit single in the U.S. Also in the same year, Costello provided vocals on a version of the <a href="/wiki/Madness_(band)" title="Madness (band)">Madness</a> song "Tomorrow's Just Another Day" released as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-side" title="B-side">B-side</a> on the single of the same name.</p>
<p>Tensions within the band—notably between Costello and bassist Bruce Thomas—were beginning to tell, and Costello announced his retirement and the break-up of the group shortly before they were to record <i><a href="/wiki/Goodbye_Cruel_World_(Elvis_Costello_album)" title="Goodbye Cruel World (Elvis Costello album)">Goodbye Cruel World</a></i> (1984). Costello would later say of this record that they had "got it as wrong as you can in terms of the execution". The record was poorly received upon its initial release; the liner notes to the 1995 <a href="/wiki/Rykodisc" title="Rykodisc">Rykodisc</a> re-release, penned by Costello, begin with the words "Congratulations! You've just purchased our worst album". Costello's retirement, although short-lived, was accompanied by two compilations, <i>Elvis Costello: The Man</i> in the UK, Europe and Australia, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Best_of_Elvis_Costello_%26_The_Attractions" title="The Best of Elvis Costello &amp; The Attractions">The Best of Elvis Costello &amp; The Attractions</a></i> in the U.S.</p>
<p>In 1985, he appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Live_Aid" title="Live Aid">Live Aid</a> benefit concert in England, singing the Beatles' "<a href="/wiki/All_You_Need_Is_Love" title="All You Need Is Love">All You Need Is Love</a>" as a solo artist. (The event was overrunning and Costello was asked to "ditch the band".) Costello introduced the song as an "old northern English folk song", and the audience was invited to sing the chorus. In the same year Costello teamed up with friend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-Bone_Burnett" title="T-Bone Burnett">T-Bone Burnett</a> for the single "The People's Limousine" under the moniker of The Coward Brothers. That year, Costello also produced <i><a href="/wiki/Rum_Sodomy_%26_the_Lash" title="Rum Sodomy &amp; the Lash">Rum Sodomy &amp; the Lash</a></i> for the Irish punk/folk band <a href="/wiki/The_Pogues" title="The Pogues">the Pogues</a>.</p>
<p>Growing antipathy between Costello and Bruce Thomas contributed to the Attractions' first split in 1986 when Costello was preparing to make a comeback. Working in the U.S. with Burnett, a band containing a number of <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>'s sidemen (including <a href="/wiki/James_Burton" title="James Burton">James Burton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Scheff" title="Jerry Scheff">Jerry Scheff</a>), and minor input from the Attractions, he produced <i><a href="/wiki/King_of_America" title="King of America">King of America</a></i>, an acoustic guitar-driven album with a country sound. It was billed as performed by "The Costello Show featuring the Attractions and Confederates" in the UK and Europe and "The Costello Show featuring Elvis Costello" in North America. Around this time he legally changed his name back to Declan MacManus, adding Aloysius as an extra middle name. Costello retooled his upcoming tour to allow for multiple nights in each city, playing one night with the Confederates, one night with the Attractions, and one night solo acoustic. In May 1986, he performed at <a href="/wiki/Self_Aid" title="Self Aid">Self Aid</a>, a benefit concert held in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> that focused on the chronic unemployment which was widespread in Ireland at that time.</p>
<p>Later that year, Costello returned to the studio with the Attractions and recorded <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_and_Chocolate_(album)" title="Blood and Chocolate (album)">Blood and Chocolate</a></i>, which was lauded for a <a href="/wiki/Post-punk" title="Post-punk">post-punk</a> fervour not heard since 1978's <i>This Year's Model</i>. It also marked the return of producer <a href="/wiki/Nick_Lowe" title="Nick Lowe">Nick Lowe</a>, who had produced Costello's first five albums. While <i>Blood and Chocolate</i> failed to chart a hit single of any significance, it did produce what has since become one of Costello's signature concert songs, "I Want You." On this album, Costello adopted the alias Napoleon Dynamite, the name he later attributed to the character of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emcee" title="Emcee">emcee</a> that he played during the <a href="/wiki/Vaudeville" title="Vaudeville">vaudeville</a>-style tour to support <i>Blood and Chocolate</i>. (The pseudonym had previously been used in 1982, when the B-side single "Imperial Bedroom" was credited to Napoleon Dynamite &amp; the Royal Guard; whether the title of the 2004 film <i><a href="/wiki/Napoleon_Dynamite" title="Napoleon Dynamite">Napoleon Dynamite</a></i> was inspired by Costello is disputed). After the tour for <i>Blood &amp; Chocolate</i>, Costello split from the Attractions, due mostly to tensions between Costello and Bruce Thomas. Costello would continue to work with Attraction Pete Thomas as a session musician for future releases.</p>
<p>Costello's recording contract with Columbia Records ended after <i>Blood &amp; Chocolate</i>. In 1987 he released compilation album, <i><a href="/wiki/Out_of_Our_Idiot" title="Out of Our Idiot">Out of Our Idiot</a></i> on his U.K. label, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demon_Records" title="Demon Records">Demon Records</a> consisting of B-sides, side projects, and unreleased songs from recording sessions from 1980 to 1987. He signed a new contract with <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a> and in early 1989 released <i><a href="/wiki/Spike_(Elvis_Costello_album)" title="Spike (Elvis Costello album)">Spike</a></i>, which spawned his biggest single in the U.S., the Top 20 hit "<a href="/wiki/Veronica_(song)" title="Veronica (song)">Veronica</a>", one of several songs Costello co-wrote with <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a>. At the <a href="/wiki/1989_MTV_Video_Music_Awards" title="1989 MTV Video Music Awards">1989 MTV Video Music Awards</a> on 6 September in Los Angeles, "Veronica" won the MTV Award for <a href="/wiki/MTV_Video_Music_Award_for_Best_Male_Video" title="MTV Video Music Award for Best Male Video">Best Male Video</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1991, Costello released <i><a href="/wiki/Mighty_Like_a_Rose" title="Mighty Like a Rose">Mighty Like a Rose</a></i>, which featured the single "The Other Side of Summer". He also co-composed and co-produced, with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Harvey" title="Richard Harvey">Richard Harvey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/G.B.H._(soundtrack)" title="G.B.H. (soundtrack)">title and incidental music for the mini-series</a> <i><a href="/wiki/G.B.H._(TV_series)" title="G.B.H. (TV series)">G.B.H.</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Bleasdale" title="Alan Bleasdale">Alan Bleasdale</a>. This entirely instrumental, and largely orchestral, soundtrack garnered a <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Film_and_Television_Arts" title="British Academy of Film and Television Arts">BAFTA</a>, for Best Music for a TV Series for the pair.</p>
<p>In 1993, Costello experimented with classical music with a critically acclaimed collaboration with the <a href="/wiki/Brodsky_Quartet" title="Brodsky Quartet">Brodsky Quartet</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Juliet_Letters" title="The Juliet Letters">The Juliet Letters</a></i>. During this period, he wrote a full album's worth of material for <a href="/wiki/Wendy_James" title="Wendy James">Wendy James</a>, and these songs became the tracks on her 1993 solo album <i>Now Ain't the Time for Your Tears</i>. Costello returned to rock and roll the following year with a project that reunited him with the Attractions, <i><a href="/wiki/Brutal_Youth" title="Brutal Youth">Brutal Youth</a></i>. In 1995, he released <i><a href="/wiki/Kojak_Variety" title="Kojak Variety">Kojak Variety</a></i>, an album of cover songs recorded five years earlier, and followed in 1996 with an album of songs originally written for other artists, <i><a href="/wiki/All_This_Useless_Beauty" title="All This Useless Beauty">All This Useless Beauty</a></i>. This was the final album of original material that he issued under his Warner Bros. contract, and also his final album with the Attractions.</p>
<p>In the spring of 1996, Costello played a series of intimate club dates, backed only by Steve Nieve on the piano, in support of <i>All This Useless Beauty</i>. An ensuing summer and fall tour with the Attractions proved to be the death knell for the band. With relations between Costello and bassist Bruce Thomas at a breaking point, Costello announced that the current tour would be the Attractions' last. The quartet performed their final U.S. show in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, Washington on 1 September 1996, before wrapping up their tour in Japan. Costello would still continue to work frequently with Attractions Steve Nieve and Pete Thomas; eventually, both would be members of Costello's new back-up band, The Imposters.</p>
<p>To fulfill his contractual obligations to Warner Bros., Costello released a greatest hits album titled <i><a href="/wiki/Extreme_Honey" title="Extreme Honey">Extreme Honey</a></i> (1997). It contained an original track titled "The Bridge I Burned", featuring Costello's son, Matt, on bass. In the intervening period, Costello had served as artistic chair for the 1995 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meltdown_Festival" title="Meltdown Festival">Meltdown Festival</a>, which gave him the opportunity to explore his increasingly eclectic musical interests. His involvement in the festival yielded a one-off live <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a> with jazz guitarist <a href="/wiki/Bill_Frisell" title="Bill Frisell">Bill Frisell</a>, which featured both cover material and a few of his own songs.</p>
<p>In 1998, Costello signed a multi-label contract with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polygram_Records" title="Polygram Records">Polygram Records</a>, sold by its parent company the same year to become part of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Group" title="Universal Music Group">Universal Music Group</a>. Costello released his new work on what he deemed the suitable imprimatur within the family of labels. His first new release as part of this contract involved a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Burt_Bacharach" title="Burt Bacharach">Burt Bacharach</a>. Their work had commenced earlier, in 1996, on a song called "God Give Me Strength" for the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Grace_of_My_Heart" title="Grace of My Heart">Grace of My Heart</a></i>. This led the pair to write and record the critically acclaimed album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Painted_From_Memory" title="Painted From Memory">Painted From Memory</a></i>, released under his new contract in 1998, on the <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Records" title="Mercury Records">Mercury Records</a> label, featuring songs that were largely inspired by the dissolution of his marriage to <a href="/wiki/Cait_O%27Riordan" title="Cait O'Riordan">Cait O'Riordan</a>. Costello and Bacharach performed several concerts with a full orchestral backing, and also recorded an updated version of Bacharach's "<a href="/wiki/I%27ll_Never_Fall_in_Love_Again" title="I'll Never Fall in Love Again">I'll Never Fall in Love Again</a>" for the soundtrack to <i><a href="/wiki/Austin_Powers:_The_Spy_Who_Shagged_Me" title="Austin Powers: The Spy Who Shagged Me">Austin Powers: The Spy Who Shagged Me</a></i>, with both appearing in the film to perform the song. He also wrote "I Throw My Toys Around" for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rugrats_Movie" title="The Rugrats Movie">The Rugrats Movie</a></i> and performed it with <a href="/wiki/No_Doubt" title="No Doubt">No Doubt</a>. The same year, he collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Paddy_Moloney" title="Paddy Moloney">Paddy Moloney</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Chieftains" title="The Chieftains">the Chieftains</a> on "The Long Journey Home" on the soundtrack of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Broadcasting_Service" title="Public Broadcasting Service">PBS</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney" title="Disney">Disney</a> <a href="/wiki/The_Long_Journey_Home" title="The Long Journey Home">mini-series of the same name</a>. The soundtrack won a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy" title="Grammy">Grammy</a> that year.</p>
<p>In 1999, Costello contributed a version of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/She_(Charles_Aznavour)" title="She (Charles Aznavour)">She</a>", released in 1974 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Aznavour" title="Charles Aznavour">Charles Aznavour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Kretzmer" title="Herbert Kretzmer">Herbert Kretzmer</a>, for the soundtrack of the film <i><a href="/wiki/Notting_Hill_(film)" title="Notting Hill (film)">Notting Hill</a></i>, with <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Jones_(composer)" title="Trevor Jones (composer)">Trevor Jones</a> producing. For the 25th anniversary of <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i>, Costello was invited to the programme, where he re-enacted his abrupt song-switch: This time, however, he interrupted the <a href="/wiki/Beastie_Boys" title="Beastie Boys">Beastie Boys</a>' "Sabotage", and they acted as his backing group for "Radio Radio".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2000s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 2000, Costello appeared at <a href="/wiki/The_Town_Hall_(New_York_City)" title="The Town Hall (New York City)">the Town Hall</a>, New York, in Steve Nieve's opera <i><a href="/wiki/Welcome_to_the_Voice" title="Welcome to the Voice">Welcome to the Voice</a></i>, alongside <a href="/wiki/Ron_Sexsmith" title="Ron Sexsmith">Ron Sexsmith</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Flansburgh" title="John Flansburgh">John Flansburgh</a> of <a href="/wiki/They_Might_Be_Giants" title="They Might Be Giants">They Might Be Giants</a>. In 2001, Costello was artist-in-residence at <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">UCLA</a> and wrote the music for a new ballet. He produced and appeared on an album of pop songs for the classical singer <a href="/wiki/Anne_Sofie_von_Otter" title="Anne Sofie von Otter">Anne Sofie von Otter</a>. He released the album <i><a href="/wiki/When_I_Was_Cruel" title="When I Was Cruel">When I Was Cruel</a></i> in 2002 on <a href="/wiki/Island_Records" title="Island Records">Island Records</a>, and toured with a new band, the Imposters (essentially the Attractions but with a different bass player, <a href="/wiki/Davey_Faragher" title="Davey Faragher">Davey Faragher</a>, formerly of <a href="/wiki/Cracker_(band)" title="Cracker (band)">Cracker</a>). He appeared as himself in the "<a href="/wiki/How_I_Spent_My_Strummer_Vacation" title="How I Spent My Strummer Vacation">How I Spent My Strummer Vacation</a>" episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simpsons" title="The Simpsons">The Simpsons</a></i>.</p>
<p>On 23 February 2003, Costello, along with <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Springsteen" title="Bruce Springsteen">Bruce Springsteen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steve_Van_Zandt" title="Steve Van Zandt">Steve Van Zandt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dave_Grohl" title="Dave Grohl">Dave Grohl</a>, performed a version of <a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">the Clash</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/London_Calling_(song)" title="London Calling (song)">London Calling</a>" at the 45th Grammy Awards ceremony, in honour of Clash frontman <a href="/wiki/Joe_Strummer" title="Joe Strummer">Joe Strummer</a>, who had died the previous December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In March, Elvis Costello &amp; the Attractions were inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a>. He announced his engagement in May to Canadian <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> singer and pianist <a href="/wiki/Diana_Krall" title="Diana Krall">Diana Krall</a>, whom he had seen in concert and then met backstage at the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Opera_House" title="Sydney Opera House">Sydney Opera House</a> in Australia. That September, he released <i><a href="/wiki/North_(Elvis_Costello_album)" title="North (Elvis Costello album)">North</a></i>, an album of piano-based ballads concerning the breakdown of his former marriage, and his falling in love with Krall. Also that year, Costello made an appearance in the television series <i><a href="/wiki/Frasier" title="Frasier">Frasier</a></i> as a folk singer in the Cafe Nervosa, sending Frasier and Niles on a search for a new coffee bar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 12 March 2003, Costello filled in for <a href="/wiki/David_Letterman" title="David Letterman">David Letterman</a> on the <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Show_with_David_Letterman" title="Late Show with David Letterman">Late Show with David Letterman</a></i> for the evening while Letterman was recovering from an eye infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>The song "Scarlet Tide" (co-written by Costello and T-Bone Burnett and used in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Cold_Mountain_(film)" title="Cold Mountain (film)">Cold Mountain</a></i>) was nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">2004 Academy Award</a>; he performed it at the awards ceremony with <a href="/wiki/Alison_Krauss" title="Alison Krauss">Alison Krauss</a>, who sang the song on the official soundtrack. Costello co-wrote many songs on Krall's 2004 CD, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Girl_in_the_Other_Room" title="The Girl in the Other Room">The Girl in the Other Room</a></i>, the first of hers to feature several original compositions. In July 2004, Costello's first full-scale orchestral work, <i><a href="/wiki/Il_Sogno" title="Il Sogno">Il Sogno</a></i>, was performed in New York. The work, a ballet after <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream" title="A Midsummer Night's Dream">A Midsummer Night's Dream</a></i>, was commissioned by Italian dance troupe Aterballeto, and received critical acclaim from the classical music critics. Performed by the <a href="/wiki/London_Symphony_Orchestra" title="London Symphony Orchestra">London Symphony Orchestra</a>, conducted by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Tilson_Thomas" title="Michael Tilson Thomas">Michael Tilson Thomas</a>, the recording was released on CD in September by <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Grammophon" title="Deutsche Grammophon">Deutsche Grammophon</a>. In September 2004, Costello released the album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Delivery_Man" title="The Delivery Man">The Delivery Man</a></i>, recorded in <a href="/wiki/Oxford,_Mississippi" title="Oxford, Mississippi">Oxford, Mississippi</a>, on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_Highway_Records" title="Lost Highway Records">Lost Highway Records</a>, and it was hailed as one of his best.</p>

<p>A CD recording of a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Marian_McPartland" title="Marian McPartland">Marian McPartland</a> on her show <i><a href="/wiki/Piano_Jazz" title="Piano Jazz">Piano Jazz</a></i> was released in 2005. It featured Costello singing six jazz standards and two of his own songs, accompanied by McPartland on piano. In November, Costello started recording a new album with <a href="/wiki/Allen_Toussaint" title="Allen Toussaint">Allen Toussaint</a> and producer <a href="/wiki/Joe_Henry" title="Joe Henry">Joe Henry</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/The_River_in_Reverse" title="The River in Reverse">The River in Reverse</a></i> was released in the UK on the <a href="/wiki/Verve_Records" title="Verve Records">Verve</a> label the following year in May.</p>
<p>A 2005 tour included a gig at <a href="/wiki/Glastonbury" title="Glastonbury">Glastonbury</a> that Costello considered so dreadful that he said "I don't care if I ever play England again. That gig made up my mind I wouldn't come back. I don't get along with it. We lost touch. It's 25 years since I lived there. I don't dig it, they don't dig me....British music fans don't have the same attitude to age as they do in America, where young people come to check out, say <a href="/wiki/Willie_Nelson" title="Willie Nelson">Willie Nelson</a>. They feel some connection with him and find a role for that music in their lives."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indep07_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-indep07-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>, Costello and Allen Toussaint performed in New York at a series of Hurricane Relief benefit concerts in September 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-39">[39]</a></sup> By week’s end, Costello had written "The River in Reverse", performed it with Toussaint and discussed plans for an album with Verve Record executives. The result was Costello's <i>The River in Reverse</i> which is a collaboration with New Orleanian, Allen Toussaint and recorded with The Crescent City Horns. Costello turned to older songs to reflect the national malaise at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a studio recording of Nieve's opera <i><a href="/wiki/Welcome_to_the_Voice" title="Welcome to the Voice">Welcome to the Voice</a></i> (2006, Deutsche Grammophon), Costello interpreted the character of Chief of Police, with <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Bonney" title="Barbara Bonney">Barbara Bonney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wyatt" title="Robert Wyatt">Robert Wyatt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sting_(musician)" title="Sting (musician)">Sting</a> and Amanda Roocroft, and the album reached No. 2 in the <i>Billboard</i> classical charts. Costello later reprised the piece on the stage of the <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9%C3%A2tre_du_Ch%C3%A2telet" title="Théâtre du Châtelet">Théâtre du Châtelet</a> in Paris in 2008, with Sting, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Sumner" title="Joe Sumner">Joe Sumner</a> (Sting's son) and Sylvia Schwartz. Also released in 2006 was a live recording of a concert with the Metropole Orkest at the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea_Jazz_Festival" title="North Sea Jazz Festival">North Sea Jazz Festival</a>, entitled <i>My Flame Burns Blue</i>. The soundtrack for <i><a href="/wiki/House_(TV_series)" title="House (TV series)">House, M.D.</a></i> featured Costello's interpretation of "Beautiful" by <a href="/wiki/Christina_Aguilera" title="Christina Aguilera">Christina Aguilera</a>, with the song appearing in the second episode of Season 2.</p>
<p>Costello was commissioned to write a <a href="/wiki/Chamber_opera" title="Chamber opera">chamber opera</a> by the Danish Royal Opera, <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>, on the subject of <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">Hans Christian Andersen</a>'s infatuation with Swedish soprano <a href="/wiki/Jenny_Lind" title="Jenny Lind">Jenny Lind</a>. Called <i>The Secret Songs</i> it was unfinished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In a performance in 2007 directed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kasper_Bech_Holten" title="Kasper Bech Holten">Kasper Bech Holten</a> at the Opera's studio theatre (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Takkelloftet" title="Takkelloftet">Takelloftet</a>), finished songs were interspersed with pieces from Costello's 1993 collaborative classical album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Juliet_Letters" title="The Juliet Letters">The Juliet Letters</a></i>, featuring Danish soprano Sine Bundgaard as Lind. The 2009 album <i><a href="/wiki/Secret,_Profane_%26_Sugarcane" title="Secret, Profane &amp; Sugarcane">Secret, Profane &amp; Sugarcane</a></i> includes material from <i>Secret Songs</i>.</p>
<p>On 22 April 2008, <i><a href="/wiki/Momofuku_(album)" title="Momofuku (album)">Momofuku</a></i> was released on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_Highway_Records" title="Lost Highway Records">Lost Highway Records</a>, the same imprint that released <i>The Delivery Man</i>, his previous studio album. The album was, at least initially, released exclusively on vinyl (with a code to download a digital copy). That summer, in support of the album, Costello toured with <a href="/wiki/The_Police" title="The Police">the Police</a> on the final leg of their <a href="/wiki/The_Police_Reunion_Tour" title="The Police Reunion Tour">2007/2008 Reunion Tour</a>. Costello played a homecoming gig at the Liverpool Philharmonic Hall on 25 June 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and, that month, gave his first performance in Poland, appearing with The Imposters for the closing gig of the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Malta" title="Lake Malta">Malta</a> theatre festival in <a href="/wiki/Pozna%C5%84" title="Poznań">Poznań</a>.</p>
<p>In July 2008, Costello (as Declan McManus) appeared in his home city <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> where he was awarded an honorary degree of Doctor of Music from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Liverpool" title="University of Liverpool">University of Liverpool</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Between 2008 and 2010, Costello hosted <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a>/<a href="/wiki/CTV_Television_Network" title="CTV Television Network">CTV's</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Spectacle:_Elvis_Costello_with..." title="Spectacle: Elvis Costello with...">Spectacle</a></i> in which Costello talked and performed with stars in various fields, styled similarly to <i><a href="/wiki/Inside_the_Actors_Studio" title="Inside the Actors Studio">Inside the Actors Studio</a></i>. Between its two seasons, the show compiled 20 episodes, including one where Costello was interviewed by actress <a href="/wiki/Mary-Louise_Parker" title="Mary-Louise Parker">Mary-Louise Parker</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Costello was featured on <a href="/wiki/Fall_Out_Boy" title="Fall Out Boy">Fall Out Boy</a>'s 2008 album <i><a href="/wiki/Folie_%C3%A0_Deux_(album)" title="Folie à Deux (album)">Folie à Deux</a></i>, providing vocals on the track "<a href="/wiki/What_a_Catch,_Donnie" title="What a Catch, Donnie">What a Catch, Donnie</a>", along with other artists who are friends with the band.</p>
<p>Costello appeared in <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Colbert" title="Stephen Colbert">Stephen Colbert</a>'s television special <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_Colbert_Christmas:_The_Greatest_Gift_of_All" title="A Colbert Christmas: The Greatest Gift of All">A Colbert Christmas: The Greatest Gift of All</a></i>. In the program, he was eaten by a bear, but later saved by <a href="/wiki/Santa_Claus" title="Santa Claus">Santa Claus</a>; he also sang a duet with Colbert. The special was first aired on 23 November 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Costello released <i><a href="/wiki/Secret,_Profane_%26_Sugarcane" title="Secret, Profane &amp; Sugarcane">Secret, Profane &amp; Sugarcane</a></i>, a collaboration with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-Bone_Burnett" title="T-Bone Burnett">T-Bone Burnett</a>, on 9 June 2009. Burnett previously worked with Costello on <i><a href="/wiki/King_of_America" title="King of America">King of America</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Spike_(Elvis_Costello_album)" title="Spike (Elvis Costello album)">Spike</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> It was his first on the Starbucks Hear Music label and a return to country music in the manner of <i>Good Year for the Roses</i>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 15 May 2010, Costello announced he would withdraw from a concert performed in Israel in opposition to <a href="/wiki/Israeli%E2%80%93Palestinian_conflict" title="Israeli–Palestinian conflict">Israel's treatment of Palestinians</a>. In a statement on his website, Costello wrote, "It has been necessary to dial out the falsehoods of propaganda, the double game and hysterical language of politics, the vanity and self-righteousness of public communiqués from cranks in order to eventually sift through my own conflicted thoughts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>
<p>Also in 2010, Elvis Costello appeared as himself in David Simon's television series, <i><a href="/wiki/Treme_(TV_series)" title="Treme (TV series)">Treme</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Costello released the album <i><a href="/wiki/National_Ransom" title="National Ransom">National Ransom</a></i> in autumn of 2010. In 2011, Costello appeared as himself on <a href="/wiki/Sesame_Street" title="Sesame Street">Sesame Street</a> to perform a song with Elmo and <a href="/wiki/Cookie_Monster" title="Cookie Monster">Cookie Monster</a>, titled "Monster Went and Ate My Red 2", a play on (The Angels Wanna Wear My) Red Shoes.</p>
<p>On 26 February 2012, Costello paid tribute to music legends <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Berry" title="Chuck Berry">Chuck Berry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Cohen" title="Leonard Cohen">Leonard Cohen</a> who were the recipients of the first annual PEN Awards for songwriting excellence, at the <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum" title="John F. Kennedy Presidential Library and Museum">JFK Presidential Library</a>, in Boston, Massachusetts on 26 February 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Boston_Globe_PEN_awards_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Boston_Globe_PEN_awards-49">[49]</a></sup> In September 2013 Costello released <i><a href="/wiki/Wise_Up_Ghost" title="Wise Up Ghost">Wise Up Ghost</a></i>, a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">the Roots</a>. On 25 October 2013, Costello was awarded an Honorary Doctorate of Music from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_England_Conservatory" title="New England Conservatory">New England Conservatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In October 2015, Costello's memoir, <i><a href="/wiki/Unfaithful_Music_%26_Disappearing_Ink" title="Unfaithful Music &amp; Disappearing Ink">Unfaithful Music &amp; Disappearing Ink</a></i>, was released.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships">Relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Costello has been married three times, the first time in 1974 to Mary Burgoyne, with whom he had a son, Matthew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indep07_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-indep07-38">[38]</a></sup> Toward the end of his first marriage, Costello became embroiled in an on-again/off-again romance with <a href="/wiki/Bebe_Buell" title="Bebe Buell">Bebe Buell</a>, then-girlfriend of <a href="/wiki/Todd_Rundgren" title="Todd Rundgren">Todd Rundgren</a> (and mother of <a href="/wiki/Steven_Tyler" title="Steven Tyler">Steven Tyler</a>'s daughter <a href="/wiki/Liv_Tyler" title="Liv Tyler">Liv</a>). Buell has said she was the inspiration behind some of Costello's most bitter love songs from the <i>Armed Forces</i> era, though Costello countered by claiming most of those songs had been written before he ever met Buell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1985, Costello became involved with <a href="/wiki/Cait_O%27Riordan" title="Cait O'Riordan">Cait O'Riordan</a>, then the bassist of the London Irish group <a href="/wiki/The_Pogues" title="The Pogues">the Pogues</a>, while he was producing the Pogues' album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rum_Sodomy_and_the_Lash" title="Rum Sodomy and the Lash">Rum Sodomy and the Lash</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> They married in 1986 and split up by the end of 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indep07_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-indep07-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>Costello became engaged to piano-vocalist <a href="/wiki/Diana_Krall" title="Diana Krall">Diana Krall</a> in May 2003,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indep07_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-indep07-38">[38]</a></sup> and married her at the home of <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a> on 6 December that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KrallMarriage_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-KrallMarriage-53">[53]</a></sup> Krall gave birth to twin sons, Dexter Henry Lorcan and Frank Harlan James, on 6 December 2006 in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Record_labels">Record labels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Record labels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 2001 to 2005, Costello re-issued his back catalogue in the U.S., from <i>My Aim Is True</i> (1977) to <i>All This Useless Beauty</i> (1996), on double-disc collections on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhino_Records" title="Rhino Records">Rhino Records</a> label. These releases, which each contained second discs of bonus material, ultimately fell out of print by 2007 after Universal Music acquired the rights to Costello's catalogue. Universal subsequently released new deluxe editions of <i>My Aim Is True</i> and <i>This Year's Model</i> with new bonus material of full-length concerts from the time of each album's release. These deluxe editions also fell out of print and Universal has reverted to re-releasing Costello's pre-1987 albums in their original context without bonus material.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vegetarianism">Vegetarianism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Vegetarianism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A vegetarian since the early 1980s, Costello says he was moved to reject meat after seeing the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/The_Animals_Film" title="The Animals Film">The Animals Film</a></i> (1982), which also helped inspire his song "Pills and Soap" from 1983's <i>Punch the Clock</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYer2010_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYer2010-18">[18]</a></sup> In January 2013, Costello teamed up with <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> to create an ad campaign backing vegetarian foods produced by the <a href="/wiki/Linda_McCartney_Foods" title="Linda McCartney Foods">Linda McCartney Foods</a> brand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Football">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Costello is a keen football fan, supporting <a href="/wiki/Premier_League" title="Premier League">Premier League</a> football club <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_F.C." title="Liverpool F.C.">Liverpool F.C.</a> since childhood, and has appeared on <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Football_Italia" title="Football Italia">Football Italia</a></i> as a pundit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Isranbul_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Isranbul-56">[56]</a></sup> On 25 May 2005, Costello was due to take the stage with his band at a gig in Norwich, which clashed with Liverpool appearing in the <a href="/wiki/2005_UEFA_Champions_League_Final" title="2005 UEFA Champions League Final">2005 UEFA Champions League Final</a> against AC Milan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Isranbul_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Isranbul-56">[56]</a></sup> With Liverpool losing 3-0 at half time, Costello was due on stage and began warming up his voice in preparation for the gig, before deciding: "I might as well see the first few minutes of the second half."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Isranbul_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-Isranbul-56">[56]</a></sup> With Liverpool staging a remarkable comeback (since dubbed the <i>Miracle of Istanbul</i>) by scoring three goals in six minutes and making it 3-3, Costello delayed his appearance on stage for over an hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Isranbul_56-3"><a href="#cite_note-Isranbul-56">[56]</a></sup> With the game going to penalties, after much delay he had no choice but to take the stage, with Costello recalling: "I tried my best to keep my eyes from the TV screen over the bar at the back of the room but the words "Oh s***, he’s missed" might have accidentally crept into the lyrics of "Good Year for the Roses".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Isranbul_56-4"><a href="#cite_note-Isranbul-56">[56]</a></sup> With Liverpool prevailing while he was on stage, an ecstatic Costello broke out into a performance of the club's anthem "<a href="/wiki/You%27ll_Never_Walk_Alone" title="You'll Never Walk Alone">You'll Never Walk Alone</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Isranbul_56-5"><a href="#cite_note-Isranbul-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Humanitarian_causes">Humanitarian causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Humanitarian causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Costello sits on the Advisory Board of the Board of Directors of the <a href="/wiki/Jazz_Foundation_of_America" title="Jazz Foundation of America">Jazz Foundation of America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Advisory_Comittee_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Advisory_Comittee-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Costello began working with the Jazz Foundation in 2001, and has been a featured performer in their annual benefit <a href="/wiki/A_Great_Night_in_Harlem" title="A Great Night in Harlem">A Great Night in Harlem</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elvis_at_The_Apollo_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elvis_at_The_Apollo-59">[59]</a></sup> since 2006. Costello has donated his time working with the Jazz Foundation of America<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> to save the homes and the lives of America's elderly jazz and blues musicians, including musicians who survived <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>. He has also performed at a benefit concert for the <a href="/wiki/Seva_Foundation" title="Seva Foundation">Seva Foundation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collaborations">Collaborations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Collaborations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to his major recorded collaborations with <a href="/wiki/Burt_Bacharach" title="Burt Bacharach">Burt Bacharach</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Brodsky_Quartet" title="Brodsky Quartet">Brodsky Quartet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anne_Sofie_von_Otter" title="Anne Sofie von Otter">Anne Sofie von Otter</a>, Costello has frequently been involved in other collaborations.</p>
<p>In 1981 <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Tilbrook" title="Glenn Tilbrook">Glenn Tilbrook</a> from <a href="/wiki/Squeeze_(band)" title="Squeeze (band)">Squeeze</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Belmont" title="Martin Belmont">Martin Belmont</a> from <a href="/wiki/The_Rumour" title="The Rumour">the Rumour</a> guested on the song "From a Whisper to a Scream" from the album "<a href="/wiki/Trust_(Elvis_Costello_album)" title="Trust (Elvis Costello album)">Trust</a>". Around this time he also collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Chris_Difford" title="Chris Difford">Chris Difford</a>, also of Squeeze, to write additional lyrics for the song "Boy With A Problem", which appeared on Costello's 1982 album <i><a href="/wiki/Imperial_Bedroom" title="Imperial Bedroom">Imperial Bedroom</a></i>. In 1984 <a href="/wiki/Daryl_Hall" title="Daryl Hall">Daryl Hall</a> provided backing vocals for the song "The Only Flame In Town", from the album "<a href="/wiki/Goodbye_Cruel_World_(Elvis_Costello_album)" title="Goodbye Cruel World (Elvis Costello album)">Goodbye Cruel World</a>". In 1985 he sang with <a href="/wiki/Annie_Lennox" title="Annie Lennox">Annie Lennox</a> on the track 'Adrian' from the <a href="/wiki/Eurythmics" title="Eurythmics">Eurythmics</a> record "<a href="/wiki/Be_Yourself_Tonight" title="Be Yourself Tonight">Be Yourself Tonight</a>".</p>
<p>In 1987, Costello began a songwriting collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a>. They wrote a number of songs together in a short period of time, that were released over a period of years. These songs included:</p>
<ul>
<li>"Back On My Feet", the B-side of McCartney's 1987 single "<a href="/wiki/Once_Upon_a_Long_Ago" title="Once Upon a Long Ago">Once Upon a Long Ago</a>", later added as a bonus track on the 1993 re-issue of McCartney's <i><a href="/wiki/Flowers_in_the_Dirt" title="Flowers in the Dirt">Flowers in the Dirt</a></i></li>
<li>Costello's "<a href="/wiki/Veronica_(song)" title="Veronica (song)">Veronica</a>" and "Pads, Paws and Claws" from his album <i><a href="/wiki/Spike_(Elvis_Costello_album)" title="Spike (Elvis Costello album)">Spike</a></i> (1989)</li>
<li>McCartney's "My Brave Face", "Don't Be Careless Love", "That Day Is Done" and the McCartney/Costello duet "You Want Her Too", all from McCartney's <i><a href="/wiki/Flowers_in_the_Dirt" title="Flowers in the Dirt">Flowers in the Dirt</a></i> (1989)</li>
<li>"So Like Candy" and "Playboy to a Man" from Costello's <i><a href="/wiki/Mighty_Like_a_Rose" title="Mighty Like a Rose">Mighty Like a Rose</a></i> (1991)</li>
<li>"The Lovers That Never Were" and "Mistress and Maid" from McCartney's <i><a href="/wiki/Off_the_Ground" title="Off the Ground">Off the Ground</a></i> (1993).</li>
<li>"Shallow Grave" from Costello's <i><a href="/wiki/All_This_Useless_Beauty" title="All This Useless Beauty">All This Useless Beauty</a></i> (1996).</li>
<li>Costello has also issued solo demo recordings of "Veronica", "Pads, Paws and Claws" and "Mistress and Maid" (a song he did not otherwise record.) Two other officially unissued McCartney/Costello compositions also exist as demos ("Tommy's Coming Home" and "Twenty-Five FIngers"). These two tracks, along with other McCartney/Costello demos of tunes they <u>did</u> release, have been widely bootlegged.</li>
</ul>
<p>In 1987, he appeared on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_Box_Office" title="Home Box Office">HBO</a> special <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roy_Orbison_and_Friends,_A_Black_and_White_Night" title="Roy Orbison and Friends, A Black and White Night">Roy Orbison and Friends, A Black and White Night</a></i>, which featured his long-time idol <a href="/wiki/Roy_Orbison" title="Roy Orbison">Roy Orbison</a>, and was invited back to <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i> for the first time since 1977. In 1988, Costello co-wrote "At the Other End (of the Telescope)" with <a href="/wiki/Aimee_Mann" title="Aimee Mann">Aimee Mann</a>; this song appears on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Til_Tuesday" title="Til Tuesday">Til Tuesday</a> album <i><a href="/wiki/Everything%27s_Different_Now" title="Everything's Different Now">Everything's Different Now</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1994, he sang "They Can't Take That Away From Me" with <a href="/wiki/Tony_Bennett" title="Tony Bennett">Tony Bennett</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/MTV_Unplugged" title="MTV Unplugged">MTV Unplugged</a></i>, appearing on <a href="/wiki/MTV_Unplugged_(Tony_Bennett_album)" title="MTV Unplugged (Tony Bennett album)">the album released from the broadcast</a>. In 2000, Costello wrote lyrics to "Green Song", a solo cello piece by <a href="/wiki/Svante_Henryson" title="Svante Henryson">Svante Henryson</a>; this song appears on the <a href="/wiki/Anne_Sofie_von_Otter" title="Anne Sofie von Otter">Anne Sofie von Otter</a> album <i><a href="/wiki/For_the_Stars" title="For the Stars">For the Stars</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 2005, Costello performed with <a href="/wiki/Green_Day" title="Green Day">Green Day</a> frontman <a href="/wiki/Billie_Joe_Armstrong" title="Billie Joe Armstrong">Billie Joe Armstrong</a>. They played both Costello and Green Day songs together, including "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alison_(song)" title="Alison (song)">Alison</a>", "<a href="/wiki/This_Year%27s_Model" title="This Year's Model">No Action</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Basket_Case_(song)" title="Basket Case (song)">Basket Case</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Good_Riddance_(Time_of_Your_Life)" title="Good Riddance (Time of Your Life)">Good Riddance (Time of Your Life)</a>". In late 2005 Costello performed with <a href="/wiki/Allen_Toussaint" title="Allen Toussaint">Allen Toussaint</a> in New York City at some Hurricane Katrina Relief Concerts and produced the studio album, "The River in Reverse." Also, Costello had a collaborative history with Mr. Toussaint, beginning with a couple of scattered album tracks in the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2007, Costello collaborated with the Argentinean/Uruguayan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electro_music" title="Electro music">electro</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tango_(music)" title="Tango (music)">tango</a> band <a href="/wiki/Bajofondo" title="Bajofondo">Bajofondo</a> on the song "Fairly Right" from the album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mar_Dulce_(album)" title="Mar Dulce (album)">Mar Dulce</a></i>. In 2008, Costello collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Fall_Out_Boy" title="Fall Out Boy">Fall Out Boy</a> on the track "<a href="/wiki/What_a_Catch,_Donnie" title="What a Catch, Donnie">What a Catch, Donnie</a>" from their album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folie_a_Deux_(album)" title="Folie a Deux (album)">Folie a Deux</a></i>. In <a href="/wiki/Jenny_Lewis" title="Jenny Lewis">Jenny Lewis</a>' 2008 release, <i><a href="/wiki/Acid_Tongue" title="Acid Tongue">Acid Tongue</a></i>, Costello provided vocals for the song "Carpetbaggers". In November 2009, Costello appeared live with <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Springsteen" title="Bruce Springsteen">Bruce Springsteen</a> and the <a href="/wiki/E_Street_Band" title="E Street Band">E Street Band</a> at <a href="/wiki/Madison_Square_Garden" title="Madison Square Garden">Madison Square Garden</a> and performed the <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Wilson" title="Jackie Wilson">Jackie Wilson</a> song "<a href="/wiki/(Your_Love_Keeps_Lifting_Me)_Higher_and_Higher" title="(Your Love Keeps Lifting Me) Higher and Higher">(Your Love Keeps Lifting Me) Higher and Higher</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>
<p>In December 2009, Costello portrayed The Shape on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Ghost_Brothers_of_Darkland_County" title="Ghost Brothers of Darkland County">Ghost Brothers of Darkland County</a></i>, a collaboration between rock singer <a href="/wiki/John_Mellencamp" title="John Mellencamp">John Mellencamp</a> and novelist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">Stephen King</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In February 2010, Costello appeared in the live <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cinecast" title="Cinecast">cinecast</a> of <a href="/wiki/Garrison_Keillor" title="Garrison Keillor">Garrison Keillor</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prairie_Home_Companion" title="Prairie Home Companion">Prairie Home Companion</a></i>, singing some of his own songs, and participating in many of the show's other musical and acting performances. On 30 April 2011, he played the song "Pump it Up" with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Odds" title="The Odds">the Odds</a> before the start of a <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_Canucks" title="Vancouver Canucks">Vancouver Canucks</a> playoff game at <a href="/wiki/Rogers_Arena" title="Rogers Arena">Rogers Arena</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vancouver_BC" title="Vancouver BC">Vancouver BC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2012, he played ukulele, mandolin, guitar and added backing vocals on Diana Krall's 11th studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Glad_Rag_Doll_(album)" title="Glad Rag Doll (album)">Glad Rag Doll</a></i> (as "Howard Coward").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coward_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coward-1">[1]</a></sup> On 10 September 2013, he played during the Apple September 2013 Event after the introduction of <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Radio" title="ITunes Radio">iTunes Radio</a>, <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a> 5C and 5S at Town Hall, at the Apple campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>
<p>On <a href="/wiki/Gov%27t_Mule" title="Gov't Mule">Gov't Mule</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Shout!_(Gov%27t_Mule_album)" title="Shout! (Gov't Mule album)">Shout!</a></i>, released in September 2013, he sang on the track "Funny Little Tragedy". On March 2014, Elvis Costello recorded <i><a href="/wiki/Lost_on_the_River:_The_New_Basement_Tapes" title="Lost on the River: The New Basement Tapes">Lost on the River: The New Basement Tapes</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Rhiannon_Giddens" title="Rhiannon Giddens">Rhiannon Giddens</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taylor_Goldsmith" title="Taylor Goldsmith">Taylor Goldsmith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_James" title="Jim James">Jim James</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Mumford" title="Marcus Mumford">Marcus Mumford</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheNewBasementTapes.com_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-TheNewBasementTapes.com-66">[66]</a></sup> Durning the 2016 Detour, he performs with <a href="/wiki/Larkin_Poe" title="Larkin Poe">Larkin Poe</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elvis_Costello&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Major artists who have been influenced by Costello include <a href="/wiki/Billy_Bragg" title="Billy Bragg">Billy Bragg</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Pogues" title="The Pogues">the Pogues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radiohead" title="Radiohead">Radiohead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dexys_Midnight_Runners" title="Dexys Midnight Runners">Dexys Midnight Runners</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulp_(band)" title="Pulp (band)">Pulp,</a> <a href="/wiki/Crowded_House" title="Crowded House">Crowded House</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_(band)" title="James (band)">James,</a> <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Vega" title="Suzanne Vega">Suzanne Vega</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Foo_Fighters" title="Foo Fighters">Foo Fighters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>
<p>Costello has worked with <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Bennett" title="Tony Bennett">Tony Bennett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burt_Bacharach" title="Burt Bacharach">Burt Bacharach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allen_Toussaint" title="Allen Toussaint">Allen Toussaint</a>, <a href="/wiki/T_Bone_Burnett" title="T Bone Burnett">T Bone Burnett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucinda_Williams" title="Lucinda Williams">Lucinda Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kid_Rock" title="Kid Rock">Kid Rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Konitz" title="Lee Konitz">Lee Konitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Eno" title="Brian Eno">Brian Eno</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rub%C3%A9n_Blades" title="Rubén Blades">Rubén Blades</a>.</p>
<p>Costello is also a music fan, and in print often champions the works of others. He has written several pieces for the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(magazine)" title="Vanity Fair (magazine)">Vanity Fair</a></i>, including the summary of what a perfect weekend of music would be. He has contributed to two <a href="/wiki/Grateful_Dead" title="Grateful Dead">Grateful Dead</a> tribute albums and covered <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Garcia" title="Jerry Garcia">Jerry Garcia</a>/<a href="/wiki/Robert_Hunter_(lyricist)" title="Robert Hunter (lyricist)">Robert Hunter</a> tunes such as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship_of_Fools" title="Ship of Fools">Ship of Fools</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Friend_of_the_Devil" title="Friend of the Devil">Friend of the Devil</a>", "It Must Have Been the Roses", "<a href="/wiki/Ripple_(song)" title="Ripple (song)">Ripple</a>" and "Tennessee Jed" in concert. His collaboration with Bacharach honoured Bacharach's place in pop music history. Costello also appeared in documentaries about singers <a href="/wiki/Dusty_Springfield" title="Dusty Springfield">Dusty Springfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Wilson" title="Brian Wilson">Brian Wilson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wanda_Jackson" title="Wanda Jackson">Wanda Jackson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Sexsmith" title="Ron Sexsmith">Ron Sexsmith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis, Tennessee</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Stax_Records" title="Stax Records">Stax Records</a>. He has also interviewed one of his own influences, <a href="/wiki/Joni_Mitchell" title="Joni Mitchell">Joni Mitchell</a>, and appeared on the release <i>A Tribute to Joni Mitchell</i> performing "Edith and the Kingpin". He also performed the title track of the <a href="/wiki/Charles_Mingus" title="Charles Mingus">Charles Mingus</a> tribute collection, <i>Weird Nightmare</i>. In addition, he appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Nick_Lowe" title="Nick Lowe">Nick Lowe</a> tribute album <i>Labour of Love</i>, performing the Lowe song "Egypt" and the <a href="/wiki/Gram_Parsons" title="Gram Parsons">Gram Parsons</a> tribute album <i>The Return of the Grievous Angel</i>, performing the Parsons song "Sleepless Nights". He was instrumental in bringing Sexsmith to a wider audience in 1995 by championing his debut album in <i>Mojo</i> magazine, even appearing on the cover with the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2004, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone_Magazine" title="Rolling Stone Magazine">Rolling Stone Magazine</a></i> ranked him No. 80 on their list of the <i>100 Greatest Artists of All Time</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> In 2012, Costello was among the <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Culture of the United Kingdom">British cultural icons</a> selected by artist Sir <a href="/wiki/Peter_Blake_(artist)" title="Peter Blake (artist)">Peter Blake</a> to appear in a new version of his most famous artwork – the Beatles' <i><a href="/wiki/Sgt._Pepper%27s_Lonely_Hearts_Club_Band" title="Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band">Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band</a></i> album cover – to celebrate the British cultural figures of his life that he most admires to mark his 80th birthday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Icons_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Icons-71">[71]</a></sup> On being chosen Costello remarked "I always dreamed that I might one day stand in the boots of <a href="/wiki/Albert_Stubbins" title="Albert Stubbins">Albert Stubbins</a> [Liverpool footballer who appeared in the original artwork]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Icons_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-Icons-71">[71]</a></sup></p>
<p>Costello has had a long collaborative history with Allen Toussaint, beginning with a couple of scattered album tracks in the 1980s and skipping ahead to the aftermath of Hurricane Katrina with the production of <i>The River in Reverse</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_39-3"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
