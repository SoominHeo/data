<p><b>Elwin Bruno Christoffel</b> (<small>German:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[kÊÉªËstÉfÉl]</a></span>; November 10, 1829 â March 15, 1900) was a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a>. He introduced fundamental concepts of <a href="/wiki/Differential_geometry" title="Differential geometry">differential geometry</a>, opening the way for the development of <a href="/wiki/Tensor_calculus" title="Tensor calculus">tensor calculus</a>, which would later provide the mathematical basis for <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Christoffel was born on 10 November 1829 in Montjoie (now <a href="/wiki/Monschau" title="Monschau">Monschau</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a> in a family of cloth merchants. He was initially educated at home in languages and mathematics, then attended the Jesuit Gymnasium and the Friedrich-Wilhelms <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(Germany)" title="Gymnasium (Germany)">Gymnasium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>. In 1850 he went to the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin" title="Humboldt University of Berlin">University of Berlin</a>, where he studied mathematics with <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gustav_Lejeune_Dirichlet" title="Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet">Gustav Dirichlet</a> (which had a strong influence over him)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Windelband_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Windelband-1">[1]</a></sup> among others, as well as attending courses in physics and chemistry. He received his doctorate in Berlin in 1856 for a thesis on the motion of <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> in homogeous bodies written under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Ohm" title="Martin Ohm">Martin Ohm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Kummer" title="Ernst Kummer">Ernst Kummer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Gustav_Magnus" title="Heinrich Gustav Magnus">Heinrich Gustav Magnus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butzer_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Butzer-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>After receiving his doctorate, Christoffel returned to Montjoie where he has spent the following three years in isolation from the academic community. However, he continued to study mathematics (especially mathematical physics) from books by <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann" title="Bernhard Riemann">Bernhard Riemann</a>, Dirichlet and <a href="/wiki/Augustin-Louis_Cauchy" title="Augustin-Louis Cauchy">Augustin-Louis Cauchy</a>. He also continued his research, publishing two papers in <a href="/wiki/Differential_geometry" title="Differential geometry">differential geometry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butzer_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Butzer-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1859 Christoffel returned to Berlin, earning his <a href="/wiki/Habilitation" title="Habilitation">habilitation</a> and becoming a <a href="/wiki/Privatdozent" title="Privatdozent">Privatdozent</a> at the University of Berlin. In 1862 he was appointed to a chair at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ETH_Z%C3%BCrich" title="ETH ZÃ¼rich">Polytechnic School</a> in ZÃ¼rich left vacant by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dedekind" title="Richard Dedekind">Dedekind</a>. He organised a new institute of mathematics at the young institution (it had been established only seven years earlier) that was highly appreciated. He also continued to publish research, and in 1868 he was elected a corresponding member of the <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Prussian Academy of Sciences">Prussian Academy of Sciences</a> and of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Istituto_Lombardo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Istituto Lombardo (page does not exist)">Istituto Lombardo</a> in Milan. In 1869 Christoffel returned to Berlin as a professor at the Gewerbeakademie (now part of the <a href="/wiki/Technical_University_of_Berlin" title="Technical University of Berlin">Technical University of Berlin</a>), with <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Schwarz" title="Hermann Schwarz">Hermann Schwarz</a> succeeding him in ZÃ¼rich. However, strong competition from the close proximity to the University of Berlin meant that the Gewerbeakademie could not attract enough students to sustain advanced mathematical courses and Christoffel left Berlin again after three years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butzer_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Butzer-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1872 Christoffel became a professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Strasbourg" title="University of Strasbourg">University of Strasbourg</a>, a centuries-old institution that was being reorganized into a modern university after Prussia's annexation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alsace-Loraine" title="Alsace-Loraine">Alsace-Loraine</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a>. Christoffel, together with his colleague <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Reye" title="Theodor Reye">Theodor Reye</a>, has built a reputable mathematics department at Strasbourg. He continued to publish research and had several doctoral students: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Otto_Pauls&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Otto Pauls (page does not exist)">Otto Pauls</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Victor_Doerr&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Victor Doerr (page does not exist)">Victor Doerr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rikitaro_Fujisawa" title="Rikitaro Fujisawa">Rikitaro Fujisawa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Maurer" title="Ludwig Maurer">Ludwig Maurer</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Wellstein&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Joseph Wellstein (page does not exist)">Joseph Wellstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Epstein" title="Paul Epstein">Paul Epstein</a>. Christoffel retired from the University of Strasbourg in 1894, being succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Martin_Weber" title="Heinrich Martin Weber">Heinrich Weber</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butzer_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Butzer-2">[2]</a></sup> After retirement he continued to work and publish, with the last treatise finished just before his death and published posthumously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Windelband_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Windelband-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Christoffel died on 15 March 1900 in Strasbourg. He was never married and left no family behind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butzer_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Butzer-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differential_geometry">Differential geometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Differential geometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Christoffel is mainly remembered for his seminal contributions to <a href="/wiki/Differential_geometry" title="Differential geometry">differential geometry</a>. In a famous 1869 paper on the equivalence problem for <a href="/wiki/Differential_form" title="Differential form">differential forms</a> in <i>n</i> variables, published in <a href="/wiki/Crelle%27s_Journal" title="Crelle's Journal">Crelle's Journal</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> he introduced the fundamental technique later called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covariant_differentiation" title="Covariant differentiation">covariant differentiation</a> and used it to define the <a href="/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor" title="Riemann curvature tensor">RiemannâChristoffel tensor</a> (the most common method used to express the <a href="/wiki/Curvature" title="Curvature">curvature</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riemannian_manifolds" title="Riemannian manifolds">Riemannian manifolds</a>). Christoffel's ideas were generalized and greatly developed by <a href="/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro" title="Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro">Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro</a> and his student <a href="/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita" title="Tullio Levi-Civita">Tullio Levi-Civita</a>, who turned them into the concept of <a href="/wiki/Tensor" title="Tensor">tensors</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_differential_calculus" title="Absolute differential calculus">absolute differential calculus</a>. The absolute differential calculus, later named <a href="/wiki/Tensor_calculus" title="Tensor calculus">tensor calculus</a>, forms the mathematical basis of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_theory_of_relativity" title="General theory of relativity">general theory of relativity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butzer_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Butzer-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Complex_analysis">Complex analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Complex analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Christoffel contributed to <a href="/wiki/Complex_analysis" title="Complex analysis">complex analysis</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Schwarz%E2%80%93Christoffel_mapping" title="SchwarzâChristoffel mapping">SchwarzâChristoffel mapping</a> is the first nontrivial constructive application of the <a href="/wiki/Riemann_mapping_theorem" title="Riemann mapping theorem">Riemann mapping theorem</a>. The SchwarzâChristoffel mapping has many applications to the theory of <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_function" title="Elliptic function">elliptic functions</a> and to areas of physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butzer_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Butzer-2">[2]</a></sup> In the field of elliptic functions he also published results concerning <a href="/wiki/Abelian_integral" title="Abelian integral">abelian integrals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theta_function" title="Theta function">theta functions</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Numerical_analysis">Numerical analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Numerical analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Christoffel generalized the <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_quadrature" title="Gaussian quadrature">Gaussian quadrature</a> method for integration and, in connection to this, he also introduced the <a href="/wiki/Christoffel%E2%80%93Darboux_formula" title="ChristoffelâDarboux formula">ChristoffelâDarboux formula</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legendre_polynomial" title="Legendre polynomial">Legendre polynomials</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (he later also published the formula for general <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthogonal_polynomial" title="Orthogonal polynomial">orthogonal polynomials</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_research">Other research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Christoffel also worked on <a href="/wiki/Potential_theory" title="Potential theory">potential theory</a> and the theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Differential_equations" title="Differential equations">differential equations</a>, however much of his research in these areas went unnoticed. He published two papers on the propagation of discontinuities in the solutions of partial differential equations which represent pioneering work in the theory of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shock_waves" title="Shock waves">shock waves</a>. He also studied physics and published research in <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a>, however his contributions here quickly lost their utility with the abandonment of the concept of the <a href="/wiki/Luminiferous_aether" title="Luminiferous aether">luminiferous aether</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butzer_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Butzer-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours">Honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</ul>
