<p><b>El Progreso</b> (<small>Spanish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Spanish" title="Help:IPA for Spanish">[el pɾoˈɣɾeso]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_Guatemala" title="Departments of Guatemala">department</a> in <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>. The departmental capital is <a href="/wiki/Guastatoya" title="Guastatoya">Guastatoya</a>. The Spanish established themselves in the region by 1551, after the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest_of_Guatemala" title="Spanish conquest of Guatemala">Spanish conquest of Guatemala</a>. El Progreso was declared a department in 1908, but was dissolved in 1920 before being reestablished in 1934. Guastatoya was badly affected by the <a href="/wiki/1976_Guatemala_earthquake" title="1976 Guatemala earthquake">1976 Guatemala earthquake</a>.</p>
<p>The department is located in northeastern Guatemala. It is bordered by the departments of <a href="/wiki/Alta_Verapaz_Department" title="Alta Verapaz Department">Alta Verapaz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baja_Verapaz_Department" title="Baja Verapaz Department">Baja Verapaz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guatemala_Department" title="Guatemala Department">Guatemala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jalapa_Department" title="Jalapa Department">Jalapa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zacapa_Department" title="Zacapa Department">Zacapa</a>. The department occupies an intermediate zone between the hot lowlands and the cooler <a href="/wiki/Guatemalan_Highlands" title="Guatemalan Highlands">Guatemalan Highlands</a>, and has a generally hot climate. The most important river is the <a href="/wiki/Motagua_River" title="Motagua River">Motagua</a>. To the north, the department is crossed by the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_de_las_Minas" title="Sierra de las Minas">Sierra de las Minas</a> mountain range. The main population centres in El Progreso are <a href="/wiki/Sanarate" title="Sanarate">Sanarate</a>, Guastatoya, and <a href="/wiki/San_Agust%C3%ADn_Acasaguastl%C3%A1n" title="San Agustín Acasaguastlán">San Agustín Acasaguastlán</a>. The department is crossed by the CA-9 Atlantic Highway, linking it with <a href="/wiki/Guatemala_City" title="Guatemala City">Guatemala City</a> and the Atlantic port of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Barrios" title="Puerto Barrios">Puerto Barrios</a>.</p>
<p>The vast majority of the population of the department are Spanish-speaking <a href="/wiki/Ladino_people" title="Ladino people">Ladinos</a>. The population is growing, but at a rate below the national average. At the same time, the illiteracy rate is falling.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Progreso_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, the area now included in the department of El Progreso was known as <i>Guastatoya</i> or <i>Huastatoya</i>, derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nahuatl_language" title="Nahuatl language">Nahuatl</a> <i>huäxyötl</i> or <i>huäxin</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Crescentia_alata" title="Crescentia alata">calabash</a>") and <i>atoyac</i> ("last"), meaning the last place that calabashes grow, a reference to the change in altitude that occurs in the department, and corresponding climatic change from cold to hot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Progreso_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Spanish colonial <i><a href="/wiki/Corregimiento" title="Corregimiento">corregimiento</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/San_Crist%C3%B3bal_Acasaguastl%C3%A1n" title="San Cristóbal Acasaguastlán">San Cristóbal Acasaguastlán</a> was established in 1551 with its seat in the town of that name, in what is now the eastern portion of the modern department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MuniSanCrisAcasa_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MuniSanCrisAcasa-3">[3]</a></sup> Most of the area of the modern department fell within the corregimiento of Chiquimula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MinEco_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MinEco-4">[4]</a></sup> Acasaguastlán was one of few pre-conquest centres of population in the middle <a href="/wiki/Motagua_River" title="Motagua River">Motagua River</a> drainage, due to the arid climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feldman98p29_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feldman98p29-5">[5]</a></sup> In El Progreso it included San Agustín de la Real Corona (modern <a href="/wiki/San_Agust%C3%ADn_Acasaguastl%C3%A1n" title="San Agustín Acasaguastlán">San Agustín Acasaguastlán</a>) and La Magdalena, but covered a broad area that also included parts of Baja Verapaz and Zacapa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MuniSanCrisAcasa_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-MuniSanCrisAcasa-3">[3]</a></sup> San Cristóbal Acasaguastlán and the surrounding area were reduced into colonial settlements by friars of the Dominican Order; at the time of the conquest the area was inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Poqomchi%27_people" title="Poqomchi' people">Poqomchi' Maya</a> and by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nahuatl_dialects" title="Nahuatl dialects">Nahuatl-speaking</a> Pipil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MuniSanCrisAcasa_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-MuniSanCrisAcasa-3">[3]</a></sup> In the 1520s, immediately after conquest, the inhabitants paid taxes to the Spanish Crown in the form of cacao, textiles, gold, silver and slaves. Within a few decades taxes were instead paid in beans, cotton and maize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feldman98p29_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Feldman98p29-5">[5]</a></sup> Acasaguastlán was first given in <i>encomienda</i> to conquistador <a href="/wiki/Diego_Salvatierra" title="Diego Salvatierra">Diego Salvatierra</a> in 1526.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The region was subject to a strong influx of Spanish colonists due to its location on the route between the colonial capital and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, and hence to Spain, resulting in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanicisation" title="Hispanicisation">hispanicisation</a> of the territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEGEPLAN2001p18_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-SEGEPLAN2001p18-7">[7]</a></sup> Guastatoya was mentioned as a village in an edict issued in <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_los_Caballeros_de_Guatemala" title="Santiago de los Caballeros de Guatemala">Santiago de los Caballeros de Guatemala</a>, the colonial capital, in 1758. In 1825, various settlements were integrated into the Acasagustlán district that would later be included in El Progreso department. In the 1870s, the territory was divided between the departments of Guatemala, Zacapa and Chiquimula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEGEPLAN2001p13_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-SEGEPLAN2001p13-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Departmental_history">Departmental history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Progreso_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Departmental history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The department of El Progreso was created by executive decree on 13 April 1908, to include the municipalities of Acasaguastlán, Cabañas, Guastatoya (which was renamed as El Progreso), Morazán, Sanarate, Sansaria (now known as Sansare), San Antonio La Paz, and San José El Golfo. For a short time from December 1919 to June of the following year, the department was renamed <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Estrada_Cabrera" title="Manuel Estrada Cabrera">Estrada Cabrera</a> at the request of its constituent municipalities, in order to honour the then-president. On 9 June 1920, after the president was overthrown, the department was dissolved by the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEGEPLAN2001p13_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-SEGEPLAN2001p13-8">[8]</a></sup> The municipalities that it had incorporated were returned to their previous jurisdictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The department was reestablished on 3 April 1934 by the legislative assembly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hernandez04_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hernandez04-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The departmental capital of Guastatoya was badly affected by the <a href="/wiki/1976_Guatemala_earthquake" title="1976 Guatemala earthquake">1976 Guatemala earthquake</a>, which completely destroyed all historic architecture in the town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hernandez04_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hernandez04-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Progreso_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The department of El Progreso is located in northeastern Guatemala. It is bordered to the southwest by the department of <a href="/wiki/Guatemala_Department" title="Guatemala Department">Guatemala</a>, to the southeast by <a href="/wiki/Jalapa_Department" title="Jalapa Department">Jalapa</a>, to the east by <a href="/wiki/Zacapa_Department" title="Zacapa Department">Zacapa</a>, to the west by <a href="/wiki/Baja_Verapaz_Department" title="Baja Verapaz Department">Baja Verapaz</a>, and to the north by the department of <a href="/wiki/Alta_Verapaz_Department" title="Alta Verapaz Department">Alta Verapaz</a>. The departmental capital is <a href="/wiki/Guastatoya" title="Guastatoya">Guastatoya</a>. The department occupies an intermediate zone between the hot lowlands and the cooler <a href="/wiki/Guatemalan_Highlands" title="Guatemalan Highlands">Guatemalan Highlands</a>. It has a surface area of approximately 1,922 square kilometres (742 sq mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hernandez04_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hernandez04-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The terrain is varied, with altitude varying between 245 and 1,240 metres (804 and 4,068 ft) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" title="Above mean sea level">above mean sea level</a>. The department has a generally hot climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MinEco_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-MinEco-4">[4]</a></sup> The most important river is the <a href="/wiki/Motagua_River" title="Motagua River">Motagua</a>. Other rivers in El Progreso include the Hato, Huija, Huyús, Las Ovejas, Morazán, Plátanos, and Sanarate rivers. To the north of the Motagua, the department is crossed by the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_de_las_Minas" title="Sierra de las Minas">Sierra de las Minas</a> mountain range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MinEco_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-MinEco-4">[4]</a></sup> The highest point in the department is Cerro El Pinalón, in the Sierra de las Minas, at 2,962 metres (9,718 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITMB_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ITMB-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The main population centres in El Progreso are Sanarate, Guastatoya, and San Agustín Acasaguastlán. The CA-9 Atlantic Highway crosses the department from west to east, en route from <a href="/wiki/Guatemala_City" title="Guatemala City">Guatemala City</a> to the Atlantic port of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Barrios" title="Puerto Barrios">Puerto Barrios</a>. Other principal highways are the CA-17 from El Rancho towards <a href="/wiki/Cob%C3%A1n" title="Cobán">Cobán</a>, and the RN-19 from Sanarate towards <a href="/wiki/Jalapa,_Jalapa" title="Jalapa, Jalapa">Jalapa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITMB_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ITMB-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Progreso_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the 2004 census, the population of El Progreso was 155,945, of which 97.9% were <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Guatemala" title="Demographics of Guatemala">non-indigenous</a>, and 2.1% were indigenous,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hernandez04_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hernandez04-10">[10]</a></sup> by 2013 the non-indigenous proportion of the population had grown to 98.2% against 1.8% indigenous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INE2014p14_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-INE2014p14-1">[1]</a></sup> The majority of the population consists of Spanish-speaking <a href="/wiki/Ladino_people" title="Ladino people">Ladinos</a>, although some traces of indigenous culture survive, such as in modes of dress, linguistic traces, and local customs and beliefs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> By 2013 the total population had grown to 163,537. The 2012–2013 population growth rate was 1.73%, below the national average of 2.32%. By gender, 51.9% of the population are female, and 48.1% male. The majority of the population, 59.8%, lives in the rural portion of the department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INE2014p14_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-INE2014p14-1">[1]</a></sup> The predominant language in El Progreso is <a href="/wiki/Guatemalan_Spanish" title="Guatemalan Spanish">Guatemalan Spanish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hernandez04_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hernandez04-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2013, 89.2% of the population were recorded as literate, with a year-on-year drop in illiteracy from 16.1% in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INE2014p23_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-INE2014p23-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy_and_agriculture">Economy and agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Progreso_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economy and agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Agricultural products include <a href="/wiki/Coffee_production_in_Guatemala" title="Coffee production in Guatemala">coffee</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" title="Sugar cane">sugar cane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_turtle_bean" title="Black turtle bean">beans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cacao_bean" title="Cacao bean">cacao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Annatto" title="Annatto">annatto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanilla" title="Vanilla">vanilla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, and a variety of other fruits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hernandez04_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hernandez04-10">[10]</a></sup> Tomato production is especially important in El Progreso, representing 6% of the national total.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MinEco_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-MinEco-4">[4]</a></sup> Local craft production includes <a href="/wiki/Basket_weaving" title="Basket weaving">basketwork</a>, ropemaking, <a href="/wiki/Leather_crafting" title="Leather crafting">leatherwork</a>, items fashioned from palm, and <a href="/wiki/Tulle_(netting)" title="Tulle (netting)">tulle</a> netting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hernandez04_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hernandez04-10">[10]</a></sup> Sanarate has the greatest economic production in the department, followed by Guastatoya, then San Agustín Acasaguastlán.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MinEco_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-MinEco-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Progreso_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Local tourist attractions include thermal baths near Sanarate and San Antonio La Paz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hernandez04_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hernandez04-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeological_sites">Archaeological sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=El_Progreso_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Archaeological sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The best preserved archaeological site in the region is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Guayt%C3%A1n&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Guaytán (page does not exist)">Guaytán</a>, which was inhabited from the Late Preclassic to the Late Classic periods of <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_chronology" title="Mesoamerican chronology">Mesoamerican chronology</a> (approximately from 250 BC to 900 AD), and was an important centre for the distribution of <a href="/wiki/Jade_use_in_Mesoamerica" title="Jade use in Mesoamerica">jade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
