<p><i><b>Emma</b></i> is a 1996 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_piece" title="Period piece">period film</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Emma_(novel)" title="Emma (novel)">novel of the same name</a> by <a href="/wiki/Jane_Austen" title="Jane Austen">Jane Austen</a>. Scripted and directed by <a href="/wiki/Douglas_McGrath" title="Douglas McGrath">Douglas McGrath</a>, the film stars <a href="/wiki/Gwyneth_Paltrow" title="Gwyneth Paltrow">Gwyneth Paltrow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Cumming" title="Alan Cumming">Alan Cumming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toni_Collette" title="Toni Collette">Toni Collette</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ewan_McGregor" title="Ewan McGregor">Ewan McGregor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Northam" title="Jeremy Northam">Jeremy Northam</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emma_(1996_theatrical_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film describes a year in the life of <a href="/wiki/Emma_Woodhouse" title="Emma Woodhouse">Emma Woodhouse</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gwyneth_Paltrow" title="Gwyneth Paltrow">Gwyneth Paltrow</a>), a congenial but naïve young woman who thinks of herself as a romantic matchmaker in her small community in early-19th-century England. When her <a href="/wiki/Governess" title="Governess">governess</a>, Miss Taylor (<a href="/wiki/Greta_Scacchi" title="Greta Scacchi">Greta Scacchi</a>), gets married and goes to live with her new husband, Mr Weston (<a href="/wiki/James_Cosmo" title="James Cosmo">James Cosmo</a>), Emma proudly takes the credit for having brought the couple together. Her father and their old family friend (and brother of Emma's sister's husband) <a href="/wiki/George_Knightley" title="George Knightley">George Knightley</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Northam" title="Jeremy Northam">Jeremy Northam</a>) dispute her claim and disapprove of her trying to make more matches, but she ignores their warnings and sets her mind on setting up Mr Elton (<a href="/wiki/Alan_Cumming" title="Alan Cumming">Alan Cumming</a>), the minister who performed the Westons' marriage ceremony, with Harriet Smith (<a href="/wiki/Toni_Collette" title="Toni Collette">Toni Collette</a>), an unsophisticated young woman on the verges of society.</p>
<p>As a close friendship develops between Emma and Harriet, it becomes clear that Harriet is being courted by Robert Martin (<a href="/wiki/Edward_Woodall" title="Edward Woodall">Edward Woodall</a>), a farmer who has known Harriet since she was a girl. When Mr Martin proposes to Harriet, she is inclined to accept, but she has come to rely heavily on Emma's advice, and Emma persuades her to reject the proposal. Meanwhile, Mr Elton has been expressing a desire for Emma by taking an interest in a picture she drew of Harriet and by giving her a riddle for a book of riddles being compiled by Harriet. Emma misinterprets this as interest in Harriet, but when Mr Elton and Emma are alone, he fervently declares his love for Emma herself, and she finally realizes her mistake. She harshly rejects his pleas, and he later marries another woman, who turns out to be a vain socialite who competes with Emma for status in the community.</p>
<p>Over the next few months, various gatherings show who loves whom among Emma's friends. Emma is briefly attracted to a charming, gallant young man named Frank Churchill (<a href="/wiki/Ewan_McGregor" title="Ewan McGregor">Ewan McGregor</a>), Mr Weston's son who comes to visit from London, but Emma soon decides to set him up with Harriet. However, Frank is revealed to have a secret engagement with a shy, pretty woman named Jane Fairfax (<a href="/wiki/Polly_Walker" title="Polly Walker">Polly Walker</a>). Harriet states that she has no interest in Frank, preferring Mr Knightley, who was the only man who would dance with her at a party. Mr Knightley only danced with Harriet out of politeness and has started to fall in love with Emma.</p>
<p>The conclusion of the story begins when Emma ridicules a poor woman named Miss Bates (<a href="/wiki/Sophie_Thompson" title="Sophie Thompson">Sophie Thompson</a>) during a picnic, after which Mr Knightley angrily scolds Emma and leaves town for a while. She finds herself thinking about him while he's away, but does not realize she loves him until Harriet expresses interest in him. When Mr Knightley returns, he and Emma cross paths in a meadow and have a conversation that begins awkwardly but ends with him asking her to marry him and her gladly accepting. The news of their engagement upsets Harriet, who avoids Emma for a while, but returns a few weeks later, engaged to Mr Martin. The film ends with Emma and Mr Knightley's wedding.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emma_(1996_theatrical_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conception_and_adaptation">Conception and adaptation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emma_(1996_theatrical_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conception and adaptation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Douglas_McGrath" title="Douglas McGrath">Douglas McGrath</a> "fell in love" with <a href="/wiki/Jane_Austen" title="Jane Austen">Jane Austen</a>'s 1815 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Emma_(novel)" title="Emma (novel)">Emma</a></i>, while he was an undergraduate at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>. He believed the book would make a great film, but it was not until a decade later that he was given a chance to work on the idea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chollet_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chollet-4">[4]</a></sup> After receiving an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award" title="Academy Award">Academy Award</a> nomination in 1995 for his work on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bullets_Over_Broadway" title="Bullets Over Broadway">Bullets Over Broadway</a></i>, McGrath decided to make the most of the moment and took his script idea for a film adaptation of <i>Emma</i> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miramax_Films" title="Miramax Films">Miramax Films</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chollet_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chollet-4">[4]</a></sup> McGrath had initially wanted to write a modern version of the novel, set on the <a href="/wiki/Upper_East_Side" title="Upper East Side">Upper East Side</a> of New York City. Miramax's co-chairman, <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Weinstein" title="Harvey Weinstein">Harvey Weinstein</a>, liked the idea of a contemporary take on the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chollet_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chollet-4">[4]</a></sup> McGrath was unaware that <a href="/wiki/Amy_Heckerling" title="Amy Heckerling">Amy Heckerling</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Clueless_(film)" title="Clueless (film)">Clueless</a></i> was already in production until plans for <i>Emma</i> were well underway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chollet_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chollet-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting">Casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emma_(1996_theatrical_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>McGrath decided to bring in American actress <a href="/wiki/Gwyneth_Paltrow" title="Gwyneth Paltrow">Gwyneth Paltrow</a> to audition for <a href="/wiki/Emma_Woodhouse" title="Emma Woodhouse">Emma Woodhouse</a>, after a suggestion from his agent and after seeing her performance in <i><a href="/wiki/Flesh_and_Bone_(film)" title="Flesh and Bone (film)">Flesh and Bone</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JClark_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-JClark-5">[5]</a></sup> Of his decision to bring Paltrow in for the part, McGrath revealed "The thing that actually sold me on her playing a young English girl was that she did a perfect Texas accent. I know that wouldn't recommend her to most people. I grew up in Texas, and I have never heard an actor or actress not from Texas sound remotely like a real Texan. I knew she had theater training, so she could carry herself. We had many actresses, big and small, who wanted to play this part. The minute she started the read-through, the very first line, I thought, 'Everything is going to be fine; she's going to be brilliant.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JClark_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-JClark-5">[5]</a></sup> Following the read through, the co-chairman of <a href="/wiki/Miramax" title="Miramax">Miramax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Weinstein" title="Harvey Weinstein">Harvey Weinstein</a>, decided to give <i>Emma</i> the green-light. However, he wanted Paltrow to appear in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pallbearer" title="The Pallbearer">The Pallbearer</a></i> first, before going ahead and allowing the film to be made.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JClark_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-JClark-5">[5]</a></sup> While she recovered from wisdom-tooth surgery, Paltrow had a month to herself do her own research for the part.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strauss_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strauss-6">[6]</a></sup> She also studied horsemanship, dancing, singing, archery and the "highly stylized" manners and dialect during a three-week rehearsal period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strauss_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Strauss-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Northam" title="Jeremy Northam">Jeremy Northam</a> revealed that when he first tried to read <i>Emma</i>, he did not get very far and was not a fan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donnelly_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donnelly-7">[7]</a></sup> When he read the script for the film, he was initially considered for another role, but he wanted to play <a href="/wiki/George_Knightley" title="George Knightley">George Knightley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donnelly_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Donnelly-7">[7]</a></sup> He stated "When I met the director, we got on very well and we talked about everything except the film. At the end of it, he said he thought Knightley was the part for me, so I didn't have to bring up the issue at all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donnelly_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Donnelly-7">[7]</a></sup> Northam added that Knightley's faith in Emma becoming a better person was one of the reasons he loved the character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donnelly_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Donnelly-7">[7]</a></sup> Australian actress <a href="/wiki/Toni_Collette" title="Toni Collette">Toni Collette</a> was cast as Harriet Smith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ganahl_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ganahl-8">[8]</a></sup> Collette also struggled to get into the Austen books when she was younger, but after reading <i>Emma</i>, which she deemed "warm and witty and clever", she began to appreciate them more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ganahl_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ganahl-8">[8]</a></sup> Collette had to gain weight to portray "the Rubenesque Harriet" and she explained "I think it's important for people to look real in films. There's a tendency to go Barbie doll and I don't agree with that at all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ganahl_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ganahl-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ewan_McGregor" title="Ewan McGregor">Ewan McGregor</a> was cast as Frank Churchill. He told Adam Higginbotham from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> that he chose to star in <i>Emma</i> because he thought it would be something different from his previous role in <i><a href="/wiki/Trainspotting_(film)" title="Trainspotting (film)">Trainspotting</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higginbotham_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Higginbotham-9">[9]</a></sup> McGregor later regretted appearing in the film, saying "My decision-making was wrong. It's the only time I've done that. And I learnt from it, you know. So I'm glad of that - because it was early on and I learnt my lesson. It's a good film, <i>Emma</i>, but I'm just... not very good in it. I'm not helped because I'm also wearing the world's worst wig. It's quite a laugh, checking that wig out."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higginbotham_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Higginbotham-9">[9]</a></sup> Real-life mother and daughter, <a href="/wiki/Phyllida_Law" title="Phyllida Law">Phyllida Law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sophie_Thompson" title="Sophie Thompson">Sophie Thompson</a>, portrayed Mrs and Miss Bates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gritten_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gritten-10">[10]</a></sup> Thompson revealed that it was a coincidence that she and her mother were cast alongside each other, as the casting director had their names on separate lists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gritten_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gritten-10">[10]</a></sup> McGrath initially believed Thompson to be too young to play Miss Bates, but he changed his mind after seeing her wearing glasses with her hair down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gritten_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gritten-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alan_Cumming" title="Alan Cumming">Alan Cumming</a> appeared as Reverend Philip Elton, who falls in love with Emma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Cumming wrote on his official website that the friendship that developed between himself and McGrath was one of the most memorable things about his time working on the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cumming_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cumming-12">[12]</a></sup> He went on to state that the worst thing about the shoot was his hair, which had been lightened and curled for the character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cumming_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cumming-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Juliet_Stevenson" title="Juliet Stevenson">Juliet Stevenson</a> portrayed the "ghastly" Mrs Elton, while <a href="/wiki/Polly_Walker" title="Polly Walker">Polly Walker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greta_Scacchi" title="Greta Scacchi">Greta Scacchi</a> starred as Jane Fairfax and Anne Taylor respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Other cast members included <a href="/wiki/Edward_Woodall" title="Edward Woodall">Edward Woodall</a> as Robert Martin, <a href="/wiki/James_Cosmo" title="James Cosmo">James Cosmo</a> as Mr Weston and Denys Hawthorne as Mr Woodhouse, in one of his last film appearances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Costume_design">Costume design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emma_(1996_theatrical_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Costume design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dress_worn_by_Gwyneth_Paltrow_in_Emma.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="427" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Dress_worn_by_Gwyneth_Paltrow_in_Emma.jpg/160px-Dress_worn_by_Gwyneth_Paltrow_in_Emma.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Dress_worn_by_Gwyneth_Paltrow_in_Emma.jpg/240px-Dress_worn_by_Gwyneth_Paltrow_in_Emma.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Dress_worn_by_Gwyneth_Paltrow_in_Emma.jpg/320px-Dress_worn_by_Gwyneth_Paltrow_in_Emma.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a>

Emma's wedding dress created by Ruth Myers.</div>

<p>British costume designer <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Myers_(costume_designer)" title="Ruth Myers (costume designer)">Ruth Myers</a> created and designed the clothing for the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-De_Witt_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-De_Witt-18">[18]</a></sup> She wanted to mirror the lightness of the script within the costumes and give "a spark of color and life" to the early 19th century setting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Calhoun_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Calhoun-19">[19]</a></sup> During her research, Myers noted a similarity between the fashions after the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1920s_in_fashion" title="1920s in fashion">1920s</a>, saying that they had "the same sort of flapperish quality".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Calhoun_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Calhoun-19">[19]</a></sup> The designer explained "The moment I set to research it, more and more it kept striking me what the similarities were between the two periods. It was a period of freedom of costume for women, and it was a period of constant diversions for the upper classes–picnics, dinners, balls, dances. What I wanted to do was make it look like the watercolors of the period, which are very bright and very clear, with very specific colors."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Calhoun_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Calhoun-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Myers went on to reveal that she did not want the costumes to have a "heavy English look" and instead she wanted "to get the freedom of bodies that you see in all the drawings, the form of the body underneath, the swell of the breasts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Calhoun_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-Calhoun-19">[19]</a></sup> Myers told Barbara De Witt from the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Daily_News" title="Los Angeles Daily News">Los Angeles Daily News</a></i> that using pastel-colored clothing to get the watercolor effect was one of her major challenges during the production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-De_Witt_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-De_Witt-18">[18]</a></sup> The designer was later criticised for being inaccurate, but she stated that she did not want the costumes to look old or <a href="/wiki/Sepia_(color)" title="Sepia (color)">sepia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Calhoun_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-Calhoun-19">[19]</a></sup> Myers only had five weeks in which to create 150 costumes for the production, and she was constantly working on the set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-De_Witt_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-De_Witt-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>Emma's wedding dress was made from silk crepe and embroidered with a small sprig pattern, while the sleeves and the train were made from embroidered net.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WeddingDress_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-WeddingDress-20">[20]</a></sup> Of the dress, Myers stated "The inspiration for Emma's wedding dress began with a small amount of exquisite vintage lace that became the overlay. I wanted a look that would work not only for the period but also one that would compliment Gwyneth Paltrow's youth, swan neck, and incredible beauty. I was also hoping to evoke happiness and the English countryside; the sun did shine on the day we shot the scene!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WeddingDress_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-WeddingDress-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>The musical score of the film was written by British composer <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Portman" title="Rachel Portman">Rachel Portman</a>. It was released on July 29, 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ankeny_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ankeny-21">[21]</a></sup> Portman told Rebecca Jones from the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> that her score was "purely classical". She continued "It is an orchestral piece, by which I mean that there is nothing in it that you wouldn't find in a symphony orchestra. It was influenced by my roots and my classical background."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Portman used various instruments to give a voice to the characters. She revealed that "a quivering violin" would represent Harriet's uneasy stomach, while "a bittersweet clarinet" would accompany Emma though her emotional journey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-23">[23]</a></sup> Josh Friedman from the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> believed Portman's "crafty score guides the audience through the heroine's game playing, and ultimately, to her romantic destiny."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-23">[23]</a></sup> He also thought the music had "a sneaky, circular feel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Playbill" title="Playbill">Playbill</a>'s</i> Ken LaFave commented that the score "underlined the period romanticism" in <i>Emma</i> and contained a "string-rich, romantic sound".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Jason Ankeny, a music critic for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a></i>, wrote that Portman's score to <i>Emma</i> employed all of her "signatures" like "whimsical yet romantic melodies, fluffy string arrangements, and woodwind solos", which would be familiar to anyone who had listened to her previous film scores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ankeny_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ankeny-21">[21]</a></sup> He stated, "it seems as if she's simply going through the motions, content to operate within the confines of an aesthetic that, admittedly, is hers and hers alone. By no means a bad score, <i>Emma</i> is nevertheless a disappointment – if you've heard a previous Rachel Portman score, you've pretty much heard this one as well."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ankeny_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ankeny-21">[21]</a></sup> On March 24, 1997, Portman became the first woman to win the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Original_Score" title="Academy Award for Best Original Score">Academy Award for Best Original Score</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The album contains 18 tracks; the first track is "Main Titles", and the final track is "End Titles".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ankeny_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ankeny-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparisons_with_the_novel">Comparisons with the novel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emma_(1996_theatrical_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Comparisons with the novel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although in general staying close to the plot of the book, the screenplay by <a href="/wiki/Douglas_McGrath" title="Douglas McGrath">Douglas McGrath</a> enlivens the banter between the staid Mr Knightley and the vivacious Emma, making the basis of their attraction more apparent.</p>
<p>Austen's original novel deals with Emma's false sense of class superiority, for which she is eventually chastised. In an essay from <i>Jane Austen in Hollywood</i>, Nora Nachumi writes that, due partly to Paltrow's star status, Emma appears less humbled by the end of this film than she does in the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>While the movie trailer shows Emma in love with John and Frank: "Emma loves John" and "Emma loves Frank" are both displayed over film from the movie, Mr Knightley's given name is George. It is George Knightley's brother who is named John, making this an error in the trailer.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emma_(1996_theatrical_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emma_(1996_theatrical_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film has received generally positive reviews from critics. Film <a href="/wiki/Review_aggregator" title="Review aggregator">review aggregation</a> site <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> classified the film as "fresh" with an 84 percent approval rating among 44 reviews, with a <a href="/wiki/Average" title="Average">rating average</a> of 7.1 out of 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, which assigns a score of 1–100 to individual film reviews, gave <i>Emma</i> an average rating of 66 based on 22 reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>Ken Eisner, writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i>, proclaimed "Gwyneth Paltrow shines brightly as Jane Austen's most endearing character, the disastrously self-assured matchmaker Emma Woodhouse. A fine cast, speedy pacing and playful direction make this a solid contender for the Austen sweepstakes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisner_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eisner-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
