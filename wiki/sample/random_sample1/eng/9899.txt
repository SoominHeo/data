<p>A <b>passenger</b> (also abbreviated as <b>pax</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Person" title="Person">person</a> who travels in a <a href="/wiki/Vehicle" title="Vehicle">vehicle</a> but bears little or no responsibility for the tasks required for that vehicle to arrive at its destination or otherwise operate the vehicle.</p>
<p>Passengers are people who ride on <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">buses</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passenger_train" title="Passenger train">passenger trains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliners</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferryboat" title="Ferryboat">ferryboats</a>, and other methods of transportation. Historically, the concept of the passenger has existed for as long as man has been able to create means of transportation capable of carrying more people than were needed to operate the vessel.</p>
<p>Crew members (if any), as well as the driver or pilot of the vehicle, are usually not considered to be passengers. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Flight_attendant" title="Flight attendant">flight attendant</a> on an airline would not be considered a "passenger" while on duty and the same with those working in the <a href="/wiki/Galley_(kitchen)" title="Galley (kitchen)">kitchen</a> or restaurant onboard a ship as well as cleaning staff, but an employee riding in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Company_car" title="Company car">company car</a> being driven by another person would be considered a <i>passenger</i>, even if the car was being driven on company business.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Railways">Railways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Passenger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Railways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway" title="Railway">railway</a> parlance, 'passenger', as well as being the end user of a service, is also a categorisation of the type of <a href="/wiki/Rolling_stock" title="Rolling stock">rolling stock</a> used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simmons_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simmons-2">[2]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> case, there are several categories of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passenger_train" title="Passenger train">passenger train</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simmons_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Simmons-2">[2]</a></sup> These categories include:</p>
<ul>
<li>'<b>Express passenger'</b>, which constitutes long distance and high speed railway travel between major locations such as ports and cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simmons_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Simmons-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>'<b>Semi-fast express passenger'</b>, a type of service that is high speed, though stops at selected destinations of high population density en route.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simmons_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Simmons-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>'<b>Local passenger'</b>, the lowest category of British passenger train, which provides a service that stops at all stations between major destinations, for the benefit of local populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simmons_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Simmons-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="No_pax">No pax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Passenger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: No pax">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>In transportation, a "no pax" trip is a trip without passengers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> For example, no-pax flights are <a href="/wiki/Cargo_airline" title="Cargo airline">freight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferry_flying" title="Ferry flying">ferry</a> and positioning flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">Legal status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Passenger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legal status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In most jurisdictions, laws have been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enactment_of_a_bill" title="Enactment of a bill">enacted</a> that dictate the legal obligations of the owner of a vehicle or vessel, or of the <a href="/wiki/Driving" title="Driving">driver</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviator" title="Aviator">pilot</a> of the same, towards the passengers. With respect to passengers riding in cars and vans, the driver may owe a duty of care to passengers, particularly where the passenger's presence in the vehicle can be seen to "confer some benefit on the driver other than the benefit of his or her company or the mere sharing of expenses".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In other situations, however, <a href="/wiki/Guest_statute" title="Guest statute">guest statutes</a> may limit the ability of passengers to sue the driver of the vehicle over an accident. Many places require cars to be outfitted with measures specifically for the protection of passengers, such as passenger-side <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_bag" title="Air bag">air bags</a>. With respect to passengers on commercial vehicles or vessels, both national laws and international treaties require that the carrier act with a certain standard of care. The number of passengers that a vehicle or vessel may legally carry is defined as its <a href="/wiki/Seating_capacity" title="Seating capacity">seating capacity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
