<p><b>(29075) 1950 DA</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-Earth_asteroid" title="Near-Earth asteroid">near-Earth asteroid</a>. Among asteroids more than 1 km in diameter, it is notable for having the highest known <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a> of <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">impacting Earth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-risk-table_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-risk-table-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2002, it had the highest <a href="/wiki/Palermo_Technical_Impact_Hazard_Scale" title="Palermo Technical Impact Hazard Scale">Palermo rating</a> with a value of 0.17 for a possible collision in 2880.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEO-1950DA_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEO-1950DA-7">[7]</a></sup> Since that time, the estimated risk has been updated several times. In December 2015, the odds of an Earth impact were revised to 1 in 8,330 (0.012%) with a Palermo rating of −1.42.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-risk-table_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-risk-table-6">[6]</a></sup> (29075) 1950 DA is not assigned a <a href="/wiki/Torino_scale" title="Torino scale">Torino scale</a> rating, because the 2880 date is over 100 years in the future.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_name">Discovery and name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(29075)_1950_DA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>(29075) 1950 DA was first discovered on February 23, 1950, by <a href="/wiki/Carl_A._Wirtanen" title="Carl A. Wirtanen">Carl A. Wirtanen</a> at <a href="/wiki/Lick_Observatory" title="Lick Observatory">Lick Observatory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-1">[1]</a></sup> It was observed for seventeen days<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giorgini2002_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Giorgini2002-8">[8]</a></sup> and then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_asteroid" title="Lost asteroid">lost</a> because the short observation arc resulted in large uncertainties in Wirtanen's orbital solution. On December 31, 2000, it was recovered as <span class="nowrap">2000 YK<sub>66</sub></span> and just two hours later was recognized as (29075) 1950 DA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giorgini2002_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Giorgini2002-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observations">Observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(29075)_1950_DA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">

</div>
<p>On March 5, 2001, (29075) 1950 DA made a close approach to Earth at a distance of 0.0520726 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> (7,789,950 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">km</a>; 4,840,450 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mi</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpl-close_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-jpl-close-9">[9]</a></sup> It was studied by radar at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goldstone_Observatory" title="Goldstone Observatory">Goldstone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo</a> observatories from March 3 to 7, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giorgini2002_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Giorgini2002-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The studies showed that the asteroid has a mean diameter of 1.1 km, assuming that (29075) 1950 DA is a retrograde rotator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farnocchia2013_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farnocchia2013-10">[10]</a></sup> Optical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lightcurve" title="Lightcurve">lightcurve</a> analysis by Lenka Sarounova and <a href="/wiki/Petr_Pravec" title="Petr Pravec">Petr Pravec</a> shows that its rotation period is 2.1216 ± 0.0001 hours. Due to its short rotation period and high radar <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>, (29075) 1950 DA is thought to be fairly dense (more than 3.5 g/cm³) and likely composed of <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>–<a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Icarus_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Icarus-3">[3]</a></sup> In August 2014, scientists from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tennessee" title="University of Tennessee">University of Tennessee</a> determined that (29075) 1950 DA is a rubble pile that is kept together by <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">van der Waals forces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_Earth_impact">Possible Earth impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=(29075)_1950_DA&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Possible Earth impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>That (29075) 1950 DA has one of the best-determined asteroid orbital solutions is due to a combination of:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giorgini2002_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Giorgini2002-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>an orbit moderately <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">inclined</a> (12 degrees)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-1">[1]</a></sup> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecliptic_plane" title="Ecliptic plane">ecliptic plane</a> (reducing in-plane <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturbations</a>);</li>
<li>high-precision radar astrometry, which provides its distance and is complementary to visual-wavelength measurements of angular positions;</li>
<li>a 64-year <a href="/wiki/Observation_arc" title="Observation arc">observation arc</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>an uncertainty region controlled by <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">resonance</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Main-belt asteroid <a href="/wiki/78_Diana" title="78 Diana">78 Diana</a> (~125 km in diameter) will pass about 0.003 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> (450,000 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">km</a>; 280,000 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mi</a>) from (29075) 1950 DA on August 5, 2150.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giorgini2002_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Giorgini2002-8">[8]</a></sup> At that distance and size, Diana will perturb (29075) 1950 DA enough so that the change in trajectory is notable by 2880 (730 years later). In addition, over the intervening time, (29075) 1950 DA's rotation will cause its orbit to slightly change as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Yarkovsky_effect" title="Yarkovsky effect">Yarkovsky effect</a>. If (29075) 1950 DA continues on its present orbit, it may approach Earth on March 16, 2880, though the mean trajectory passes many millions of kilometers from Earth, so (29075) 1950 DA does not have a significant chance of impacting Earth. As of the 7 December 2015 solution, the probability of an impact in 2880 is 1 in 8,330 (0.012%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-impact-risk-summary_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-impact-risk-summary-12">[12]</a></sup> (A collision is likely to be ruled out as more data becomes available).</p>
<p>The energy released by a collision with an object the size of (29075) 1950 DA would cause major effects on the <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>, which would be devastating to <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a>. The discovery of the potential impact heightened interest in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroid_deflection_strategies" title="Asteroid deflection strategies">asteroid deflection strategies</a>.</p>
