<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Linear_visible_spectrum.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="115" data-file-width="605" height="67" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Linear_visible_spectrum.svg/350px-Linear_visible_spectrum.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Linear_visible_spectrum.svg/525px-Linear_visible_spectrum.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Linear_visible_spectrum.svg/700px-Linear_visible_spectrum.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Linear_visible_spectrum.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The notion of "green" in modern European languages corresponds to about 520–570 nm, but many historical and non-European languages make other choices, e.g. using a term for the range of ca. 450–530 nm ("blue/green") and another for ca. 530–590 nm ("green/yellow").</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Many languages do not distinguish between what in English are described as "blue" and "green". They instead use a cover term spanning both. When the issue is discussed in linguistics, this cover term is sometimes called <i>grue</i> in English.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The exact definition of "blue" and "green" may be complicated by the speakers not primarily distinguishing the hue, but using terms that describe other color components such as saturation and luminosity, or other properties of the object being described. For example, "blue" and "green" might be distinguished, but a single term might be used for both if the color is dark. Furthermore, green might be associated with yellow, and blue with black or gray.</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Brent_Berlin" title="Brent Berlin">Brent Berlin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kay" title="Paul Kay">Paul Kay</a>'s 1969 study <i><a href="/wiki/Basic_Color_Terms:_Their_Universality_and_Evolution" title="Basic Color Terms: Their Universality and Evolution">Basic Color Terms: Their Universality and Evolution</a></i>, distinct terms for <a href="/wiki/Brown" title="Brown">brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Purple" title="Purple">purple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pink" title="Pink">pink</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orange_(colour)" title="Orange (colour)">orange</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grey" title="Grey">grey</a> will not emerge in a language until the language has made a distinction between green and blue. In their account of the development of color terms the first terms to emerge are those for <a href="/wiki/White" title="White">white</a>/<a href="/wiki/Black" title="Black">black</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dark" title="Dark">dark</a>), <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a>/<a href="/wiki/Yellow" title="Yellow">yellow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Afro-Asiatic"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afro-Asiatic" title="Afro-Asiatic">Afro-Asiatic</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Afro-Asiatic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amazigh">Amazigh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Amazigh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The word for blue in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazigh" title="Amazigh">Amazigh</a> language is <i>Azerwal</i>. In some dialects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazigh" title="Amazigh">Amazigh</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Shilha_language" title="Shilha language">Shelha</a>, the word <i>Azegzaw</i> is used for both green and blue.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic">Arabic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Arabic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The color of the sky is sometimes referred to as "the green" in Classical Arabic poetry, in which al-khaḍrā’ (الخضراء). In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> the word for blue is generally <i>azraq</i> (أزرق). The Arabic word for green is <i>akhḍar</i> (أخضر).</p>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moroccan_Arabic" title="Moroccan Arabic">Moroccan Arabic</a>, the word for light blue is <i>šíbi</i>, whereas <i>zraq</i> (زرق) stands for blue and <i>khḍar</i> (خضر) for green. The word <i>zrag</i> (زرڭ) is used to describe the color of a suffocated person, and is also used pejoratively as synonym to "dumb".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egyptian">Egyptian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Egyptian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ancient <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_language" title="Egyptian language">Egyptian</a> word <i><a href="/wiki/Wadjet" title="Wadjet">wadjet</a></i> covers the range of blue, blue-green, and green. On the one hand, it was the name of a goddess, the patroness of Lower Egypt, represented as a cobra called Wadjet, "the green one," or as the <a href="/wiki/Eye_of_Horus" title="Eye of Horus">Eye of Horus</a>, also called by the same name. On the other hand, <i>wedjet</i> was the word used for <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_blue" title="Egyptian blue">Egyptian blue</a> in faience ceramics.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hebrew">Hebrew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hebrew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Hebrew, the word "כחול" (pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kaˈχol/</span>) means blue, while "ירוק" (pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/jaˈʁok/</span>) means green and has the same <a href="/wiki/Semitic_root" title="Semitic root">root</a>, י־ר־ק (j-r-q), as the word for vegetables (ירקות, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">jeʁaˈkot</span>). However, in classical Hebrew, ירוק can mean both green and yellow, giving rise to such expressions as ירוק כרתי, "leek green", to specify green to the exclusion of yellow. Like Russian and Italian, Hebrew has a separate name for light blue (תכלת, "<a href="/wiki/Tekhelet" title="Tekhelet">t'khelet</a>")—the color of the sky and of fringes on the ritual garment <i><a href="/wiki/Tzitzit" title="Tzitzit">Tzitzit</a></i>. This color has <a href="/wiki/Blue_in_Judaism" title="Blue in Judaism">special symbolic significance</a> in <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> and Jewish culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indo-European">Indo-European</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Indo-European">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>There are separate words for green (<i>zaļš</i>) and blue (<i>zils</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Latvian</a>. Both <i>zils</i> and <i>zaļš</i> stem from the same Proto-Indo-European word for yellow (<i>*ghel</i>). Several other words in Latvian have been derived from these colors, namely grass is called <i>zāle</i> (from <i>zaļš</i>), while the name for iris is <i>zīlīte</i> (from <i>zils</i>).</p>
<p>The now archaic word <i>mēļš</i> was used to describe both dark blue and black (probably indicating that previously <i>zils</i> was used only for lighter shades of blue). For instance, blueberries and blackberries are called <i>mellenes</i>.</p>
<p>In Latvian black is "melns" (in some local dialects "mells").</p>
<p>In Lithuanian <i>žalias</i> is green, <i>mėlynas</i> is blue and <i>žilas</i> is grey (hair), grizzled.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slavic">Slavic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Slavic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Slavic_language" title="South Slavic language">South Slavic language</a>, makes a clear distinction between blue (синьо, <i>sinyo</i>), green (зелено, <i>zeleno</i>), and black (черно, <i>cherno</i>).</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish language</a>, blue (<i>niebieski</i>) and green (<i>zielony</i>) are treated as separate colors. The word for <a href="/wiki/Sky_blue" title="Sky blue">sky blue</a> or <a href="/wiki/Azure_(color)" title="Azure (color)">azure</a>—<i>błękitny</i>—might be considered either a basic color or a <a href="/wiki/Shades_of_blue" title="Shades of blue">shade of blue</a> by different speakers. Similarly dark blue or <a href="/wiki/Navy_blue" title="Navy blue">navy blue</a> (<i>granatowy</i>—deriving from the name of <a href="/wiki/Pomegranate" title="Pomegranate">pomegranate</a> (granat), some <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> of which are dark purplish blue in color) can be considered by some speakers as a separate basic color. Black (<i>czarny</i>) is completely distinguished from blue. As in English, Polish distinguishes pink ("różowy") from red ("czerwony").</p>
<p>The word <i>siwy</i> means blue-gray in Polish (literally it means the color of gray hair). The word <i>siny</i> refers to violet-blue and is used to describe the color of bruises ("siniaki"), <a href="/wiki/Hematoma" title="Hematoma">hematoma</a>, and the blue skin discoloration that can result from moderate <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">hypothermia</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> does not have a single word referring to the whole range of colors denoted by the English term "blue". Instead, it traditionally treats light blue (голубой, <i>goluboy</i>) as a separate color independent from plain or dark blue (синий, <i>siniy</i>), with all seven "basic" colors of the spectrum (red–orange–yellow–green–<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%B3%D0%BE%D0%BB%D1%83%D0%B1%D0%BE%D0%B9#Russian" title="wikt:голубой">голубой</a> / <i>goluboy</i> (sky blue, light azure, but <i>does not equal</i> <a href="/wiki/Cyan" title="Cyan">cyan</a>)–<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%81%D0%B8%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%B9#Russian" title="wikt:синий">синий</a> / <i>siniy</i> ("true" deep <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a>, like synthetic <a href="/wiki/Ultramarine" title="Ultramarine">ultramarine</a>)–violet) while in English the light blues like azure and cyan are considered mere shades of "blue" and not different colors. To better understand this, consider that English makes a similar distinction between "<a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a>" and light red (<a href="/wiki/Pink" title="Pink">pink</a>, which is considered a different color and not merely a kind of red), but such a distinction is unknown in several other languages; for example, both "red" (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">红</span> / <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">紅</span>, <i>hóng</i>) and "pink" (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">粉红</span>, <i>fěn hóng</i>, lit. "powder red") have traditionally been considered varieties of a single color in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>. The Russian language also distinguishes between red (красный, krasniy) and pink (розовый, rozoviy).</p>
<p>Similarly English descriptions of rainbows have often distinguished between blue or <a href="/wiki/Turquoise_(color)" title="Turquoise (color)">turquoise</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Indigo" title="Indigo">indigo</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the latter of which is often described as dark blue or <a href="/wiki/Ultramarine" title="Ultramarine">ultramarine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">Serbo-Croatian</a> color system makes a distinction between blue, green and black:</p>
<ul>
<li>Blue: <i>plava</i> (indicates any blue)
<ul>
<li>Navy blue: <i>teget</i></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Green: <i>zelena</i></li>
<li>Black: <i>crna</i></li>
</ul>
<p>Shades are defined with a prefix (e.g. "tamno-" for dark, or "svetlo-" for light), for example, dark blue = "tamnoplava". "Sinje", analogue to Bulgarian синьо, <i>sinyo</i>/Russian синий, <i>siniy</i>, is archaic, and denotes blue-gray, usually used to describe dark skies or seas. Turquoise is usually described as "tirkizna", and similarly, azure will use a loan word "azurna". There is no specific word for cyan. Blond hair is called <i>plava</i> ('blue'), reflecting likely the archaic use of "plav" for any bright white/blue colors (like the sky).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Celtic">Celtic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Celtic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> word <i>glas</i> is usually translated as "blue"; however, it can also refer, variously, to the color of the sea, of grass, or of silver. The word <i>gwyrdd</i> (a borrowing from Latin <i>viridis</i>) is the standard translation for "green". In traditional <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> (and related languages), <i>glas</i> could refer to certain shades of green and grey as well as blue, and <i>llwyd</i> could refer to various shades of grey and brown; however, modern Welsh is tending toward the <a href="/wiki/Color_term" title="Color term">11-color Western scheme</a>, restricting <i>glas</i> to blue and using <i>gwyrdd</i> for green, <i>llwyd</i> for grey and <i>brown</i> for brown.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Modern Irish</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a> the word for "blue" is <i>gorm</i> (whence the name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cairngorm_mountains" title="Cairngorm mountains">Cairngorm mountains</a> derives) – a borrowing of the now obsolete Early Welsh word <i>gwrm</i>, meaning "dark blue" or "dusky". A relic of the original meaning ("dusky") survives in the Irish term <i>daoine gorma</i>, meaning "<a href="/wiki/Black_people" title="Black people">Black people</a>".</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_Irish" title="Middle Irish">Middle Irish</a>, like in Welsh, <i>glas</i> was a blanket term for colors ranging from green to blue to various shades of grey (e.g. the <i>glas</i> of a sword, the <i>glas</i> of stone, etc.). In Modern Irish, it has come to mean various shades of green, with specific reference to plant hues, and grey (like the sea); other shades of green<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (January 2015)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> would be referred to in Modern Irish as <i>uaine</i> or <i>uaithne</i>, while <i>liath</i> is grey proper (like a stone).</p>
<p>Scottish Gaelic uses the term <i>uaine</i> for "green". However, the dividing line between it and <i>gorm</i> is somewhat different than between the English "green" and "blue", with <i>uaine</i> signifying a light green or yellow-green, and <i>gorm</i> extending from dark blue (what in English might be navy blue) to include the dark green or blue-green of vegetation. Grass, for instance, is <i>gorm</i>, rather than <i>uaine</i>. In addition, <i>liath</i> covers a range from light blue to light grey. However, the term for a green apple, such as a Granny Smith, would be <i>ubhal glas</i>.</p>
<p>The boundary between colors varies much more than the "focal point": e.g. an island known in <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a> as <i>Enez glas ("the blue island")</i> is called in French <i>l'Île Verte ("the green island")</i>, in both cases referring to the greyish-green color of its bushes, even though both languages distinguish green (as in lawn grass) from blue (as in a cloudless midday sky).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romance">Romance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Romance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Romance terms for "green" (Catalan <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verd#Catalan" title="wikt:verd">verd</a></span></i>, French <i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vert#French" title="wikt:vert">vert</a></span></i>, Italian, Portuguese, Romanian and Spanish <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verde" title="wikt:verde">verde</a></i>) are all from Latin <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/viridis#Latin" title="wikt:viridis">viridis</a></span></i>. The terms for "blue", on the other hand, vary: Catalan <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blau#Catalan" title="wikt:blau">blau</a></span></i>, Occitan <i><span lang="oc" xml:lang="oc"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blau#Occitan" title="wikt:blau">blau</a></span></i> and French <i><span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bleu#French" title="wikt:bleu">bleu</a></span></i> are from Germanic; French <i>bleu</i> was in turn loaned into many other languages, including English.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, as most Romance languages, makes roughly the same distinctions as English and has a specific term for each of blue ("<span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bleu#French" title="wikt:bleu">bleu</a></span>"), green ("<span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vert#French" title="wikt:vert">vert</a></span>") and grey ("<span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gris#French" title="wikt:gris">gris</a></span>"). For all three, different shades can be indicated with different (compound) terms, none of them being considered as basic color terms: "bleu <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/clair#French" title="wikt:clair">clair</a></span>" (light blue), "bleu <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ciel#French" title="wikt:ciel">ciel</a></span>" (sky blue), "bleu <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/marine#French" title="wikt:marine">marine</a></span>" (Navy blue), "bleu <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/roi#French" title="wikt:roi">roi</a></span>" (royal blue); "vert clair" (light green), "vert <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pomme#French" title="wikt:pomme">pomme</a></span>" (literally: apple green); "gris <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anthracite#French" title="wikt:anthracite">anthracite</a></span>" (very dark grey), "gris <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/souris#French" title="wikt:souris">souris</a></span>" (literally: mouse grey).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a> distinguishes blue (<i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blau#Catalan" title="wikt:blau">blau</a></span></i>) from green (<i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verd#Catalan" title="wikt:verd">verd</a></span></i>) and grey (<i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gris#Catalan" title="wikt:gris">gris</a></span></i>). Other basic or common colors by its own right are <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/porpra#Catalan" title="wikt:porpra">porpra</a></span></i> "purple", <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/groc#Catalan" title="wikt:groc">groc</a></span></i> "yellow", <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/taronja#Catalan" title="wikt:taronja">taronja</a></span></i> "orange", <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vermell#Catalan" title="wikt:vermell">vermell</a></span></i> "red", <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rosa#Catalan" title="wikt:rosa">rosa</a></span></i> "pink", <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/marr%C3%B3#Catalan" title="wikt:marró">marró</a></span></i> "brown", <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gris#Catalan" title="wikt:gris">gris</a></span></i> "grey", <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/negre#Catalan" title="wikt:negre">negre</a></span></i> "black" and <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blanc#Catalan" title="wikt:blanc">blanc</a></span></i> "white". For all these colors except black and white it is possible to indicate different shades using <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/clar#Catalan" title="wikt:clar">clar</a></span></i> "light" and <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fosc#Catalan" title="wikt:fosc">fosc</a></span></i> "dark"; for blue, though, it generally is <i>blau cel</i> "sky blue" and <i>blau marí</i> "sea blue". Other words and compounds are common to indicate more elaborated shades (<i>verd llimona</i> "lemon green", <i>rosa pàlid</i> "pale pink", <i>lila</i> "lilac", <i>granat</i> "carmine", <i>ocre</i> "ochre", <i>verd oliva</i> "olive green", etc.). Catalan actually distinguishes two reds with different and common words: while <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vermell#Catalan" title="wikt:vermell">vermell</a></span></i> refers to the color of arterial blood, <i><span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/roig#Catalan" title="wikt:roig">roig</a></span></i> is a red tending towards yellow or the color of clay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> distinguishes blue (<i><span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blu#Italian" title="wikt:blu">blu</a></span></i>) and green (<i><span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verde#Italian" title="wikt:verde">verde</a></span></i>). There are also two words for light blue (e.g. sky's color): <i><span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azzurro#Italian" title="wikt:azzurro">azzurro</a></span></i> and <i><span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/celeste#Italian" title="wikt:celeste">celeste</a></span></i>. <i><span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azzurro#Italian" title="wikt:azzurro">Azzurro</a></span></i>, the English equivalent of <a href="/wiki/Azure_(color)" title="Azure (color)">azure</a>, is not considered to be a shade of <i>blu,</i> but rather the opposite, i.e. <i>blu</i> is a darker shade of <i>azzurro</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <i>Celeste</i> literally means '(the color) of the sky' and is often used as synonym of <i>azzurro</i>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> although it may be considered a lighter color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> To indicate a mix of green and <i>celeste</i>, Italians say <i>verde <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/acqua#Italian" title="wikt:acqua">acqua</a></span></i>, literally <i>water green</i> or <i><span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/acquamarina#Italian" title="wikt:acquamarina">acquamarina</a></span></i> (aquamarine), or <i><span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/glauco#Italian" title="wikt:glauco">glauco</a></span></i>, which is also used to indicate a mix of green and gray in plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, the word "<span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azul#Portuguese" title="wikt:azul">azul</a></span>" means blue and the word "<span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verde#Portuguese" title="wikt:verde">verde</a></span>" means green. Furthermore, "azul-<span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/claro#Portuguese" title="wikt:claro">claro</a></span>" means light-blue, and "azul-<span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/escuro#Portuguese" title="wikt:escuro">escuro</a></span>" means dark-blue. More distinctions can be made between several hues of blue. For instance, "azul-<span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/celeste#Portuguese" title="wikt:celeste">celeste</a></span>" means sky blue, "azul-<span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/marinho#Portuguese" title="wikt:marinho">marinho</a></span>" means navy-blue and "azul-<span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/turquesa#Portuguese" title="wikt:turquesa">turquesa</a></span>" means turquoise-blue. One can also make the distinction between "verde-claro" and "verde-escuro", meaning light and dark-green respectively, and more distinctions between several qualities of green: for instance, "verde-<span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azeitona#Portuguese" title="wikt:azeitona">azeitona</a></span>" means olive-green and "verde-<span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/esmeralda#Portuguese" title="wikt:esmeralda">esmeralda</a></span>" means emerald-green. <a href="/wiki/Cyan" title="Cyan">Cyan</a> is usually called "azul-celeste" (sky blue) or "azul-<span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/piscina#Portuguese" title="wikt:piscina">piscina</a></span>", meaning pool blue, but also less commonly "<span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ciano#Portuguese" title="wikt:ciano">ciano</a></span>", and "verde-<span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%C3%A1gua#Portuguese" title="wikt:água">água</a></span>", meaning water green.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a> clearly distinguishes between the colors green (<i><span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verde#Romanian" title="wikt:verde">verde</a></span></i>) and blue (<i><span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/albastru#Romanian" title="wikt:albastru">albastru</a></span></i>). It also uses separate words for different hues of the same color, e.g. light blue (<i><span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bleu#Romanian" title="wikt:bleu">bleu</a></span></i>), blue (<i>albastru</i>), dark-blue (<i>bleu-<span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/marin#Romanian" title="wikt:marin">marin</a></span></i> or <i><span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bleomarin#Romanian" title="wikt:bleomarin">bleomarin</a></span></i>), along with a word for turquoise (<i><span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/turcoaz#Romanian" title="wikt:turcoaz">turcoaz</a></span></i>) and azure (<i><span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azur#Romanian" title="wikt:azur">azur</a></span></i> or <i><span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azuriu#Romanian" title="wikt:azuriu">azuriu</a></span></i>).</p>
<p>Similarly to French, Romanian, Italian and Portuguese, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> distinguishes blue (<i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azul#Spanish" title="wikt:azul">azul</a></span></i>) and green (<i><span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verde#Spanish" title="wikt:verde">verde</a></span></i>) and has an additional term for the tone of blue visible in the sky, namely "<span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/celeste#Spanish" title="wikt:celeste">celeste</a></span>", which is nonetheless considered a shade of blue.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germanic">Germanic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Germanic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a>, the word <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bl%C3%A1r" title="wikt:blár">blár</a></i> "blue" (from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">proto-Germanic</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bl%C4%93waz" title="wikt:blēwaz">blēwaz</a>) was also used to describe <a href="/wiki/Black" title="Black">black</a> (and the common word for people of African descent was thus <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bl%C3%A1menn" title="wikt:blámenn">blámenn</a></i> 'blue/black men'). In <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>, <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bl%C3%A5" title="wikt:blå">blå</a></i>, the modern word for blue, was used this way until the early 20th century, and it still is to a limited extent in modern <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">Faroese</a>.</p>
<p>German and Dutch distinguish blue (respectively <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blau" title="wikt:blau">blau</a></i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blauw" title="wikt:blauw">blauw</a></i>) and green (<i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gr%C3%BCn" title="wikt:grün">grün</a></i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/groen" title="wikt:groen">groen</a></i>) very similarly to English. There are (compound) terms for light blue (<i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hellblau" title="wikt:hellblau">hellblau</a></i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lichtblauw" title="wikt:lichtblauw">lichtblauw</a></i>) and darker shades of blue (<i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dunkelblau" title="wikt:dunkelblau">dunkelblau</a></i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/donkerblauw" title="wikt:donkerblauw">donkerblauw</a></i>). In addition, adjective forms of most traditional color names are inflected to match the corresponding noun's case and gender. A number of "modern" color names (such as <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rosa" title="wikt:rosa">rosa</a></i>, meaning 'pink' or 'rose') are not inflected; instead, in Standard German, it is necessary to add the suffix <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/farben" title="wikt:farben">farben</a></i> or <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/farbig" title="wikt:farbig">farbig</a></i> (colored), and inflect the result (for example: <i>ein rosafarbenes Auto</i>, lit. 'a pink-colored car'). This, however, is often disregarded in colloquial speech, resulting in forms like <i>ein rosanes Auto</i> or simply <i>ein rosa Auto</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek">Greek</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Greek">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The terms for "blue" and "green" have changed completely in the transition from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> to <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">Modern Greek</a>. Ancient Greek had γλαυκός (<i>glaukos</i>) "clear light blue", contrasting with χλωρός (<i>chloros</i>) "bright green" (cf. words such as chlorophyll). κυανός (which turned into "cyan" in English) meant either a "dark blue substance" or just "blue". Modern Greek has πράσινο (<i>prásino</i>) for green and γαλάζιο (<i>galázio</i>) or θαλασσί (<i>thalassí</i>, 'sea colored') for light blue/sea blue. The recent loan μπλε (<i>ble</i>, French <i>bleu</i>) is used for blue.</p>
<p>In the Modern Greek language, there are additional names for light and dark blues/greens:</p>
<ol>
<li>τυρκουάζ (<i>tyrkouáz</i>) for turquoise</li>
<li>κυανό (<i>kyanó</i>) for azure</li>
<li>λαχανί (<i>lachaní</i> 'cabbage colored') for lime green</li>
<li>λαδί (<i>ladí</i>) for olive and</li>
<li>χακί (<i>chakí</i>) for dark <a href="/wiki/Khaki" title="Khaki">khaki</a></li>
<li>κυπαρισσί (<i>kyparissí</i> 'cypress colored') for brownish green.</li>
</ol>
<p>As a rule, the first two words of the list are accepted as shades of blue, and the rest as shades of green. Also βιολέ (<i>violé</i>) / βιολετί (<i>violetí</i>) for violet blue (which is, however, usually considered as a shade of purple, rather than blue).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iranian">Iranian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Iranian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ossetian_language" title="Ossetian language">Ossetian</a> has one word for blue, light blue and green—цъæх (tsəh), which also means "grey" and "glaucous"—but it also has a separate word for green, кæрдæгхуыз (kərdəghuɨz), literally "grassy" (from кæрдæг "grass"). The latter derives from кæрдын (kərdɨn) "to mow" (like in German <i>Heu</i> (<i>hau</i>) &lt; <i>hauen</i> (to mow)).</p>
<p>Ossetian also has separate words for these colors:</p>
<ul>
<li>light blue: æрвхуыз (ərvhuɨz) from æрвон "sky"</li>
<li>glaucous: бæлонхуыз (bəlonhuɨz) from бæлон "pigeon" (a calque from Russian, cf. голубой (light blue) &lt; голубь (pigeon)); also фæздæгхуыз (fəzdəghuɨz) from <i>fazdag</i> "smoke", from Old Persian *pazdaka-, cognate of Latin <i>pedis</i> (louse) and Russian (obscene) <i>пизда</i> (cunt), because of its stinking.</li>
<li>blue: копрадзхуыз (kopradzhuɨz), from копрадз (kopradz) - bluing for laundering, transliteration of Russian купорос "vitriol" from Latin <i>cuprum</i> "copper"</li>
<li>grey: фæныкхуыз (fənykhuɨz), from фæнык "ashes", originating from Persian *pa(s)nu-ka, cognate or Russian песок "sand"</li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pashto" title="Pashto">Pashto</a> uses the word <i>shīn</i> to denote blue as well as green. <i>Shinkay</i>, a word derived from <i>shīn</i>, means 'greenery' but <i>shīn āsmān</i> means 'blue sky'. When there is ambiguity, it is common to ask (as in Vietnamese), "<i>Shīn</i> like the sky? Or <i>shīn</i> like plants?"</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> words for blue include <span lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">آبی</span> <i>ābi</i> (literally the color of water, from <i>āb</i> 'water'), for blue generally; <span lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">نیلی</span> <i>nili</i> (from <i>nil</i>, '<a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo dye</a>'), for deeper shades of blue such as the color of rain clouds; <span lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">فیروزه</span> <i>fayruzeh</i> '<a href="/wiki/Turquoise" title="Turquoise">turquoise</a> stone', used to describe the color of blue eyes; <span lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">لاجوردی</span> <i>lājvardi</i> or <span lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">لاژوردی</span> <i>lāzhvardi</i> '<a href="/wiki/Lapis_lazuli" title="Lapis lazuli">lapis lazuli</a> color', source of the words <i>lazuli</i> and <i>azure</i>; <span lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">نیلوفری</span> <i>nilufari</i> '<a href="/wiki/Nymphaeaceae" title="Nymphaeaceae">water lily</a> color'; and <span lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">کبود</span> <i>kabud</i>, an old literary word for 'blue'.</p>
<p>The Persian word for green is <span lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">سبز</span> <i>sabz</i>. As in <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, dark-skinned people may be described as "green".</p>
<p>The color of the sky is variously described in <a href="/wiki/Persian_literature#Poetry" title="Persian literature">Persian poetry</a> using the words <i>sabz</i>, <i>fayruzeh</i>, <i>nil</i>, <i>lājvardi</i>, or <i>nilufari</i>— literally "green", "indigo", "turquoise", "azure" or "the color of water lilies". For example, <i>sabz-ākhor</i> "green stable", <i>sabz-āshyāneh</i> "green ceiling", <i>sabz-ayvān</i> "green balcony", <i>sabz-bādbān</i> "green sail", <i>sabz-bāgh</i> "green garden", <i>sabz-farsh</i> "green carpet", <i>sabz-golshan</i> "green flower-garden", <i>sabz-kārgāh</i> "green workshop", <i>sabz-kh</i><sup><i>v</i></sup><i>ān</i> "green table", <i>sabz-manzareh</i> "green panorama", <i>sabz-maydān</i> "green field" <i>sabz-pol</i> "green bridge", <i>sabz-tāq</i> "green arch", <i>sabz-tasht</i> "green bowl", and <i>sabz-tā’us</i> "green peacock" are poetic epithets for the sky—in addition to similar compounds using the words for blue, e.g. <i>lājvardi-saqf</i> "lapis lazuli-colored roof" or <i>fayruzeh-tasht</i> "turquoise bowl". Moreover, the words for green of Arabic origin <span lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">اخضر</span> <i>akhzar</i> and <span lang="fa" xml:lang="fa">خضرا</span> <i>khazrā</i> are used for epithets of the sky or heaven, such as <i>charkh-e akhzar</i> "green wheel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indo-Aryan">Indo-Aryan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Indo-Aryan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">Indo-Aryan languages</a> distinguish blue from green. In Urdu, blue is <i>neela</i> and green is <i>sabz</i>. There are some names of shades of blue as well, like <i>ferozi</i>. In Hindi, blue is <i>neela</i> and green is <i>hara</i>. In Marathi, blue is निळा (<i>niḽā</i>) and green is हिरवा (<i>hiravā</i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basque">Basque</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Basque">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the Basque language there are three native color words derived from "ur" (water). <i>Urdin</i>, is nowadays used in most cases for blue. "Ubel", originally meant flash flood and, with respect to colors, is often used in relation to bruises. "Begi ubela" would be translated into English by "a black and blue eye". But in Basque, unlike English, ubel remains in use after the hit skin has lost its purple color and become pale, why this word is used for both purple in particular and pale hue in general. "Uher", originally meant dirty, still water or rusty and is used for gray or sienna tones or impure, dark color in general. Green is usually expressed with the loan-word <i>berde</i> from Spanish "verde"/ French "vert". The authenticity of the less common Basque terms for green <i>(h)orlegi</i> and <i>musker</i> is disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uralic">Uralic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Uralic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finnish">Finnish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Finnish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> makes a distinction between <i>vihreä</i> (green) and <i>sininen</i> (blue). <a href="/wiki/Turquoise_(color)" title="Turquoise (color)">Turquoise</a> or <a href="/wiki/Teal" title="Teal">teal</a> (<i>turkoosi</i> or <i>sinivihreä</i>) is considered to be a separate, intermediate color between green and blue, and black (<i>musta</i>) is also differentiated from blue.</p>
<p>The name for blue, <i>sininen</i>, is shared with other <a href="/wiki/Finnic_languages" title="Finnic languages">Finnic languages</a> and is thus dated to the era of the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Finnic_language" title="Proto-Finnic language">Proto-Finnic language</a> (ca. 5000 years old). However, it is also shared with the unrelated Russian language (синий, <i>siniy</i>), suggesting that it is a loanword. (Alternatively, the Russian word синий could represent Finnic <a href="/wiki/Stratum_(linguistics)#Substratum" title="Stratum (linguistics)">substratum</a>). The word <i>vihreä</i> (<i>viher-</i>, archaic <i>viheriä</i>, <i>viheriäinen</i>) is related to <i>vehreä</i> "verdant" and <i>vihanta</i> "green", and <i>viha</i> "hate", originally "poison". It is not shared with Estonian, in which it is <i>roheline</i>, probably related with the Estonian word <i>rohi</i> "grass". However, the form <i>viha</i> does have correspondences in related languages as far as <a href="/wiki/Permic_languages" title="Permic languages">Permic languages</a>, where it means not only "poison" but "bile" or "green or yellow". It has been originally loaned from an Indo-Iranian protolanguage and is related to Latin <i>virus</i> "poison". Furthermore, the word <i>musta</i> "black" is also of Finnic origin.</p>
<p>The differentiation of several colors by <a href="/wiki/Hue" title="Hue">hue</a> is at least <a href="/wiki/Finnic_languages" title="Finnic languages">Finnic</a> (a major subgroup of Uralic) in origin. Before this, only red (<i>punainen</i>) was clearly distinguished by hue, with other colors described in terms of brightness (<i>valkea</i> vs. <i>musta</i>), using non-color adjectives for further specificity. Alternatively, it appears that the distinction between <i>valkea</i> and <i>musta</i> was in fact "clean, shining" vs. "dirty, murky". The original meaning of <i>sini</i> was possibly either "black/dark" or "green". Mauno Koski's theory is that dark colors of high saturation—both blue and green—would be <i>sini</i>, while shades of color with low saturation, such as dark brown or black, would be <i>musta</i>. Although it is theorized that originally <i>vihreä</i> was not a true color name and was used to describe plants only, the occurrence of <i>vihreä</i> or <i>viha</i> as a name of a color in several related languages shows that it was probably <a href="/wiki/Polysemy" title="Polysemy">polysemic</a> (meaning both "green" and "verdant") already in early Baltic-Finnic. However, whatever the case with these theories, differentiation of blue and green must be at least as old as the Baltic-Finnic languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungarian">Hungarian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Hungarian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a> makes the distinction between green (<i>zöld</i>) and blue (<i>kék</i>), and also distinguishes black (<i>fekete</i>). Intermediate colors between green and blue are commonly referred to as <i>zöldeskék</i> (literally greenish-blue) or <i>kékeszöld</i> (bluish-green), but names for specific colors in this continuum—like turquoise (<i>türkiz</i>)—also exist. Particular shades of a color can also have separate names, such as azure (<i>azúr</i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkic">Turkic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Turkic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kazakh_language" title="Kazakh language">Kazakh language</a>, like many <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic languages</a>, distinguishes between <i>kök</i> for blue and <i>jasâl</i> for green. In Kazakh, many adjectival variations can be found referring to perceived gradations in saturation level of "blue", such as <i>kögildir</i>, <i>kökshil</i>, and <i>kökboz</i>, which respectively denominate the gradual decrease in the intensity, <i>kökboz</i> being often used as a color referent in its own right. Interestingly, <i>kök</i> is occasionally used to denote green plants (e.g. 'kök' shöp'), but such usage is mostly confined to poetic utterances or certain localized dialects.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> treats dark or navy blue (<i>lacivert</i>, from the same Persian root as English <i>azure</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Lapis_lazuli" title="Lapis lazuli">lapis lazuli</a></i>) as a separate color from plain or light blue (<i>mavi</i>). <i>Mavi</i> is derived from the Arabic word مائي <i>mā’ī</i> 'like water' (ماء <i>mā’</i> being the Arabic word for water) and <i>lacivert</i> is derived from Persian لاجورد <i>lājvard</i> '<a href="/wiki/Lapis_lazuli" title="Lapis lazuli">lapis lazuli</a>', a semiprecious stone with the color of navy blue. In the <a href="/wiki/Tengrism" title="Tengrism">pre-Islamic religion of the Turks</a>, blue is the color that represented the east, as well as the zodiac sign Aquarius (the Water Bearer). A characteristic tone of blue, <a href="/wiki/Turquoise_(color)" title="Turquoise (color)">turquoise</a>, was much used by the Turks for their traditional decorations and jewelry.</p>
<p>In traditional pre-Islamic Turkic culture, both blue and green were represented by the same name, <i>gök</i> 'sky'. The name is still in use in many rural areas. For instance, in many regions of Turkey, when mold is formed on cheese, the phenomenon is called <i>gögermek</i> 'turning into the color of <i>gök</i>/sky'.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dravidian">Dravidian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Dravidian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil language</a> clearly distinguishes between the colors <span lang="ta" xml:lang="ta">பச்சை</span> green (<i>paccai</i>), <span lang="ta" xml:lang="ta">நீலம்</span> blue (<i>neelam</i>) and <span lang="ta" xml:lang="ta">கருப்பு</span> black (<i>karuppu</i>). The prefix <i>karu-</i> would indicate dark colors while the suffix <i>iḷam</i> would indicate light colors. Thus <span lang="ta" xml:lang="ta">கரும்பச்சை</span> <i>karumpaccai</i> would be dark green.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu language</a> uses a single word, <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu</a>: <span lang="te" xml:lang="te">పచ్చ</span> <i>pacca</i>, for green and yellow. To differentiate between the two shades, another word is prefixed in some cases. For example, green will be called <span lang="te" xml:lang="te">ఆకుపచ్చ</span> <i>ākupacca</i> "leaf-<i>pacca</i>" and yellow <span lang="te" xml:lang="te">పసుపుపచ్చ</span> <i>pasupupacca</i> "<a href="/wiki/Turmeric" title="Turmeric">turmeric</a>-<i>pacca</i>"</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asian_languages">East Asian languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: East Asian languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese">Chinese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Chinese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The modern <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese language</a> has the blue–green distinction (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%93%9D" title="wikt:蓝">蓝</a>/ <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%97%8D" title="wikt:藍">藍</a> <i>lán</i> for blue and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%BB%BF" title="wikt:绿">绿</a> / <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%B6%A0" title="wikt:綠">綠</a> <i>lǜ</i> for green); however, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%E9%9D%92" title="青">another word</a> that predates the modern vernacular, <i>qīng</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92" title="wikt:青">青</a></span>), is also used. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">character</a> depicts the budding of a young plant and it could be understood as "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verdant" title="wikt:verdant">verdant</a>", but the word is used to describe colors ranging from light and yellowish green through deep blue all the way to black, as in <i>xuánqīng</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%8E%84%E9%9D%92" title="wikt:玄青">玄青</a></span>). For example, the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Republic_of_China" title="Flag of the Republic of China">Flag of the Republic of China</a> is today still referred to as <i>qīng tiān, bái rì, mǎn dì hóng</i> ("'Blue' Sky, White Sun, Whole Ground Red"—<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92" title="wikt:青">青</a>天，白日，滿地紅</span>); whereas <i>qīngcài</i> (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92%E8%8F%9C" title="wikt:青菜">青菜</a></span>) is the Chinese word for "green <a href="/wiki/Bok_choy" title="Bok choy">bok choy</a>". A <a href="/wiki/Cucumber" title="Cucumber">cucumber</a> is known as either huángguā (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%BB%83%E7%93%9C" title="wikt:黃瓜">黃瓜</a></span>) "yellow melon" while (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92%E7%93%9C" title="wikt:青瓜">青瓜</a></span>) meaning "green melon" is more commonly used in Cantonese. <i>Qīng</i> 青 was the traditional designation of both blue and green for much of the history of the Chinese language, while 蓝 <i>lán</i> and 绿 <i>lǜ</i> were introduced relatively more recently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup>, as a part of the adoption of modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernacular_Chinese" title="Vernacular Chinese">Vernacular Chinese</a> as the social norm, replacing <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">Classical Chinese</a>. The two forms can also be encountered combined as 青蓝 and 青绿, with 青 being used as an <a href="/wiki/Intensifier" title="Intensifier">intensifier</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese">Japanese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Japanese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:278px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arrows_in_Okazaki_edit.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="158" data-file-width="276" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/af/Arrows_in_Okazaki_edit.jpeg" width="276"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

A billboard in <a href="/wiki/Okazaki,_Aichi" title="Okazaki, Aichi">Okazaki, Aichi</a>, depicting <a href="/wiki/Traffic_light" title="Traffic light">traffic lights</a> with a blue, rather than green, 'go' signal. The distinction of blue and green (<i><a href="/wiki/Ao_(color)" title="Ao (color)">ao</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92" title="wikt:青">青</a></span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>) in Japanese is fluid enough to allow these variances in the depiction of the color.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The Japanese word <i><a href="/wiki/Ao_(color)" title="Ao (color)">ao</a></i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92" title="wikt:青">青</a></span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">n.</a>, <i>aoi</i> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92" title="wikt:青">青</a>い</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adj.</a>)</span>)</span>, the same <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> character as the Chinese <i>qīng</i>, can refer to either blue or green depending on the situation. Modern <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> has a word for green <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%B7%91" title="wikt:緑">緑</a></span><span class="t_nihongo_comma" style="display:none">,</span> <i>midori</i><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>)</span>, but it is a relatively recent usage. Ancient Japanese did not have this distinction: the word <i>midori</i> only came into use in the <a href="/wiki/Heian_period" title="Heian period">Heian period</a>, and at that time (and for a long time thereafter) <i>midori</i> was still considered a shade of <i>ao</i>. Educational materials distinguishing green and blue only came into use after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> thus, even though most Japanese consider them to be green, the word <i>ao</i> is still used to describe certain <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apples</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">vegetation</a>. <i>Ao</i> is also the word used to refer to the color on a <a href="/wiki/Traffic_light" title="Traffic light">traffic light</a> that signals one to "go". However, most other objects—a green <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">car</a>, a green <a href="/wiki/Sweater" title="Sweater">sweater</a>, and so forth—will generally be called <i>midori</i>. Japanese people also sometimes use the word <i>gurīn</i> <span style="font-weight:normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">グリーン</span><span class="t_nihongo_help"><sup><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span style="color:#00e; font:bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration:none; padding:0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup></span>)</span>, based on the English word "green", for colors. The language also has several other words meaning specific shades of green and blue.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korean">Korean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Korean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The native <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a> word <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">푸르다</span> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization" title="Revised Romanization">Revised Romanization</a>: <i>pureu-da</i> <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adj.</a>) may mean either blue or green, or bluish green. This word <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">푸르다</span> is used as in <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">푸른 하늘</span> (<i>pureun haneul</i>, blue sky) for blue or as in <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">푸른 숲</span> (<i>pureun sup</i>, green forest) for green. Distinct words for blue and green are also used; <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">파란</span> (<i>paran</i> <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adj.</a>), <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">파란색</span>/<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">파랑</span> (<i>paransaek</i>/<i>parang</i> <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">n.</a>) for blue, <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">초록</span> (<i>chorok</i> <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adj.</a>/<a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">n.</a>), <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">초록색</span> (<i>choroksaek</i> <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">n.</a> or for short, <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">녹색</span> <i>noksaek</i> <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">n.</a>) for green. However, in the case of a traffic light, <i>paran</i> is used for the green light meaning go, even though the word is typically used to mean blue. <i>Cheong</i> <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">청</span> is also used for both blue and green. It is a loan from Chinese (<span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%91" title="wikt:靑">靑</a></span>, <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i>qing</i>) and is used in the proper name Cheong Wa Dae (<span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">청와대</span> or <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%91" title="wikt:靑">靑</a>瓦臺</span>), the <a href="/wiki/Blue_House" title="Blue House">Blue House</a>, which is the executive office and official residence of the President of the Republic of Korea.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tibetan">Tibetan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Tibetan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Classical_Tibetan" title="Classical Tibetan">Tibetan</a>, སྔོན་པོ། (<a href="/wiki/Wylie_transliteration" title="Wylie transliteration">Wylie</a> sngon po) is the term traditionally given for the color of the sky and of grass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This term also falls into the general pattern of naming colors by appending the suffix "po", as in "mar po" (red); "ser po" (yellow); "nag po" (black); and "dkar po" (white). Conspicuously, the term for "green" is "ljang khu", likely related to "ljang bu," and defined as—"the 'blue' (sngon po) sprout of wheat or barley."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnamese">Vietnamese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Vietnamese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a> usually does not use separate words for green and refers to that color using a word that can also refer to <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a>. In Vietnamese, blue and green are denoted by <i>xanh</i> (青). This is a colloquial rendering of <i>thanh</i> (青), as with Chinese and Japanese. Blue is specifically described as <i>xanh</i>, as in the skin of the sky (<i>xanh da trời</i>) or "<i>xanh</i>, as in the ocean" (<i>xanh dương, xanh nước biển</i>) and green as "<i>xanh</i>, as in the leaves" (<i>xanh lá cây</i>).</p>
<p>Modern Vietnamese occasionally does employ the terms <i>xanh lam</i> and <i>xanh lục</i> (in which the second syllables derive from the Chinese: <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%97%8D" title="wikt:藍">藍</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%B6%A0" title="wikt:綠">綠</a>) for <i>blue</i> and <i>green</i>, respectively.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mongolian">Mongolian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Mongolian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">Mongolian</a>, the word for green is ногоон (<i>nogoon</i>). Mongolian distinguishes between dark and light blue. The word for light blue is цэнхэр (<i>tsenher</i>) and the word for dark blue is хѳх (<i>höh</i>).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Austronesian_languages">Austronesian languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Austronesian languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filipino_.28Tagalog.29">Filipino (Tagalog)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Filipino (Tagalog)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Speakers of <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog</a> most commonly use the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Philippines" title="Spanish language in the Philippines">Spanish</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_loanwords_in_Tagalog" title="List of loanwords in Tagalog">loanwords</a> for blue and green—<i>asul</i> (from Spanish <i>azul</i>) and <i>berde</i> (from Spanish <i>verde</i>), respectively. Although these words are much more common in spoken use, Tagalog has native terms: <i>bugháw</i> for blue and <i>lunti(án)</i> for green, which are seen as archaic and more flowery. These are mostly confined to formal and academic writings, alongside artistic fields such literature, music, and poetry.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Cebuano_language" title="Cebuano language">Cebuano</a>, another major <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_the_Philippines" title="Languages of the Philippines">Philippine language</a>, the native words for "blue" and "green" end in the same syllable: <i>bughaw</i> and <i>lunhaw</i>, respectively.</p>
<p>Humour and jokes of a sexual or derogatory nature, which are called "blue" in English (e.g. "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_comedy" title="Blue comedy">blue comedy</a>", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dirty_joke" title="Dirty joke">blue joke</a>"), are otherwise called "green" in <a href="/wiki/Philippine_English" title="Philippine English">Philippine English</a> (e.g. "green joke", "green-minded"), a <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">calque</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hispanidad" title="Hispanidad">Hispanic</a> term <i>chiste verde</i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Javanese">Javanese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Javanese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Javanese_language" title="Javanese language">Modern Javanese</a> has distinct words for blue <i>biru</i> and green <i>ijo</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> These words are derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Javanese_language" title="Old Javanese language">Old Javanese</a> <i>birū</i> and <i>hijo</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> However, in Old Javanese <i>birū</i> could mean pale blue, greyish blue, greenish blue or even turquoise, while <i>hijo</i> which means green, could also means the blue-green colour of clear water. <i>Biru</i> and <i>ijo</i> in Modern Javanese are cognates of <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>/<a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a> <i>biru</i> and <i>hijau</i> which have the same meaning.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Niger-Congo">Niger-Congo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Niger-Congo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_language" title="Yoruba language">Yoruba</a> word for green is <i>àwö ewéko</i>, which translates to "color of leaves", and blue is <i>àwö sánmà</i>, which translates to "color of sky".</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bantu">Bantu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Bantu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">Swahili</a> word for blue is <i>buluu</i>, which is derived directly from English and has been in the language for a relatively short time. For other colors, Swahili uses either <i>rangi ya ___</i> (<i>the color of ___</i>) or a shortened version, <i>-a ___</i>. For example, green is <i>rangi ya kijani</i> or <i>rangi ya majani</i>, which means <i>the color of grass/leaves</i>. Sky blue is <i>rangi ya samawati</i>, or <i>the color of the sky</i> from the Arabic word for sky. (Note: all of these can be written as <i>-a kijani</i>, <i>-a majani</i>, <i>-a samwati</i>, etc.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Zulu_language" title="Zulu language">Zulu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xhosa_language" title="Xhosa language">Xhosa</a> use the word <i>-luhlaza</i> (the prefix changes according to the class of the noun) for <i>blue/green</i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tswana_language" title="Tswana language">Tswana</a> uses the same word <i>tala</i> to refer to both blue and green. One has to deduce from the context and prior knowledge, of what is being talked about, to be able to pinpoint exactly the color in question.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Himba_people" title="Himba people">Himba people</a> use a single word for shades of green and blue: <i>buru</i>. They curiously have only three other colour names, and their color perception has been studied by anthropologists.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="American_languages">American languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: American languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The language of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohawk_Nation" title="Mohawk Nation">Kanien'kehá:ka</a> Nation at <a href="/wiki/Akwesasne" title="Akwesasne">Akwesasne</a> is at Stage VII on the <a href="/wiki/Basic_Color_Terms:_Their_Universality_and_Evolution" title="Basic Color Terms: Their Universality and Evolution">Berlin–Kay Scale</a>, and thus possesses distinct terms for a broad range of <a href="/wiki/Spectral_color" title="Spectral color">spectral</a> and non-spectral colours, including blue (<i>oruía</i>), green (<i>óhute</i>), black (<i>kahúji</i>), white (<i>karákA’</i>), and grey (<i>atakArókwa</i>). According to one researcher, the Nation’s term for purple (<i>arihwawakunéha</i>), which translates to ‘bishop[’s colour]’, “is definitely a recent addition to the language”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frisch_.E2.80.9CMohawk_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frisch_.E2.80.9CMohawk-18">[18]</a></sup> The way in which purple was categorized and referenced prior to the addition of the latter term is not clear.</p>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lakota_Sioux" title="Lakota Sioux">Lakota Sioux</a> <a href="/wiki/Lakota_language" title="Lakota language">language</a>, the word <i>tȟó</i> is used for both blue and green, though the word <i>tȟózi</i> (a mixture of the words <i>tȟó</i> meaning "blue (green)," and <i>zí</i> meaning "yellow") has become common (<i>zítȟo</i> can also be used). This is in line with common practice of using <i>zíša/šázi</i> for Orange (<i>šá</i> meaning "red"), and <i>šátȟo/tȟóša</i> for "purple/violet."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Choctaw" title="Choctaw">Choctaw</a> <a href="/wiki/Choctaw_language" title="Choctaw language">language</a> has two words, <i>okchʋko</i> and <i>okchʋmali</i>, which have different meanings depending on the source. In 1852 <i>okchakko</i> is translated variously as pale blue or pale green, <i>okchakko chohmi</i> (<i>somewhat okchakko</i>) is given as swarthy, and <i>okchamali</i> is defined as deep blue, gray, green, or sky blue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1880 <i>okchakko</i> and <i>okchʋmali</i> are both given as blue, and green is not specifically listed as a color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In an 1892 dictionary, <i>okchamali</i> is deep blue or green, <i>okchakko</i> is pale blue or bright green, and a third word <i>kili̱koba</i> is bright green (resembling a <i>kili̱kki</i>, a species of parrot).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> By 1915, the authoritative Byington dictionary gives <i>okchako</i> as blue and <i>okchamali</i> as green, blue, gray, verdant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A current coursebook differentiates based on brightness, giving <i>okchʋko</i> as bright blue/green and <i>okchʋmali</i> as pale or dull blue/green.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Modern usage in the <a href="/wiki/Choctaw_Nation_of_Oklahoma" title="Choctaw Nation of Oklahoma">Choctaw Nation of Oklahoma</a> language school is to use <i>okchʋko</i> for blue and <i>okchʋmali</i> for green, with no distinction for brightness.</p>
<p>Single words for blue/green are also found in <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan languages</a>; for example in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yukatek_Maya_language" title="Yukatek Maya language">Yukatek Maya language</a> <i>blue/green</i> is <i>yax</i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tupian_languages" title="Tupian languages">Tupian languages</a> did not originally differ between the two colors, though they may now as a result of interference of Spanish (in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guaran%C3%AD_language" title="Guaraní language">Guaraní</a>) or Portuguese (in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nheengatu_language" title="Nheengatu language">Nheengatu</a>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Tupi_language" title="Old Tupi language">Tupi</a> word <i>oby</i> (<small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[ɔˈβɨ]</a></span>) meant both as does the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guaran%C3%AD_language" title="Guaraní language">Guaraní</a> <i>hovy</i> (<small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[ɦɔvɨ]</a></span>). In modern tupi (<a href="/wiki/Nheengatu" title="Nheengatu">nheengatu</a>) you can use the word "suikiri" for green and "iakira/akira" for blue, but they are still interchangeable, because "iakira/akira" also means immature, as in "pakua akira" (green banana/immature banana), and "suikiri" can also mean blue. In modern <a href="/wiki/Guarani_language" title="Guarani language">guarani</a>, they use "hovy" for blue and "hovy'û" (which literally means "dark green/blue) for green. So "hovy" can still be used for green" and "hovy'û" can be used for dark blue shades. They also have the word "aky", cognate with nheengatu "akira", and it means green/immature too.</p>
<p>The Yebamasa of the Rio Piraparana region in <a href="/wiki/Vaup%C3%A9s_Department" title="Vaupés Department">Vaupés Department</a>, southeastern <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, use the term <i>sumese</i> for blue/green. The letter "u" is pronounced like the German "ü".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>
