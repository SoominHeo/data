<p>The <b>Asian grass lizard</b>, <b>six-striped long-tailed lizard</b>, or <b>long-tailed grass lizard</b> (<i><b>Takydromus sexlineatus</b></i>) is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arboreal" title="Arboreal">arboreal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diurnal_animal" title="Diurnal animal">diurnal</a> species of <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizard</a>. The tail length is usually over three times the body (snout to vent) length in this species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Males and females are similar, males being distinguishable by the presence of pre-anal pores. On average they grow to around 12 centimetres (4.7Â in) snout-to-vent length, with the addition of a distinctive, prehensile long tail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Some individuals may have small circular spots on the sides of the bodies. This species of lizard is kept as a pet.</p>
<p>Like geckos they can drop their tail and grow a new one when attacked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E0.B8.AA.E0.B8.B2.E0.B8.87_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-.E0.B8.AA.E0.B8.B2.E0.B8.87-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takydromus_sexlineatus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Long Tailed grass lizard is easily identifiable by a long tail, and has a white to cream coloured underbelly with a brown, green or beige back, often adorned with brown stripes of different shades. It typically has a small head with a sharply pointed snout and black or pink tongue. Its body is slightly elongated and thin with small pointy scales beneath the chin resembling a beard. Males have white spots on their sides, while females do not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-.E0.B8.AA.E0.B8.B2.E0.B8.87_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-.E0.B8.AA.E0.B8.B2.E0.B8.87-4">[4]</a></sup> Males have tails that thicken past the vent and are generally thicker than the female's down the entire length of the tail. The light stripes on the length of the body are yellower than the female's, which are more cream colored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> They grow up to 12 inches (30Â cm.) long, with the tail usually being three times their body length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takydromus_sexlineatus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <i>Takydromus sexlineatus</i> is found throughout South East Asia, and is native to a number of countries including India, China, Thailand, and Indonesia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icn_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-icn-7">[7]</a></sup> The subspecies <i>ocellatus</i> is found in areas such as southern China, north Burma and north Malaysia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icn_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-icn-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takydromus_sexlineatus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Taky_sexlin_P_070607_130_ckoep.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="862" data-file-width="900" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Taky_sexlin_P_070607_130_ckoep.jpg/220px-Taky_sexlin_P_070607_130_ckoep.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Taky_sexlin_P_070607_130_ckoep.jpg/330px-Taky_sexlin_P_070607_130_ckoep.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Taky_sexlin_P_070607_130_ckoep.jpg/440px-Taky_sexlin_P_070607_130_ckoep.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Taky_sexlin_P_070607_130_ckoep.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
In a pair</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>These are entirely diurnal lizards that emerge in the early morning to bask in the sun. If a potential predator approaches they will first remain completely still, and then if the danger persists, they will flee to the safety of foliage. Both sexes use arm-waving gestures (similar to a <a href="/wiki/Front_crawl" title="Front crawl">front crawl</a> swimming action), apparently to communicate with each other. They are very agile and fast.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takydromus_sexlineatus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Long-Tailed_Grass_Lizard.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1004" data-file-width="1687" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Long-Tailed_Grass_Lizard.jpg/220px-Long-Tailed_Grass_Lizard.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Long-Tailed_Grass_Lizard.jpg/330px-Long-Tailed_Grass_Lizard.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Long-Tailed_Grass_Lizard.jpg/440px-Long-Tailed_Grass_Lizard.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Long-Tailed_Grass_Lizard.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
in captive</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><i>Takydromus sexlineatus</i> feeds on small insects such as flies, In captivity they can be reared on <a href="/wiki/Cricket_(insect)" title="Cricket (insect)">Crickets</a> and like other small lizards may require a calcium substitute. It is advisable in captivity to vary food including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mealworms" title="Mealworms">mealworms</a>, sterile <a href="/wiki/Maggot" title="Maggot">maggots</a> or waxworms in addition to crickets although it is possible to feed them garden caught insects. Unlike some larger reptiles, these lizards have extremely fast reactions and have been observed jumping into the air to catch flying prey such as flies.</p>
