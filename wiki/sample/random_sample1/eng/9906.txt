<p><b>Lesbian sexual practices</b> are <a href="/wiki/Human_sexual_activity" title="Human sexual activity">sexual activities</a> involving <a href="/wiki/Women_who_have_sex_with_women" title="Women who have sex with women">women who have sex with women</a>, irrespective of their <a href="/wiki/Sexual_orientation" title="Sexual orientation">sexual orientation</a>. A woman who has sex with another woman may identify as a <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">lesbian</a> if she is exclusively sexually attracted to women, or <a href="/wiki/Bisexuality" title="Bisexuality">bisexual</a> if she is not exclusively sexually attracted to women, or dispense with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexual_identification" title="Sexual identification">sexual identification</a> altogether. The term may also be applied to a <a href="/wiki/Heterosexuality" title="Heterosexuality">heterosexual</a> or <a href="/wiki/Asexuality" title="Asexuality">asexual</a> woman who is unsure of or is exploring her sexuality.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Romance_(love)" title="Romance (love)">Romantic</a> or sexual <a href="/wiki/Interpersonal_relationship" title="Interpersonal relationship">interpersonal relationships</a> are often subject to <a href="/wiki/Sexual_desire" title="Sexual desire">sexual desire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sexual_arousal" title="Sexual arousal">arousal</a>, which then leads to sexual activity for sexual release. A physical expression of intimacy between women depends on the context of the relationship along with social, cultural and other influences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hite_reports_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hite_reports-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Commonly, women engage in displays of affection, such as kissing on the cheek or hugging. A kiss on the lips, a prolonged kiss or hug, or other forms of touching, may or may not be regarded as <a href="/wiki/Human_sexuality" title="Human sexuality">sexual</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sex_educator" title="Sex educator">Sex educator</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist" title="Feminist">feminist</a> <a href="/wiki/Shere_Hite" title="Shere Hite">Shere Hite</a> stated that one of her female research subjects had written, "Sex with a woman includes: touching, kissing, smiling, looking serious, embracing, talking, digital intercourse, caressing, looking, cunnilingus, undressing, remembering later, making sounds, sometimes gently biting, sometimes crying, and breathing and sighing together."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hite.2Flesbianism_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hite.2Flesbianism-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Disrobing before a partner, removing the clothing of a partner or performing acts of <a href="/wiki/Physical_intimacy" title="Physical intimacy">physical intimacy</a>, such as the touching of a partner's <a href="/wiki/Erogenous_zone" title="Erogenous zone">erogenous zones</a> by the use of hands, tongue or rest of the mouth, may be regarded as indicators of the partner's desire for sexual activity and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erotic_stimuli" title="Erotic stimuli">erotic stimuli</a> or turn-on (sexual arousal), which may generate erotic sensations in both partners. Allowing such acts of physical intimacy, especially the stimulation of a partner's breasts and nipples, is an indication of reciprocal interest in sexual activity.</p>
<p>A woman's mouth, lips and tongue may be sensitive erogenous zones; they are commonly used by partners during the preliminary stages and throughout a sexual activity, whether for kissing, sucking, licking or specifically for <a href="/wiki/Oral_sex" title="Oral sex">oral sex</a>. The stimulation of a partner's breasts, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stimulation_of_nipples" title="Stimulation of nipples">oral or manual stimulation of the nipples</a>, is a form of <a href="/wiki/Foreplay" title="Foreplay">foreplay</a>. Breast and nipple stimulation of women is a near-universal aspect of sexual activity where the woman is the recipient of sexual attention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levin_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-levin-3">[3]</a></sup> The stimulation of a woman's nipples promotes the production and release of <a href="/wiki/Oxytocin" title="Oxytocin">oxytocin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prolactin" title="Prolactin">prolactin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levin_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levin-4">[4]</a></sup> During the stimulation of the nipples, large amounts of oxytocin are released, which would normally prepare the breast for breastfeeding. Besides creating maternal feelings in a woman, it also decreases her <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">anxiety</a> and increases bonding and trust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid19482229_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19482229-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Orgasm" title="Orgasm">orgasm</a> includes involuntary actions, including muscular <a href="/wiki/Spasm" title="Spasm">spasms</a> in multiple areas of the body, a general <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euphoria_(emotion)" title="Euphoria (emotion)">euphoric</a> sensation and, frequently, body movements and <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">vocalizations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenthal_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenthal-7">[7]</a></sup> The period after orgasm, known as a <a href="/wiki/Refractory_period_(sex)" title="Refractory period (sex)">refractory period</a>, is often a relaxing experience, attributed to the release of the <a href="/wiki/Neurohormone" title="Neurohormone">neurohormones</a> oxytocin and prolactin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenthal_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenthal-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schacter_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schacter-8">[8]</a></sup> While, due to factors concerning the possibility of females being able to achieve an additional orgasm soon after their first orgasm, research often indicates that they do not experience a refractory period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenthal_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenthal-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOC_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-SOC-9">[9]</a></sup> other sources define <i>refractory period</i> in females partly as a stage where further sexual stimulation does not produce excitement and therefore include females in the definition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schacter_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schacter-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weiner_.26_Craighead_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weiner_.26_Craighead-10">[10]</a></sup></p>

<p>Oral stimulation of the <a href="/wiki/Clitoris" title="Clitoris">clitoris</a> or other parts of <a href="/wiki/Vulva" title="Vulva">vulva</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Vagina" title="Vagina">vagina</a> is commonly practiced between women and, like oral stimulation of the nipples, can include the use of teeth in addition to use of the lips or tongue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll2-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zenilman_.26_Shahmanesh_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zenilman_.26_Shahmanesh-12">[12]</a></sup> Oral sex that involves the stimulation of the woman's clitoris or other parts of the vulva or the vagina is called <a href="/wiki/Cunnilingus" title="Cunnilingus">cunnilingus</a>. <p>In 1953, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Kinsey" title="Alfred Kinsey">Alfred Kinsey</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Kinsey_Reports" title="Kinsey Reports">Sexual Behavior in the Human Female</a></i> documented that, over the previous five years of sexual activity, 78% of women had orgasms in 60% to 100% of sexual encounters with other women, compared with 55% for heterosexual sex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Kinsey attributed this difference to female partners knowing more about women's sexuality and how to optimize women's sexual satisfaction than male partners do.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weiten_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weiten-26">[26]</a></sup> Similarly, studies by several scholars, including <a href="/wiki/Masters_and_Johnson" title="Masters and Johnson">Masters and Johnson</a>, concluded that lesbian sexual behaviors more often have qualities associated with sexual satisfaction than their heterosexual counterparts, or that female partners are more likely to emphasize the emotional aspects of lovemaking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenberg_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenberg-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weiten_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weiten-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iasenza_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Iasenza-27">[27]</a></sup> Masters and Johnson's 1979 study on lesbian sexual practices concluded that lesbian sexual encounters include more full-body sexual contact, rather than genital-focused contact, less preoccupation or anxiety about achieving orgasm, more sexual assertiveness and communication about sexual needs, <a href="/wiki/Human_sexual_response_cycle" title="Human sexual response cycle">longer lasting sexual encounters</a> and greater satisfaction with the overall quality of one's sexual life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenberg_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenberg-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iasenza_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Iasenza-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<p>Studies by <a href="/wiki/Pepper_Schwartz" title="Pepper Schwartz">Pepper Schwartz</a> and Philip Blumstein (1983) and Diane Holmberg and Karen L. Blair (2009) contradict research indicating that women in same-sex relationships are more sexually satisfied than their heterosexual counterparts. Schwartz concluded that lesbian couples in committed relationships have less sex than any other type of couple, and that they generally experience less sexual intimacy the longer the relationship lasts, though this study has been subject to debate (see <a href="/wiki/Lesbian_bed_death" title="Lesbian bed death">Lesbian bed death</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenberg_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-Greenberg-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iasenza_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Iasenza-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCroy_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCroy-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dowling_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dowling-29">[29]</a></sup> Holmberg and Blair's study, on the other hand, published in <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Sex_Research" title="Journal of Sex Research">The Journal of Sex Research</a></i>, found that women in same-sex relationships enjoyed identical sexual desire, sexual communication, sexual satisfaction, and satisfaction with orgasm as their heterosexual counterparts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Additional research by Blair &amp; Pukall (2014) reported similar findings, such that women in same-sex relationships reported similar levels of overall sexual satisfaction and lower levels of sexual frequency. However, this recent study also reported that women in same-sex relationships spent significantly longer amounts of time on individual sexual encounters - often spending upwards of two hours on an individual encounter. Consequently, what lesbians may lack in frequency, they may make up for with longer durations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>
<p>With regard to the ease or difficulty of achieving orgasm, Hite's research (while subject to methodological limitations) showed that most women need clitoral (exterior) stimulation for orgasm, which can be "easy and strong, given the right stimulation" and that the need for clitoral stimulation in addition to knowing one's own body is the reason that most women reach orgasm more easily by masturbation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hite_reports_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hite_reports-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Hite_Report_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Hite_Report-32">[32]</a></sup> Replicating Kinsey's findings, studies by scholars such as Peplau, Fingerhut and Beals (2004) and Diamond (2006) indicate that lesbians have orgasms more often and more easily in sexual interactions than heterosexual women do.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weiten_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-Weiten-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
<p>Preferences for specific sexual practices among female same-sex relationships have also been studied. Masters and Johnson concluded that vaginal penetration with dildos is rare and lesbians tend to do more overall genital stimulation than direct clitoral stimulation, which is also often the case for heterosexual relationships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenberg_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-Greenberg-19">[19]</a></sup> Concerning oral sex, the common belief that all women who have sex with women engage in cunnilingus contrasts research on the subject. Some lesbian or bisexual women dislike oral sex because they do not like the experience or due to psychological or social factors, such as finding it unclean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belge2_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Belge2-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCormick_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCormick-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vida_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vida-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bigner_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bigner-36">[36]</a></sup> Other lesbian or bisexual women believe it is a necessity or largely defines lesbian sexual activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vida_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vida-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bigner_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bigner-36">[36]</a></sup> Lesbian couples are more likely to consider a woman's dislike of cunnilingus as a problem than heterosexual couples are, and it is common for them to seek therapy to overcome <a href="/wiki/Sexual_inhibition" title="Sexual inhibition">inhibitions</a> regarding it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vida_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vida-35">[35]</a></sup> Similarly, there are lesbians who may or may not like <a href="/wiki/Anal_sex" title="Anal sex">anal sex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harding_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harding-37">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1987, a non-scientific study (Munson) was conducted of more than 100 members of a lesbian social organization in Colorado. When asked what techniques they used in their last 10 sexual encounters, 100% reported kissing, sucking on breasts, and manual stimulation of the clitoris; more than 90% reported French kissing, oral sex, and fingers inserted into the vagina; and 80% reported tribadism. Lesbians in their 30s were twice as likely as other age groups to engage in anal stimulation (with a finger or dildo).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carroll2_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll2-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, Julia V Bailey and her research team published data based on a sample from the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> of 803 lesbian and bisexual women attending two London lesbian sexual health clinics and 415 women who have sex with women (WSW) from a community sample; the study reported that the most commonly cited sexual practices between women "were oral sex, digital vaginal penetration, mutual masturbation, and tribadism (frottage with genital-to-genital contact or rubbing of the genitals against another part of the partner's body), each of which occurred in 85% of [women who have sex with women]". Like older studies, the data also showed that vaginal penetration with dildos, or with other sex toys, among women who have sex with women is rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zenilman_.26_Shahmanesh_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Zenilman_.26_Shahmanesh-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bailey_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bailey-38">[38]</a></sup></p>
<p>A 2012 online survey of 3,116 women who have sex with women, published in <a href="/wiki/The_BMJ" title="The BMJ">The BMJ</a>, found the majority of women reporting a history of genital rubbing (99.8%), vaginal fingering (99.2%), genital scissoring (90.8%), cunnilingus (98.8%) and vibrator use (74.1%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_risks">Health risks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lesbian_sexual_practices&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Health risks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like most sexual activity, lesbian sexual activities can carry risks of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sexually_transmitted_disease" title="Sexually transmitted disease">sexually transmitted infections</a> (STIs/STDs), such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herpes_genitalis" title="Herpes genitalis">genital herpes</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogenic</a> infections. When lesbian sexual activity is of a non-penetrative nature, the risk of exchange of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bodily_fluid" title="Bodily fluid">bodily fluids</a> is generally lower and therefore the incidence of transmission of sexually transmitted infections is also relatively low,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hales_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hales-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alexander_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander-41">[41]</a></sup> especially when contrasted by <a href="/wiki/Sexual_penetration" title="Sexual penetration">penetrative</a> sexual activities between male-female or male-male couples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDC-42">[42]</a></sup> The use of the same sex toys by more than one person increases the risk of transmitting such infections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDC-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mravack_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mravack-43">[43]</a></sup> Though the risk for <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> transmission from lesbian sexual activity is significantly lower than it is for HIV transmission from male-female and male-male sexual activity, it is present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-CDC-42">[42]</a></sup> HIV can be spread through bodily fluids, such as blood (including <a href="/wiki/Menstruation" title="Menstruation">menstrual</a> blood), <a href="/wiki/Vaginal_lubrication" title="Vaginal lubrication">vaginal fluid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breast_milk" title="Breast milk">breast milk</a>, or by oral sex if the person has cuts or sores in her mouth or poor oral hygiene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hales_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hales-40">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control and Prevention</a> (CDC) did not recognize female-to-female transmission as a possible method of HIV infection until 1995. The CDC reported there is little data available regarding the risk of spreading sexually transmitted infections between women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_42-3"><a href="#cite_note-CDC-42">[42]</a></sup> However, the CDC states that pathogens such as metronidazole-resistant <a href="/wiki/Trichomoniasis" title="Trichomoniasis">trichomoniasis</a>, genotype-concordant HIV, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_papillomavirus" title="Human papillomavirus">human papillomavirus</a> (HPV, which has been linked to nearly all cases of <a href="/wiki/Cervical_cancer" title="Cervical cancer">cervical cancer</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Syphilis" title="Syphilis">syphilis</a> can be spread through sexual contact between women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_42-4"><a href="#cite_note-CDC-42">[42]</a></sup> While the rates of these pathologies are unknown, one study showed 30% of lesbians and bisexual women had medical histories with sexually transmitted infections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> This does not mean that sexually active lesbians are exposed to higher health risks than the general population. Health Canada noted "the prevalence of all types of HPV (cancer and non-cancer-causing) in different groups of Canadian women ranges from 20%–33%" and an American university study found 60% of sexually active females were infected with HPV at some time within a three-year period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/American_Family_Physician" title="American Family Physician">American Family Physician</a></i> suggests that lesbian and bisexual women "cover sex toys that penetrate more than one person's vagina or anus with a new condom for each person" and to "consider using different toys for each person", to use a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrier_contraception" title="Barrier contraception">protection barrier</a> (for example, a latex sheet, <a href="/wiki/Dental_dam" title="Dental dam">dental dam</a>, cut-open condom or, plastic wrap) during oral sex and to "use latex or vinyl gloves and lubricant for any manual sex that might cause bleeding unprotected contact with a sexual partner's menstrual blood and with any visible genital lesions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mravack_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mravack-43">[43]</a></sup> However, there "is no good evidence" that using a dental dam reduces STI transmission risks between women who have sex with women; studies show that using a dental dam as a protection barrier is rarely practiced, and that, among women who have sex with women, this may be because the individuals have "limited knowledge about the possibilities of STI transmission or [feel] less vulnerable to STIs [such as HIV]".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zenilman_.26_Shahmanesh_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Zenilman_.26_Shahmanesh-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
