<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Reynard</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Reynard</b> (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl"><i>Reinaert</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Renart</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Reineke</i></span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>Renartus</i></span>) is the main character in a <a href="/wiki/List_of_literary_cycles" title="List of literary cycles">literary cycle</a> of <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegorical</a> <a href="/wiki/Folklore_of_the_Low_Countries" title="Folklore of the Low Countries">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_folklore" title="English folklore">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_folklore" title="French folklore">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_folklore" title="German folklore">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Fable" title="Fable">fables</a>. Those stories are largely concerned with Reynard, an <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphism" title="Anthropomorphism">anthropomorphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Red_fox" title="Red fox">red fox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trickster" title="Trickster">trickster</a> figure. His adventures usually involve him deceiving other anthropomorphic animals for his own advantage or trying to avoid retaliations from them. His main enemy and victim across the cycle is his uncle, the wolf Isengrim (or Ysengrim). While the character of Reynard appears in later works, the core stories were written during the Middle Ages by multiple authors and are often seen as parodies of medieval literature such as <a href="/wiki/Courtly_love" title="Courtly love">courtly love</a> stories and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chansons_de_geste" title="Chansons de geste">chansons de geste</a>, as well as a satire of political and religious institutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bianciotto_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bianciotto-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_of_the_name">Etymology of the name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology of the name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Theories about the origin of the name Reynard are:</p>
<ul>
<li>from the Germanic man's name Reginhard, which came from '<a href="/wiki/Regin" title="Regin">regin</a>' = "the <a href="/wiki/Divinity" title="Divinity">divine</a> powers of the old <a href="/wiki/Germanic_paganism" title="Germanic paganism">Germanic religion</a>" and "hard": "made hard by the gods", but with the disuse of the old Germanic religion was later likely interpreted as "<a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>-<a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">hard</a>" meaning "staying steady under a rain of blows from weapons in battle" or similar.</li>
<li>from the Germanic man's name Reginhard (later condensed to <a href="/wiki/Reinhard" title="Reinhard">Reinhard</a>), which comes from 'regin' = "counsel" and 'harti' = "strong", denoting someone who is wise, clever, or resourceful.</li>
</ul>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fox_preaching_to_rooster.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="185" data-file-width="248" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Fox_preaching_to_rooster.jpg/220px-Fox_preaching_to_rooster.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/06/Fox_preaching_to_rooster.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fox_preaching_to_rooster.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Defaced Reyard preaches to a rooster. <a class="external text" href="http://dla.library.upenn.edu/dla/medren/pageturn.html?q=ms+codex+724&amp;id=MEDREN_2487544&amp;rotation=0&amp;currentpage=496" rel="nofollow">Rare Book &amp; Manuscript Library University of Pennsylvania Ms. Codex 724</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Because of the popularity of the Reynard stories, <i>renard</i> became the standard French word for "fox", replacing the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">old French</a> word for "fox", which was <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/goupil" title="wikt:goupil">goupil</a></i> from Latin <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vulpecula" title="wikt:vulpecula">vulpecula</a></i>. <i>Goupil</i> is now dialectal or archaic.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_medieval_European_folklore_and_literature">In medieval European folklore and literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In medieval European folklore and literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fuchs.margin_(MMW10F50_f6r)_detail.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="321" data-file-width="250" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Fuchs.margin_%28MMW10F50_f6r%29_detail.jpg/180px-Fuchs.margin_%28MMW10F50_f6r%29_detail.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9e/Fuchs.margin_%28MMW10F50_f6r%29_detail.jpg 1.5x" width="180"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fuchs.margin_(MMW10F50_f6r)_detail.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A studious fox in a monk's <a href="/wiki/Cowl" title="Cowl">cowl</a>, in the margins of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_Hours" title="Book of Hours">Book of Hours</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utrecht_(city)" title="Utrecht (city)">Utrecht</a>, c 1460</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The figure of Reynard is thought to have originated in <a href="/wiki/Lorraine" title="Lorraine">Lorraine</a> <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> from where it spread to France, the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>, and Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longman_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Longman-2">[2]</a></sup> An extensive treatment of the character is the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> <i>Le Roman de Renart</i> written by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pierre_de_Saint-Cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pierre de Saint-Cloud (page does not exist)">Pierre de Saint-Cloud</a> around 1170, which sets the typical setting. Reynard has been summoned to the court of king Noble, or Leo, the lion, to answer charges brought against him by Isengrim the wolf. Other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropomorphic" title="Anthropomorphic">anthropomorphic</a> animals, including Bruin the bear, Baldwin the ass, and Tibert (Tybalt) the cat, all attempt one stratagem or another. The stories typically involve <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satire</a> whose usual butts are the <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocracy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a>, making Reynard a peasant-hero character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longman_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Longman-2">[2]</a></sup> The story of the preaching fox found in the Reynard literature was used in church art by the Catholic Church as propaganda against the <a href="/wiki/Lollardy" title="Lollardy">Lollards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Reynard's principal castle, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maleperduys" title="Maleperduys">Maupertuis</a>, is available to him whenever he needs to hide away from his enemies. Some of the tales feature Reynard's funeral, where his enemies gather to deliver maudlin <a href="/wiki/Elegy" title="Elegy">elegies</a> full of insincere piety, and which feature Reynard's posthumous revenge. Reynard's wife Hermeline appears in the stories, but plays little active role, although in some versions she remarries when Reynard is thought dead, thereby becoming one of the people he plans revenge upon. Isengrim (alternate French spelling: <i>Ysengrin</i>) is Reynard's most frequent antagonist and foil, and generally ends up outwitted, though he occasionally gets revenge.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ysengrimus"><i>Ysengrimus</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ysengrimus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reynard appears first in the medieval <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> poem <i><a href="/wiki/Ysengrimus" title="Ysengrimus">Ysengrimus</a></i>, a long Latin mock-epic written c. 1148-1153 by the poet Nivardus in <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a>, that collects a great store of Reynard's adventures. He also puts in an early appearance in a number of Latin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sequence_(poetry)" title="Sequence (poetry)">sequences</a> by the preacher <a href="/wiki/Odo_of_Cheriton" title="Odo of Cheriton">Odo of Cheriton</a>. Both of these early sources seem to draw on a pre-existing store of <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a> featuring the character.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_de_Renart"><i>Roman de Renart</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roman de Renart">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first "branch" (or chapter) of the <i>Roman de Renart</i> appears in 1174, written by Pierre de St. Cloud, although in all French editions it is designated as "Branch II". The same author wrote a sequel in 1179—called "Branch I"—but from that date onwards, many other French authors composed their own adventures for Renart <i>li goupil</i> ("the fox"). There is also the text <i>Reinhard Fuchs</i> by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_der_Gl%C3%AFchez%C3%A4re" title="Heinrich der Glïchezäre">Heinrich der Glïchezäre</a>, dated to <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr>1180.</p>
<p>Pierre de St. Cloud opens his work on the fox by situating it within the larger tradition of epic poetry, the <a href="/wiki/Fabliau" title="Fabliau">fabliaux</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthurian_romance" title="Arthurian romance">Arthurian romance</a>:</p>
<table border="0" cellpadding="5" cellspacing="0" style="margin:auto;">
<tr>
<td></td>
<td><i>This would roughly translate as:</i><br/></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>
<p><i>Seigneurs, oï avez maint conte<br/></i> <i>Que maint conterre vous raconte<br/></i> <i>Conment Paris ravi Elaine,<br/></i> <i>Le mal qu'il en ot et la paine,<br/></i> <i>De Tristan que la Chievre fist<br/></i> <i>Qui assez bellement en dist<br/></i> <i>Et fabliaus et chançons de geste<br/></i> <i>Romanz d'Yvain et de sa beste<br/></i> <i>Maint autre conte par la terre.<br/></i> <i>Mais onques n'oïstes la guerre<br/></i> <i>Qui tant fu dure de gran fin,<br/></i> <i>Entre Renart et Ysengrin.</i><br/></p>
</td>
<td>
<p>Lords, you have heard many tales,<br/>
That many tellers have told to you.<br/>
How <a href="/wiki/Paris_(mythology)" title="Paris (mythology)">Paris</a> took <a href="/wiki/Helen_of_Troy" title="Helen of Troy">Helen</a>,<br/>
The evil and the pain he felt<br/>
Of <a href="/wiki/Tristan" title="Tristan">Tristan</a> that <a href="/wiki/Chevrefoil" title="Chevrefoil">la Chevre</a><br/>
Spoke rather beautifully about;<br/>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Van_den_vos_Reynaerde"><i>Van den vos Reynaerde</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Van den vos Reynaerde">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span id="Middle_Dutch"></span> A mid 13th-century <a href="/wiki/Middle_Dutch" title="Middle Dutch">Middle Dutch</a> version of the story by <a href="/wiki/Willem_die_Madoc_maecte" title="Willem die Madoc maecte">Willem die Madoc maecte</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Van_den_vos_Reynaerde" title="Van den vos Reynaerde">Van den vos Reynaerde</a></i>, Of Reynaert the Fox), is also made up of <a href="/wiki/Rhyme" title="Rhyme">rhymed</a> verses (the same <a href="/wiki/Rhyme_scheme" title="Rhyme scheme">AA BB scheme</a>). Like Pierre, very little is known of the author, other than the description by the copyist in the first sentences:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rey_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-rey-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<p><i>Madocke</i> or <i>Madoc</i> is thought to be another one of Willem's works that at one point existed but was lost. The <i>Arnout</i> mentioned was an earlier Reynard poet whose work Willem (the writer) alleges to have finished. However, there are serious objections to this notion of joint authorship, and the only thing deemed likely is that Arnout was French-speaking ("Walschen" in Middle Dutch referred to northern French-speaking people, specifically the <a href="/wiki/Walloons" title="Walloons">Walloons</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Willem's work became one of the standard versions of the legend, and was the foundation for most later adaptations in Dutch, German, and English, including those of <a href="/wiki/William_Caxton" title="William Caxton">William Caxton</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goethe" title="Goethe">Goethe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Startridge_Ellis" title="Frederick Startridge Ellis">F. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chaucer">Chaucer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chaucer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">Geoffrey Chaucer</a> used Reynard material in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canterbury_Tales" title="Canterbury Tales">Canterbury Tales</a></i>; in "<a href="/wiki/The_Nun%27s_Priest%27s_Tale" title="The Nun's Priest's Tale">The Nun's Priest's Tale</a>", Reynard appears as "Rossel" and an ass as "Brunel". In 1481 <a href="/wiki/William_Caxton" title="William Caxton">William Caxton</a> printed <i>The Historie of Reynart the Foxe</i>, which was translated from a <a href="/wiki/Middle_Dutch" title="Middle Dutch">Middle Dutch</a> version of the fables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longman_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Longman-2">[2]</a></sup> Also in the 1480s, the Scottish poet <a href="/wiki/Robert_Henryson" title="Robert Henryson">Robert Henryson</a> devised a highly sophisticated development of Reynardian material as part of his <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Morall_Fabillis" title="The Morall Fabillis">Morall Fabillis</a></i> in the sections known as <i>The <a href="/wiki/Fable" title="Fable">Talking</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">Tod</a></i>. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hans_van_Ghetelen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hans van Ghetelen (page does not exist)">Hans van Ghetelen</a>, a printer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incunabulum" title="Incunabulum">Incunabula</a> in <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCbeck" title="Lübeck">Lübeck</a> printed an early German version called <i>Reinke de Vos</i> in 1498. It was translated to Latin and other languages, which made the tale popular across Europe. Reynard is also referenced in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> poem <i><a href="/wiki/Sir_Gawain_and_the_Green_Knight" title="Sir Gawain and the Green Knight">Sir Gawain and the Green Knight</a></i> during the third hunt.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renert">Renert</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Renert">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Renert</i> [full original title: <i>Renert oder de Fuuß am Frack an a Ma’nsgrëßt</i>],<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RenertELC_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-RenertELC-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RenertAF_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-RenertAF-7">[7]</a></sup> was published in 1872 by <a href="/wiki/Michel_Rodange" title="Michel Rodange">Michel Rodange</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourgeois</a> author.</p>
<p>An epic satirical work—adapted from the 1858 Cotta Edition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goethe" title="Goethe">Goethe</a>'s fox epic <i>Reineke Fuchs</i> to a setting in Luxembourg—<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RenertELC_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-RenertELC-6">[6]</a></sup> it is known for its insightful analysis of the unique characteristics of the people of Luxembourg, using regional and sub-regional dialects to depict the fox and his companions.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antisemitic_version">Antisemitic version</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Antisemitic version">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Van den vos Reynaerde</i> (Of Reynaert the Fox) was an <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">anti-Semitic</a> children's story, written by the Dutch-Belgian <a href="/wiki/Robert_van_Genechten" title="Robert van Genechten">Robert van Genechten</a>, and named after the medieval <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> poem. It was first published in 1937 in <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nieuw-Nederland_(publication)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nieuw-Nederland (publication) (page does not exist)">Nieuw-Nederland</a></i>, a monthly publication of the Dutch <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a>'s front, the <a href="/wiki/National_Socialist_Movement_in_the_Netherlands" title="National Socialist Movement in the Netherlands">NSB</a>. In 1941 it was published as a book.</p>
<p>The story features a <a href="/wiki/Rhinoceros" title="Rhinoceros">rhinoceros</a> called Jodocus, somewhat akin to the Dutch word <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/jood#Dutch" title="wiktionary:jood">jood</a>; and a donkey, Boudewijn, who occupies the throne. <i>Boudewijn</i>, as King of "Belgium", was the Dutch name for the contemporary real-world <a href="/wiki/Baudouin_of_Belgium" title="Baudouin of Belgium">Belgian crown prince</a>. In the story, Jodocus is an outsider who comes to the Empire and subsequently introduces new ideas that drastically alter the natural order. The land is then declared a "Republic", where "liberty, equality and fraternity" are to be exercised. This <a href="/wiki/Dystopia" title="Dystopia">dystopian</a> view of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist_republic" title="Socialist republic">socialist republics</a> fits the Nazi ideology on equality and liberty as something degenerate: "There was no one who kept to the rules of the race. Rabbits crept into foxholes, the chickens wanted to build an eyrie." Eventually, Reynard and the others trick and kill Jodocus and his colleagues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>Van den vos Reynaerde</i> was also produced as a cartoon film by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nederlandfilm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nederlandfilm (page does not exist)">Nederlandfilm</a> in 1943.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The film was mostly financed with German money. While lavishly budgeted, it was never presented publicly, possibly because most Dutch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">Jews</a> had already been transported to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concentration_camp" title="Concentration camp">concentration camps</a> and the film came too late to be useful as a propaganda piece, possibly also because the Dutch collaborationist Department of People's Information, Service and Arts objected to the fact that the fox, an animal traditionally seen as "villainous", should be used as a hero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1991, parts of the film were found again in the German <i>Bundesarchiv</i>. In 2005, more pieces were found, and the film has been restored. The reconstructed film was shown during the 2006 Holland Animation Film Festival in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utrecht_(city)" title="Utrecht (city)">Utrecht</a> and during the KLIK! Amsterdam Animation Festival in 2008, in the Netherlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_adaptations.2C_versions.2C_and_references">Other adaptations, versions, and references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other adaptations, versions, and references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_movies_and_television_series">In movies and television series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In movies and television series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ladislas_Starevich" title="Ladislas Starevich">Ladislas Starevich</a>'s <a href="/wiki/1930_in_film" title="1930 in film">1930</a> puppet-animated feature film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_the_Fox" title="The Tale of the Fox">Le Roman de Renard</a></i> (The Tale of the Fox) featured the Reynard character as the protagonist.</li>
<li>The documentary film <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Fox:_The_Rise_and_Fall_of_Adolf_Hitler" title="Black Fox: The Rise and Fall of Adolf Hitler">Black Fox</a></i> (1962) parallels <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Hitler</a>'s rise to power with the Reynard fable.</li>
<li>Initially, <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Animation_Studios" title="Walt Disney Animation Studios">Walt Disney Animation Studios</a> considered a movie about Reynard. However, due to <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">Walt Disney</a>'s concern that Reynard was an unsuitable choice for a hero, the studio decided to make Reynard the antagonist of a single narrative feature film named <i>Chanticleer and Reynard</i> (based on <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Rostand" title="Edmond Rostand">Edmond Rostand</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Chantecler_(play)" title="Chantecler (play)">Chanticleer</a></i>) but the production was scrapped in the mid-1960s, in favor of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sword_in_the_Stone_(film)" title="The Sword in the Stone (film)">The Sword in the Stone</a></i> (1963). Ken Anderson used the character designs for <i><a href="/wiki/Robin_Hood_(1973_film)" title="Robin Hood (1973 film)">Robin Hood</a></i> (1973) such as the animal counterparts (e.g. Robin Hood like Reynard is a fox while the Sheriff of Nottingham like Isengrim is a wolf).</li>
<li>In 1985, a French animated series, <i>"<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Moi_Renart&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Moi Renart (page does not exist)">Moi Renart</a>"</i> (<i>I Reynard</i>), was created that was loosely based on Reynard's tales. In it, the original animals are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropomorphic" title="Anthropomorphic">anthropomorphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Humanoid" title="Humanoid">humanoid</a> animals (to the point that, primary, <a href="/wiki/Theriocephaly" title="Theriocephaly">only their heads are that of animals</a>) and the action occurs in modern <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> with other anthropomorphic animals in human roles. Reynard is a young mischievous fox with a little monkey pet called Marmouset (an original creation). He sets off into Paris in order to discover the city, get a job and visit his grumpy and stingy uncle, Isengrim, who is a deluxe car salesman, and his reasonable yet dreamy she-wolf aunt, Hersent. Reynard meets Hermeline, a young and charming motorbike-riding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vixen" title="Vixen">vixen</a> journalist. He immediately falls in love with her and tries to win her heart during several of the episodes. As Reynard establishes himself in Paris, he creates a small company that shares his name which offers to do any job for anyone, from impersonating female maids to opera singers. To help with this, he is a master of disguise and is a bit of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kleptomaniac" title="Kleptomaniac">kleptomaniac</a>, which gets him into trouble from police chief Chantecler (a rooster) who often sends cat police inspector Tybalt after him to thwart his plans.</li>
<li>In 2005, a <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>-based animation studio released an all CGI film titled <i>Le Roman de Renart</i>, based on the same fable.</li>
<li>In 2016, <a href="/wiki/Syfy" title="Syfy">Syfy</a>'s television series, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magicians_(U.S._TV_series)" title="The Magicians (U.S. TV series)">The Magicians</a></i>, Reynard is summoned accidentally.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_music">In music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reynard the Fox is the name of a number of traditional folk songs (<a href="/wiki/Roud_Folk_Song_Index" title="Roud Folk Song Index">Roud</a> 190, 358 and 1868).</p>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Renard_(Stravinsky)" title="Renard (Stravinsky)">Renard</a></i> is a one-act chamber opera-ballet by <a href="/wiki/Igor_Stravinsky" title="Igor Stravinsky">Igor Stravinsky</a>, written in 1916, with text by the composer based on Russian folk tales from the collection by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Afanasyev" title="Alexander Afanasyev">Alexander Afanasyev</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andy_Irvine_(musician)" title="Andy Irvine (musician)">Andy Irvine</a> recorded the traditional Irish song "Reynard The Fox" with <a href="/wiki/Sweeney%27s_Men" title="Sweeney's Men">Sweeney's Men</a> on their 1968 debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Sweeney%27s_Men_(album)" title="Sweeney's Men (album)">Sweeney's Men</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fairport_Convention" title="Fairport Convention">Fairport Convention</a> recorded the traditional English song called "Reynard The Fox"<small>(Roud 190)</small> (collected by <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Vaughan_Williams" title="Ralph Vaughan Williams">Ralph Vaughan Williams</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FCReynard_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FCReynard-17">[17]</a></sup> on their 1978 album <i><a href="/wiki/Tipplers_Tales" title="Tipplers Tales">Tipplers Tales</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Martin_Carthy" title="Martin Carthy">Martin Carthy</a> recorded the song "Reynard the Fox"<small>(Roud 1868)</small> on his 1982 album <i><a href="/wiki/Out_of_the_Cut_(Martin_Carthy_album)" title="Out of the Cut (Martin Carthy album)">Out of the Cut</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Julian_Cope" title="Julian Cope">Julian Cope</a> wrote a song called "Reynard the Fox" which he recorded on his 1984 album <i><a href="/wiki/Fried_(album)" title="Fried (album)">Fried</a></i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brass_Monkey_(band)" title="Brass Monkey (band)">Brass Monkey</a> recorded a version of the song collected by Ralph Vaughan Williams as "The Foxhunt"<small>(Roud 190)</small> sung by Martin Carthy on their 1986 album <i>See How It Runs</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Country_Teasers" title="Country Teasers">Country Teasers</a> wrote a song called "Reynard the Fox", which appears on the 1999 album <i><a href="/wiki/Destroy_All_Human_Life" title="Destroy All Human Life">Destroy All Human Life</a></i>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_advertising">In advertising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In advertising">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bevo" title="Bevo">Bevo</a>, a popular U.S. brand of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_beer" title="Near beer">near beer</a>, advertised with Reynard the Fox in the 1910s and 1920s.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_comics">In comics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: In comics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Reynard is portrayed as a character in <i><a href="/wiki/Gunnerkrigg_Court" title="Gunnerkrigg Court">Gunnerkrigg Court</a></i> as Reynardine, a fox demon who can possess "anything with eyes", including living beings and, in his current form, a plush wolf toy. Gunnerkrigg Court also has Ysengrin, an <a href="/wiki/Ysengrimus" title="Ysengrimus">Ysengrim</a> analog.</li>
<li>Reynard is portrayed as a character in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fables_(comic)" title="Fables (comic)">Fables</a></i>, as a smart and cunning fox who is loyal to Snow White and Fabletown, despite being one of the Fables segregated to the upstate New York "Farm" due to his non-human appearance. He initially appears as a physically normal fox, anthropomorphized only in his ability to think and speak as humans do; later, he is granted the ability to assume a handsome human appearance.</li>
<li>The French comic <i><a href="/wiki/De_cape_et_de_crocs" title="De cape et de crocs">De cape et de crocs</a></i> takes place in an alternative 17th century where anthropomorphic animals live among humans. One of the two main characters, Armand Raynal de <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maleperduys" title="Maleperduys">Maupertuis</a>, is a French fox based on Reynard while his companion, Don Lope de Villalobos y Sangrin, is a Spanish wolf based on Isengrim (which is spelled Ysengrin in French).</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_video_games">In video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reynard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: In video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In the 2016 video game, <a href="/wiki/Stories:_The_Path_of_Destinies" title="Stories: The Path of Destinies">Stories: The Path of Destinies</a>, the protagonist is an anthropomorphic fox named Reynardo. The plot centers around Reynardo's time-bending attempts to thwart an evil emperor, Isengrim (in this case an anthropomorphic frog, rather than a wolf), as the emperor attempts to sacrifice his adopted daughter to the old gods.</li>
</ul>
