<p><i><b>Vulpes</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Canidae" title="Canidae">Canidae</a>. The members of this genus are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colloquially" title="Colloquially">colloquially</a> referred to as true foxes, meaning they form a proper <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a>. The word "<a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">fox</a>" occurs on the common names of species. True foxes are distinguished from members of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Canis" title="Canis">Canis</a></i>, such as <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolves" title="Wolves">wolves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyotes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jackal" title="Jackal">jackals</a>, by their smaller size (5-11 kg) and flatter skulls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macdonald.2C_David_1984_P._31_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Macdonald.2C_David_1984_P._31-2">[2]</a></sup> They have black, triangular markings between their eyes and noses, and the tips of their tails are often a different color from the rest of their pelts. The typical lifespan for this genus is between two and four years, but can reach up to a decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macdonald.2C_David_1984_P._31_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Macdonald.2C_David_1984_P._31-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>For animals commonly known as "foxes", but which are not true foxes, see <a href="/wiki/Fox#Classification" title="Fox">Fox#Classification</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extant_species">Extant species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulpes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Extant species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Within <i>Vulpes</i>, 12 separate and distinct extant species and four fossil species are described:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arctic_fox" title="Arctic fox">Arctic fox</a> (<i>V. lagopus</i>)</li>

<p>The Arctic fox is sometimes included in this genus as <i>Vulpes lagopus</i> based on the definitive mammal taxonomy list, as well as <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msw3_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-msw3-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-complete_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-complete-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Foxes of this group (including the fennec and Arctic foxes) possess eyes with pupils that retract into vertical slits in bright light.</p>
<p>The red fox, Ruppell's fox,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and Tibetan sand fox <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> possess white-tipped tails. The Arctic fox's tail-tip is of the same color as the rest of the tail (white or blue-gray)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Blanford's fox usually possesses a black-tipped tail, but a small number of specimens (2% in Israel, 24% in the United Arab Emirates) possess a light-tipped tail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The other foxes in this group (Bengal, Cape, corsac, fennec, kit, pale, and swift) all possess black-tipped or dark-tipped tails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulpes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The oldest known <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> species within <i>Vulpes</i> is <i>V. riffautae</i>, dating back to the late Miocene of <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>, which is within the <a href="/wiki/Neogene" title="Neogene">Neogene</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deposit_(geology)" title="Deposit (geology)">deposits</a> where these fossils are found are about 7 million years old, which might make them the earliest Canidae in the Old World. They are estimated to have weighed between 1.5 and 3.5 lb. <i>V. skinneri</i>, from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Malapa_fossil_site&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Malapa fossil site (page does not exist)">Malapa fossil site</a> from <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, is younger than <i>V. riffautae</i> by roughly 5 million years, and shows up in the early Pleistocene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Two other extinct, less documented fossils are known: <i>V. praeglacialis</i> and <i>V. hassani</i>. <i>V. praeglacialis</i> was discovered in the Petralona Cave in <a href="/wiki/Chalkidiki" title="Chalkidiki">Chalkidiki</a>, Greece. The age of the deposits (Early <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>) makes it the earliest occurrence of <i>Vulpes</i> in Europe. <i>V. hassani</i> is found in a Miocene-Pliocene deposit in northwestern Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the Pleistocene, <i>Vulpes</i> had a fairly wide distribution, with eight species found in North America. Of these eight, six are not fossil, and three species still remain in North America (<i>V. velox</i>, <i>V. macrotis</i>, and <i>V. chama</i>). The remaining three moved on to sections of Africa over time. <i>V. stenognathus</i> is extinct, but has extant sister taxa including <i>V. chama</i>, <i>V. rueppellii</i>, <i>V. velox</i>, and <i>V. vulpes</i>, which fits with these species all evolving together in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Cape fox is only found in the south of Africa, including <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. They thrive in semiarid and <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> environments with rich <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a>.</p>

<p>Corsac foxes live in central Asia. Like <i>V. chama</i> and <i>V. cana</i>, they do best in semiarid deserts. This fox is within the <a href="/wiki/Holarctic" title="Holarctic">holarctic</a> clade of foxes. This clade also contains the Arctic fox, swift fox, and red fox. Their possible ancestor is <i>V. praecorsac</i>, meaning they may have had a much wider distribution in the past (Europe and <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Tibetan sand fox, as the name suggests, is endemic to the Tibetan and <a href="/wiki/Ladakh" title="Ladakh">Ladakh</a> plateau in <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sikkim" title="Sikkim">Sikkim</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>. This species lives at altitudes up to 5300 m and semideserts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>Arctic foxes inhabit all of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a>), <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, and hold the title of being the only native land mammal in all of Iceland. This fox arrived in Iceland during the climax of the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>, when the seas were frozen solid enough to walk across. Two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extant_taxon" title="Extant taxon">extant</a> subpopulations of this species are alive today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The Arctic fox is most closely related to kit (<i>V. macrotis</i>) and corsac foxes (<i>V. corsac</i>).</p>

<p>Kit foxes are an arid area-dwelling North American species. They are found in <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>. They also have a small population in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.</p>

<p>The pale fox lives in upper middle Africa and is an arid area-dwelling species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>Ruppel's foxes are specific to northern Africa and sections of the Middle East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

<p>The swift fox is found in the western grasslands of North America, specifically <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>, Colorado, New Mexico, Oklahoma, and Texas, as well as some sections of Canada. This species is most closely related to the kit fox, but lives in a different section of North America. The two can <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interbreed" title="Interbreed">interbreed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="V._vulpes"><i>V. vulpes</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulpes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: V. vulpes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki-Vulpes_vulpes.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="1250" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Wiki-Vulpes_vulpes.png/220px-Wiki-Vulpes_vulpes.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Wiki-Vulpes_vulpes.png/330px-Wiki-Vulpes_vulpes.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Wiki-Vulpes_vulpes.png/440px-Wiki-Vulpes_vulpes.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wiki-Vulpes_vulpes.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The red fox is the most abundant and most widely distributed species of <i>Vulpes</i>. They currently live in most sections of the Northern Hemisphere. They also are present in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, though were brought there by humans for fox hunting in the 1830s and are considered an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>. This species’ ancestor (either <i>V. alopecoides</i> or the related Chinese <i>V. chikushanensi</i>) originated in the Early Pleistocene and are most closely related to Ruppell’s fox (<i>V. rueppellii</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>The fennec fox lives in the northernmost sections of Africa. It was not previously within <i>Vulpes</i>, but genetic evidence shows its close relation with Blanford's fox, making it a true fox.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anatomy">Anatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulpes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Anatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Vulpes</i> has a very similar bone structure to its canid relatives, but does have some modifications. Although canid limbs are designed specifically for running quickly on land to catch prey, <i>Vulpes</i> species avoid rapid sprints, excluding being chased, and have become more specialized for leaping and grasping prey.</p>
<p>The adaptions for leaping, grasping, and climbing include the lengthening of hind limbs in relation to fore limbs, as well as overall slenderizing of both hind and fore limbs. Muscles are also emphasized along the axis of limbs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulpes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This genus is omnivorous and prone to scavenging. The foods of choice for <i>Vulpes</i> consist of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invertebrates" title="Invertebrates">invertebrates</a>, a variety of small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrates" title="Vertebrates">vertebrates</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grasses" title="Grasses">grasses</a>, and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperms" title="Angiosperms">angiosperms</a>. The typical intake per day is about 1 kg. True foxes exhibit hoarding behavior or <a href="/wiki/Hoarding_(animal_behavior)" title="Hoarding (animal behavior)">caching</a> where they store away food for another day out of sight from other animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat">Habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulpes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These foxes can dwell in a number of habitats, including <a href="/wiki/Alpine_tundra" title="Alpine tundra">alpine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastal" title="Coastal">coastal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farm</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban areas</a>, but thrive in environments rich in food and shelter. They can be found in great numbers in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suburban" title="Suburban">suburban</a>/residential regions. For the most part, this coexistence is agreeable for both fox and man, but can sometimes result in house pet (cat) disappearances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predators">Predators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulpes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Predators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">Predators</a> are dependent on location, but commonly include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humans" title="Humans">humans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bears" title="Bears">bears</a>, and large birds of prey, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eagles" title="Eagles">eagles</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulpes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_overview">General overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulpes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: General overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though this varies in intensity from species to species, foxes operate within a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hierarchical" title="Hierarchical">hierarchical</a> society, where dominance is established early in life. Dominant kits receive more food and are subsequently larger. If a dispute in the hierarchy occurs, dominance is determined by fighting. The loser may be subjected to rejection from its social group, as well as serious injuries. These social groups usually consist of three or four adults and have not been documented to surpass 10 adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris.2C_Steven_2010_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harris.2C_Steven_2010-24">[24]</a></sup> <i>Vulpes</i> species are usually nocturnal, but do occasionally hunt and scavenge in daylight during winter.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction">Reproduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulpes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Reproduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A male is referred to as a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dog_fox&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dog fox (page does not exist)">dog</a>, and the female as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vixen" title="Vixen">vixen</a>. They are very similar in appearance, though dogs have larger heads. Mating occurs in late winter. This mating process starts when the vixen digs out an undisclosed number of potential breeding dens and begins to release a mating scent. Gestation takes 7–8 weeks, putting typical birth occurrence in March, and on average, kits begin to emerge in late April. The parents work as a unit in the upbringing of their offspring, but do not mate for life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris.2C_Steven_2010_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harris.2C_Steven_2010-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_birth">After birth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulpes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: After birth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Born deaf and blind, kits or cubs require their mother’s milk and complete supervision for the first four to five weeks out of the womb, but begin to be progressively weaned after the first month. Once fully weaned, kits seek out various insects. The parents supplement this diet with a variety of mammals and birds. During early to middle July, the kits are able to hunt on their own and soon move away from their parents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris.2C_Steven_2010_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Harris.2C_Steven_2010-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestication">Domestication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulpes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Domestication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though rare, <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">domestication</a> has been documented. The most notable case documented is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated_silver_fox" title="Domesticated silver fox">domestication of the silver fox</a> in <a href="/wiki/Novosibirsk" title="Novosibirsk">Novosibirsk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, at the Siberian Institute of Cytology and Genetics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In this study, generations of silver foxes were divided into those with friendly traits and those with unfriendly traits. After 50 years, the friendly foxes developed “dog-like” domesticated traits such as spots, tail wagging, enjoyment of human touch, and barking.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fox_hunting">Fox hunting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vulpes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Fox hunting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fox_hunting" title="Fox hunting">Fox hunting</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fox_hunting" title="Fox hunting">Fox hunting</a> was started in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> in the 16th century that involves tracking, chasing, and killing a fox with the aid of foxhounds and horses. It has since then spread to Europe, the United States, and Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>
