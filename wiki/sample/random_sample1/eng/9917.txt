<p><i><b>The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo</b></i> (original title in <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <span lang="sv" xml:lang="sv"><i>Män som hatar kvinnor</i></span>; in English: <i>Men Who Hate Women</i>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychological_thriller_novel" title="Psychological thriller novel">psychological thriller novel</a> by the late <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> author and journalist <a href="/wiki/Stieg_Larsson" title="Stieg Larsson">Stieg Larsson</a> (1954–2004), which was published posthumously in 2005 to become an international bestseller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smh_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-smh-1">[1]</a></sup> It is the first book of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millennium_series" title="Millennium series"><i>Millennium</i> series</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Girl_with_the_Dragon_Tattoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Larsson spoke of an incident which he said occurred when he was 15: he stood by as three men <a href="/wiki/Gang_rape" title="Gang rape">gang raped</a> an acquaintance of his named Lisbeth. Days later, racked with guilt for having done nothing to help her, he begged her forgiveness—which she refused to grant. The incident, he said, haunted him for years afterward and in part inspired him to create a character named Lisbeth who was also a rape survivor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The veracity of this story has been questioned since Larsson's death, after a colleague from <i>Expo</i> magazine reported to <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> that Larsson had told him he had heard the story secondhand and retold it as his own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Murder_of_Catrine_da_Costa" title="Murder of Catrine da Costa">murder of Catrine da Costa</a> was also an inspiration when he wrote the book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the exception of the fictional Hedestad,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> the novel takes place in actual Swedish towns. The magazine <i>Millennium</i> in the books has characteristics similar to that of Larsson's magazine, <i><a href="/wiki/Expo_(magazine)" title="Expo (magazine)">Expo</a></i>, such as its socio-political leanings and its financial difficulties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Both Larsson's longtime partner <a href="/wiki/Eva_Gabrielsson" title="Eva Gabrielsson">Eva Gabrielsson</a> and English translator <a href="/wiki/Steven_T._Murray" title="Steven T. Murray">Steven T. Murray</a> have said that Christopher Maclehose (who works for British publisher <a href="/wiki/Quercus_(publisher)" title="Quercus (publisher)">Quercus</a>) "needlessly prettified" the English translation; as such, Murray requested he be credited under the pseudonym "Reg Keeland."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcgrath_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcgrath-8">[8]</a></sup> The English release also changed the title, even though Larsson specifically refused to allow the Swedish publisher to do so, and the size of Salander's dragon tattoo; from a large piece covering her entire back, to a small shoulder tattoo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gabrielsson_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gabrielsson-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Girl_with_the_Dragon_Tattoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In December 2002, <a href="/wiki/Mikael_Blomkvist" title="Mikael Blomkvist">Mikael Blomkvist</a>, publisher of the Swedish political magazine <i>Millennium</i>, loses a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libel" title="Libel">libel</a> case involving allegations about billionaire industrialist Hans-Erik Wennerström. Blomkvist is sentenced to three months (deferred) in prison, and ordered to pay hefty damages and costs. Soon afterwards, he is invited to meet Henrik Vanger, the retired CEO of the Vanger Corporation, unaware that Vanger has checked into his personal and professional history; the investigation of Blomkvist's circumstances has been carried out by <a href="/wiki/Lisbeth_Salander" title="Lisbeth Salander">Lisbeth Salander</a>, a brilliant but deeply troubled researcher and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hacker_(computer_security)" title="Hacker (computer security)">computer hacker</a>.</p>
<p>Vanger promises to provide Blomkvist with evidence against Wennerström in return for discovering what happened to Vanger's grandniece, Harriet, who disappeared in 1966; Vanger believes she was murdered by a member of the family. Harriet disappeared during a family gathering at the Vanger estate on Hedeby Island, when the island was temporarily cut off from the mainland by a traffic accident on the bridge.</p>
<p>Blomkvist moves to the island and begins to research the Vanger family history and Harriet's disappearance. As he does so, he meets most of the remaining Vanger clan, including Martin, current CEO of the company; Isabella, Martin's mother; and Cecilia, a headmistress who was Harriet's friend, though her sister Anita was closer both in age and friendship to the missing girl.</p>
<p>Salander was ruled legally incompetent as a child and is under the care of a <a href="/wiki/Legal_guardian" title="Legal guardian">legal guardian</a>, Holger Palmgren. When Palmgren suffers a <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>, he is replaced by Nils Bjurman, who uses his position to extort sexual favors from her and eventually <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rapes</a> her. After using a <a href="/wiki/Hidden_camera" title="Hidden camera">hidden camera</a> to record her assault, Salander takes her revenge, torturing Bjurman and threatening to ruin him unless he gives her full control of her life and finances. She then uses a <a href="/wiki/Tattoo_machine" title="Tattoo machine">tattoo machine</a> to brand him as a rapist.</p>
<p>Despite all expectation to the contrary, Blomkvist identifies new evidence in Harriet's disappearance. One clue is a pair of photographs, detailing Harriet's sudden discomfort at the sight of a young man in a prep school blazer. Another lies in Harriet's journal, which contains a set of five names and five-digit numbers believed to be <a href="/wiki/Telephone_numbers_in_Sweden" title="Telephone numbers in Sweden">old telephone numbers</a>; however, Blomkvist's daughter Pernilla, passing through on the way to Bible camp, identifies them as passages from the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Leviticus" title="Book of Leviticus">Book of Leviticus</a>. They describe rules about the treatment and punishment of women, and Blomkvist correlates one of them with the grotesque murder of a Vanger Corporation secretary in 1949. Blomkvist realizes that he may be on the trail of a <a href="/wiki/Serial_killer" title="Serial killer">serial killer</a>, and the scope of the resulting research makes Blomkvist request a research assistant. Vanger's lawyer suggests Salander.</p>
<p>When he sees the report she prepared for Vanger, Blomkvist realises that Salander has hacked into his computer. He confronts her and asks her to help him with the investigation, to which she agrees. The two eventually become lovers, but Salander continues to keep Blomkvist at an emotional distance. Their suspicions are heightened when a local cat is left dismembered on Blomkvist's porch, and Blomkvist is fired upon from a distance during an afternoon jog.</p>
<p>Blomkvist and Salander uncover the remaining four murders described in Harriet's journal, as well as several more that fit the profile: women who are sexually assaulted and then slain in accordance with (or in an exaggerated parody of) a Leviticus verse. Moreover, most of the murders occurred in locations where the Vanger Corporation did business.</p>
<p>They settle on Gottfried Vanger, Martin and Harriet's father, as a likely candidate but are stymied when he is discovered to have predeceased the last victim. While Salander continues to hunt through Vanger Corporation archives, Blomkvist manages to identify the antagonist who frightened Harriet so: her brother Martin. Martin takes Blomkvist prisoner, reveals that Gottfried "initiated" him into the ritual rape and murder of women before his own death, and implies that Gottfried <a href="/wiki/Child_sexual_abuse" title="Child sexual abuse">sexually abused</a> both him and Harriet. Martin admits to murdering dozens of women but denies killing his sister. He decides to rid himself of Blomkvist once and for all, but Salander—who had discovered the connections independently—arrives and hits Martin over the head with a golf club before cutting Blomkvist out of his restraints. Martin flees by car, pursued by Salander on her motorbike, and chooses his own fate by purposely colliding headlong with an oncoming truck.</p>
<p>Believing that Cecilia's sister Anita, who now lives in London, is the only relative who might know something about Harriet's fate, Blomkvist and Salander pursue that lead and learn Harriet is still alive and is living under Anita's name in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. When Blomkvist flies there to meet her, Harriet tells him the truth about her disappearance: her father and brother had repeatedly raped her, until she killed her father in self-defense. Martin was sent away to preparatory school, but he returned the day of her disappearance. Harriet realized she needed to escape, so she found a place to hide during the tumult of the traffic accident, and Anita smuggled her to the mainland the next morning.</p>
<p>Blomkvist persuades her to return to Sweden, where she reunites with Henrik. Blomkvist then accompanies Salander to her mother's funeral.</p>
<p>Blomkvist learns that the evidence against Wennerström that Vanger promised him is useless, long past its statute of limitations. However, Salander has already hacked Wennerström's computer and discovered that his crimes go far beyond what Blomkvist documented. Using her evidence, Blomkvist prints an exposé and book which ruin Wennerström and catapults <i>Millennium</i> to national prominence. Salander, using her hacking skills, succeeds in stealing some 2.6 billion <a href="/wiki/Swedish_krona" title="Swedish krona">kr</a> (about $260 million <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>) from Wennerström's secret bank account.</p>
<p>Blomkvist and Salander spend Christmas together in his holiday retreat. A couple of days later, she goes to Blomkvist's home, intending to declare her love for him, but backs away when she sees him with his long-time lover and business partner Erika Berger.</p>
<p>As a postscript, Salander continues to monitor Wennerström and after six months, anonymously informs a lawyer in <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a> of his whereabouts. He is found in <a href="/wiki/Marbella" title="Marbella">Marbella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, dead, shot three times in the head.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characters">Characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Girl_with_the_Dragon_Tattoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mikael_Blomkvist" title="Mikael Blomkvist">Mikael Blomkvist</a> – Journalist, publisher, and part-owner of the monthly political magazine <i>Millennium</i></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_themes">Major themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Girl_with_the_Dragon_Tattoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Major themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Larsson makes several literary references to the genre's classic forerunners and comments on contemporary Swedish society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Reviewer <a href="/wiki/Robert_Dessaix" title="Robert Dessaix">Robert Dessaix</a> writes, "His favourite targets are violence against women, the incompetence and cowardice of investigative journalists, the moral bankruptcy of big capital and the virulent strain of Nazism still festering away ... in Swedish society."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smh_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-smh-1">[1]</a></sup> Cecilia Ovesdotter Alm and Anna Westerstahl Stenport write that the novel "reflects—implicitly and explicitly—gaps between rhetoric and practice in Swedish policy and public discourse about complex relations between <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a> retrenchment, <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberal</a> corporate and economic practices, and politicised gender construction. The novel, according to one article, endorses a pragmatic acceptance of a neoliberal world order that is delocalized, dehumanized and <a href="/wiki/Misogyny" title="Misogyny">misogynistic</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alm_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alm-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Alm and Stenport add, "What most international (and Swedish) reviewers overlook is that the financial and moral corruptibility at the heart of <i>The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo</i> is so profound as to indict most attributes associated with contemporary Sweden as democratic and gender-equal. The novel is in fact far from what American critic <a href="/wiki/Maureen_Corrigan" title="Maureen Corrigan">Maureen Corrigan</a> calls an "unflinching ... commonsense feminist social commentary". (Corrigan's article was "Super-Smart Noir With a Feminist Jolt," <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Public_Radio" title="National Public Radio">National Public Radio</a>, 23 September 2008.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alm_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alm-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Larsson further enters the debate as to how responsible criminals are for their crimes, and how much is blamed on upbringing or society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smh_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-smh-1">[1]</a></sup> For instance, Salander has a strong will and assumes that everyone else does, too. She is portrayed as having suffered every kind of abuse in her young life, including an unjustly ordered commitment to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Psychiatric_clinic" title="Psychiatric clinic">psychiatric clinic</a> and subsequent instances of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_assault" title="Sexual assault">sexual assault</a> suffered at the hands of her court-appointed guardian.</p>
<p>Maria de Lurdes Sampaio, in the journal <i>Cross-Cultural Communication</i>, asserts that, "Blomkvist, a modern <a href="/wiki/Theseus" title="Theseus">Theseus</a>, leads us to the labyrinth of the globalized world, while the series' protagonist, Lisbeth Salander, modeled on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_woman" title="Amazon woman">Amazon</a>, is an example of the empowerment of women in crime fiction by playing the role of the 'tough guy' detective, while also personifying the popular roles of the victim, the outcast and the avenger." In this context, she discusses "Dialogues with <a href="/wiki/Greek_tragedy" title="Greek tragedy">Greek tragedy</a>... namely Salander's struggles with strong father figures." Sampaio also argues,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Then, like so many other writers and moviemakers, Larsson plays with people's universal fascination for religious mysteries, enigmas and hermeneutics, while highlighting the way the Bible and other religious books have inspired hideous serial criminals throughout history. There are many passages dedicated to the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>, to the <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">Apocrypha</a> and to the controversies surrounding different Church's branches. The transcription of Latin expressions (e.g., "sola fide" or "claritas scripturae") together with the biblical passages, which provide the clues to unveil the secular mysteries, proves that Larsson was well acquainted with <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Eco" title="Umberto Eco">Umberto Eco</a>'s bestsellers and with similar plots. There are many signs of both <i><a href="/wiki/The_Name_of_the_Rose" title="The Name of the Rose">The Name of the Rose</a></i> and of <i><a href="/wiki/Foucault%27s_Pendulum" title="Foucault's Pendulum">Foucault's Pendulum</a></i> in the <i>Millennium</i> series, and in some sense these two works are contained in the first novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Locked_room_mystery">Locked room mystery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Girl_with_the_Dragon_Tattoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Locked room mystery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Larsson writes within the novel, in Chapter 12, "It's actually a fascinating case. What I believe is known as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locked_room_mystery" title="Locked room mystery">locked room mystery</a>, on an island. And nothing in the investigation seems to follow normal logic. Every question remains unanswered, every clue leads to a dead end." He supplies a family tree delineating the relationships of five generations of the Vanger family.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_and_awards">Reception and awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Girl_with_the_Dragon_Tattoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception and awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The novel was released to great acclaim in Sweden and later, on its publication in many other European countries. In the original language, it won Sweden's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_Key_Award" title="Glass Key Award">Glass Key Award</a> in 2006 for best crime novel of the year. It also won the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_Books_Boeke_Prize" title="Exclusive Books Boeke Prize">Boeke Prize</a>, and in 2009 the Galaxy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Book_Awards" title="British Book Awards">British Book Awards</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> for Books Direct Crime Thriller of the Year, and the prestigious Anthony Award<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> for Best First Novel.</p>
<p>Larsson was awarded the <a href="/wiki/ITV3" title="ITV3">ITV3</a> Crime Thriller Award for International Author of the Year in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><i>The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo</i> received mixed reviews from American critics. It debuted at number four on <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Best_Seller_list" title="The New York Times Best Seller list"><i>The New York Times</i> Best Seller list</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alm_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Alm-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alex_Berenson" title="Alex Berenson">Alex Berenson</a> wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> , "The novel offers a thoroughly ugly view of human nature"; while it "opens with an intriguing mystery" and the "middle section of <i>Girl</i> is a treat, the rest of the novel doesn't quite measure up. The book's original Swedish title was <i>Men Who Hate Women</i>, a label that just about captures the subtlety of the novel's sexual politics."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> said "the book takes off, in the fourth chapter: From there, it becomes classic parlor crime fiction with many modern twists....The writing is not beautiful, clipped at times (though that could be the translation by Reg Keeland) and with a few too many falsely dramatic endings to sections or chapters. But it is a compelling, well-woven tale that succeeds in transporting the reader to rural Sweden for a good crime story."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Several months later, <a href="/wiki/Matt_Selman" title="Matt Selman">Matt Selman</a> said the book "rings false with piles of easy super-victories and far-fetched one-in-a-million clue-findings."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Richard Alleva, in <i><a href="/wiki/Commonweal" title="Commonweal">Commonweal</a></i>, wrote that the novel is marred by "its inept backstory, banal characterizations, flavorless prose, surfeit of themes (Swedish Nazism, uncaring bureaucracy, corporate malfeasance, abuse of women, etc.), and—worst of all—author Larsson's penchant for always telling us exactly what we should be feeling."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the other hand, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Abdallah_Daar" title="Abdallah Daar">Abdallah Daar</a>, writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i>, said, "The events surrounding the great-niece's disappearance are meticulously and ingeniously pieced together, with plenty of scientific insight."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette" title="Pittsburgh Post-Gazette">Pittsburgh Post-Gazette</a></i> wrote, "It's a big, intricately plotted, darkly humorous work, rich with ironies, quirky but believable characters and a literary playfulness that only a master of the genre and its history could bring off."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>As of 3 June 2011, <i>The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo</i> had sold over 3.4 million copies in hardcover or ebook formats, and 15 million copies altogether, in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Book_of_essays">Book of essays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Girl_with_the_Dragon_Tattoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Book of essays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wiley published a collection of essays, edited by Eric Bronson, titled <i>The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo and Philosophy</i> (2011).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_adaptations">Film adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Girl_with_the_Dragon_Tattoo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Film adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/The_Girl_with_the_Dragon_Tattoo_(2009_film)" title="The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo (2009 film)">The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo (2009 film)</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Girl_with_the_Dragon_Tattoo_(2011_film)" title="The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo (2011 film)">The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo (2011 film)</a></div>
<ul>
<li>The Swedish film production company <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Bird_(company)" title="Yellow Bird (company)">Yellow Bird</a> created film versions of the first three <i>Millennium</i> books, all three films released in 2009, beginning with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Girl_with_the_Dragon_Tattoo_(2009_film)" title="The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo (2009 film)">The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo</a></i>, directed by Danish filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Niels_Arden_Oplev" title="Niels Arden Oplev">Niels Arden Oplev</a>. The protagonists were played by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Nyqvist" title="Michael Nyqvist">Michael Nyqvist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noomi_Rapace" title="Noomi Rapace">Noomi Rapace</a>.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/The_Girl_with_the_Dragon_Tattoo_(2011_film)" title="The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo (2011 film)">Hollywood film adaptation of the book</a>, directed by <a href="/wiki/David_Fincher" title="David Fincher">David Fincher</a>, was released in December 2011. The main characters were portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Craig" title="Daniel Craig">Daniel Craig</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Rooney_Mara" title="Rooney Mara">Rooney Mara</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Millennium_(miniseries)" title="Millennium (miniseries)">Millennium</a></i>, a Swedish six-part television miniseries based on the film adaptations of Stieg Larsson's series of the same name, was broadcast on SVT1 from 20 March 2010 to 24 April 2010. The series was produced by Yellow Bird in cooperation with several production companies, including SVT, Nordisk Film, Film i Västm, and ZDF Enterprises.</li>
<li><i>Dragon Tattoo Trilogy: Extended Edition</i> is the title of the TV miniseries release on DVD, Blu-ray, and video on demand in the US. This version of the miniseries comprises nine hours of story content, including over two hours of additional footage not seen in the theatrical versions of the original Swedish films. The four-disc set includes: <i>THE GIRL WITH THE DRAGON TATTOO – EXTENDED EDITION</i>, <i>THE GIRL WHO PLAYED WITH FIRE – EXTENDED EDITION</i>, <i>THE GIRL WHO KICKED THE HORNET’S NEST – EXTENDED EDITION</i>, and a BONUS DISC including two hours of special features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
