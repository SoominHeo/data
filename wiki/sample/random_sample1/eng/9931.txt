<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mechanics</h1>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">This article is about an area of scientific study. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Mechanic_(disambiguation)" title="Mechanic (disambiguation)">Mechanic (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Mechanics</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">μηχανική</span>) is an area of <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> concerned with the behaviour of <a href="/wiki/Physical_body" title="Physical body">physical bodies</a> when subjected to <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">forces</a> or <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(vector)" title="Displacement (vector)">displacements</a>, and the subsequent effects of the bodies on their environment. The scientific discipline has its origins in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a> with the writings of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> (see <a href="/wiki/History_of_classical_mechanics" title="History of classical mechanics">History of classical mechanics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_classical_mechanics" title="Timeline of classical mechanics">Timeline of classical mechanics</a>). During the <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">early modern period</a>, scientists such as <a href="/wiki/Omar_Khayyam" title="Omar Khayyam">Khayaam</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo">Galileo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">Kepler</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a>, laid the foundation for what is now known as <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>. It is a branch of classical physics that deals with particles that are either at rest or are moving with velocities significantly less than the speed of light. It can also be defined as a branch of science which deals with the motion of and forces on objects.</p>

<p>Historically, <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a> came first, while <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> is a comparatively recent invention. Classical mechanics originated with <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">laws of motion</a> in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Philosophic_Naturalist_Principal_Mathematical&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Philosophic Naturalist Principal Mathematical (page does not exist)"><i>Principal Mathematical</i></a>; Quantum Mechanics was discovered in the early 20th century. Both are commonly held to constitute the most certain knowledge that exists about physical nature. Classical mechanics has especially often been viewed as a model for other so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exact_science" title="Exact science">exact sciences</a>. Essential in this respect is the relentless use of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> in theories, as well as the decisive role played by <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experiment</a> in generating and testing them.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">Quantum</a> mechanics is of a bigger scope, as it encompasses classical mechanics as a sub-discipline which applies under certain restricted circumstances. According to the <a href="/wiki/Correspondence_principle" title="Correspondence principle">correspondence principle</a>, there is no contradiction or conflict between the two subjects, each simply pertains to specific situations. The correspondence principle states that the behavior of systems described by quantum theories reproduces classical physics in the limit of large quantum numbers. Quantum mechanics has superseded classical mechanics at the foundation level and is indispensable for the explanation and prediction of processes at the molecular, atomic, and sub-atomic level. However, for macroscopic processes classical mechanics is able to solve problems which are unmanageable difficult in quantum mechanics and hence remains useful and well used. Modern descriptions of such behavior begin with a careful definition of such quantities as displacement (distance moved), time, velocity, acceleration, mass, and force. Until about 400 years ago, however, motion was explained from a very different point of view. For example, following the ideas of Greek philosopher and scientist Aristotle, scientists reasoned that a cannonball falls down because its natural position is in the Earth; the sun, the moon, and the stars travel in circles around the earth because it is the nature of heavenly objects to travel in perfect circles.</p>
<p>Often cited as the father of modern science, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo">Galileo</a> brought together the ideas of other great thinkers of his time and began to analyze motion in terms of distance traveled from some starting position and the time that it took. He showed that the speed of falling objects increases steadily during the time of their fall. This acceleration is the same for heavy objects as for light ones, provided air friction (air resistance) is discounted. The English mathematician and physicist Isaac Newton improved this analysis by defining force and mass and relating these to acceleration. For objects traveling at speeds close to the speed of light, Newton’s laws were superseded by Albert Einstein’s theory of relativity. For atomic and subatomic particles, Newton’s laws were superseded by quantum theory. For everyday phenomena, however, Newton’s three laws of motion remain the cornerstone of dynamics, which is the study of what causes motion.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relativistic_versus_Newtonian_mechanics">Relativistic versus Newtonian mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relativistic versus Newtonian mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In analogy to the distinction between quantum and classical mechanics, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Einstein" title="Einstein">Einstein</a>'s <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general</a> and <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special</a> theories of <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">relativity</a> have expanded the scope of <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo">Galileo</a>'s formulation of mechanics. The differences between relativistic and Newtonian mechanics become significant and even dominant as the velocity of a massive body approaches the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a>. For instance, in <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">Newtonian mechanics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">Newton's laws of motion</a> specify that <b>F</b> = <i>m</i><b>a</b>, whereas in <a href="/wiki/Relativistic_mechanics" title="Relativistic mechanics">Relativistic mechanics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorentz_transformations" title="Lorentz transformations">Lorentz transformations</a>, which were first discovered by <a href="/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz" title="Hendrik Lorentz">Hendrik Lorentz</a>, <b>F</b> = γ<i>m</i><b>a</b> (where γ is the <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_factor" title="Lorentz factor">Lorentz factor</a>, which is almost equal to 1 for low speeds).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_relativistic_versus_quantum">General relativistic versus quantum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: General relativistic versus quantum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Relativistic corrections are also needed for quantum mechanics, although general relativity has not been integrated. The two theories remain incompatible, a hurdle which must be overcome in developing a <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_everything" title="Theory of everything">theory of everything</a>.</p>

<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_classical_mechanics" title="History of classical mechanics">History of classical mechanics</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_quantum_mechanics" title="History of quantum mechanics">History of quantum mechanics</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aristotelian_mechanics" title="Aristotelian mechanics">Aristotelian mechanics</a></div>
<p>The main theory of mechanics in antiquity was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aristotelian_mechanics" title="Aristotelian mechanics">Aristotelian mechanics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A later developer in this tradition is <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">Hipparchus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mechanics_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-mechanics-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_age">Medieval age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Medieval age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In the Middle Ages, Aristotle's theories were criticized and modified by a number of figures, beginning with <a href="/wiki/John_Philoponus" title="John Philoponus">John Philoponus</a> in the 6th century. A central problem was that of <a href="/wiki/Projectile_motion" title="Projectile motion">projectile motion</a>, which was discussed by Hipparchus and Philoponus. This led to the development of the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_impetus" title="Theory of impetus">theory of impetus</a> by 14th-century French priest <a href="/wiki/Jean_Buridan" title="Jean Buridan">Jean Buridan</a>, which developed into the modern theories of <a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">acceleration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a>. This work and others was developed in 14th-century England by the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Calculators" title="Oxford Calculators">Oxford Calculators</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bradwardine" title="Thomas Bradwardine">Thomas Bradwardine</a>, who studied and formulated various laws regarding falling bodies.</p>
<p>On the question of a body subject to a constant (uniform) force, the 12th-century Jewish-Arab <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hibat_Allah_Abu%27l-Barakat_al-Baghdaadi" title="Hibat Allah Abu'l-Barakat al-Baghdaadi">Nathanel</a> (Iraqi, of Baghdad) stated that constant force imparts constant acceleration, while the main properties are uniformly accelerated motion (as of falling bodies) was worked out by the 14th-century Oxford Calculators.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_age">Early modern age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Early modern age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two central figures in the early modern age are <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>. Galileo's final statement of his mechanics, particularly of falling bodies, is his <i><a href="/wiki/Two_New_Sciences" title="Two New Sciences">Two New Sciences</a></i> (1638). Newton's 1687 <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica">Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica</a></i> provided a detailed mathematical account of mechanics, using the newly developed mathematics of <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a> and providing the basis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics" title="Newtonian mechanics">Newtonian mechanics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mechanics_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-mechanics-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>There is some dispute over priority of various ideas: Newton's <i>Principia</i> is certainly the seminal work and has been tremendously influential, and the systematic mathematics therein did not and could not have been stated earlier because calculus had not been developed. However, many of the ideas, particularly as pertain to inertia (impetus) and falling bodies had been developed and stated by earlier researchers, both the then-recent Galileo and the less-known medieval predecessors. Precise credit is at times difficult or contentious because scientific language and standards of proof changed, so whether medieval statements are <i>equivalent</i> to modern statements or <i>sufficient</i> proof, or instead <i>similar</i> to modern statements and <i>hypotheses</i> is often debatable.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_age">Modern age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Modern age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two main modern developments in mechanics are <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einstein</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>, both developed in the 20th century based in part on earlier 19th-century ideas. The development in the modern continuum mechanics, particularly in the areas of elasticity, plasticity, fluid dynamics, electrodynamics and thermodynamics of deformable media, started in the second half of the 20th century.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_mechanical_bodies">Types of mechanical bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Types of mechanical bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The often-used term <b><a href="/wiki/Physical_body" title="Physical body">body</a></b> needs to stand for a wide assortment of objects, including particles, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Projectiles" title="Projectiles">projectiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>, parts of <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">machinery</a>, parts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solids" title="Solids">solids</a>, parts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluids" title="Fluids">fluids</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gases" title="Gases">gases</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquids" title="Liquids">liquids</a>), etc.</p>
<p>Other distinctions between the various sub-disciplines of mechanics, concern the nature of the bodies being described. Particles are bodies with little (known) internal structure, treated as mathematical points in classical mechanics. Rigid bodies have size and shape, but retain a simplicity close to that of the particle, adding just a few so-called <a href="/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(physics_and_chemistry)" title="Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)">degrees of freedom</a>, such as orientation in space.</p>
<p>Otherwise, bodies may be semi-rigid, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(physics)" title="Elasticity (physics)">elastic</a>, or non-rigid, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluid</a>. These subjects have both classical and quantum divisions of study.</p>
<p>For instance, the motion of a spacecraft, regarding its <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> and attitude (<a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotation</a>), is described by the relativistic theory of classical mechanics, while the analogous movements of an <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">atomic nucleus</a> are described by quantum mechanics.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-disciplines_in_mechanics">Sub-disciplines in mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sub-disciplines in mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following are two lists of various subjects that are studied in mechanics.</p>
<p>Note that there is also the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_theory_(physics)" title="Field theory (physics)">theory of fields</a>" which constitutes a separate discipline in physics, formally treated as distinct from mechanics, whether <a href="/wiki/Classical_field_theory" title="Classical field theory">classical fields</a> or <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">quantum fields</a>. But in actual practice, subjects belonging to mechanics and fields are closely interwoven. Thus, for instance, forces that act on particles are frequently derived from fields (<a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitational" title="Gravitational">gravitational</a>), and particles generate fields by acting as sources. In fact, in quantum mechanics, particles themselves are fields, as described theoretically by the <a href="/wiki/Wave_function" title="Wave function">wave function</a>.</p>

<p>The following are described as forming classical mechanics:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">Newtonian mechanics</a>, the original theory of motion (<a href="/wiki/Kinematics" title="Kinematics">kinematics</a>) and forces (<a href="/wiki/Analytical_dynamics" title="Analytical dynamics">dynamics</a>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Analytical_mechanics" title="Analytical mechanics">Analytical mechanics</a> is a reformulation of Newtonian mechanics with an emphasis on system energy, rather than on forces. There are two main branches of analytical mechanics:
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics" title="Hamiltonian mechanics">Hamiltonian mechanics</a>, a theoretical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formalism_(mathematics)" title="Formalism (mathematics)">formalism</a>, based on the principle of conservation of energy.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics" title="Lagrangian mechanics">Lagrangian mechanics</a>, another theoretical formalism, based on the principle of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Least_action" title="Least action">least action</a>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_statistical_mechanics" title="Classical statistical mechanics">Classical statistical mechanics</a> generalizes ordinary classical mechanics to consider systems in an unknown state; often used to derive <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamic</a> properties.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Celestial_mechanics" title="Celestial mechanics">Celestial mechanics</a>, the motion of bodies in space: planets, comets, stars, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxies" title="Galaxies">galaxies</a>, etc.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrodynamics" title="Astrodynamics">Astrodynamics</a>, spacecraft <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a>, etc.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Solid_mechanics" title="Solid mechanics">Solid mechanics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(physics)" title="Elasticity (physics)">elasticity</a>, the properties of deformable bodies.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fracture_mechanics" title="Fracture mechanics">Fracture mechanics</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">Acoustics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> ( = density variation propagation) in solids, fluids and gases.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Statics" title="Statics">Statics</a>, semi-rigid bodies in <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium" title="Mechanical equilibrium">mechanical equilibrium</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">Fluid mechanics</a>, the motion of fluids</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Soil_mechanics" title="Soil mechanics">Soil mechanics</a>, mechanical behavior of soils</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Continuum_mechanics" title="Continuum mechanics">Continuum mechanics</a>, mechanics of continua (both solid and fluid)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydraulics" title="Hydraulics">Hydraulics</a>, mechanical properties of liquids</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_statics" title="Fluid statics">Fluid statics</a>, liquids in equilibrium</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Applied_mechanics" title="Applied mechanics">Applied mechanics, or Engineering mechanics</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biomechanics" title="Biomechanics">Biomechanics</a>, solids, fluids, etc. in biology</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biophysics" title="Biophysics">Biophysics</a>, physical processes in living organisms</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relativistic_physics" title="Relativistic physics">Relativistic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Einsteinian</a> mechanics, universal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">gravitation</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantum_mechanics">Quantum mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mechanics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Quantum mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following are categorized as being part of <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrödinger equation">Schrödinger wave mechanics</a>, used to describe the movements of the wavefunction of a single particle.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matrix_mechanics" title="Matrix mechanics">Matrix mechanics</a> is an alternative formulation that allows considering systems with a finite-dimensional state space.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Quantum_statistical_mechanics" title="Quantum statistical mechanics">Quantum statistical mechanics</a> generalizes ordinary quantum mechanics to consider systems in an unknown state; often used to derive <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamic</a> properties.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">Particle physics</a>, the motion, structure, and reactions of particles</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">Nuclear physics</a>, the motion, structure, and reactions of nuclei</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics" title="Condensed matter physics">Condensed matter physics</a>, quantum gases, solids, liquids, etc.</li>
</ul>
