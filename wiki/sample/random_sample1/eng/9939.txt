<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Federalist No. 10</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Federalist No. 10</b> (<b>Federalist Number 10</b>) is an essay written by <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">James Madison</a> as the tenth of <a href="/wiki/The_Federalist_Papers" title="The Federalist Papers"><i>The Federalist</i> Papers</a>, a series of essays initiated by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Hamilton" title="Alexander Hamilton">Alexander Hamilton</a> arguing for the ratification of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>. Hamilton chose and invited <a href="/wiki/John_Jay" title="John Jay">John Jay</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">James Madison</a> to contribute as co-authors under the pseudonym "Publius" (the pseudonym under which all of <i>The Federalist</i> Papers were published, and under which Alexander Hamilton started the series with the <a href="/wiki/Federalist_No._1" title="Federalist No. 1">Federalist No. 1</a>). Published on November 29, 1787, <i>Federalist No. 10</i> is among the most highly regarded of all American political writings.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Ep59"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_Ep59">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>No. 10 addresses the question of how to guard against "<a href="/wiki/Political_faction" title="Political faction">factions</a>", or groups of citizens, with interests contrary to the rights of others or the interests of the whole community. Madison saw factions as inevitable due to the nature of man - that is, as long as men hold differing opinions, have differing amounts of wealth, and own differing amount of property, they will continue to form alliances with people who are most similar to them, and they will sometimes work against the public interest, and infringe upon the rights of others. Thus, he questions how to guard against those dangers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Federalist No. 10 continues a theme begun in <a href="/wiki/Federalist_No._9" title="Federalist No. 9">Federalist No. 9</a>; it is titled, "The Same Subject Continued: The Utility of the Union as a Safeguard Against Domestic Faction and Insurrection". The whole series is cited by scholars and <a href="/wiki/Jurist" title="Jurist">jurists</a> as an authoritative interpretation and explication of the meaning of the Constitution. Jurists have frequently read No. 10 to mean that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Founding_Fathers" title="Founding Fathers">Founding Fathers</a> did not intend the United States government to be <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">partisan</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and others have argued that given Madison's clear understanding that partisanship is inevitable, he suggests that a <a href="/wiki/Representative_democracy" title="Representative democracy">representative republic</a> form of government is more effective against factions than a <a href="/wiki/Direct_democracy" title="Direct democracy">direct democracy</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Thus, Madison saw the Constitution as forming a "happy combination" of a republic and a democracy and with "the great and aggregate interests being referred to the national, the local and particular to the State legislatures" the power would not be centralized in a way that would make it "more difficult for unworthy candidates to practice the vicious arts by which elections are too often carried."</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federalist_No._10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Constitution_of_the_United_States,_page_1.jpg"><img alt='Light-brown parchment with "We the people" in large black cursive' class="thumbimage" data-file-height="8785" data-file-width="7258" height="266" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Constitution_of_the_United_States%2C_page_1.jpg/220px-Constitution_of_the_United_States%2C_page_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Constitution_of_the_United_States%2C_page_1.jpg/330px-Constitution_of_the_United_States%2C_page_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Constitution_of_the_United_States%2C_page_1.jpg/440px-Constitution_of_the_United_States%2C_page_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Constitution_of_the_United_States,_page_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Preamble to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">U.S. Constitution</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Prior to the Constitution, the thirteen states were bound together by the <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_Confederation" title="Articles of Confederation">Articles of Confederation</a>. These were in essence a military alliance between sovereign nations adopted to better fight the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revolutionary_War" title="Revolutionary War">Revolutionary War</a>. Congress had no power to tax, and as a result was not able to pay debts resulting from the Revolution. Madison, <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> and others feared a break-up of the union and national bankruptcy.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Bernstein"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_Bernstein">[2]</a></sup> Like Washington, Madison felt the revolution had not resolved the social problems that had triggered it, and the excesses ascribed to the King were now being repeated by the state legislatures. In this view, <a href="/wiki/Shays%27_Rebellion" title="Shays' Rebellion">Shays' Rebellion</a>, an armed uprising in Massachusetts in 1786, was simply one, albeit extreme, example of "democratic excess" in the aftermath of the War<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Wood104"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_Wood104">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Convention_(United_States)" title="Constitutional Convention (United States)">national convention</a> was called for May 1787, to revise the Articles of Confederation. Madison believed that the problem was not with the Articles, but rather the state legislatures, and so the solution was not to fix the articles but to restrain the excesses of the states. The principal questions before the convention became whether the states should remain sovereign, whether sovereignty should be transferred to the national government, or whether a settlement should rest somewhere in between.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Wood183"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_Wood183">[4]</a></sup> By mid-June it was clear that the convention was drafting a new plan of government around these issues—a constitution. Madison's nationalist position shifted the debate increasingly away from a position of pure state sovereignty, and toward the compromise.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Stewart182"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_Stewart182">[5]</a></sup> In debate on June 26, he said that government ought to "protect the minority of <a href="/wiki/Tyranny_of_the_majority" title="Tyranny of the majority">the opulent against the majority</a>" and that unchecked, democratic communities were subject to "the turbulency and weakness of unruly passions".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Yates"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_Yates">[6]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication">Publication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federalist_No._10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Publication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/w/index.php?title=File:The_Federalist_(Ford_ed,_1898).djvu&amp;page=132"><img alt="Black text on white in old-fashioned type" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2320" data-file-width="1518" height="353" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/The_Federalist_%28Ford_ed%2C_1898%29.djvu/page132-220px-The_Federalist_%28Ford_ed%2C_1898%29.djvu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/The_Federalist_%28Ford_ed%2C_1898%29.djvu/page132-330px-The_Federalist_%28Ford_ed%2C_1898%29.djvu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/The_Federalist_%28Ford_ed%2C_1898%29.djvu/page132-440px-The_Federalist_%28Ford_ed%2C_1898%29.djvu.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Federalist_(Ford_ed,_1898).djvu?page=132" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Paul Leicester Ford's summary preceding Federalist No. 10, from his 1898 edition of <i>The Federalist</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>September 17, 1787 marked the signing of the final document. By its own <a href="/wiki/Article_Seven_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article Seven of the United States Constitution">Article Seven</a>, the constitution drafted by the convention needed ratification by at least nine of the thirteen states, through special conventions held in each state. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-Federalist" title="Anti-Federalist">Anti-Federalist</a> writers began to publish essays and letters arguing against ratification,<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_who"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_who">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Hamilton" title="Alexander Hamilton">Alexander Hamilton</a> recruited <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">James Madison</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Jay" title="John Jay">John Jay</a> to write a series of pro-ratification letters in response.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Ball"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_Ball">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Like most of the Federalist essays and the vast majority of <i>The Federalist</i> Papers, No. 10 first appeared in popular newspapers. It was first printed in the <i>Daily Advertiser</i> under the name adopted by the Federalist writers, "Publius"; in this it was remarkable among the essays of Publius, as almost all of them first appeared in one of two other papers: the <i>Independent Journal</i> and the <i>New-York Packet</i>. <a href="/wiki/Federalist_No._37" title="Federalist No. 37">Federalist No. 37</a>, also by Madison, was the only other essay to appear first in the <i>Advertiser</i>.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_const"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_const">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Considering the importance later ascribed to the essay, it was reprinted only on a limited scale. On November 23, it appeared in the <i>Packet</i> and the next day in the <i>Independent Journal</i>. Outside New York City, it made four appearances in early 1788: January 2 in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Gazette_(newspaper)" title="Pennsylvania Gazette (newspaper)">Pennsylvania Gazette</a></i>, January 10 in the <i>Hudson Valley Weekly</i>, January 15 in the Lansingburgh <i>Northern Centinel</i>, and January 17 in the <i>Albany Gazette</i>. Though this number of reprintings was typical for <i>The Federalist</i> essays, many other essays, both Federalist and Anti-Federalist, saw much wider distribution.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_DHRC"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_DHRC">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>On January 1, 1788, the publishing company J. &amp; A. McLean announced that they would publish the first 36 of the essays in a single volume. This volume, titled <i>The Federalist</i>, was released on March 2, 1788. George Hopkins' 1802 edition revealed that Madison, Hamilton, and Jay were the authors of the series, with two later printings dividing the work by author. In 1818, James Gideon published a third edition containing corrections by Madison, who by that time had completed his two terms as President of the United States.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_spark"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_spark">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Henry B. Dawson's edition of 1863 sought to collect the original newspaper articles, though he <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Page:Federalist,_Dawson_edition,_1863.djvu/199" title="s:Page:Federalist, Dawson edition, 1863.djvu/199">did not always find the first instance</a>. It was much reprinted, albeit without his introduction.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_fordintro"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_fordintro">[12]</a></sup> Paul Leicester Ford's 1898 edition included a table of contents which summarized the essays, with the summaries again used to preface their respective essays. The first date of publication and the newspaper name were recorded for each essay. Of modern editions, Jacob E. Cooke's 1961 edition is seen as authoritative, and is most used today.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_cooke"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_cooke">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_question_of_faction">The question of faction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federalist_No._10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The question of faction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Federalist No. 10 continues the discussion of the question broached in Hamilton's <a href="/wiki/Federalist_No._9" title="Federalist No. 9">Federalist No. 9</a>. Hamilton there addressed the destructive role of a <a href="/wiki/Political_faction" title="Political faction">faction</a> in breaking apart the republic. The question Madison answers, then, is how to eliminate the negative effects of faction. Madison defines a faction as "a number of citizens, whether amounting to a minority or majority of the whole, who are united and actuated by some common impulse of passion, or of interest, adverse to the rights of other citizens, or to the permanent and aggregate interests of the community".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_fed1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_fed1">[14]</a></sup> He identifies the most serious source of faction to be the diversity of opinion in political life which leads to dispute over fundamental issues such as what regime or religion should be preferred.</p>
<p>At the heart of Madison's fears about factions was the unequal distribution of property in society. Ultimately, "the most common and durable source of factions has been the various and unequal distribution of property," Madison argues (Dawson 1863, p. 58). Since some people owned property and others owned none, Madison felt that people would form different factions that pursued different interests. "Those who hold and those who are without property have ever formed distinct interests in society," he notes (Dawson 1863, p. 58). Providing some examples of the distinct interests, Madison identified a landed interest, a manufacturing interest, a mercantile interest, a moneyed interest, and "many lesser interests" (Dawson 1863, p. 58). They all belonged to "different classes" that were "actuated by different sentiments and views," Madison insists (Dawson 1863, p. 58). In other words, Madison argued that the unequal distribution of property led to the creation of different classes that formed different factions and pursued different class interests.</p>
<p>Moreover, Madison feared the formation of a certain kind of faction. Recognizing that the country's wealthiest property owners formed a minority and that the country's unpropertied classes formed a majority, Madison feared that the unpropertied classes would come together to form a majority faction that gained control of the government. Against "the minor party," there could emerge "an interested and overbearing majority," Madison warns (Dawson 1863, p. 55-56). Specifically, Madison feared that the unpropertied classes would use their majority power to implement a variety of measures that redistributed wealth. There could be "a rage for paper money, for an abolition of debts, for an equal division of property, or for any other improper or wicked project," Madison warns (Dawson 1863, p. 64). In short, Madison feared that a majority faction of the unpropertied classes might emerge to redistribute wealth and property in a way that benefited the majority of the population at the expense of the country's richest and wealthiest people.</p>
<p>Like the anti-Federalists who opposed him, Madison was substantially influenced by the work of Montesquieu, though Madison and Montesquieu disagreed on the question addressed in this essay. He also relied heavily on the philosophers of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Enlightenment" title="Scottish Enlightenment">Scottish Enlightenment</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a>, whose influence is most clear in Madison's discussion of the types of faction and in his argument for an extended republic.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_cohler"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_cohler">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Adairhume"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_Adairhume">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Madison.27s_arguments">Madison's arguments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federalist_No._10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Madison's arguments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Madison first assessed that there are two ways to limit the damage caused by faction: either remove the causes of faction or control its effects. He then describes the two methods to removing faction: first, destroying liberty, which would work because "liberty is to faction what air is to fire",<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_fed4"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_fed4">[17]</a></sup> but it is impossible to perform because liberty is essential to political life. After all, Americans fought for it during the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. The second option, creating a society homogeneous in opinions and interests, is impracticable. The diversity of the people's ability is what makes them succeed more or less, and inequality of property is a right that the government should protect. Madison particularly emphasizes that economic stratification prevents everyone from sharing the same opinion. Madison concludes that the damage caused by faction can be limited only by controlling its effects.</p>
<p>He then argues that the only problem comes from majority factions because the principle of popular sovereignty should prevent minority factions from gaining power. Madison offers two ways to check majority factions: prevent the "existence of the same passion or interest in a majority at the same time" or render a majority faction unable to act.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_fed5"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_fed5">[18]</a></sup> Madison concludes that a small democracy cannot avoid the dangers of majority faction because small size means that undesirable passions can very easily spread to a majority of the people, which can then enact its will through the democratic government without difficulty.</p>
<p>Madison states, "The latent causes of faction are thus sown in the nature of man",<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_fed6"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_fed6">[19]</a></sup> so the cure is to control their effects. He makes an argument on how this is not possible in a pure democracy but possible in a republic. With pure democracy, he means a system in which every citizen votes directly for laws, and, with republic, he intends a society in which citizens elect a small body of representatives who then vote for laws. He indicates that the voice of the people pronounced by a body of representatives is more conformable to the interest of the community, since, again, common people's decisions are affected by their self-interest.</p>
<p>He then makes an argument in favor of a large republic against a small republic for the choice of "fit characters"<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_fed7"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_fed7">[20]</a></sup> to represent the public's voice. In a large republic, where the number of voters and candidates is greater, the probability to elect competent representatives is broader. The voters have a wider option. In a small republic, it would also be easier for the candidates to fool the voters but more difficult in a large one. The last argument Madison makes in favor of a large republic is that as, in a small republic, there will be a lower variety of interests and parties, a majority will more frequently be found. The number of participants of that majority will be lower, and, since they live in a more limited territory, it would be easier for them to agree and work together for the accomplishment of their ideas. While in a large republic the variety of interests will be greater so to make it harder to find a majority. Even if there is a majority, it would be harder for them to work together because of the large number of people and the fact they are spread out in a wider territory.</p>
<p>A republic, Madison writes, is different from a democracy because its government is placed in the hands of delegates, and, as a result of this, it can be extended over a larger area. The idea is that, in a large republic, there will be more "fit characters" to choose from for each delegate. Also, the fact that each representative is chosen from a larger constituency should make the "vicious arts" of electioneering <sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_fed8"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_fed8">[21]</a></sup> (a reference to rhetoric) less effective. For instance, in a large republic, a corrupt delegate would need to bribe many more people in order to win an election than in a small republic. Also, in a republic, the delegates both filter and refine the many demands of the people so as to prevent the type of frivolous claims that impede purely democratic governments.</p>
<p>Though Madison argued for a large and diverse republic, the writers of the Federalist Papers recognized the need for a balance. They wanted a republic diverse enough to prevent faction but with enough commonality to maintain cohesion among the states. In <a href="/wiki/Federalist_No._2" title="Federalist No. 2">Federalist No. 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Jay" title="John Jay">John Jay</a> counted as a blessing that America possessed "one united people—a people descended from the same ancestors, the same language, professing the same religion".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_jay1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_jay1">[22]</a></sup> Madison himself addresses a limitation of his conclusion that large constituencies will provide better representatives. He notes that if constituencies are too large, the representatives will be "too little acquainted with all their local circumstances and lesser interests".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_fed9"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_fed9">[23]</a></sup> He says that this problem is partly solved by <a href="/wiki/Federalism" title="Federalism">federalism</a>. No matter how large the constituencies of federal representatives, local matters will be looked after by state and local officials with naturally smaller constituencies.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporaneous_counterarguments">Contemporaneous counterarguments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federalist_No._10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Contemporaneous counterarguments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:George_Clinton_by_Ezra_Ames.jpg"><img alt="Portrait of George Clinton" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="547" data-file-width="423" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/George_Clinton_by_Ezra_Ames.jpg/170px-George_Clinton_by_Ezra_Ames.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/George_Clinton_by_Ezra_Ames.jpg/255px-George_Clinton_by_Ezra_Ames.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/George_Clinton_by_Ezra_Ames.jpg/340px-George_Clinton_by_Ezra_Ames.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:George_Clinton_by_Ezra_Ames.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
George Clinton, believed to be the Anti-Federalist writer Cato</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-Federalists" title="Anti-Federalists">Anti-Federalists</a> vigorously contested the notion that a republic of diverse interests could survive. The author Cato (another pseudonym, most likely that of <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(vice_president)" title="George Clinton (vice president)">George Clinton</a>)<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_anti-fedintro"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_anti-fedintro">[24]</a></sup> summarized the Anti-Federalist position in the article Cato no. 3:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Whoever seriously considers the immense extent of territory comprehended within the limits of the United States, with the variety of its climates, productions, and commerce, the difference of extent, and number of inhabitants in all; the dissimilitude of interest, morals, and policies, in almost every one, will receive it as an intuitive truth, that a consolidated republican form of government therein, can never <i>form a perfect union, establish justice, insure domestic tranquility, promote the general welfare, and secure the blessings of liberty to you and your posterity,</i> for to these objects it must be directed: this unkindred legislature therefore, composed of interests opposite and dissimilar in their nature, will in its exercise, emphatically be, like a house divided against itself.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_cato"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_cato">[25]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Generally, it was their position that republics about the size of the individual states could survive, but that a republic on the size of the Union would fail. A particular point in support of this was that most of the states were focused on one industry—to generalize, commerce and shipping in the northern states and plantation farming in the southern. The Anti-Federalist belief that the wide disparity in the economic interests of the various states would lead to controversy was perhaps realized in the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, which some scholars attribute to this disparity.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_ransom"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_ransom">[26]</a></sup> Madison himself, in a letter to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Thomas Jefferson</a>, noted that differing economic interests had created dispute, even when the Constitution was being written.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_mad-jef"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_mad-jef">[27]</a></sup> At the convention, he particularly identified the distinction between the northern and southern states as a "line of discrimination" that formed "the real difference of interests".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_cohler151"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_cohler151">[28]</a></sup></p>
<p>The discussion of the ideal size for the republic was not limited to the options of individual states or encompassing union. In a letter to <a href="/wiki/Richard_Price" title="Richard Price">Richard Price</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Rush" title="Benjamin Rush">Benjamin Rush</a> noted that "Some of our enlightened men who begin to despair of a more complete union of the States in Congress have secretly proposed an Eastern, Middle, and Southern Confederacy, to be united by an alliance offensive and defensive".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_rush"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_rush">[29]</a></sup></p>
<p>In making their arguments, the Anti-Federalists appealed to both historical and theoretic evidence. On the theoretical side, they leaned heavily on the work of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_de_Secondat,_Baron_de_Montesquieu" title="Charles de Secondat, Baron de Montesquieu">Charles de Secondat, Baron de Montesquieu</a>. The Anti-Federalists <a href="/wiki/Robert_Yates_(politician)" title="Robert Yates (politician)">Brutus</a> and Cato both quoted Montesquieu on the issue of the ideal size of a republic, citing his statement in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spirit_of_the_Laws" title="The Spirit of the Laws">The Spirit of the Laws</a></i> that:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>It is natural to a republic to have only a small territory, otherwise it cannot long subsist. In a large republic there are men of large fortunes, and consequently of less moderation; there are trusts too great to be placed in any single subject; he has interest of his own; he soon begins to think that he may be happy, great and glorious, by oppressing his fellow citizens; and that he may raise himself to grandeur on the ruins of his country. In a large republic, the public good is sacrificed to a thousand views; it is subordinate to exceptions, and depends on accidents. In a small one, the interest of the public is easier perceived, better understood, and more within the reach of every citizen; abuses are of less extent, and of course are less protected.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_inbrutus"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_inbrutus">[30]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Greece and Rome were looked to as model republics throughout this debate,<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_yatescon"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_yatescon">[31]</a></sup> and authors on both sides took Roman pseudonyms. Brutus points out that the Greek and Roman states were small, whereas the U.S. is vast. He also points out that the expansion of these republics resulted in a transition from free government to tyranny.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_brutus"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_brutus">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_analysis_and_reaction">Modern analysis and reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federalist_No._10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern analysis and reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the first century of the American republic, No. 10 was not regarded as among the more important numbers of <i>The Federalist</i>. For instance, in <i><a href="/wiki/Democracy_in_America" title="Democracy in America">Democracy in America</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Alexis_de_Tocqueville" title="Alexis de Tocqueville">Alexis de Tocqueville</a> refers specifically to more than fifty of the essays, but No. 10 is not among them.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_adair110"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_adair110">[33]</a></sup> Today, however, No. 10 is regarded as a seminal work of American democracy. In "The People's Vote", a popular survey conducted by the <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_and_Records_Administration" title="National Archives and Records Administration">National Archives and Records Administration</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_History_Day" title="National History Day">National History Day</a>, and <i>U.S. News and World Report</i>, No. 10 (along with <a href="/wiki/Federalist_No._51" title="Federalist No. 51">Federalist No. 51</a>, also by Madison) was chosen as the 20th most influential document in United States history.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_people"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_people">[34]</a></sup> David Epstein, writing in 1984, described it as among the most highly regarded of all American political writing.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Eps59"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_Eps59">[35]</a></sup></p>
<p>The historian <a href="/wiki/Charles_A._Beard" title="Charles A. Beard">Charles A. Beard</a> identified Federalist No. 10 as one of the most important documents for understanding the Constitution. In his book <i><a href="/wiki/An_Economic_Interpretation_of_the_Constitution_of_the_United_States" title="An Economic Interpretation of the Constitution of the United States">An Economic Interpretation of the Constitution of the United States</a></i> (1913), Beard argued that Madison produced a detailed explanation of the economic factors that lay behind the creation of the Constitution. At the outset of his study, Beard makes his point when he writes that Madison provided "a masterly statement of the theory of economic determinism in politics" (Beard 1913, p. 15). Later in his study, Beard repeated his point, only providing more emphasis. "The most philosophical examination of the foundations of political science is made by Madison in the tenth number," Beard writes. "Here he lays down, in no uncertain language, the principle that the first and elemental concern of every government is economic" (Beard 1913, p. 156).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Douglass_Adair" title="Douglass Adair">Douglass Adair</a> attributes the increased interest in the tenth number to <a href="/wiki/Charles_A._Beard" title="Charles A. Beard">Charles A. Beard</a>'s book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/An_Economic_Interpretation_of_the_Constitution" title="An Economic Interpretation of the Constitution">An Economic Interpretation of the Constitution</a></i>, published in 1913. Adair also contends that Beard's selective focus on the issue of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_struggle" title="Class struggle">class struggle</a>, and his political <a href="/wiki/Progressivism" title="Progressivism">progressivism</a>, has colored modern scholarship on the essay. According to Adair, Beard reads No. 10 as evidence for his belief in "the Constitution as an instrument of class exploitation".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Adair2"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_Adair2">[36]</a></sup> Adair's own view is that Federalist No. 10 should be read as "eighteenth-century political theory directed to an eighteenth-century problem; and ... one of the great creative achievements of that intellectual movement that later ages have christened 'Jeffersonian democracy'".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Adair131"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_Adair131">[37]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Garry_Wills" title="Garry Wills">Garry Wills</a> is a noted critic of Madison's argument in Federalist No. 10. In his book <i>Explaining America</i>, he adopts the position of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Dahl" title="Robert Dahl">Robert Dahl</a> in arguing that Madison's framework does not necessarily enhance the protections of minorities or ensure the common good. Instead, Wills claims: "Minorities can make use of dispersed and staggered governmental machinery to clog, delay, slow down, hamper, and obstruct the majority. But these weapons for delay are given to the minority irrespective of its factious or nonfactious character; and they can be used against the majority irrespective of its factious or nonfactious character. What Madison prevents is not faction, but action. What he protects is not the common good but delay as such".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Wills195"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_Wills195">[38]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application">Application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federalist_No._10&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Federalist No. 10 is the classic citation for the belief that the <a href="/wiki/Founding_Fathers_of_the_United_States" title="Founding Fathers of the United States">Founding Fathers</a> and the constitutional framers did not intend American politics to be <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">partisan</a>. For instance, United States Supreme Court justice <a href="/wiki/John_Paul_Stevens" title="John Paul Stevens">John Paul Stevens</a> cites the paper for the statement, "Parties ranked high on the list of evils that the Constitution was designed to check".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_stevens"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_stevens">[39]</a></sup> Discussing a California provision that forbids candidates from running as independents within one year of holding a partisan affiliation, Justice <a href="/wiki/Byron_White" title="Byron White">Byron White</a> made apparent the Court's belief that Madison spoke for the framers of the Constitution: "California apparently believes with the Founding Fathers that splintered parties and unrestrained factionalism may do significant damage to the fabric of government. See The Federalist, No. 10 (Madison)".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_white"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_white">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>Madison's argument that restraining liberty to limit faction is an unacceptable solution has been used by opponents of campaign finance limits. Justice <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Thomas" title="Clarence Thomas">Clarence Thomas</a>, for example, invoked Federalist No. 10 in a dissent against a ruling supporting limits on campaign contributions, writing: "The Framers preferred a political system that harnessed such faction for good, preserving liberty while also ensuring good government. Rather than adopting the repressive 'cure' for faction that the majority today endorses, the Framers armed individual citizens with a remedy".<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Thomas"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federalist_No._10#endnote_Thomas">[41]</a></sup></p>
