<p>An <b>operational amplifier</b> (often <b>op-amp</b> or <b>opamp</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Direct_coupling" title="Direct coupling">DC-coupled</a> high-<a href="/wiki/Gain_(electronics)" title="Gain (electronics)">gain</a> electronic voltage <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_amplifier" title="Electronic amplifier">amplifier</a> with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Differential_input" title="Differential input">differential input</a> and, usually, a <a href="/wiki/Single-ended_signaling" title="Single-ended signaling">single-ended</a> output.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In this configuration, an op-amp produces an output potential (relative to circuit ground) that is typically hundreds of thousands of times larger than the potential difference between its input terminals. Operational amplifiers had their origins in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analog_computers" title="Analog computers">analog computers</a>, where they were used to perform mathematical operations in many linear, non-linear and frequency-dependent circuits. The popularity of the op-amp as a building block in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analog_circuit" title="Analog circuit">analog circuits</a> is due to its versatility. Due to <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a>, the characteristics of an op-amp circuit, its gain, input and <a href="/wiki/Output_impedance" title="Output impedance">output impedance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a> etc. are determined by external components and have little dependence on temperature coefficients or manufacturing variations in the op-amp itself.</p>
<p>Op-amps are among the most widely used electronic devices today, being used in a vast array of consumer, industrial, and scientific devices. Many standard IC op-amps cost only a few cents in moderate production volume; however some integrated or hybrid operational amplifiers with special performance specifications may cost over $100 US in small quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Op-amps may be packaged as components, or used as elements of more complex integrated circuits.</p>
<p>The op-amp is one type of <a href="/wiki/Differential_amplifier" title="Differential amplifier">differential amplifier</a>. Other types of differential amplifier include the <a href="/wiki/Fully_differential_amplifier" title="Fully differential amplifier">fully differential amplifier</a> (similar to the op-amp, but with two outputs), the <a href="/wiki/Instrumentation_amplifier" title="Instrumentation amplifier">instrumentation amplifier</a> (usually built from three op-amps), the <a href="/wiki/Isolation_amplifier" title="Isolation amplifier">isolation amplifier</a> (similar to the instrumentation amplifier, but with tolerance to <a href="/wiki/Common-mode_signal" title="Common-mode signal">common-mode voltages</a> that would destroy an ordinary op-amp), and <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback_amplifier" title="Negative feedback amplifier">negative feedback amplifier</a> (usually built from one or more op-amps and a resistive feedback network).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Op-amp_open-loop_1.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="128" data-file-width="136" height="207" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Op-amp_open-loop_1.svg/220px-Op-amp_open-loop_1.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Op-amp_open-loop_1.svg/330px-Op-amp_open-loop_1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Op-amp_open-loop_1.svg/440px-Op-amp_open-loop_1.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Op-amp_open-loop_1.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An op-amp without negative feedback (a comparator)</div>

<p>The amplifier's differential inputs consist of a non-inverting input (+) with voltage <i>V</i><sub>+</sub> and an inverting input (–) with voltage <i>V</i><sub>−</sub>; ideally the op-amp amplifies only the difference in voltage between the two, which is called the <i>differential input voltage</i>. The output voltage of the op-amp <i>V</i><sub>out</sub> is given by the equation:</p>

<p>where <i>A</i><sub>OL</sub> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_feedback_loops" title="Electronic feedback loops">open-loop</a> gain of the amplifier (the term "open-loop" refers to the absence of a feedback loop from the output to the input).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_loop_amplifier">Open loop amplifier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Open loop amplifier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The magnitude of <i>A</i><sub>OL</sub> is typically very large—100,000 or more for integrated circuit op-amps—and therefore even a quite small difference between <i>V</i><sub>+</sub> and <i>V</i><sub>−</sub> drives the amplifier output nearly to the supply voltage. Situations in which the output voltage is equal to or greater than the supply voltage are referred to as <i>saturation</i> of the amplifier. The magnitude of <i>A</i><sub>OL</sub> is not well controlled by the manufacturing process, and so it is impractical to use an open loop amplifier as a stand-alone <a href="/wiki/Differential_amplifier" title="Differential amplifier">differential amplifier</a>.</p>
<p>Without <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback_amplifier" title="Negative feedback amplifier">negative feedback</a>, and perhaps with <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> for <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_circuit" title="Regenerative circuit">regeneration</a>, an op-amp acts as a <a href="/wiki/Comparator" title="Comparator">comparator</a>. If the inverting input is held at ground (0 V) directly or by a resistor R<sub>g</sub>, and the input voltage V<sub>in</sub> applied to the non-inverting input is positive, the output will be maximum positive; if V<sub>in</sub> is negative, the output will be maximum negative. Since there is no feedback from the output to either input, this is an <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_feedback_loops" title="Electronic feedback loops">open loop</a></i> circuit acting as a <a href="/wiki/Comparator" title="Comparator">comparator</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Closed_loop">Closed loop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Closed loop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Operational_amplifier_noninverting.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="144" data-file-width="146" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Operational_amplifier_noninverting.svg/220px-Operational_amplifier_noninverting.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Operational_amplifier_noninverting.svg/330px-Operational_amplifier_noninverting.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Operational_amplifier_noninverting.svg/440px-Operational_amplifier_noninverting.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Operational_amplifier_noninverting.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An op-amp with negative feedback (a non-inverting amplifier)</div>

<p>If predictable operation is desired, negative feedback is used, by applying a portion of the output voltage to the inverting input. The <i>closed loop</i> feedback greatly reduces the gain of the circuit. When negative feedback is used, the circuit's overall gain and response becomes determined mostly by the feedback network, rather than by the op-amp characteristics. If the feedback network is made of components with values small relative to the op amp's input impedance, the value of the op-amp's open loop response <i>A</i><sub>OL</sub> does not seriously affect the circuit's performance. The response of the op-amp circuit with its input, output, and feedback circuits to an input is characterized mathematically by a <a href="/wiki/Transfer_function" title="Transfer function">transfer function</a>; designing an op-amp circuit to have a desired transfer function is in the realm of <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>. The transfer functions are important in most applications of op-amps, such as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analog_computers" title="Analog computers">analog computers</a>. High input <a href="/wiki/Electrical_impedance" title="Electrical impedance">impedance</a> at the input terminals and low output impedance at the output terminal(s) are particularly useful features of an op-amp.</p>
<p>In the non-inverting amplifier on the right, the presence of negative feedback via the <a href="/wiki/Voltage_divider" title="Voltage divider">voltage divider</a> <i>R</i><sub>f</sub>, <i>R</i><sub>g</sub> determines the <i>closed-loop gain</i> <i>A</i><sub>CL</sub> = <i>V</i><sub>out</sub> / <i>V</i><sub>in</sub>. Equilibrium will be established when <i>V</i><sub>out</sub> is just sufficient to "reach around and pull" the inverting input to the same voltage as <i>V</i><sub>in</sub>. The voltage gain of the entire circuit is thus 1 + <i>R</i><sub>f</sub>/<i>R</i><sub>g</sub>. As a simple example, if <i>V</i><sub>in</sub> = 1 V and R<sub>f</sub> = R<sub>g</sub>, V<sub>out</sub> will be 2 V, exactly the amount required to keep <i>V</i><sub>-</sub> at 1 V. Because of the feedback provided by the <i>R</i><sub>f</sub>, <i>R</i><sub>g</sub> network, this is a <i>closed loop</i> circuit.</p>
<p>Another way to analyze this circuit proceeds by making the following (usually valid) assumptions:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>When an op-amp operates in linear (i.e., not saturated) mode, the difference in voltage between the non-inverting (+) pin and the inverting (−) pin is negligibly small.</li>
<li>The input impedance between (+) and (−) pins is much larger than other resistances in the circuit.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Op-amp_characteristics">Op-amp characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Op-amp characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideal_op-amps">Ideal op-amps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ideal op-amps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Op-Amp_Internal.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="400" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Op-Amp_Internal.svg/250px-Op-Amp_Internal.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Op-Amp_Internal.svg/375px-Op-Amp_Internal.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Op-Amp_Internal.svg/500px-Op-Amp_Internal.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Op-Amp_Internal.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An equivalent circuit of an operational amplifier that models some resistive non-ideal parameters.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>An ideal op-amp is usually considered to have the following characteristics:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Infinite <a href="/wiki/Open-loop_gain" title="Open-loop gain">open-loop gain</a> G = <i>v</i><sub>out</sub> / <i>v</i><sub>in</sub></li>
<li>Infinite <a href="/wiki/Input_impedance" title="Input impedance">input impedance</a> R<sub>in</sub>, and so zero input current</li>
<li>Zero <a href="/wiki/Input_offset_voltage" title="Input offset voltage">input offset voltage</a></li>
<li>Infinite output voltage range</li>
<li>Infinite <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a> with zero <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_shift" title="Phase shift">phase shift</a> and infinite <a href="/wiki/Slew_rate" title="Slew rate">slew rate</a></li>
<li>Zero <a href="/wiki/Output_impedance" title="Output impedance">output impedance</a> R<sub>out</sub></li>
<li>Zero <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_noise" title="Electronic noise">noise</a></li>
<li>Infinite <a href="/wiki/Common-mode_rejection_ratio" title="Common-mode rejection ratio">common-mode rejection ratio</a> (CMRR)</li>
<li>Infinite <a href="/wiki/Power_supply_rejection_ratio" title="Power supply rejection ratio">power supply rejection ratio</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>These ideals can be summarized by the two "golden rules":</p>
<div>
<ol style="list-style-type:upper-roman">
<li><b>In a closed loop</b> the output attempts to do whatever is necessary to make the voltage difference between the inputs zero.</li>
<li>The inputs draw no current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AoE_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-AoE-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:177</sup></li>
</ol>
</div>
<p>The first rule only applies in the usual case where the op-amp is used in a closed-loop design (negative feedback, where there is a signal path of some sort feeding back from the output to the inverting input). These rules are commonly used as a good first approximation for analyzing or designing op-amp circuits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AoE_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-AoE-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:177</sup></p>
<p>None of these ideals can be perfectly realized. A real op-amp may be modeled with non-infinite or non-zero parameters using equivalent resistors and capacitors in the op-amp model. The designer can then include these effects into the overall performance of the final circuit. Some parameters may turn out to have negligible effect on the final design while others represent actual limitations of the final performance that must be evaluated.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Real_op-amps">Real op-amps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Real op-amps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Real op-amps differ from the ideal model in various aspects.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="DC_imperfections">DC imperfections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: DC imperfections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Real operational amplifiers suffer from several non-ideal effects:</p>
<dl>
<dt>Finite gain</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Open-loop_gain" title="Open-loop gain">Open-loop gain</a> is infinite in the ideal operational amplifier but finite in real operational amplifiers. Typical devices exhibit open-loop DC gain ranging from 100,000 to over 1 million. So long as the <a href="/wiki/Loop_gain" title="Loop gain">loop gain</a> (i.e., the product of open-loop and feedback gains) is very large, the circuit gain will be determined entirely by the amount of negative feedback (i.e., it will be independent of open-loop gain). In cases where <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Closed-loop_gain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Closed-loop gain (page does not exist)">closed-loop gain</a> must be very high, the feedback gain will be very low, and the low feedback gain causes low loop gain; in these cases, the operational amplifier will cease to behave ideally.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Finite <a href="/wiki/Input_impedance" title="Input impedance">input impedances</a> </dt>
<dd>The <i>differential input impedance</i> of the operational amplifier is defined as the impedance <i>between</i> its two inputs; the <i>common-mode input impedance</i> is the impedance from each input to ground. <a href="/wiki/MOSFET" title="MOSFET">MOSFET</a>-input operational amplifiers often have protection circuits that effectively short circuit any input differences greater than a small threshold, so the input impedance can appear to be very low in some tests. However, as long as these operational amplifiers are used in a typical high-gain negative feedback application, these protection circuits will be inactive. The input bias and leakage currents described below are a more important design parameter for typical operational amplifier applications.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Non-zero <a href="/wiki/Output_impedance" title="Output impedance">output impedance</a></dt>
<dd>Low output impedance is important for low-impedance loads; for these loads, the voltage drop across the output impedance effectively reduces the open loop gain. In configurations with a voltage-sensing negative feedback, the output impedance of the amplifier is effectively lowered; thus, in linear applications, op-amp circuits usually exhibit a very low output impedance indeed.</dd>
<dd>Low-impedance outputs typically require high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quiescent_current" title="Quiescent current">quiescent (i.e., idle) current</a> in the output stage and will dissipate more power, so low-power designs may purposely sacrifice low output impedance.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Input current</dt>
<dd>Due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biasing_(electronics)" title="Biasing (electronics)">biasing</a> requirements or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leakage_(semiconductors)" title="Leakage (semiconductors)">leakage</a>, a small amount of current (typically ~10 nanoamperes for <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_junction_transistor" title="Bipolar junction transistor">bipolar</a> op-amps, tens of picoamperes (pA) for <a href="/wiki/JFET" title="JFET">JFET</a> input stages, and only a few pA for <a href="/wiki/MOSFET" title="MOSFET">MOSFET</a> input stages) flows into the inputs. When large resistors or sources with high output impedances are used in the circuit, these small currents can produce large unmodeled voltage drops. If the input currents are matched, <i>and</i> the impedance looking <i>out</i> of <i>both</i> inputs are matched, then the voltages produced at each input will be equal. Because the operational amplifier operates on the <i>difference</i> between its inputs, these matched voltages will have no effect. It is more common for the input currents to be slightly mismatched. The difference is called input offset current, and even with matched resistances a small <i>offset voltage</i> (different from the input offset voltage below) can be produced. This offset voltage can create offsets or drifting in the operational amplifier.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Input offset <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a></dt>
<dd>This voltage, which is what is required across the op-amp's input terminals to drive the output voltage to zero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[nb 1]</a></sup> In the perfect amplifier, there would be no input offset voltage. However, it exists in actual op-amps because of imperfections in the differential amplifier that constitutes the input stage of the vast majority of these devices. Input offset voltage creates two problems: First, due to the amplifier's high voltage gain, it virtually assures that the amplifier output will go into saturation if it is operated without negative feedback, even when the input terminals are wired together. Second, in a closed loop, negative feedback configuration, the input offset voltage is amplified along with the signal and this may pose a problem if high precision DC amplification is required or if the input signal is very small.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[nb 2]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Common-mode gain</dt>
<dd>A perfect operational amplifier amplifies only the voltage difference between its two inputs, completely rejecting all voltages that are common to both. However, the differential input stage of an operational amplifier is never perfect, leading to the amplification of these common voltages to some degree. The standard measure of this defect is called the <a href="/wiki/Common-mode_rejection_ratio" title="Common-mode rejection ratio">common-mode rejection ratio</a> (denoted CMRR). Minimization of common mode gain is usually important in non-inverting amplifiers (described below) that operate at high amplification.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Power-supply rejection</dt>
<dd>The output of a perfect operational amplifier will be completely independent from its power supply. Every real operational amplifier has a finite <a href="/wiki/Power_supply_rejection_ratio" title="Power supply rejection ratio">power supply rejection ratio</a> (PSRR) that reflects how well the op-amp can reject changes in its supply voltage.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Temperature effects</dt>
<dd>All parameters change with temperature. Temperature drift of the input offset voltage is especially important.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Drift</dt>
<dd>Real op-amp parameters are subject to slow change over time and with changes in temperature, input conditions, etc.</dd>
</dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="AC_imperfections">AC imperfections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: AC imperfections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The op-amp gain calculated at DC does not apply at higher frequencies. Thus, for high-speed operation, more sophisticated considerations must be used in an op-amp circuit design.</p>
<dl>
<dt>Finite <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a></dt>
<dd>All amplifiers have finite bandwidth. To a first approximation, the op-amp has the frequency response of an <a href="/wiki/Integrator" title="Integrator">integrator</a> with gain. That is, the gain of a typical op-amp is inversely proportional to frequency and is characterized by its <a href="/wiki/Gain%E2%80%93bandwidth_product" title="Gain–bandwidth product">gain–bandwidth product</a> (GBWP). For example, an op-amp with a GBWP of 1 MHz would have a gain of 5 at 200 kHz, and a gain of 1 at 1 MHz. This dynamic response coupled with the very high DC gain of the op-amp gives it the characteristics of a first-order <a href="/wiki/Low-pass_filter" title="Low-pass filter">low-pass filter</a> with very high DC gain and low cutoff frequency given by the GBWP divided by the DC gain.
<div class="paragraphbreak" style="margin-top:0.5em"></div>
The finite bandwidth of an op-amp can be the source of several problems, including:
<dl class="glossary">
<dt class="glossary" id="stability" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary">Stability</dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">Associated with the bandwidth limitation is a phase difference between the input signal and the amplifier output that can lead to <a href="/wiki/Electronic_oscillation" title="Electronic oscillation">oscillation</a> in some feedback circuits. For example, a sinusoidal output signal meant to interfere destructively with an input signal of the same frequency will interfere constructively if delayed by 180 degrees forming <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a>. In these cases, the feedback circuit can be <a href="/wiki/BIBO_stability" title="BIBO stability">stabilized</a> by means of <a href="/wiki/Frequency_compensation" title="Frequency compensation">frequency compensation</a>, which increases the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gain_margin" title="Gain margin">gain or phase margin</a> of the open-loop circuit. The circuit designer can implement this compensation externally with a separate circuit component. Alternatively, the compensation can be implemented within the operational amplifier with the addition of a <a href="/wiki/Frequency_compensation#Dominant-pole_compensation" title="Frequency compensation">dominant pole</a> that sufficiently attenuates the high-frequency gain of the operational amplifier. The location of this pole may be fixed internally by the manufacturer or configured by the circuit designer using methods specific to the op-amp. In general, dominant-pole frequency compensation reduces the bandwidth of the op-amp even further. When the desired closed-loop gain is high, op-amp frequency compensation is often not needed because the requisite open-loop gain is sufficiently low; consequently, applications with high closed-loop gain can make use of op-amps with higher bandwidths.</dd>
<dt class="glossary" id="distortion.2C_and_other_effects" style="margin-top: 0.4em;"><dfn class="glossary">Distortion, and other effects</dfn></dt>
<dd class="glossary">Limited bandwidth also results in lower amounts of feedback at higher frequencies, producing higher distortion, and output impedance as the frequency increases.</dd>
</dl>
<div class="paragraphbreak" style="margin-top:0.5em"></div>
Typical low-cost, general-purpose op-amps exhibit a GBWP of a few megahertz. Specialty and high-speed op-amps exist that can achieve a GBWP of hundreds of megahertz. For very high-frequency circuits, a <a href="/wiki/Current-feedback_operational_amplifier" title="Current-feedback operational amplifier">current-feedback operational amplifier</a> is often used.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Noise</dt>
<dd>Amplifiers generate random voltage at the output even when there is no signal applied. This can be due to thermal noise and flicker noise of the devices. For applications with high gain or high bandwidth, noise becomes a very important consideration.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Input <a href="/wiki/Capacitance" title="Capacitance">capacitance</a></dt>
<dd>Most important for high frequency operation because it reduces input impedance and may cause phase shifts.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Common-mode gain</dt>
<dd>See DC imperfections, above.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Power-supply rejection</dt>
<dd>With increasing frequency the power-supply rejection usually gets worse. So it can be important to keep the supply clean of higher frequency ripples and signals, e.g. by the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bypass_capacitor" title="Bypass capacitor">bypass capacitors</a>.</dd>
</dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-linear_imperfections">Non-linear imperfections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Non-linear imperfections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Inverting_Amplifier_Signal_Clipping.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="658" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Inverting_Amplifier_Signal_Clipping.png/220px-Inverting_Amplifier_Signal_Clipping.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Inverting_Amplifier_Signal_Clipping.png/330px-Inverting_Amplifier_Signal_Clipping.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Inverting_Amplifier_Signal_Clipping.png/440px-Inverting_Amplifier_Signal_Clipping.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Inverting_Amplifier_Signal_Clipping.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The input (yellow) and output (green) of a saturated op amp in an inverting amplifier</div>
</div>
</div>
<dl>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Clipping_(signal_processing)" title="Clipping (signal processing)">Saturation</a></dt>
<dd>Output voltage is limited to a minimum and maximum value close to the <a href="/wiki/Power_supply" title="Power supply">power supply</a> voltages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[nb 3]</a></sup> The output of older op-amps can reach to within one or two volts of the supply rails. The output of newer so-called "rail to rail" op-amps can reach to within millivolts of the supply rails when providing low output currents.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Slewing</dt>
<dd>The amplifier's output voltage reaches its maximum rate of change, the <a href="/wiki/Slew_rate" title="Slew rate">slew rate</a>, usually specified in volts per microsecond. When slewing occurs, further increases in the input signal have no effect on the rate of change of the output. Slewing is usually caused by the input stage saturating; the result is a constant current <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">i</span> driving a capacitance <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">C</span> in the amplifier (especially those capacitances used to implement its <a href="/wiki/Frequency_compensation" title="Frequency compensation">frequency compensation</a>); the slew rate is limited by <span class="texhtml"><i>dv</i>/<i>dt</i>=<i>i</i>/<i>C</i></span>.
<div class="paragraphbreak" style="margin-top:0.5em"></div>
Slewing is associated with the <i>large-signal</i> performance of an op-amp. Consider, for example, an op-amp configured for a gain of 10. Let the input be a 1 V, 100 kHz sawtooth wave. That is, the amplitude is 1 V and the period is 10 microseconds. Accordingly, the rate of change (i.e., the slope) of the input is 0.1 V per microsecond. After 10x amplification, the output should be a 10 V, 100 kHz sawtooth, with a corresponding slew rate of 1 V per microsecond. However, the classic 741 op-amp has a 0.5 V per microsecond slew rate specification, so that its output can rise to no more than 5 V in the sawtooth's 10 microsecond period. Thus, if one were to measure the output, it would be a 5 V, 100 kHz sawtooth, rather than a 10 V, 100 kHz sawtooth.
<div class="paragraphbreak" style="margin-top:0.5em"></div>
Next consider the same amplifier and 100 kHz sawtooth, but now the input amplitude is 100 mV rather than 1 V. After 10x amplification the output is a 1 V, 100 kHz sawtooth with a corresponding slew rate of 0.1 V per microsecond. In this instance the 741 with its 0.5 V per microsecond slew rate will amplify the input properly.
<div class="paragraphbreak" style="margin-top:0.5em"></div>
Modern high speed op-amps can have slew rates in excess of 5,000 V per microsecond. However, it is more common for op-amps to have slew rates in the range 5-100 V per microsecond. For example, the general purpose TL081 op-amp has a slew rate of 13 V per microsecond. As a general rule, low power and small bandwidth op-amps have low slew rates. As an example, the LT1494 micropower op-amp consumes 1.5 microamp but has a 2.7 kHz gain-bandwidth product and a 0.001 V per microsecond slew rate.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear" title="Linear">linear</a> input-output relationship</dt>
<dd>The output voltage may not be accurately proportional to the difference between the input voltages. It is commonly called distortion when the input signal is a waveform. This effect will be very small in a practical circuit where substantial negative feedback is used.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Phase reversal</dt>
<dd>In some integrated op-amps, when the published common mode voltage is violated (e.g., by one of the inputs being driven to one of the supply voltages), the output may slew to the opposite polarity from what is expected in normal operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[9]</a></sup> Under such conditions, negative feedback becomes positive, likely causing the circuit to "lock up" in that state.</dd>
</dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_considerations">Power considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Power considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<dl>
<dt>Limited <a href="/wiki/Current_limiting" title="Current limiting">output current</a></dt>
<dd>The output current must be finite. In practice, most op-amps are designed to limit the output current so as not to exceed a specified level – around 25 mA for a type 741 IC op-amp – thus protecting the op-amp and associated circuitry from damage. Modern designs are electronically more rugged than earlier implementations and some can sustain direct <a href="/wiki/Short_circuit" title="Short circuit">short circuits</a> on their outputs without damage.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Output sink current</dt>
<dd>The output sink current is the maximum current allowed to sink into the output stage. Some manufacturers show the output voltage vs. the output sink current plot, which gives an idea of the output voltage when it is sinking current from another source into the output pin.</dd>
<dt>Limited dissipated <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">power</a></dt>
<dd>The output current flows through the op-amp's internal output impedance, dissipating heat. If the op-amp dissipates too much power, then its temperature will increase above some safe limit. The op-amp may enter thermal shutdown, or it may be destroyed.</dd>
</dl>
<p>Modern integrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_effect_transistor" title="Field effect transistor">FET</a> or <a href="/wiki/MOSFET" title="MOSFET">MOSFET</a> op-amps approximate more closely the ideal op-amp than bipolar ICs when it comes to input impedance and input bias currents. Bipolars are generally better when it comes to input <i>voltage</i> offset, and often have lower noise. Generally, at room temperature, with a fairly large signal, and limited bandwidth, FET and MOSFET op-amps now offer better performance.</p>

<p>Sourced by many manufacturers, and in multiple similar products, an example of a bipolar transistor operational amplifier is the 741 integrated circuit designed in 1968 by David Fullagar at <a href="/wiki/Fairchild_Semiconductor" title="Fairchild Semiconductor">Fairchild Semiconductor</a> after <a href="/wiki/Bob_Widlar" title="Bob Widlar">Bob Widlar</a>'s LM301 integrated circuit design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-13">[10]</a></sup> In this discussion, we use the parameters of the <a href="/wiki/Hybrid-pi_model" title="Hybrid-pi model">Hybrid-pi model</a> to characterize the small-signal, grounded emitter characteristics of a transistor. In this model, the current gain of a transistor is denoted <i>h</i><sub>fe</sub>, more commonly called the β.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lu_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lu-14">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A small-scale <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuit</a>, the 741 op-amp shares with most op-amps an internal structure consisting of three gain stages:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[12]</a></sup></p>
<ol>
<li><a href="/wiki/Differential_amplifier" title="Differential amplifier">Differential amplifier</a> (outlined <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a>) — provides high differential amplification (gain), with rejection of common-mode signal, low noise, high <a href="/wiki/Input_impedance" title="Input impedance">input impedance</a>, and drives a</li>
<li>Voltage amplifier (outlined <a href="/wiki/Magenta" title="Magenta">magenta</a>) — provides high voltage gain, a single-pole frequency <a href="/wiki/Roll-off" title="Roll-off">roll-off</a>, and in turn drives the</li>
<li>Output amplifier (outlined <a href="/wiki/Cyan" title="Cyan">cyan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a>) — provides high current gain (low <a href="/wiki/Output_impedance" title="Output impedance">output impedance</a>), along with output current limiting, and output short-circuit protection.</li>
</ol>
<p>Additionally, it contains <a href="/wiki/Current_mirror" title="Current mirror">current mirror</a> (outlined red) bias circuitry and <a href="/wiki/Frequency_compensation" title="Frequency compensation">compensation</a> capacitor (30 pF).</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Differential_amplifier">Differential amplifier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Differential amplifier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The input stage consists of a cascaded <a href="/wiki/Differential_amplifier" title="Differential amplifier">differential amplifier</a> (outlined in <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a>) followed by a current-mirror <a href="/wiki/Active_load" title="Active load">active load</a>. This constitutes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transconductance_amplifier" title="Transconductance amplifier">transconductance amplifier</a>, turning a differential voltage signal at the bases of Q1, Q2 into a current signal into the base of Q15.</p>
<p>It entails two cascaded transistor pairs, satisfying conflicting requirements. The first stage consists of the matched NPN <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emitter_follower" title="Emitter follower">emitter follower</a> pair Q1, Q2 that provide high input impedance. The second is the matched PNP <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common-base" title="Common-base">common-base</a> pair Q3, Q4 that eliminates the undesirable <a href="/wiki/Miller_effect" title="Miller effect">Miller effect</a>; it drives an <a href="/wiki/Active_load" title="Active load">active load</a> Q7 plus matched pair Q5, Q6.</p>
<p>That active load is implemented as a modified <a href="/wiki/Wilson_current_mirror" title="Wilson current mirror">Wilson current mirror</a>; its role is to convert the (differential) input current signal to a single-ended signal without the attendant 50% losses (increasing the op-amp's open-loop gain by 3 dB).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[nb 4]</a></sup> Thus, a small-signal differential current in Q3 versus Q4 appears summed (doubled) at the base of Q15, the input of the voltage gain stage.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Voltage_amplifier">Voltage amplifier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Voltage amplifier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_amplifier#Class_A" title="Electronic amplifier">class-A</a>) voltage gain stage (outlined in <a href="/wiki/Magenta" title="Magenta">magenta</a>) consists of the two NPN transistors Q15/Q19 connected in a <a href="/wiki/Darlington_transistor" title="Darlington transistor">Darlington configuration</a> and uses the output side of current mirror Q12/Q13 as its collector (dynamic) load to achieve its high voltage gain. The output sink transistor Q20 receives its base drive from the common collectors of Q15 and Q19; the level-shifter Q16 provides base drive for the output source transistor Q14. .</p>
<p>The transistor Q22 prevents this stage from delivering excessive current to Q20 and thus limits the output sink current.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Output_amplifier">Output amplifier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Output amplifier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The output stage (Q14, Q20, outlined in <a href="/wiki/Cyan" title="Cyan">cyan</a>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_AB" title="Class AB">Class AB</a> push-pull emitter follower amplifier. It provides an output drive with impedance of ≈50Ω, in essence, current gain. Transistor Q16 (outlined in <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a>) provides the quiescent current for the output transistors, and Q17 provides output current limiting.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biasing_circuits">Biasing circuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Biasing circuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Provide appropriate quiescent current for each stage of the op-amp.</p>
<p>The resistor (39 kΩ) connecting the (diode-connected) Q11 and Q12, and the given supply voltage (<i>V</i><sub><i>S</i>+</sub>−<i>V</i><sub><i>S</i>−</sub>), determine the current in the <a href="/wiki/Current_mirror" title="Current mirror">current mirrors</a>, (matched pairs) Q10/Q11 and Q12/Q13. The collector current of Q11, <i>i</i><sub>11</sub> * 39 kΩ = <i>V</i><sub><i>S</i>+</sub> − <i>V</i><sub><i>S</i>−</sub> − 2 <i>V</i><sub>BE</sub>. For the typical <i>V</i><sub><i>S</i></sub> = ±20 V, the standing current in Q11/Q12 (as well as in Q13) would be ≈1 mA. A supply current for a typical 741 of about 2 mA agrees with the notion that these two bias currents dominate the quiescent supply current.</p>
<p>Transistors Q11 and Q10 form a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Widlar_current_mirror" title="Widlar current mirror">Widlar current mirror</a>, with quiescent current in Q10 <i>i</i><sub>10</sub> such that ln( <i>i</i><sub>11</sub> / <i>i</i><sub>10</sub> ) = <i>i</i><sub>10</sub> * 5 kΩ / 28 mV, where 5 kΩ represents the emitter resistor of Q10, and 28 mV is V<sub>T</sub>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_voltage" title="Thermal voltage">thermal voltage</a> at room temperature. In this case <i>i</i><sub>10</sub> ≈ 20 μA.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Differential_amplifier_2">Differential amplifier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Differential amplifier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The biasing circuit of this stage is set by a feedback loop that forces the collector currents of Q10 and Q9 to (nearly) match. The small difference in these currents provides the drive for the common base of Q3/Q4 (note that the base drive for input transistors Q1/Q2 is the input bias current and must be sourced externally). The summed quiescent currents of Q1/Q3 plus Q2/Q4 is mirrored from Q8 into Q9, where it is summed with the collector current in Q10, the result being applied to the bases of Q3/Q4.</p>
<p>The quiescent currents of Q1/Q3 (resp., Q2/Q4) <i>i</i><sub>1</sub> will thus be half of <i>i</i><sub>10</sub>, of order ≈ 10 μA. Input bias current for the base of Q1 (resp. Q2) will amount to <i>i</i><sub>1</sub> / β; typically ≈50 nA, implying a current gain <i>h</i><sub>fe</sub> ≈ 200 for Q1(Q2).</p>
<p>This feedback circuit tends to draw the common base node of Q3/Q4 to a voltage <i>V</i><sub>com</sub> − 2 * <i>V</i><sub>BE</sub>, where <i>V</i><sub>com</sub> is the input common-mode voltage. At the same time, the magnitude of the quiescent current is relatively insensitive to the characteristics of the components Q1–Q4, such as <i>h</i><sub>fe</sub>, that would otherwise cause temperature dependence or part-to-part variations.</p>
<p>Transistor Q7 drives Q5 and Q6 into conduction until their (equal) collector currents match that of Q1/Q3 and Q2/Q4. The quiescent current in Q7 is <i>V</i><sub>BE</sub> / 50 kΩ, about 35μA, as is the quiescent current in Q15, with its matching operating point. Thus, the quiescent currents are pairwise matched in Q1/Q2, Q3/Q4, Q5/Q6, and Q7/Q15.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Voltage_amplifier_2">Voltage amplifier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Voltage amplifier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Quiescent currents in Q16 and Q19 are set by the current mirror Q12/Q13, which is running at ≈ 1 mA. Through some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (January 2016)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> mechanism, the collector current in Q19 tracks that standing current.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Output_amplifier_2">Output amplifier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Output amplifier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the circuit involving Q16 (variously named <a href="/wiki/Rubber_diode" title="Rubber diode">rubber diode</a> or <i>V</i><sub>BE</sub> multiplier), the 4.5 kΩ resistor must be conducting about 100 μA, with the Q16 <i>V</i><sub>BE</sub> roughly 700 mV. Then the <i>V</i><sub>CB</sub> must be about 0.45 V and <i>V</i><sub>CE</sub> at about 1.0 V. Because the Q16 collector is driven by a current source and the Q16 emitter drives into the Q19 collector current sink, the Q16 transistor establishes a voltage difference between Q14 base and Q20 base of ≈ 1 V, regardless of the common-mode voltage of Q14/Q20 base. The standing current in Q14/Q20 will be a factor <a href="/wiki/Diode_modelling" title="Diode modelling">exp(100 mV / V<sub>T</sub> ) ≈ 36</a> smaller than the 1 mA quiescent current in the class A portion of the op amp. This (small) standing current in the output transistors establishes the output stage in class AB operation and reduces the <a href="/wiki/Crossover_distortion" title="Crossover distortion">crossover distortion</a> of this stage.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Small-signal_differential_mode">Small-signal differential mode</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Small-signal differential mode">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A small differential input voltage signal gives rise, through multiple stages of current amplification, to a much larger voltage signal on output.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Input_impedance">Input impedance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Input impedance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The input stage with Q1 and Q3 is similar to an emitter-coupled pair (long-tailed pair), with Q2 and Q4 adding some degenerating impedance. The input impedance is relatively high because of the small current through Q1-Q4. A typical 741 op amp has an differential input impedance of about 2 MΩ. The common mode input impedance is even higher, as the input stage works at an essentially constant current.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Differential_amplifier_3">Differential amplifier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Differential amplifier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A differential voltage <i>V</i><sub>In</sub> at the op-amp inputs (pins 3 and 2, respectively) gives rise to a small differential current in the bases of Q1 and Q2 <i>i</i><sub>In</sub> ≈ <i>V</i><sub>In</sub> / ( 2 <i>h</i><sub>ie</sub> * <i>h</i><sub>fe</sub>). This differential base current causes a change in the differential collector current in each leg by <i>i</i><sub>In</sub> * <i>h</i><sub>fe</sub>. Introducing the transconductance of Q1, <i>g</i><sub><i>m</i></sub> = <i>h</i><sub>fe</sub> / <i>h</i><sub>ie</sub>, the (small-signal) current at the base of Q15 (the input of the voltage gain stage) is <i>V</i><sub>In</sub> * <i>g</i><sub><i>m</i></sub> / 2.</p>
<p>This portion of the op amp cleverly changes a differential signal at the op amp inputs to a single-ended signal at the base of Q15, and in a way that avoids wastefully discarding the signal in either leg. To see how, notice that a small negative change in voltage at the inverting input (Q2 base) drives it out of conduction, and this incremental decrease in current passes directly from Q4 collector to its emitter, resulting in an decrease in base drive for Q15. On the other hand, a small positive change in voltage at the non-inverting input (Q1 base) drives this transistor into conduction, reflected in an increase in current at the collector of Q3. This current drives Q7 further into conduction, which turns on current mirror Q5/Q6. Thus, the increase in Q3 emitter current is mirrored in an increase in Q6 collector current; the increased collector currents shunts more from the collector node and results in a decrease in base drive current for Q15. Besides avoiding wasting 3 dB of gain here, this technique decreases common-mode gain and feedthrough of power supply noise.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Voltage_amplifier_3">Voltage amplifier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Voltage amplifier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A current signal <i>i</i> at Q15's base gives rise to a current in Q19 of order <i>i</i> * β<sup>2</sup> (the product of the <i>h</i><sub>fe</sub> of each of Q15 and Q19, which are connected in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darlington_pair" title="Darlington pair">Darlington pair</a>). This current signal develops a voltage at the bases of output transistors Q14/Q20 proportional to the <i>h</i><sub>ie</sub> of the respective transistor.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Output_amplifier_3">Output amplifier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Output amplifier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Output transistors Q14 and Q20 are each configured as an emitter follower, so no voltage gain occurs there; instead, this stage provides current gain, equal to the <i>h</i><sub>fe</sub> of Q14 (resp. Q20).</p>
<p>The output impedance is not zero, as it would be in an ideal op-amp, but with negative feedback it approaches zero at low frequencies.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Overall_open-loop_voltage_gain">Overall open-loop voltage gain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Overall open-loop voltage gain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The net open-loop small-signal voltage gain of the op amp involves the product of the current gain <i>h</i><sub>fe</sub> of some 4 transistors. In practice, the voltage gain for a typical 741-style op amp is of order 200,000, and the current gain, the ratio of input impedance (≈2−6 MΩ) to output impedance (≈50Ω) provides yet more (power) gain.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_linear_characteristics">Other linear characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Other linear characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Small-signal_common_mode_gain">Small-signal common mode gain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Small-signal common mode gain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The ideal op amp has infinite <a href="/wiki/Common-mode_rejection_ratio" title="Common-mode rejection ratio">common-mode rejection ratio</a>, or zero common-mode gain.</p>
<p>In the present circuit, if the input voltages change in the same direction, the negative feedback makes Q3/Q4 base voltage follow (with 2<i>V</i><sub>BE</sub> below) the input voltage variations. Now the output part (Q10) of Q10-Q11 current mirror keeps up the common current through Q9/Q8 constant in spite of varying voltage. Q3/Q4 collector currents, and accordingly the output current at the base of Q15, remain unchanged.</p>
<p>In the typical 741 op amp, the common-mode rejection ratio is 90 dB, implying an open-loop common-mode voltage gain of about 6.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequency_compensation">Frequency compensation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Frequency compensation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The innovation of the Fairchild μA741 was the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Frequency_compensation" title="Frequency compensation">frequency compensation</a> via an on-chip (monolithic) capacitor, simplifying application of the op amp by eliminating the need for external components for this function. The 30 pF capacitor stabilizes the amplifier via <a href="/wiki/Miller_effect" title="Miller effect">Miller compensation</a> and functions in a manner similar to an op-amp <a href="/wiki/Integrator" title="Integrator">integrator</a> circuit. Also known as 'dominant <a href="/wiki/Pole_(complex_analysis)" title="Pole (complex analysis)">pole</a> compensation' because it introduces a pole that masks (dominates) the effects of other poles into the <a href="/wiki/Open_loop" title="Open loop">open loop</a> frequency response; in a 741 op amp this pole can be as low as 10 Hz (where it causes a −3 dB loss of open loop voltage gain).</p>
<p>This internal compensation is provided to achieve unconditional <a href="/wiki/BIBO_stability" title="BIBO stability">stability</a> of the amplifier in negative feedback configurations where the feedback network is non-reactive and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_feedback_loops" title="Electronic feedback loops">closed loop</a> gain is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unity_(mathematics)" title="Unity (mathematics)">unity</a> or higher. By contrast, amplifiers requiring external compensation, such as the μA748, may require external compensation or closed-loop gains significantly higher than unity.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Input_offset_voltage">Input offset voltage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Input offset voltage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The "offset null" pins may be used to place external resistors (typically in the form of the two ends of a potentiometer, with the slider connected to <i>V</i><sub><i>S</i>–</sub>) in parallel with the emitter resistors of Q5 and Q6, to adjust the balance of the Q5/Q6 current mirror. The potentiometer is adjusted such that the output is null (midrange) when the inputs are shorted together.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-linear_characteristics">Non-linear characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Non-linear characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Input_breakdown_voltage">Input breakdown voltage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Input breakdown voltage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The transistors Q3, Q4 help to increase the reverse <i>V</i><sub>BE</sub> rating: the base-emitter junctions of the NPN transistors Q1 and Q2 break down at around 7V, but the PNP transistors Q3 and Q4 have <i>V</i><sub>BE</sub> breakdown voltages around 50 V.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Output-stage_voltage_swing_and_current_limiting">Output-stage voltage swing and current limiting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Output-stage voltage swing and current limiting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Variations in the quiescent current with temperature, or between parts with the same type number, are common, so <a href="/wiki/Crossover_distortion" title="Crossover distortion">crossover distortion</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quiescent_current" title="Quiescent current">quiescent current</a> may be subject to significant variation.</p>
<p>The output range of the amplifier is about one volt less than the supply voltage, owing in part to <i>V</i><sub>BE</sub> of the output transistors Q14 and Q20.</p>
<p>The 25 Ω resistor at the Q14 emitter, along with Q17, acts to limit Q14 current to about 25 mA; otherwise, Q17 conducts no current.</p>
<p>Current limiting for Q20 is performed in the voltage gain stage: Q22 senses the voltage across Q19's emitter resistor (50Ω); as it turns on, it diminishes the drive current to Q15 base.</p>
<p>Later versions of this amplifier schematic may show a somewhat different method of output current limiting.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Applicability_considerations">Applicability considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Applicability considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Note: while the 741 was historically used in audio and other sensitive equipment, such use is now rare because of the improved <a href="/wiki/Colors_of_noise" title="Colors of noise">noise</a> performance of more modern op-amps. Apart from generating noticeable hiss, 741s and other older op-amps may have poor <a href="/wiki/Common-mode_rejection_ratio" title="Common-mode rejection ratio">common-mode rejection ratios</a> and so will often introduce cable-borne mains hum and other common-mode interference, such as switch 'clicks', into sensitive equipment.</i></p>
<p>The "741" has come to often mean a generic op-amp IC (such as μA741, LM301, 558, LM324, TBA221 — or a more modern replacement such as the TL071). The description of the 741 output stage is qualitatively similar for many other designs (that may have quite different input stages), except:</p>
<ul>
<li>Some devices (μA748, LM301, LM308) are not internally compensated (require an external capacitor from output to some point within the operational amplifier, if used in low closed-loop gain applications).</li>
<li>Some modern devices have "rail-to-rail output" capability, meaning that the output can range from within a few millivolts of the positive supply voltage to within a few millivolts of the negative supply voltage.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Op-amps may be classified by their construction:</p>
<ul>
<li>discrete (built from individual <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">tubes/valves</a>)</li>
<li>IC (fabricated in an <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">Integrated circuit</a>) — most common</li>
<li>hybrid</li>
</ul>
<p>IC op-amps may be classified in many ways, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Military, Industrial, or Commercial grade (for example: the LM301 is the commercial grade version of the LM101, the LM201 is the industrial version). This may define <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a> ranges and other environmental or quality factors.</li>
<li>Classification by package type may also affect environmental hardiness, as well as manufacturing options; <a href="/wiki/Dual_in-line_package" title="Dual in-line package">DIP</a>, and other through-hole packages are tending to be replaced by <a href="/wiki/Surface-mount_technology" title="Surface-mount technology">surface-mount devices</a>.</li>
<li>Classification by internal compensation: op-amps may suffer from high frequency <a href="/wiki/Nyquist_stability_criterion" title="Nyquist stability criterion">instability</a> in some <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a> circuits unless a small compensation capacitor modifies the phase and frequency responses. Op-amps with a built-in capacitor are termed "<i>compensated</i>", or perhaps compensated for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_feedback_loops" title="Electronic feedback loops">closed-loop</a> gains down to (say) 5. All others are considered uncompensated.</li>
<li>Single, dual and quad versions of many commercial op-amp IC are available, meaning 1, 2 or 4 operational amplifiers are included in the same package.</li>
<li>Rail-to-rail input (and/or output) op-amps can work with input (and/or output) signals very close to the power supply rails.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a> op-amps (such as the CA3140E) provide extremely high input resistances, higher than <a href="/wiki/JFET" title="JFET">JFET</a>-input op-amps, which are normally higher than <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_junction_transistor" title="Bipolar junction transistor">bipolar</a>-input op-amps.</li>
<li>other varieties of op-amp include programmable op-amps (simply meaning the quiescent current, bandwidth and so on can be adjusted by an external resistor).</li>
<li>manufacturers often tabulate their op-amps according to purpose, such as low-noise pre-amplifiers, wide bandwidth amplifiers, and so on.</li>
</ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_electronics_system_design">Use in electronics system design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Use in electronics system design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of op-amps as circuit blocks is much easier and clearer than specifying all their individual circuit elements (transistors, resistors, etc.), whether the amplifiers used are integrated or discrete circuits. In the first approximation op-amps can be used as if they were ideal differential gain blocks; at a later stage limits can be placed on the acceptable range of parameters for each op-amp.</p>
<p>Circuit design follows the same lines for all electronic circuits. A specification is drawn up governing what the circuit is required to do, with allowable limits. For example, the gain may be required to be 100 times, with a tolerance of 5% but drift of less than 1% in a specified temperature range; the input impedance not less than one megohm; etc.</p>
<p>A basic <a href="/wiki/Electronic_circuit" title="Electronic circuit">circuit</a> is designed, often with the help of circuit modeling (on a computer). Specific commercially available op-amps and other components are then chosen that meet the design criteria within the specified tolerances at acceptable cost. If not all criteria can be met, the specification may need to be modified.</p>
<p>A prototype is then built and tested; changes to meet or improve the specification, alter functionality, or reduce the cost, may be made.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_without_using_any_feedback">Applications without using any feedback</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Applications without using any feedback">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>That is, the op-amp is being used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voltage_comparator" title="Voltage comparator">voltage comparator</a>. Note that a device designed primarily as a comparator may be better if, for instance, speed is important or a wide range of input voltages may be found, since such devices can quickly recover from full on or full off ("saturated") states.</p>
<p>A <i>voltage level detector</i> can be obtained if a reference voltage <i>V</i><sub>ref</sub> is applied to one of the op-amp's inputs. This means that the op-amp is set up as a comparator to detect a positive voltage. If the voltage to be sensed, <i>E</i><sub>i</sub>, is applied to op amp's (+) input, the result is a noninverting positive-level detector: when <i>E</i><sub>i</sub> is above <i>V</i><sub>ref</sub>, <i>V</i><sub>O</sub> equals +<i>V</i><sub>sat</sub>; when <i>E</i><sub>i</sub> is below <i>V</i><sub>ref</sub>, <i>V</i><sub>O</sub> equals −<i>V</i><sub>sat</sub>. If <i>E</i><sub>i</sub> is applied to the inverting input, the circuit is an inverting positive-level detector: When <i>E</i><sub>i</sub> is above <i>V</i><sub>ref</sub>, <i>V</i><sub>O</sub> equals −<i>V</i><sub>sat</sub>.</p>
<p>A <i>zero voltage level detector</i> (<i>E</i><sub>i</sub> = 0) can convert, for example, the output of a sine-wave from a function generator into a variable-frequency square wave. If <i>E</i><sub>i</sub> is a sine wave, triangular wave, or wave of any other shape that is symmetrical around zero, the zero-crossing detector's output will be square. Zero-crossing detection may also be useful in triggering <a href="/wiki/TRIAC" title="TRIAC">TRIACs</a> at the best time to reduce mains interference and current spikes.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Positive_feedback_applications">Positive feedback applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Positive feedback applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another typical configuration of op-amps is with positive feedback, which takes a fraction of the output signal back to the non-inverting input. An important application of it is the comparator with hysteresis, the <a href="/wiki/Schmitt_trigger" title="Schmitt trigger">Schmitt trigger</a>. Some circuits may use <i>Positive</i> feedback and <i>Negative</i> feedback around the same amplifier, for example <a href="/wiki/Triangle_wave" title="Triangle wave">Triangle wave</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oscillators" title="Oscillators">oscillators</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_filters" title="Active filters">active filters</a>.</p>
<p>Because of the wide slew-range and lack of positive feedback, the response of all the open-loop level detectors described <a href="#Applications_without_using_any_feedback">above</a> will be relatively slow. External overall positive feedback may be applied but (unlike internal positive feedback that may be applied within the latter stages of a purpose-designed comparator) this markedly affects the accuracy of the zero-crossing detection point. Using a general-purpose op-amp, for example, the frequency of <i>E</i><sub>i</sub> for the sine to square wave converter should probably be below 100 Hz.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>The non-inverting input of the operational amplifier needs a path for DC to ground; if the signal source does not supply a DC path, or if that source requires a given load impedance, then the circuit will require another resistor from the non-inverting input to ground. <p>A resistor is often inserted between the non-inverting input and ground (so both inputs "see" similar resistances), reducing the <a href="/wiki/Input_offset_voltage" title="Input offset voltage">input offset voltage</a> due to different voltage drops due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bias_current" title="Bias current">bias current</a>, and may reduce distortion in some op-amps.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Capacitive_coupling" title="Capacitive coupling">DC-blocking</a> <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">capacitor</a> may be inserted in series with the input resistor when a <a href="/wiki/Frequency_response" title="Frequency response">frequency response</a> down to DC is not needed and any DC voltage on the input is unwanted. That is, the capacitive component of the input impedance inserts a DC <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_zero" title="Complex zero">zero</a> and a low-frequency <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_pole" title="Complex pole">pole</a> that gives the circuit a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bandpass" title="Bandpass">bandpass</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-pass" title="High-pass">high-pass</a> characteristic.</p>
<p>The potentials at the operational amplifier inputs remain virtually constant (near ground) in the inverting configuration. The constant operating potential typically results in distortion levels that are lower than those attainable with the non-inverting topology.</p>

<p>Most single, dual and quad op-amps available have a standardized pin-out which permits one type to be substituted for another without wiring changes. A specific op-amp may be chosen for its open loop gain, bandwidth, noise performance, input impedance, power consumption, or a compromise between any of these factors.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_timeline">Historical timeline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operational_amplifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Historical timeline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>1941: A vacuum tube op-amp.</b> An op-amp, defined as a general-purpose, DC-coupled, high gain, inverting feedback <a href="/wiki/Amplifier" title="Amplifier">amplifier</a>, is first found in <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US2401779" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 2,401,779</a></span> "Summing Amplifier" filed by <a href="/wiki/Karl_D._Swartzel_Jr." title="Karl D. Swartzel Jr.">Karl D. Swartzel Jr.</a> of Bell Labs in 1941. This design used three <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tubes</a> to achieve a gain of <span class="nowrap">90 dB</span> and operated on voltage rails of <span class="nowrap">±350 V</span>. It had a single inverting input rather than differential inverting and non-inverting inputs, as are common in today's op-amps. Throughout <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Swartzel's design proved its value by being liberally used in the M9 <a href="/wiki/Director_(military)" title="Director (military)">artillery director</a> designed at Bell Labs. This artillery director worked with the SCR584 <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> system to achieve extraordinary hit rates (near 90%) that would not have been possible otherwise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jung-2004_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jung-2004-20">[16]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:K2-w_Vacuum_Tube_Op-amp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1086" data-file-width="546" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/K2-w_Vacuum_Tube_Op-amp.jpg/120px-K2-w_Vacuum_Tube_Op-amp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/K2-w_Vacuum_Tube_Op-amp.jpg/180px-K2-w_Vacuum_Tube_Op-amp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/K2-w_Vacuum_Tube_Op-amp.jpg/240px-K2-w_Vacuum_Tube_Op-amp.jpg 2x" width="120"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:K2-w_Vacuum_Tube_Op-amp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
GAP/R's K2-W: a vacuum-tube op-amp (1953)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><b>1947: An op-amp with an explicit non-inverting input.</b> In 1947, the operational amplifier was first formally defined and named in a paper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[17]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/John_R._Ragazzini" title="John R. Ragazzini">John R. Ragazzini</a> of Columbia University. In this same paper a footnote mentioned an op-amp design by a student that would turn out to be quite significant. This op-amp, designed by <a href="/wiki/Loebe_Julie" title="Loebe Julie">Loebe Julie</a>, was superior in a variety of ways. It had two major innovations. Its input stage used a long-tailed <a href="/wiki/Triode" title="Triode">triode</a> pair with loads matched to reduce drift in the output and, far more importantly, it was the first op-amp design to have two inputs (one inverting, the other non-inverting). The differential input made a whole range of new functionality possible, but it would not be used for a long time due to the rise of the chopper-stabilized amplifier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jung-2004_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jung-2004-20">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1949: A chopper-stabilized op-amp.</b> In 1949, Edwin A. Goldberg designed a <a href="/wiki/Chopper_(electronics)" title="Chopper (electronics)">chopper</a>-stabilized op-amp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[18]</a></sup> This set-up uses a normal op-amp with an additional <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">AC</a> amplifier that goes alongside the op-amp. The chopper gets an AC signal from <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">DC</a> by switching between the DC voltage and ground at a fast rate (60 Hz or 400 Hz). This signal is then amplified, rectified, filtered and fed into the op-amp's non-inverting input. This vastly improved the gain of the op-amp while significantly reducing the output drift and DC offset. Unfortunately, any design that used a chopper couldn't use their non-inverting input for any other purpose. Nevertheless, the much improved characteristics of the chopper-stabilized op-amp made it the dominant way to use op-amps. Techniques that used the non-inverting input regularly would not be very popular until the 1960s when op-amp <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">ICs</a> started to show up in the field.</p>
<p><b>1953: A commercially available op-amp.</b> In 1953, vacuum tube op-amps became commercially available with the release of the model K2-W from <a href="/wiki/George_A._Philbrick" title="George A. Philbrick">George A. Philbrick</a> Researches, Incorporated. The designation on the devices shown, GAP/R, is an acronym for the complete company name. Two nine-pin <a href="/wiki/12AX7" title="12AX7">12AX7</a> vacuum tubes were mounted in an octal package and had a model K2-P chopper add-on available that would effectively "use up" the non-inverting input. This op-amp was based on a descendant of Loebe Julie's 1947 design and, along with its successors, would start the widespread use of op-amps in industry.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Discrete_opamp.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="197" data-file-width="253" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Discrete_opamp.png/120px-Discrete_opamp.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Discrete_opamp.png/180px-Discrete_opamp.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Discrete_opamp.png/240px-Discrete_opamp.png 2x" width="120"/></a>

GAP/R's model P45: a solid-state, discrete op-amp (1961).</div>

<p><b>1961: A discrete IC op-amp.</b> With the birth of the <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistor</a> in 1947, and the silicon transistor in 1954, the concept of ICs became a reality. The introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Planar_process" title="Planar process">planar process</a> in 1959 made transistors and ICs stable enough to be commercially useful. By 1961, solid-state, discrete op-amps were being produced. These op-amps were effectively small circuit boards with packages such as <a href="/wiki/Edge_connector" title="Edge connector">edge connectors</a>. They usually had hand-selected resistors in order to improve things such as voltage offset and drift. The P45 (1961) had a gain of 94 dB and ran on ±15 V rails. It was intended to deal with signals in the range of <span class="nowrap">±10 V</span>.</p>
<p><b>1961: A varactor bridge op-amp.</b> There have been many different directions taken in op-amp design. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varactor" title="Varactor">Varactor</a> bridge op-amps started to be produced in the early 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[20]</a></sup> They were designed to have extremely small input current and are still amongst the best op-amps available in terms of common-mode rejection with the ability to correctly deal with hundreds of volts at their inputs.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Modular_opamp.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="199" data-file-width="206" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Modular_opamp.png/120px-Modular_opamp.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Modular_opamp.png/180px-Modular_opamp.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/97/Modular_opamp.png 2x" width="120"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">

GAP/R's model PP65: a solid-state op-amp in a potted module (1962)</div>

<p><b>1962: An op-amp in a potted module.</b> By 1962, several companies were producing modular potted packages that could be plugged into <a href="/wiki/Printed_circuit_board" title="Printed circuit board">printed circuit boards</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These packages were crucially important as they made the operational amplifier into a single <a href="/wiki/Black_box" title="Black box">black box</a> which could be easily treated as a component in a larger circuit.</p>
<p><b>1963: A monolithic IC op-amp.</b> In 1963, the first monolithic IC op-amp, the μA702 designed by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Widlar" title="Bob Widlar">Bob Widlar</a> at Fairchild Semiconductor, was released. Monolithic <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">ICs</a> consist of a single chip as opposed to a chip and discrete parts (a discrete IC) or multiple chips bonded and connected on a circuit board (a hybrid IC). Almost all modern op-amps are monolithic ICs; however, this first IC did not meet with much success. Issues such as an uneven supply voltage, low gain and a small dynamic range held off the dominance of monolithic op-amps until 1965 when the μA709<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[21]</a></sup> (also designed by Bob Widlar) was released.</p>
<p><b>1968: Release of the μA741.</b> The popularity of monolithic op-amps was further improved upon the release of the LM101 in 1967, which solved a variety of issues, and the subsequent release of the μA741 in 1968. The μA741 was extremely similar to the LM101 except that Fairchild's facilities allowed them to include a 30 pF compensation capacitor inside the chip instead of requiring external compensation. This simple difference has made the 741 <i>the</i> canonical op-amp and many modern amps base their pinout on the 741s. The μA741 is still in production, and has become ubiquitous in electronics—many manufacturers produce a version of this classic chip, recognizable by part numbers containing <i>741</i>. The same part is manufactured by several companies.</p>
<p><b>1970: First high-speed, low-input current FET design.</b> In the 1970s high speed, low-input current designs started to be made by using <a href="/wiki/Field-effect_transistor" title="Field-effect transistor">FETs</a>. These would be largely replaced by op-amps made with <a href="/wiki/MOSFET" title="MOSFET">MOSFETs</a> in the 1980s.</p>

ADI's HOS-050: a high speed hybrid IC op-amp (1979)</div>

<p><b>1972: Single sided supply op-amps being produced.</b> A single sided supply op-amp is one where the input and output voltages can be as low as the negative power supply voltage instead of needing to be at least two volts above it. The result is that it can operate in many applications with the negative supply pin on the op-amp being connected to the signal ground, thus eliminating the need for a separate negative power supply.</p>
<p>The LM324 (released in 1972) was one such op-amp that came in a quad package (four separate op-amps in one package) and became an industry standard. In addition to packaging multiple op-amps in a single package, the 1970s also saw the birth of op-amps in hybrid packages. These op-amps were generally improved versions of existing monolithic op-amps. As the properties of monolithic op-amps improved, the more complex hybrid ICs were quickly relegated to systems that are required to have extremely long service lives or other specialty systems.</p>

<p><b>Recent trends.</b> Recently supply voltages in analog circuits have decreased (as they have in digital logic) and low-voltage op-amps have been introduced reflecting this. Supplies of 5 V and increasingly 3.3 V (sometimes as low as 1.8 V) are common. To maximize the signal range modern op-amps commonly have rail-to-rail output (the output signal can range from the lowest supply voltage to the highest) and sometimes rail-to-rail inputs.</p>

<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-8"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-8">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">This definition hews to the convention of measuring op-amp parameters with respect to the zero voltage point in the circuit, which is usually half the total voltage between the amplifier's positive and negative power rails.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-9"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-9">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Many older designs of operational amplifiers have offset null inputs to allow the offset to be manually adjusted away. Modern precision op-amps can have internal circuits that automatically cancel this offset using <a href="/wiki/Chopper_(electronics)" title="Chopper (electronics)">choppers</a> or other circuits that measure the offset voltage periodically and subtract it from the input voltage.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-10"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-10">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">That the output cannot reach the power supply voltages is usually the result of limitations of the amplifier's output stage transistors. See <a href="#Output_stage">Output stage</a>.</span></li>
