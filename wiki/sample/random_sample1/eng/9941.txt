<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Order of operations</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">computer programming</a>, the <b>order of operations</b> (or <b>operator precedence</b>) is a collection of rules that reflect conventions about which procedures to perform first in order to evaluate a given <a href="/wiki/Expression_(mathematics)" title="Expression (mathematics)">mathematical expression</a>.</p>
<p>For example, in mathematics and most computer languages, multiplication is granted a higher precedence than addition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup> Thus, the expression 2 + 3 × 4 is interpreted so as to have the value 14, not 20. These conventions exist to eliminate ambiguity while allowing for notation that is as brief as possible. Where it is desired to override the precedence conventions, or even simply to emphasize them, brackets, <tt>(</tt> and <tt>)</tt>, <tt>{</tt> and <tt>}</tt>, or <tt>[</tt> and <tt>]</tt> — which have their own rules — can indicate an alternate order or reinforce the default order to avoid confusion. Thus the preceding expression could be changed to (2 + 3) × 4 to produce 20, or 2 + (3 × 4) to produce 14 (the default interpretation if there are no brackets).</p>
<p>Since the introduction of modern algebraic notation, multiplication has taken precedence over addition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Thus 3 + 4 × 5 = 4 × 5 + 3 = 23. When exponents were first introduced in the 16th and 17th centuries, exponents took precedence over both addition and multiplication and could be placed only as a superscript to the right of their base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup> Thus 3 + 5<sup>2</sup> = 28 and 3 × 5<sup>2</sup> = 75. To change the order of operations, originally a <a href="/wiki/Vinculum_(symbol)" title="Vinculum (symbol)">vinculum</a> (an overline or underline) was used. Today, <a href="/wiki/Bracket_(mathematics)" title="Bracket (mathematics)">parentheses or brackets</a> are used to explicitly denote precedence by grouping parts of an expression that should be evaluated first. Thus resulting in (2 + 3) × 4 = 20 to force addition to precede multiplication or (3 + 5)<sup>2</sup> = 64 to force addition to precede exponentiation.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_of_operations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The order of operations used throughout mathematics, science, technology and many computer <a href="/wiki/Programming_language" title="Programming language">programming languages</a> is expressed here:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Exponentiation" title="Exponentiation">exponents</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Nth_root" title="Nth root">roots</a></dd>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Multiplication" title="Multiplication">multiplication</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Division_(mathematics)" title="Division (mathematics)">division</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup></dd>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Addition" title="Addition">addition</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Subtraction" title="Subtraction">subtraction</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>This means that if a mathematical expression is preceded by one binary operator and followed by another, the operator higher on the list should be applied first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Commutative_property" title="Commutative property">commutative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Associative_property" title="Associative property">associative</a> laws of addition and multiplication allow adding terms in any order, and multiplying factors in any order—but mixed operations must obey the standard order of operations.</p>
<p>It is helpful to treat division as multiplication by the reciprocal (multiplicative inverse) and subtraction as addition of the opposite (additive inverse). Thus 3/4 = 3 ÷ 4 = 3 • ¼; in other words the quotient of 3 and 4 equals the product of 3 and  ¼. Also 3 − 4 = 3 + (−4); in other words the difference of 3 and 4 equals the sum of positive three and negative four. Thus, 1 − 3 + 7 can be thought of as the sum of 1, negative 3, and 7, and add in any order: (1 − 3) + 7 = −2 + 7 = 5 and in reverse order (7 − 3) + 1 = 4 + 1 = 5, always keeping the negative sign with the 3.</p>
<p>The root symbol, √, requires a symbol of grouping around the radicand. The usual symbol of grouping is a bar (called <a href="/wiki/Vinculum_(symbol)" title="Vinculum (symbol)">vinculum</a>) over the radicand. Other functions use parentheses around the input to avoid ambiguity. The parentheses are sometimes omitted if the input is a <a href="/wiki/Monomial" title="Monomial">monomial</a>. Thus, sin 3x = sin(3x), but sin x + y = sin(x) + y, because x + y is not a monomial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup> Some calculators and programming languages require parentheses around function inputs, some do not.</p>
<p>Stacked exponents are applied from the top down, i.e., from right to left.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST_2010_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIST_2010-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Symbols of grouping can be used to override the usual order of operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup> Grouped symbols can be treated as a single expression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup> Symbols of grouping can be removed using the associative and distributive laws, also they can be removed if the expression inside the symbol of grouping is sufficiently simplified so no ambiguity results from their removal.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exceptions">Exceptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_of_operations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Exceptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There exist differing conventions concerning the unary operator − (usually read "minus"). In written or printed mathematics, the expression −3<sup>2</sup> is interpreted to mean 0 − (3<sup>2</sup>) = −9,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> but in some applications and programming languages, notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microsoft_Office_Excel" title="Microsoft Office Excel">Microsoft Office Excel</a> (and other spreadsheet applications) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bc_programming_language" title="Bc programming language">the programming language bc</a>, unary operators have a higher priority than binary operators, that is, the unary minus (negation or <kbd class="keyboard-key nowrap" style="border: 1px solid #aaa; -moz-border-radius: 0.2em; -webkit-border-radius: 0.2em; border-radius: 0.2em; -moz-box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); -webkit-box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); background-color: #f9f9f9; background-image: -moz-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: -o-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: -webkit-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: linear-gradient(to bottom, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); padding: 0.1em 0.3em; font-family: inherit; font-size: 0.85em;">+/-</kbd>) has higher precedence than exponentiation, so in those languages −3<sup>2</sup> will be interpreted as (−3)<sup>2</sup> = 9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This does not apply to the binary minus operator −; for example while the formulas <code>=-2^2</code> and <code>=0+-2^2</code> return 4 in Microsoft Excel, the formula <code>=0-2^2</code> returns −4. In cases where there is the possibility that the notation might be misinterpreted, a binary minus operation can be enforced by explicitly specifying a leading 0 (as in 0-2^2 instead of just -2^2), or parentheses can be used to clarify the intended meaning.</p>

<p>With this interpretation 1/2<i>x</i> is equal to (1/2)<i>x</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bronstein_1987_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-Bronstein_1987-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, in some of the academic literature, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiplication_denoted_by_juxtaposition" title="Multiplication denoted by juxtaposition">multiplication denoted by juxtaposition</a> (also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Implied_multiplication" title="Implied multiplication">implied multiplication</a>) is interpreted as having higher precedence than division, so that 1/2<i>x</i> equals 1/(2<i>x</i>), not (1/2)<i>x</i>. For example, the manuscript submission instructions for the <i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review" title="Physical Review">Physical Review</a></i> journals state that multiplication is of higher precedence than division with a slash,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and this is also the convention observed in prominent physics textbooks such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Course_of_Theoretical_Physics" title="Course of Theoretical Physics">Course of Theoretical Physics</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Lev_Landau" title="Lev Landau">Landau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evgeny_Lifshitz" title="Evgeny Lifshitz">Lifshitz</a> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feynman_Lectures_on_Physics" title="Feynman Lectures on Physics">Feynman Lectures on Physics</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[nb 1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Please note many academics consider PEMDAS as non-applicable with implied groupings that are ambiguous such as 1/2<i>x</i> where the lack of an explicit operator × between the 2 and the <i>x</i> implies a grouping of the 2 with the <i>x</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mnemonics"><span id="PEMDAS"></span><span id="PEDMAS"></span><span id="BEDMAS"></span><span id="BEMDAS"></span><span id="BODMAS"></span><span id="BIDMAS"></span><span id="BIMDAS"></span><span id="BOMDAS"></span><span id="GEMDAS"></span><span id="BERDMAS"></span><span id="PERDMAS"></span><span id="BPODMAS"></span>Mnemonics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_of_operations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mnemonics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mnemonics" title="Mnemonics">Mnemonics</a> are often used to help students remember the rules, but the rules taught by the use of acronyms can be misleading. In the United States the acronym <i>PEMDAS</i> is common. It stands for <i>P</i>arentheses, <i>E</i>xponents, <i>M</i>ultiplication, <i>D</i>ivision, <i>A</i>ddition, <i>S</i>ubtraction. PEMDAS is often expanded to "Please Excuse My Dear Aunt Sally", with the first letter of each word creating the acronym PEMDAS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ball_1978_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ball_1978-7">[7]</a></sup> Canada and New Zealand use <i>BEDMAS</i>, standing for <i>B</i>rackets, <i>E</i>xponents, <i>D</i>ivision, <i>M</i>ultiplication, <i>A</i>ddition, <i>S</i>ubtraction. Most common in the UK and Australia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> are <i>BODMAS</i> meaning "B"rackets, "O"f or "O"rder, "D"ivision, "M"ultiplication, "A"ddition and "S"ubtraction in Nigeria and some other West African countries and <i>BIDMAS</i>. In some English speaking countries, <i>P</i>arentheses may be called <i>B</i>rackets, or symbols of inclusion and <i>E</i>xponents may be called either <i>I</i>ndices, <i>P</i>owers or <i>O</i>rders, which have the same precedence as <i>R</i>oots or <i>R</i>adicals. Since multiplication and division are of equal precedence, <i>M</i> and <i>D</i> are often interchanged, leading to such acronyms as <i>BOMDAS</i>. The original order of operations in some countries was <i>BODMAS</i>, which stands for <i>B</i>rackets, <i>O</i>rders or p<i>O</i>wers, <i>D</i>ivision, <i>M</i>ultiplication, <i>A</i>ddition, <i>S</i>ubtraction. The <i>O</i> is sometimes associated with <i>O</i>f. This mnemonic was common until exponentials were added into the mnemonic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Programming_languages">Programming languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_of_operations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Programming languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Programming_languages" title="Programming languages">programming languages</a> use precedence levels that conform to the order commonly used in mathematics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Codeplea_2016_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Codeplea_2016-16">[15]</a></sup> though others, such as <a href="/wiki/APL_(programming_language)" title="APL (programming language)">APL</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smalltalk" title="Smalltalk">Smalltalk</a> or <a href="/wiki/Occam_(programming_language)" title="Occam (programming language)">Occam</a>, have no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operator_(programming)" title="Operator (programming)">operator</a> precedence rules (in APL, evaluation is strictly right to left; in Smalltalk and Occam, it's strictly left to right).</p>
<p>In addition, because many operators are not associative, the order within any single level is usually defined by grouping left to right so that 16/4/4 = (16/4)/4 = 1 rather than 16/(4/4) = 16.</p>

<p>Examples:</p>
<p>(Note: in the examples below, '≡' is used to mean "is equivalent to", and not to be interpreted as an actual assignment operator used as part of the example expression.)</p>
<ul>
<li><code>!A + !B</code> ≡ <code>(!A) + (!B)</code></li>
<li><code>++A + !B</code> ≡ <code>(++A) + (!B)</code></li>
<li><code>A + B * C</code> ≡ <code>A + (B * C)</code></li>
<li><code>A || B &amp;&amp; C</code> ≡ <code>A || (B &amp;&amp; C)</code></li>
<li><code>A &amp;&amp; B == C</code> ≡ <code>A &amp;&amp; (B == C)</code></li>
<li><code>A &amp; B == C</code> ≡ <code>A &amp; (B == C)</code></li>
</ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Source-to-source_compiler" title="Source-to-source compiler">Source-to-source compilers</a> that compile to multiple languages need to explicitly deal with the issue of different order of operations across languages. <a href="/wiki/Haxe" title="Haxe">Haxe</a> for example standardizes the order and enforces it by inserting brackets where it is appropriate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The accuracy of software developer knowledge about binary operator precedence has been found to closely follow their frequency of occurrence in source code.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
