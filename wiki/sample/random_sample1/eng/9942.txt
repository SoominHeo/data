<p><b>Yeon Sang-ho</b> (born 1978) is a <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korean</a> <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">film director</a> and <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">screenwriter</a>. He wrote and directed the <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">animated</a> films <i><a href="/wiki/The_King_of_Pigs" title="The King of Pigs">The King of Pigs</a></i> (2011) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fake_(2013_film)" title="The Fake (2013 film)">The Fake</a></i> (2013), and the live-action film <i><a href="/wiki/Train_to_Busan" title="Train to Busan">Train to Busan</a></i> (2016).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeon_Sang-ho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a> in 1978, Yeon Sang-ho graduated from <a href="/wiki/Sangmyung_University" title="Sangmyung University">Sangmyung University</a> with a degree in Western Painting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He directed his first animated <a href="/wiki/Short_film" title="Short film">short film</a>, <i>Megalomania of D</i> in 1997, followed by <i>D-Day</i> in 2000 and <i>The Hell</i> in 2003, then set up his own production house Studio Dadashow in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> His next two animated shorts <i>The Hell: Two Kinds of Life</i> (2006) and <i>Love Is Protein</i> (2008) were invited to various international film festivals. <i>The Hell: Two Kinds of Life</i> won the Asian Ghost Award at the Short Shorts Film Festival Asia and the Public Award for Best Film School (Short Film Battle Royal) at the 2007 Lyon Asian Film Festival, and <i>Love Is Protein</i> screened in competition at the 2009 Curtocircuit International Short Film Festival of Santiago de Compostela in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>Love Is Protein</i> was later included in the three-short <a href="/wiki/Anthology" title="Anthology">omnibus</a> <i>Indie Anibox: Selma's Protein Coffee</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Yeon also directed the animated opening trailer for the <a href="/wiki/Busan_International_Film_Festival" title="Busan International Film Festival">Busan International Film Festival</a> in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Yeon's first feature-length animation was <i><a href="/wiki/The_King_of_Pigs" title="The King of Pigs">The King of Pigs</a></i> (2011), about a man who kills his wife after his business goes bankrupt, and seeks out his long-lost friend, a <a href="/wiki/Ghostwriter" title="Ghostwriter">ghostwriter</a>, 15 years after both had been severely <a href="/wiki/School_bullying" title="School bullying">bullied</a> as adolescents in middle school. Inspired by the works of <a href="/wiki/Satoshi_Kon" title="Satoshi Kon">Satoshi Kon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minoru_Furuya" title="Minoru Furuya">Minoru Furuya</a>, Yeon said the incidents in the film were drawn from his own life, and he cried while writing the screenplay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The low-budget (<span style="white-space: nowrap">US$150,000</span>) film drew widespread critical acclaim for its raw portrayal of <a href="/wiki/Bullying" title="Bullying">bullying</a>, violence and systemic poverty (and the lifelong effects of such oppression), as well as the psychology of public attitudes toward a hero figure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It became the first Korean animated film to be invited to the <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a>, where it screened in the <a href="/wiki/2012_Cannes_Film_Festival" title="2012 Cannes Film Festival">2012</a> <a href="/wiki/Directors%27_Fortnight" title="Directors' Fortnight">Directors' Fortnight</a> sidebar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It won numerous awards at domestic and international film festivals, including the Director's Guild of Korea Award for Best Director, CGV Movie Collage Award, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NETPAC_Award" title="NETPAC Award">NETPAC Award</a> at the <a href="/wiki/16th_Busan_International_Film_Festival" title="16th Busan International Film Festival">2011 Busan International Film Festival</a>, the Satoshi Kon Award for Achievement in Animation and Special Mention (New Flesh Award for Best First Feature) at the 2012 <a href="/wiki/Fantasia_International_Film_Festival" title="Fantasia International Film Festival">Fantasia International Film Festival</a>, and the Jury Prize at the 2013 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jameson_Dublin_International_Film_Festival" title="Jameson Dublin International Film Festival">Jameson Dublin International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>His follow-up <i>The Window</i> was a 30-minute animated short depicting violence in the military, and was the first film of the Independent Short Film Release Project organized by Indiespace, an <a href="/wiki/Independent_film" title="Independent film">independent</a>-only theater and Indieplug, a digital distributor of independent films. Yeon said the script (illustrated by cartoonist Choi Gyu-seok) was 100% based on his own personal experience while doing his <a href="/wiki/Conscription_in_South_Korea" title="Conscription in South Korea">mandatory military service</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <i>The Window</i> won a Special Mention from the Jury at the 2013 Fantasia International Film Festival.</p>
<p>Yeon continued to make animation targeted at adults with dark, controversial themes that brutally and incisively explore human nature and social realism. His second feature <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fake_(2013_film)" title="The Fake (2013 film)">The Fake</a></i> (2013) critiqued organized religion, as a cult leader swindles rural, uneducated villagers out of their compensation money, while no one believes the local wastrel who discovers the truth (the characters were <a href="/wiki/Voice_acting" title="Voice acting">voiced</a> by <a href="/wiki/Oh_Jung-se" title="Oh Jung-se">Oh Jung-se</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yang_Ik-june" title="Yang Ik-june">Yang Ik-june</a>, who previously starred in <i>Love Is Protein</i> and <i>The King of Pigs</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Yeon said he wrote the script in 2009 because of his political dissatisfaction regarding issues about the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_trade_agreement_between_the_United_States_of_America_and_the_Republic_of_Korea" title="Free trade agreement between the United States of America and the Republic of Korea">FTA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Four_Major_Rivers_Project" title="Four Major Rivers Project">Four Major Rivers Project</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <i>The Fake</i> made its world premiere at the <a href="/wiki/2013_Toronto_International_Film_Festival" title="2013 Toronto International Film Festival">2013 Toronto International Film Festival</a>, and won Best Film of AnimaFICX at the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Gij%C3%B3n_International_Film_Festival" title="Gijón International Film Festival">Gijón International Film Festival</a>, Best Animated Feature Film at the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Sitges_Film_Festival" title="Sitges Film Festival">Sitges Film Festival</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIPRESCI_Award" title="FIPRESCI Award">FIPRESCI Award</a> at the 2014 <a href="/wiki/Korean_Association_of_Film_Critics_Awards" title="Korean Association of Film Critics Awards">Korean Association of Film Critics Awards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>He then cast <a href="/wiki/Ryu_Seung-ryong" title="Ryu Seung-ryong">Ryu Seung-ryong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shim_Eun-kyung" title="Shim Eun-kyung">Shim Eun-kyung</a> as voice actors in his third animated feature, <i><a href="/wiki/Seoul_Station_(film)" title="Seoul Station (film)">Seoul Station</a></i> (2015).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Yeon said he wanted to depict society's collective rage in a "simple, powerful way" by making a <a href="/wiki/Zombie" title="Zombie">zombie</a> film in which zombies are among people protesting for the democratization of Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zombie_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-zombie-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Yeon has also begun shooting his first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Live-action" title="Live-action">live-action</a> film <i>Busan-Bound</i>, which follows a man who jumps aboard a <a href="/wiki/Busan" title="Busan">Busan</a>-bound <a href="/wiki/Korea_Train_Express" title="Korea Train Express">KTX</a> train to meet his wife in the early morning. He is likewise writing the draft of another live-action film <i>Seonsan</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Thriller_(genre)" title="Thriller (genre)">thriller</a> that deals with the inheritance of a family graveyard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zombie_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-zombie-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
