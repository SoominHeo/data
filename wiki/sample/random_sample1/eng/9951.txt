<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Serial (literature)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Serial_(publishing)" title="Serial (publishing)">Serial (publishing)</a>.</div>
<p>In literature, a <b>serial</b> is a printed format by which a single larger work, often a work of narrative fiction, is published in sequential installments. The installments are also known as <i>numbers</i>, <i>parts</i> or <i>fascicles</i>, and are either issued as separate publications or within in sequential issues of the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periodical_publication" title="Periodical publication">periodical publication</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serial_(literature)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The growth of moveable type in the 17th century prompted episodic and often disconnected narratives such as <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Astr%C3%A9e" title="L'Astrée">L'Astrée</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Artam%C3%A8ne" title="Artamène">Le Grand Cyrus</a></i>. At that time, books remained a premium item, so to reduce the price and expand the market, publishers produced large works in lower-cost installments called fascicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century_including_early_1900s">19th century including early 1900s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serial_(literature)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 19th century including early 1900s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Serialized fiction surged in popularity during Britain's <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a>, due to a combination of the rise of literacy, technological advances in printing, and improved economics of distribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Law_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Law-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:34</sup> A significant majority of "original" novels from the Victorian era actually first appeared in either monthly or weekly installments in magazines or newspapers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Law_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Law-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:13</sup> The wild success of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pickwick_Papers" title="The Pickwick Papers">The Pickwick Papers</a></i>, first published in 1836, is widely considered to have established the viability and appeal of the serialized format within periodical literature. During that era, the line between "quality" and "commercial" literature was not distinct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Law_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Law-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:31</sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German-speaking_countries" title="German-speaking countries">German-speaking countries</a>, the serialized novel was widely popularized by the weekly family magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Gartenlaube" title="Die Gartenlaube">Die Gartenlaube</a></i>, which reached a circulation of 382,000 by 1875.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belgum1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Belgum1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas" title="Alexandre Dumas">Alexandre Dumas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Sue" title="Eugène Sue">Eugène Sue</a> were masters of the serialized genre. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Three_Musketeers" title="The Three Musketeers">The Three Musketeers</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Count_of_Monte_Cristo" title="The Count of Monte Cristo">The Count of Monte Cristo</a></i> each appeared as a <i><a href="/wiki/Feuilleton" title="Feuilleton">feuilleton</a></i>. <i>The Count of Monte Cristo</i> was stretched out to 139 installments. Eugène Sue's serial novel <i>Le Juif errant</i> increased circulation of <i>Le Constitutionnel</i> from 3,600 to 25,000. Production in book form soon followed and serialization was one of the main reasons that nineteenth-century novels were so long. Authors and publishers kept the story going if it was successful since authors were paid by line and by episode.</p>
<p>Some writers were prolific. Alexandre Dumas wrote at an incredible pace, oftentimes writing with his partner twelve to fourteen hours a day, working on several novels for serialized publication at once. However, not every writer could keep up with the serial writing pace. <a href="/wiki/Wilkie_Collins" title="Wilkie Collins">Wilkie Collins</a>, for instance, was never more than a week before publication. The difference in writing pace and output in large part determined the author's success, as audience appetite created demand for further installments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>While American periodicals first syndicated British writers, over time they drew from a growing base of domestic authors. The rise of the periodicals like <i><a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Magazine" title="Harper's Magazine">Harper's</a></i> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Monthly" title="Atlantic Monthly">Atlantic Monthly</a></i> grew in symbiotic tandem with American literary talent. The magazines nurtured and provided an economic sustainability for writers, while the writers helped grow the periodicals' circulation base. During the late 19th century, those that were considered the best American writers first published their work in serial form and then only later in a completed volume format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lund_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lund-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:51</sup> As a piece in <i><a href="/wiki/Scribner%27s_Monthly" title="Scribner's Monthly">Scribner's Monthly</a></i> explained in 1878, "Now it is the second or third rate novelist who cannot get publication in a magazine, and is obliged to publish in a volume, and it is in the magazine that the best novelist always appears first."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lund_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lund-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:52</sup> Among the American writers that wrote in serial form were <a href="/wiki/Henry_James" title="Henry James">Henry James</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harriet_Beecher_Stowe" title="Harriet Beecher Stowe">Harriet Beecher Stowe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herman_Melville" title="Herman Melville">Herman Melville</a>. A large part of the appeal for writers at the time was the broad audiences that serialization could reach, which would then grow their following for published works.</p>
<p>One of the first significant American works to be released in serial format is <i><a href="/wiki/Uncle_Tom%27s_Cabin" title="Uncle Tom's Cabin">Uncle Tom's Cabin</a></i>, by Harriet Beecher Stowe, which was published over a 40-week period by <i><a href="/wiki/The_National_Era" title="The National Era">The National Era</a></i>, an abolitionist periodical, starting with the June 5, 1851 issue.</p>
<p>Serialization was so standard in American literature that authors from that era often built installment structure into their creative process. <a href="/wiki/Henry_James" title="Henry James">Henry James</a>, for example, often had his works divided into multi-part segments of similar length.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lund_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lund-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:30</sup> The consumption of fiction during that time was different than in the 20th century. Instead of being read in a single volume, a novel would often be consumed by readers in installments over a period as long as a year, with the authors and periodicals often responding to audience reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lund_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lund-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Serialization was also popular throughout Europe. In France, <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">Gustave Flaubert</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Madame_Bovary" title="Madame Bovary">Madame Bovary</a></i> was serialized in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Revue_de_Paris" title="La Revue de Paris">La Revue de Paris</a></i> in 1856. In Russia, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Russian_Messenger" title="The Russian Messenger">The Russian Messenger</a></i> serialized <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">Leo Tolstoy</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Anna_Karenina" title="Anna Karenina">Anna Karenina</a></i> from 1873 to 1877 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fyodor_Dostoevsky" title="Fyodor Dostoevsky">Fyodor Dostoevsky</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Brothers_Karamazov" title="The Brothers Karamazov">The Brothers Karamazov</a></i> from 1879 to 1880.</p>
<p>In addition, works in late <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a> China had been serialized. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nine-tailed_Turtle" title="The Nine-tailed Turtle">The Nine-tailed Turtle</a></i> was serialized from 1906 to 1910.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WangDeweip181_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-WangDeweip181-7">[7]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Bizarre_Happenings_Eyewitnessed_Over_Two_Decades" title="Bizarre Happenings Eyewitnessed Over Two Decades">Bizarre Happenings Eyewitnessed Over Two Decades</a></i> was serialized in <i>Xin Xiaoshuo</i> (T: 新小說, S: 新小说, P: <i>Xīn Xiǎoshuō</i>; W: <i>Hsin Hsiao-shuo</i>; "New Fiction"), a magazine by <a href="/wiki/Liang_Qichao" title="Liang Qichao">Liang Qichao</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chineseuniversityinsidecover_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chineseuniversityinsidecover-8">[8]</a></sup> The first half of <i><a href="/wiki/Officialdom_Unmasked" title="Officialdom Unmasked">Officialdom Unmasked</a></i> appeared in installments of <i><a href="/wiki/Shanghai_Shijie_Fanhua_Bao" title="Shanghai Shijie Fanhua Bao">Shanghai Shijie Fanhua Bao</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holochp76_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Holochp76-9">[9]</a></sup> serialized there from April 1903 to June 1905.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other famous English language writers who wrote serial literature for popular magazines included <a href="/wiki/Wilkie_Collins" title="Wilkie Collins">Wilkie Collins</a>, inventor of the English <a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">detective novel</a> and author of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Moonstone" title="The Moonstone">The Moonstone</a></i>; Sir <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a>, who created the <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> stories originally for serialization in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strand_Magazine" title="Strand Magazine">The Strand</a></i> magazine; and the <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> writer <a href="/wiki/Boles%C5%82aw_Prus" title="Bolesław Prus">Bolesław Prus</a>, author of the serialized novels <i><a href="/wiki/The_Outpost_(Prus_novel)" title="The Outpost (Prus novel)">The Outpost</a></i> (1885–86), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Doll_(novel)" title="The Doll (novel)">The Doll</a></i> (1887–89), <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Woman" title="The New Woman">The New Woman</a></i> (1890–93), and his sole <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_novel" title="Historical novel">historical novel</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pharaoh_(novel)" title="Pharaoh (novel)">Pharaoh</a></i> (the latter, exceptionally, written entire over a year's time in 1894–95 and serialized only after completion, in 1895–96).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_20th.2Fearly_21st_centuries">Late 20th/early 21st centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serial_(literature)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Late 20th/early 21st centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the rise of broadcast—both radio and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_series" title="Television series">television series</a>—in the first half of the 20th century, printed periodical fiction began a slow decline as newspapers and magazines shifted their focus from entertainment to information and news. However, some serialization of novels in periodicals continued, with mixed success.</p>
<p>Starting in 1984, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wolfe" title="Tom Wolfe">Tom Wolfe</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bonfire_of_the_Vanities" title="The Bonfire of the Vanities">The Bonfire of the Vanities</a></i>, about 1980s New York City, ran in 27 parts in <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i>, partially inspired by the model of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>. <i>Rolling Stone</i> paid $200,000 for his work, but Wolfe heavily revised the work before publication as a standalone novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michael_Chabon" title="Michael Chabon">Michael Chabon</a> also serialized <i><a href="/wiki/Gentlemen_of_the_Road" title="Gentlemen of the Road">Gentlemen of the Road</a></i> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Magazine" title="The New York Times Magazine">The New York Times Magazine</a></i> in 2007.</p>
<p>During the late 20th century, the emergence of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> prompted some authors to again try a serial format. <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">Stephen King</a> experimented with this format with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Plant" title="The Plant">The Plant</a></i> (2000), and <a href="/wiki/Michel_Faber" title="Michel Faber">Michel Faber</a> allowed <i>The Guardian</i> to serialise his novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crimson_Petal_and_the_White" title="The Crimson Petal and the White">The Crimson Petal and the White</a></i>. In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Orson_Scott_Card" title="Orson Scott Card">Orson Scott Card</a> serialized his out-of-print novel <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Sleep" title="Hot Sleep">Hot Sleep</a></i> in the first issue of his online magazine, Orson Scott Card's <i><a href="/wiki/InterGalactic_Medicine_Show" title="InterGalactic Medicine Show">InterGalactic Medicine Show</a></i>. In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonymous</a> author Wildbow published <i><a href="/wiki/Worm_(web_serial)" title="Worm (web serial)">Worm</a></i>, which remains one of the most popular web serials of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beacon-Villager_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beacon-Villager-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Star_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Star-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>The rise of <a href="/wiki/Fan_fiction" title="Fan fiction">fan fiction</a> on the internet also follows a serial fiction style of publication, as seen on popular fan fiction websites such as <a href="/wiki/FanFiction.Net" title="FanFiction.Net">FanFiction.Net</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archive_of_Our_Own#Archive_of_Our_Own" title="Archive of Our Own">Archive of Our Own (AO3)</a>.</p>
<p>Many aspiring authors also used the web to publish free-to-read works in serialized format independently as well as web-based communities such as <a href="/wiki/LiveJournal" title="LiveJournal">LiveJournal</a>, Fictionpress.com,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> fictionhub,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Wattpad" title="Wattpad">Wattpad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Many of these books receive as many readers as successful novels; some have received the same number of readers as New York Times bestsellers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition, the prevalence of mobile devices made the serial format even more popular with the likes of JukePop Serials<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> with iOS<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and Android<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> apps that focuses entirely on curating and promoting serialized novels.</p>
