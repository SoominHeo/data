<p>The <b>Battle of Yan Ford</b> was a battle that took place along the southern banks of the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow River</a> in the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Han_dynasty" title="Eastern Han dynasty">Eastern Han dynasty</a>. The battle was closely preceded by the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Boma" title="Battle of Boma">Battle of Boma</a>, and was part of a series of engagements leading to the decisive confrontation between the rival warlords <a href="/wiki/Yuan_Shao" title="Yuan Shao">Yuan Shao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cao_Cao" title="Cao Cao">Cao Cao</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Guandu" title="Battle of Guandu">Battle of Guandu</a> in 200. Following the death of one of Yuan Shao's elite generals, <a href="/wiki/Yan_Liang" title="Yan Liang">Yan Liang</a>, in the previous battle, the death of another famed general <a href="/wiki/Wen_Chou" title="Wen Chou">Wen Chou</a> in this battle greatly demoralised Yuan's army, which was heading towards Guandu.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Yan_Ford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cao Cao and Yuan Shao were heading toward a direct confrontation since the late 190s, and the first months of 200 saw the opening of hostilities at the Battle of Boma after years of manoeuvring. With a feint, Cao Cao was able to split off Yuan Shao's main force gathered at Liyang (黎陽; northwest of present-day <a href="/wiki/Xun_County" title="Xun County">Xun County</a>, Henan) across the river from Boma (白馬; near present-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hua_County,_Henan" title="Hua County, Henan">Hua County</a>, Henan) and hence relieve the siege at Boma, killing Yuan Shao's attacking general Yan Liang. Considering that the position at Boma was unsuitable for a determined defence, Cao Cao voluntarily abandoned the outpost and evacuated its population and supplies to the west along the river. With that, Yuan Shao finally crossed the Yellow River in pursuit of Cao Cao's baggage train. However, Cao Cao was not satisfied with just abandoning all lands between Guandu and the river to the enemy — he had also wanted to make his enemy pay dearly by resisting the enemy advance during the predetermined fallback to Guandu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_battle">The battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Yan_Ford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a result of Cao Cao's feint during the Battle of Boma, Yuan Shao had brought the advance-guard of his army up the Yellow River toward the western Yan Ford (延津; north of present-day <a href="/wiki/Yanjin_County,_Henan" title="Yanjin County, Henan">Yanjin</a>, Henan). It was probably at this ford that Yuan Shao's army made the crossing, which was unhindered by Cao Cao.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deCJ4n15_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-deCJ4n15-2">[2]</a></sup> By this time, Yuan Shao was almost due north of Cao Cao's defensive positions at Guandu and his base in the capital of <a href="/wiki/Xuchang" title="Xuchang">Xu City</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Cao Cao's men also reached Yan Ford and made camp below a ridge known as the Southern Slope (南阪), 20 <i><a href="/wiki/Li_(unit)" title="Li (unit)">li</a></i> west and 50 <i>li</i> south of Boma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As Cao Cao's position was on the southern side of a dyke some distance away from the river (meant to control seasonal floodwaters),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deCJ4n15_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-deCJ4n15-2">[2]</a></sup> his men were concealed from Yuan Shao's view while Cao Cao had to send lookouts to scout for enemy movements. The lookout first reported five to six hundred enemy cavalry on their way, then reported that there were slightly more cavalry than before and the size of the infantry force was beyond measurement. At this point Cao Cao stopped the lookout from reporting, and had his horsemen undo the saddles and release the horses.</p>
<p>The baggage train from Boma appeared on the road north of the dyke at this time, in plain view of Yuan Shao's men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Some of Cao Cao's officers became uneasy at the prospect of so many enemy horsemen, and suggested to return and defend the camp. Cao Cao's strategist <a href="/wiki/Xun_You" title="Xun You">Xun You</a> objected, saying: "This is the way we bait the enemy! How can we leave?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L358_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-L358-6">[6]</a></sup> Cao Cao glanced at him and smiled.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wen_Chou" title="Wen Chou">Wen Chou</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liu_Bei" title="Liu Bei">Liu Bei</a>, commanders of Yuan Shao's cavalry, leading five to six thousand cavalry, came one after the other. Cao Cao's men asked to mount their horses, but Cao Cao denied them until Yuan Shao's horsemen, ever increasing in number, split off to raid the baggage train. Cao Cao then gave the permission to mount the horses, and all of Cao Cao's horsemen, less than 600 in number, jumped onto their horses and charged at the enemy. Yuan Shao's army was defeated and Wen Chou was killed in action.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Yan_Ford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yan Liang and Wen Chou were the most highly regarded generals in Yuan Shao's army, and both were killed in two successive battles. Yuan Shao's men became greatly shaken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L358_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-L358-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>With the victory at Yan Ford, Cao Cao was able to retreat back to his base in Guandu (官渡; northeast of present-day <a href="/wiki/Zhongmu_County" title="Zhongmu County">Zhongmu County</a>, Henan) with the men and supplies without incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L358_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-L358-6">[6]</a></sup> Yuan Shao followed close behind and made camp at Yangwu (陽武; near present-day <a href="/wiki/Yuanyang_County,_Henan" title="Yuanyang County, Henan">Yuanyang County</a>, Henan), immediately north of Guandu. He had ignored <a href="/wiki/Ju_Shou" title="Ju Shou">Ju Shou</a>'s advice to leave a garrison at Yan Ford as a cautious step, and had all his forded forces concentrated at Yangwu, and now a decisive battle became imminent.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Romance_of_the_Three_Kingdoms">In <i>Romance of the Three Kingdoms</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_of_Yan_Ford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In Romance of the Three Kingdoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Battle of Yan Ford was the opening event in chapter 26 of <a href="/wiki/Luo_Guanzhong" title="Luo Guanzhong">Luo Guanzhong</a>'s historical novel <i><a href="/wiki/Romance_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Romance of the Three Kingdoms">Romance of the Three Kingdoms</a></i>.</p>
<p>The battle described in the novel closely followed the one in history until Cao Cao made the call to charge at Wen Chou's forces. At that time <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Liao" title="Zhang Liao">Zhang Liao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xu_Huang" title="Xu Huang">Xu Huang</a>, two of Cao Cao's best generals, gave chase. Wen Chou fired two arrows from atop his horse, one of which sliced off the feather on Zhang Liao's helmet and the other hit Zhang Liao's horse in the face. Brandishing his poleaxe, Xu Huang came for Wen Chou but had to retreat when a band of enemy soldiers came to their commander's rescue.</p>
<p>Leading a dozen riders, <a href="/wiki/Guan_Yu" title="Guan Yu">Guan Yu</a> then cut off Wen Chou's escape and engaged in a duel with the enemy. Within three bouts, Wen Chou attempted to flee. However, Guan Yu's horse, the <a href="/wiki/Red_Hare" title="Red Hare">Red Hare</a>, was of a superior breed and soon caught up. Guan Yu then slew Wen Chou from behind.</p>
