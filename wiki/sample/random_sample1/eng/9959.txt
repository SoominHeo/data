<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tropical rainforest climate</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>tropical rainforest climate</b>, also known as an <b>equatorial climate</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical climate</a> usually (but not always) found along the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. Regions with this climate typically feature <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforests</a>, and it is designated <i>Af</i> by the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a>. </p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_rainforest_climate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tropical rainforests have a type of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical climate</a> in which there is no <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a> – all months have an average <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> value of at least 60 mm. Tropical rainforests have no summer or winter; it is typically hot and wet throughout the year and rainfall is both heavy and frequent. One day in an equatorial climate can be very similar to the next, while the change in temperature between day and night may be larger than the average change in temperature along the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKnight_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-McKnight-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_rainforest_climate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rainbow_Jungle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2088" data-file-width="7256" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Rainbow_Jungle.jpg/500px-Rainbow_Jungle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Rainbow_Jungle.jpg/750px-Rainbow_Jungle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Rainbow_Jungle.jpg/1000px-Rainbow_Jungle.jpg 2x" width="500"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rainbow_Jungle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Upland rainforest in Borneo. Sabah, Malaysia</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A tropical rainforest is usually found at <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a> within 10 degrees North and South of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, which are dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a>. The climate is most commonly found in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>. However, tropical rainforest <a href="/wiki/Microclimate" title="Microclimate">microclimates</a> are found in many other regions (e.g., far <a href="/wiki/North_Queensland" title="North Queensland">North Queensland</a>), while not everywhere along the equatorial region features a tropical rainforest climate.</p>
<p>Some of the places that have this rainforests are indeed uniformly and monotonously wet throughout the year (e.g., the northwest <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> to <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, see for instance, <a href="/wiki/Andagoya" title="Andagoya">Andagoya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>), but in many cases the period of higher sun and longer days is distinctly driest (as at <a href="/wiki/Palembang" title="Palembang">Palembang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>) or the time of lower sun and shorter days may have more rain (as at <a href="/wiki/Sitiawan" title="Sitiawan">Sitiawan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>).</p>
<p>Additionally, while tropical rainforest climates are typically located near the equator (hence the alternate name “equatorial climate”), there are a number of instances where the climate is found some distance away from the equator. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Santos,_S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="Santos, São Paulo">Santos, Brazil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">USA</a> are not only far removed from the equator, but are actually located just outside the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>. Yet both of these cities feature a tropical rainforest climate, albeit with noticeably cooler and warmer periods of the year.</p>
