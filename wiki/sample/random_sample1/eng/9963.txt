<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Stone row</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>stone row</b> (or <b>stone alignment</b>), is a linear arrangement of upright, parallel <a href="/wiki/Megalith" title="Megalith">megalithic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standing_stone" title="Standing stone">standing stones</a> set at intervals along a common axis or series of axes, usually dating from the later <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Rows may be individual or grouped, and three or more stones aligned can constitute a stone row.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stone_row&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stone rows can be few metres or several kilometres in length and made from stones that can be as tall as 2m, although 1m high stones are more common. The terminals of many rows have the largest stones and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megalithic" title="Megalithic">megalithic</a> features are sometimes sited at the ends, especially burial <a href="/wiki/Cairn" title="Cairn">cairns</a>. The stones are placed at intervals and may vary in height along the sequence, to provide a graduated appearance, though it is not known whether this was done deliberately. Stone rows were erected by the later <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> peoples in the British Isles, parts of <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> and northern France.</p>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alignement_Kerlescan_Carnac.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Alignement_Kerlescan_Carnac.jpg/220px-Alignement_Kerlescan_Carnac.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Alignement_Kerlescan_Carnac.jpg/330px-Alignement_Kerlescan_Carnac.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Alignement_Kerlescan_Carnac.jpg/440px-Alignement_Kerlescan_Carnac.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alignement_Kerlescan_Carnac.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Part of the Kerlescan alignment in Carnac (Brittany, France)</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The most famous example is the <a href="/wiki/Carnac_stones" title="Carnac stones">Carnac stones</a>, a complex of stone rows around <a href="/wiki/Carnac" title="Carnac">Carnac</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>. There are a number of examples on <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a> including the row at <a href="/wiki/Stalldown_Barrow" title="Stalldown Barrow">Stall Down</a> and three rows at <a href="/wiki/Drizzlecombe" title="Drizzlecombe">Drizzlecombe</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hill_O_Many_Stanes" title="Hill O Many Stanes">Hill O Many Stanes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a>. In Britain they are exclusively found in isolated moorland areas. The term alignment is sometimes taken to imply that the rows were placed purposely in relation to other factors such as other monuments or topographical or astronomical features. Archaeologists treat stone rows as discrete features however and alignment refers to the stones being lined up with one another rather than anything else. Their purpose is thought to be religious or ceremonial perhaps marking a processual route. Another theory is that each generation would erect a new stone to contribute to a sequence that demonstrated a people's continual presence.</p>
