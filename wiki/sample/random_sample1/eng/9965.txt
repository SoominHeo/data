<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carnassial</h1>

<p><b>Carnassials</b> are paired upper and lower teeth (either molars or premolars and molars) modified in such a way as to allow enlarged and often self-sharpening edges to pass by each other in a shearing manner. The modification arose separately in several groups of carnivorous mammals. Different pairs of teeth were involved in the separate modifications. In modern <a href="/wiki/Carnivora" title="Carnivora">Carnivora</a>, the carnassials are the modified fourth upper <a href="/wiki/Premolar" title="Premolar">premolar</a> and the first lower <a href="/wiki/Molar_(tooth)" title="Molar (tooth)">molar</a>. These teeth are also referred to as <b>sectorial</b> teeth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osborn1907_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osborn1907-1">[1]</a></sup> Humans lack carnassial teeth.</p>

<p>Carnassial teeth are modified molars (and in the case of carnivorans premolars) which are adapted to allow for the shearing (rather than tearing) of flesh to permit the more efficient consumption of meat. These modifications are not limited to the members of the order Carnivora, but are seen in a number of different mammal groups. Not all carnivorous mammals, however, developed carnassial teeth. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesonychids" title="Mesonychids">Mesonychids</a>, for example, had no carnassial adaptations, and as a result, the blunt, rounded cusps on its molars had a much more difficult time reducing meat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Likewise, neither members of Oxyclaenidae nor <a href="/wiki/Arctocyonidae" title="Arctocyonidae">Arctocyonidae</a> had carnassial teeth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the other hand, carnivorous marsupials have carnassial teeth. Both the living <a href="/wiki/Tasmanian_devil" title="Tasmanian devil">Tasmanian devil</a> (<i>Sarcophilus harrisii</i>) and the recently extinct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tasmanian_wolf" title="Tasmanian wolf">Tasmanian wolf</a> (<i>Thylacinus cynocephalus</i>) possessed modified molars to allow for shearing, although the Tasmanian wolf, the larger of the two, had adaptation more similar to the modern dog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Marsupial_lion" title="Marsupial lion">marsupial lion</a> (<i>Thylacoleo carnifex</i>) had massive carnassial molars. A recent study concludes that these teeth produced the strongest bite of any mammal in history. Moreover, these carnassial molars appear to have been used, unlike in any other known mammal, to inflict the killing blow to the prey by severing the spinal cord, crushing the windpipe or severing a major artery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Like these true marsupials, the closely related <a href="/wiki/Borhyaenidae" title="Borhyaenidae">borhyaenids</a> of South America had three carnassial teeth involving the first three upper molars (M1-M3) and the second through fourth lower molars (m2-m4). In the borhyaenids the upper carnassials appear to have been rotated medially around the anteriorposterior axis of the tooth row in order to maintain tight occlusional contact between the upper and lower shearing teeth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Creodonta" title="Creodonta">Creodonts</a> have two or three pairs of carnassial teeth, but only one pair performed the cutting function: either M1/m2 or M2/m3, depending on the family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Oxyaenidae" title="Oxyaenidae">Oxyaenidae</a>, it is M1 and m2 that form the carnassials. Among the <a href="/wiki/Hyaenodontidae" title="Hyaenodontidae">hyaenodontids</a> it is M2 and m3. Unlike most modern carnivorans, which the carnassials are the sole shearing teeth, in the creodonts other molars have a subordinate shearing functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The fact that the two lineages developed carnassials from different types of teeth has been used as evidence against the validity of Creodonta as a clade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Modern carnivorous bats generally lack true carnassial teeth, but the extinct <i><a href="/wiki/Necromantis" title="Necromantis">Necromantis</a></i> had particularly convergent teeth, in particular M1 and M2, which bore expanded heels and broad stylar shelves. These were particularly suited for crushing over an exclusively slicing action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>Though not superficially similar, the triconodont teeth of some early mammals such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutriconodont" title="Eutriconodont">eutriconodonts</a> are thought to have had a function similar to those of carnassials, sharing a similar shearing function. Eutriconodonts possess several speciations towards animalivory, and the larger forms such as <i><a href="/wiki/Repenomamus" title="Repenomamus">Repenomamus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gobiconodon" title="Gobiconodon">Gobiconodon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Jugulator_(mammal)" title="Jugulator (mammal)">Jugulator</a></i> probably fed on vertebrate prey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Similarly the "tooth lips" of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Clevosaurid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Clevosaurid (page does not exist)">clevosaurid</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphenodontia" title="Sphenodontia">sphenodontians</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Clevosaurus" title="Clevosaurus">Clevosaurus</a></i> are described as "carnassial-like".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>In modern carnivorans the carnassial teeth pairs are found on either side of the jaw and are composed of the fourth upper pre-molar and the first lower molar (P4/m1).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The location these carnassial pairs is determined primarily by the <a href="/wiki/Masseter_muscle" title="Masseter muscle">masseter muscle</a>. In this position, the carnassial teeth benefit from most of the force generated by this <a href="/wiki/Mastication" title="Mastication">mastication</a> muscle, allowing for efficient shearing and cutting of flesh, tendon and muscle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cope1879_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cope1879-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The scissor-like motion is created by the movement between the carnassial pair when the jaw occludes. The inside of the fourth upper pre-molar closely passes by the outer surface of the first lower molar, thus allowing the sharp cusps of the carnassial teeth to slice through meat.</p>
<p>The length and size of the carnassial teeth vary between species, taking into account factors such as:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savage77_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Savage77-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>the size of the carnivorous animal</li>
<li>the extent to which the <a href="/wiki/Diet_(nutrition)" title="Diet (nutrition)">diet</a> is carnivorous</li>
<li>the size of the chunk of meat that can be swallowed.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_of_carnassial_teeth">Evolution of carnassial teeth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnassial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evolution of carnassial teeth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Group_Carnassial_teeth.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="436" data-file-width="1571" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Group_Carnassial_teeth.jpg/450px-Group_Carnassial_teeth.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Group_Carnassial_teeth.jpg/675px-Group_Carnassial_teeth.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Group_Carnassial_teeth.jpg/900px-Group_Carnassial_teeth.jpg 2x" width="450"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Group_Carnassial_teeth.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a> indicates the presence of carnassial teeth 50 million years ago, implying that <a href="/wiki/Carnivora" title="Carnivora">Carnivora</a> family members descend from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_ancestor" title="Common ancestor">common ancestor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The shape and size of sectorial teeth of different carnivorous animals vary depending on diet, illustrated by the comparisons of bear (<i>Ursus</i>) carnassials with those of a leopard (<i>Panthera</i>). Bears, being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omnivores" title="Omnivores">omnivores</a>, have a flattened, more blunt carnassial pair than leopards. This reflects the bear's diet, as the flattened carnassials are useful both in slicing meat and grinding up vegetation, whereas the leopard's sharp carnassial pairs are more adapted for its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypercarnivorous" title="Hypercarnivorous">hypercarnivorous</a> diet. During the <a href="/wiki/Late_Pleistocene" title="Late Pleistocene">Late Pleistocene</a> – early <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> a now extinct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypercarnivorous" title="Hypercarnivorous">hypercarnivorous</a> wolf <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecomorph" title="Ecomorph">ecomorph</a> existed that was similar in size to a large extant <a href="/wiki/Gray_wolf" title="Gray wolf">gray wolf</a> but with a shorter, broader <a href="/wiki/Palate" title="Palate">palate</a> and with large carnassial teeth relative to its overall skull size. This adaptation allowed the <a href="/wiki/Megafaunal_wolf" title="Megafaunal wolf">megafaunal wolf</a> to predate and scavenge on <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene_megafauna" title="Pleistocene megafauna">Pleistocene megafauna</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disease">Disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnassial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wear and cracking of the carnassial teeth in a wild carnivore (e.g. a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolf</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lion</a>) may result in the death of the individual due to starvation.</p>
<p>Carnassial teeth infections are common in domestic dogs, and present as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abscesses" title="Abscesses">abscesses</a>. Extraction of the tooth and <a href="/wiki/Antibiotics" title="Antibiotics">antibiotics</a> are necessary to ensure that no further complications occur.</p>
