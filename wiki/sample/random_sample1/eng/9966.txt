<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hotspot (geology)</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, the places known as <b>hotspots</b> or <b>hot spots</b> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> regions thought to be fed by underlying <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> that is anomalously hot compared with the surrounding mantle. They may be on, near to, or far from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plate_boundary" title="Plate boundary">tectonic plate boundaries</a>. Currently, there are two hypotheses that attempt to explain their origins. One suggests that hotspots are due to <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">mantle plumes</a> that rise as thermal <a href="/wiki/Diapir" title="Diapir">diapirs</a> from the core–mantle boundary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morgan_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan-1">[1]</a></sup> The other hypothesis is that lithospheric extension permits the passive rising of melt from shallow depths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foulger_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foulger-3">[3]</a></sup> This hypothesis considers the term "hotspot" to be a misnomer, asserting that the mantle source beneath them is, in fact, not anomalously hot at all. Well known examples include <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_hotspot" title="Hawaii hotspot">Hawaii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_hotspot" title="Yellowstone hotspot">Yellowstone</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hotspot_(geology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Partial_melting_asthenosphere_EN.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="1000" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Partial_melting_asthenosphere_EN.svg/220px-Partial_melting_asthenosphere_EN.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Partial_melting_asthenosphere_EN.svg/330px-Partial_melting_asthenosphere_EN.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Partial_melting_asthenosphere_EN.svg/440px-Partial_melting_asthenosphere_EN.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Partial_melting_asthenosphere_EN.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Schematic diagram showing the physical processes inside the Earth that lead to the generation of magma. Partial melting begins above the fusion point.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The origins of the concept of hotspots lie in the work of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._Tuzo_Wilson" title="J. Tuzo Wilson">J. Tuzo Wilson</a>, who postulated in 1963 that the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a> result from the slow movement of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plate</a> across a hot region beneath the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It was later postulated that hotspots are fed by narrow streams of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">hot</a> <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> rising from the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Core%E2%80%93mantle_boundary" title="Core–mantle boundary">core–mantle boundary</a> in a structure called a <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">mantle plume</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Whether or not such mantle plumes exist is currently the subject of a major controversy in Earth science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foulger_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Foulger-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Estimates for the number of hotspots postulated to be fed by mantle plumes has ranged from about 20 to several thousands, over the years, with most geologists considering a few tens to exist. <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_hotspot" title="Hawaii hotspot">Hawaii</a>, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union_hotspot" title="Réunion hotspot">Réunion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_hotspot" title="Yellowstone hotspot">Yellowstone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_hotspot" title="Galápagos hotspot">Galápagos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iceland_hotspot" title="Iceland hotspot">Iceland</a> are some of the currently most active volcanic regions to which the hypothesis is applied.</p>
<p>Most hotspot volcanoes are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basaltic" title="Basaltic">basaltic</a> (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hawaiian_hotspot" title="Hawaiian hotspot">Hawaii</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tahiti_hotspot" title="Tahiti hotspot">Tahiti</a>). As a result, they are less explosive than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subduction_zone" title="Subduction zone">subduction zone</a> volcanoes, in which water is trapped under the overriding plate. Where hotspots occur in <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental regions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> rises through the continental crust, which melts to form <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">rhyolites</a>. These <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">rhyolites</a> can form violent eruptions. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_Caldera" title="Yellowstone Caldera">Yellowstone Caldera</a> was formed by some of the most powerful volcanic explosions in geologic history. However, when the rhyolite is completely erupted, it may be followed by eruptions of basaltic magma rising through the same lithospheric fissures (cracks in the lithosphere). An example of this activity is the <a href="/wiki/Ilgachuz_Range" title="Ilgachuz Range">Ilgachuz Range</a> in British Columbia, which was created by an early complex series of <a href="/wiki/Trachyte" title="Trachyte">trachyte</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">rhyolite</a> eruptions, and late extrusion of a sequence of basaltic lava flows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>The hotspot hypothesis is now closely linked to the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">mantle plume</a> hypothesis.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_island_arc_volcanoes">Comparison with island arc volcanoes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hotspot_(geology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Comparison with island arc volcanoes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hotspot volcanoes are considered to have a fundamentally different origin from <a href="/wiki/Island_arc" title="Island arc">island arc</a> volcanoes. The latter form over <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> zones, at converging plate boundaries. When one oceanic plate meets another, the denser plate is forced downward into a deep ocean trench. This plate, as it is subducted, releases water into the base of the over-riding plate, and this water mixes with the rock, thus changing its composition causing some rock to melt and rise. It is this that fuels a chain of volcanoes, such as the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_Islands" title="Aleutian Islands">Aleutian Islands</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.</p>

<p>The joint <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">mantle plume</a>/hotspot hypothesis envisages the feeder structures to be fixed relative to one another, with the continents and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seafloor" title="Seafloor">seafloor</a> drifting overhead. The hypothesis thus predicts that time-progressive chains of volcanoes are developed on the surface. Examples are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellowstone" title="Yellowstone">Yellowstone</a>, which lies at the end of a chain of extinct calderas, which become progressively older to the west. Another example is the Hawaiian archipelago, where islands become progressively older and more deeply eroded to the northwest.</p>
<p>Geologists have tried to use hotspot volcanic chains to track the movement of the Earth's tectonic plates. This effort has been vexed by the lack of very long chains, by the fact that many are not time-progressive (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">Galápagos</a>) and by the fact that hotspots do not appear to be fixed relative to one another (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>)</p>
