<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Soil salinity</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Soil salinity</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a> content in the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>; the process of increasing the salt content is known as <b>salinization</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Salts occur naturally within soils and water. Salination can be caused by natural processes such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mineral_weathering" title="Mineral weathering">mineral weathering</a> or by the gradual withdrawal of an ocean. It can also come about through artificial processes such as <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrence">Natural occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soil_salinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Natural occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">Salts</a> are a natural component in soils and water. The ions responsible for salination are: Na<sup>+</sup>, K<sup>+</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup>, Mg<sup>2+</sup> and Cl<sup>−</sup>.<br/>
As the Na<sup>+</sup> (sodium) predominates, soils can become <b>sodic</b>. <a href="/wiki/Sodic_soil" title="Sodic soil">Sodic soils</a> present particular challenges because they tend to have very poor structure which limits or prevents <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_infiltration" title="Water infiltration">water infiltration</a> and drainage.</p>
<p>Over long periods of time, as soil minerals <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weather</a> and release salts, these salts are flushed or leached out of the soil by drainage water in areas with sufficient precipitation. In addition to mineral weathering, salts are also deposited via dust and precipitation. In dry regions salts may accumulate, leading to naturally saline soils. This is the case, for example, in large parts of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. Human practices can increase the salinity of soils by the addition of salts in irrigation water. Proper irrigation management can prevent salt accumulation by providing adequate drainage water to leach added salts from the soil. Disrupting drainage patterns that provide leaching can also result in salt accumulations. An example of this occurred in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> in 1970 when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aswan_dam" title="Aswan dam">Aswan High Dam</a> was built. The change in the level of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_water" title="Ground water">ground water</a> before the construction had enabled <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a>, which led to high concentration of salts in the water table. After the construction, the continuous high level of the water table led to the salination of the <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dry_land_salinity">Dry land salinity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soil_salinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dry land salinity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dryland_salinity" title="Dryland salinity">Salinity in drylands</a> can occur when the water table is between two and three metres from the surface of the soil. The salts from the groundwater are raised by capillary action to the surface of the soil. This occurs when groundwater is saline (which is true in many areas), and is favored by land use practices allowing more rainwater to enter the aquifer than it could accommodate. For example, the clearing of trees for agriculture is a major reason for dryland salinity in some areas, since deep rooting of trees has been replaced by shallow rooting of annual crops.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Salinity_due_to_irrigation">Salinity due to irrigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soil_salinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Salinity due to irrigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Salinity_from_irrigation.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="276" data-file-width="878" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Salinity_from_irrigation.png/400px-Salinity_from_irrigation.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Salinity_from_irrigation.png/600px-Salinity_from_irrigation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Salinity_from_irrigation.png/800px-Salinity_from_irrigation.png 2x" width="400"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Salinity_from_irrigation.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Rain or irrigation, in the absence of leaching, can bring salts to the surface by capillary action</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impacts_of_irrigation" title="Environmental impacts of irrigation">Environmental impacts of irrigation</a></div>
<p>Salinity from <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> can occur over time wherever irrigation occurs, since almost all water (even natural rainfall) contains some dissolved salts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> When the plants use the water, the salts are left behind in the soil and eventually begin to accumulate. Since soil salinity makes it more difficult for plants to absorb soil moisture, these salts must be leached out of the plant root zone by applying additional water. This water in excess of plant needs is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaching_model" title="Leaching model">leaching fraction</a>. Salination from irrigation water is also greatly increased by poor <a href="/wiki/Drainage" title="Drainage">drainage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biosalinity" title="Biosalinity">use of saline water</a> for irrigating agricultural crops.</p>
<p>Salinity in urban areas often results from the combination of irrigation and groundwater processes. Irrigation is also now common in cities (gardens and recreation areas).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences_of_salinity">Consequences of salinity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soil_salinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Consequences of salinity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The consequences of salinity are</p>
<ul>
<li>detrimental effects on plant growth and yield</li>
<li>damage to infrastructure (roads, bricks, corrosion of pipes and cables)</li>
<li>reduction of water quality for users, sedimentation problems</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a> ultimately, when crops are too strongly affected by the amounts of salts.</li>
</ul>
<p>Salinity is an important <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a> problem. Soil salinity can be reduced by <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(agriculture)" title="Leaching (agriculture)">leaching</a> soluble salts out of soil with excess irrigation water. <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity_control" title="Soil salinity control">Soil salinity control</a> involves <a href="/wiki/Watertable_control" title="Watertable control">watertable control</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaching_model" title="Leaching model">flushing</a> in combination with <a href="/wiki/Tile_drainage" title="Tile drainage">tile drainage</a> or another form of <a href="/wiki/Drainage_system_(agriculture)" title="Drainage system (agriculture)">subsurface drainage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waterlog_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Waterlog-4">[4]</a></sup> A comprehensive treatment of soil salinity is available from the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>High levels of soil salinity can be tolerated if salt-tolerant plants are grown. Sensitive crops lose their vigor already in slightly saline soils, most crops are negatively affected by (moderately) saline soils, and only salinity resistant crops thrive in severely saline soils. The University of Wyoming <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alan_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alan-6">[6]</a></sup> and the Government of Alberta <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albert_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Albert-7">[7]</a></sup> report data on the salt tolerance of plants.</p>
