<p><b>Ran Min</b> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">冉闵</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">冉閔</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Rǎn Mǐn</span></i>; died 352), also known as <b>Shi Min</b> (石閔), <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_name" title="Posthumous name">posthumously</a> honored by <a href="/wiki/Former_Yan" title="Former Yan">Former Yan</a> as <b><a href="/wiki/Tian_Wang" title="Tian Wang">Heavenly Prince</a> Daowu of (Ran) Wei</b> ((冉)魏悼武天王), <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_name" title="Courtesy name">courtesy name</a> <b>Yongzeng</b> (永曾), nickname <b>Jinu</b> (棘奴), was a <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> leader during the era of <a href="/wiki/Sixteen_Kingdoms" title="Sixteen Kingdoms">Sixteen Kingdoms</a> in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and the only emperor of the short-lived state <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ran_Wei" title="Ran Wei">Ran Wei</a></b> (冉魏). Ran (冉) is an uncommon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_family_name" title="Chinese family name">Chinese family name</a>. He was known for committing the <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jie_(ethnic_group)" title="Jie (ethnic group)">Jie</a> people under Later Zhao.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_background">Family background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ran_Min&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ran Min's father Ran Liang (冉良), who later changed his name to Ran Zhan (冉瞻), and from Wei Commandery (魏郡, roughly modern <a href="/wiki/Anyang" title="Anyang">Anyang</a>, Northern <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">Henan</a>) and was a descendant of an aristocratic family, but one who must have, in the serious famines circa 310, joined a group of refugees led by Chen Wu (陳午). When Later Zhao's founder <a href="/wiki/Shi_Le" title="Shi Le">Shi Le</a> defeated Chen in 311, he captured the 11-year-old Ran Zhan as well, and for reasons unknown, he had his nephew <a href="/wiki/Shi_Hu" title="Shi Hu">Shi Hu</a> adopt Ran Zhan as his son and change his name accordingly to Shi Zhan. Ran Min's mother was named Wang (王). It is not known when he was born, but he would have been known as Shi Min.</p>
<p>A Shi Zhan was mentioned to have died in battle when Shi Hu was defeated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Zhao" title="Han Zhao">Han Zhao</a>'s emperor <a href="/wiki/Liu_Yao" title="Liu Yao">Liu Yao</a> in 328, but it is not clear whether this Shi Zhan was Shi Min's father.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="During_Shi_Hu.27s_reign">During Shi Hu's reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ran_Min&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: During Shi Hu's reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As Shi Min grew in age, Shi Hu became impressed by his bravery in battle and battlefield tactics, and he treated Shi Min as his own son. The first mention in history of him as a general was in 338, when Shi Hu unsuccessfully tried to destroy the rival state <a href="/wiki/Former_Yan" title="Former Yan">Former Yan</a> but saw his army collapse after sieging the Former Yan capital Jicheng (棘城, in modern <a href="/wiki/Jinzhou" title="Jinzhou">Jinzhou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liaoning" title="Liaoning">Liaoning</a>) for about 20 days but failing to capture it. The only army group that remained intact was the one commanded by Shi Min.</p>
<p>During the remainder of Shi Hu's reign, Shi Min was often referred to as a general he turned out to be. For example, in 339, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jin_Dynasty_(265-420)" title="Jin Dynasty (265-420)">Jin</a> general <a href="/wiki/Yu_Liang" title="Yu Liang">Yu Liang</a> considered launching a major campaign against Later Zhao, Shi Hu chose to react, and he had his general Kui An (夔安) command five generals, one of whom was Shi Min, to attack Jin's northern regions. (Shi Min's later ally Li Nong (李農) was one of the other generals, while Shi Hu's son <a href="/wiki/Shi_Jian" title="Shi Jian">Shi Jian</a> the Prince of Yiyang was another.) Shi Min was successful in his task, and the five generals together inflicted heavy damages, thwarting Yu's plans. For his accomplishments, Shi Min was created the Duke of Wuxing.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="During_the_confusion_after_Shi_Hu.27s_death">During the confusion after Shi Hu's death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ran_Min&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: During the confusion after Shi Hu's death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After Shi Hu's death in 349, his youngest son and <a href="/wiki/Crown_prince" title="Crown prince">crown prince</a> <a href="/wiki/Shi_Shi" title="Shi Shi">Shi Shi</a> became emperor, but the government was controlled by Shi Shi's mother <a href="/wiki/Empress_Liu_(Shi_Hu%27s_wife)" title="Empress Liu (Shi Hu's wife)">Empress Dowager Liu</a> and the official Zhang Chai (張豺). Shi Shi's older brother <a href="/wiki/Shi_Zun" title="Shi Zun">Shi Zun</a>, the Prince of Pengcheng, was unhappy about the situation, and a number of generals who were unimpressed with Empress Dowager Liu and Zhang, including Shi Min, suggested that he march to the capital <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ye,_China" title="Ye, China">Yecheng</a> and overthrow them. Shi Zun did so — and also promised to create Shi Min crown prince if they were victorious. In summer 349, Shi Zun defeated Shi Shi's forces and deposed and killed him, along with Empress Dowager Liu and Zhang Chai. Shi Zun claimed the imperial title. However, he did not appoint Shi Min crown prince as promised, but rather appointed another nephew Shi Yan (石衍) crown prince. Further, while he gave Shi Min important posts, he did not allow him to have control of the government, as Shi Min wished. Shi Min became disgruntled.</p>
<p>In winter 349, in fear of Shi Min, Shi Zun summoned a meeting of the princes before his mother, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress_Zheng_Yingtao" title="Empress Zheng Yingtao">Empress Dowager Zheng</a>, announcing that he would execute Shi Min. Empress Dowager Zheng opposed, reasoning that Shi Min's contributions during the coup against Shi Shi had to be remembered. Shi Zun hesitated, and meanwhile, Shi Jian, one of the princes attending the meeting, quickly reported the news to Shi Min, who acted quickly and surrounded the palace, capturing and executing Shi Zun, Empress Dowager Zheng, Shi Zun's wife <a href="/wiki/Empress_Zhang_(Later_Zhao)" title="Empress Zhang (Later Zhao)">Empress Zhang</a>, Shi Yan, and several key officials loyal to Shi Zun. He made Shi Jian emperor, but he and Li Nong seized the control of the government.</p>
<p>Shi Jian could not endure Shi Min's hold on power, and he sent his brother Shi Bao, the Prince of Leping, and the generals Li Song (李松) and Zhang Cai (張才) against Shi Min, but after they were defeated Shi Jian pretended as if they had acted independently and executed them all. Another brother of his, <a href="/wiki/Shi_Zhi" title="Shi Zhi">Shi Zhi</a> the Prince of Xinxing, then rose in the old capital Xiangguo (襄國, in modern <a href="/wiki/Xintai" title="Xintai">Xintai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hebei" title="Hebei">Hebei</a>), in alliance with the <a href="/wiki/Qiang_people" title="Qiang people">Qiang</a> chieftain <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yao_Yizhong&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yao Yizhong (page does not exist)">Yao Yizhong</a> (姚弋仲) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Di_(Wu_Hu)" title="Di (Wu Hu)">Di</a> chieftain <a href="/wiki/Fu_Hong" title="Fu Hong">Pu Hong</a> (蒲洪) against Shi Min and Li Nong. Shi Jian then tried to have the general Sun Fudu (孫伏都), a fellow ethnic Jie, attack Shi Min, but Shi Min quickly defeated him, and Shi Jian, trying to absolve himself, then ordered Shi Min to execute Sun. Shi Min, however, began to realize that Shi Jian was behind Sun's attack, and he decided that he needed to disarm the Jie people, who knew that he was not a Jie but ethnically a Han. He ordered that all non-Han not be allowed to carry arms, and most non-Hans fled Yecheng after that. Shi Min put Shi Jian under house arrest with no outside communication.</p>
<p>As the non-Han continued fleeing Yecheng, Shi Min realized that he would not be able to use the Hu (胡 <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a>), so he issued an order to the Hans according to which each civil servant who killed one Hu (胡) and brought his head to him would be promoted in rank by three degrees, and a military officer would be transferred to the service at his Supreme Command. Shi Min himself led Hans in killing the Hu (胡) and <a href="/wiki/Jie_people" title="Jie people">Jie</a> (羯) people without regard for sex or age; during the day tens of thousands of heads were severed. In total over 200 thousand people were killed; their bodies were dumped outside the city. Troop commanders in various parts of the state received a prescript from Shi Min to kill the Hus (胡); as a result half of the people with high noses and bushy beards were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Among the 200,000 people who died in the massacre were some ethnic Hans who had high noses or thick beards, both considered to be the indicators of non-Hanness.</p>
<p>In 350 Shi Jian, still the nominal head of the state, changed the name of his state from Zhao to Wei (衛) and the imperial clan name from Shi to Li (李), under pressure from Shi Min. Many key officials fled to Shi Zhi. Local generals throughout the empire effectively became independent, waiting for the conflict to be resolved. As Shi Min's troops were busy against Shi Zhi's, Shi Jian made one final attempt against Shi Min, ordering general Zhang Shen (張沈) to attack the capital after Shi Min had left it. However, Shi Jian's <a href="/wiki/Eunuch" title="Eunuch">eunuchs</a> reported that to Shi Min and Li Nong, and they quickly returned to Yecheng and executed Shi Jian, also killing 38 of Shi Hu's grandsons and the rest of the Shi clan. Shi Min, restoring his father's original family name of Ran (冉), then took the throne as the emperor of a new state, Wei (魏, note different character from the state name declared previously).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_emperor_of_Ran_Wei">As emperor of Ran Wei</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ran_Min&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: As emperor of Ran Wei">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wei-Jie_war" title="Wei-Jie war">Wei-Jie war</a></div>
<p>Ran Min honored his mother Lady Wang with a title of <a href="/wiki/Empress_dowager" title="Empress dowager">empress dowager</a>. He appointed his wife <a href="/wiki/Empress_Dong_(Ran_Min%27s_wife)" title="Empress Dong (Ran Min's wife)">Lady Dong</a> an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empress" title="Empress">empress</a>, and his oldest son <a href="/wiki/Ran_Zhi" title="Ran Zhi">Ran Zhi</a> a crown prince. His other sons and his ally Li Nong were made princes, Li Nong sons were given titles of dukes. He proclaimed a general amnesty, hoping to have the generals who became independent abide by his edicts, but few of them accepted, though most Han generals outwardly did not defy him either. For unknown reasons, he soon killed Li. He sent a letter to <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Mu_of_Jin" title="Emperor Mu of Jin">Emperor Mu of Jin</a>'s court with a mixed message, appearing to invite Jin to send forces north and agreeing to submit, but the letter could also be read as a defiant challenge. Jin did not react, although it began to also seek allegiance of the generals in the former territory of Later Zhao southern provinces.</p>
<p>Ran Min's brief reign was characterized by rash decisions and massive executions. He would often react violently to advisors who suggested ideas different from his own, including killing them, and then regret those violent reactions after he realized that he was wrong.</p>
<p>In spring 351, Ran Min set a siege of the Shi Zhi's capital Xiangguo. Shi Zhi sought aid from Former Yan's prince <a href="/wiki/Murong_Jun" title="Murong Jun">Murong Jun</a> and was able to deal Ran a major defeat. At this time, the Xiongnu soldiers in Yecheng rebelled, captured his son Ran Yin, and surrendered to Shi Zhi, who executed Ran Yin. Ran Min was thought to be dead, but when he appeared in Yecheng, the city was calmed. Shi Zhi had his general Liu Xian (劉顯) siege Yecheng, but Ran Min defeated Liu in battle and awed him so much that Liu agreed that once he returned to Xiangguo, he would kill Shi Zhi and surrender. He did so and sent Shi Zhi's head to Ran Min, and Ran Min had Shi Zhi's head be burned on a busy street in Yecheng. Later Zhao was at its final end.</p>
<p>However, wars continued. Liu Xian, after briefly submitting to Ran Min, proclaimed himself emperor. The western provinces were taken over by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fu_Ji%C3%A0n" title="Fu Jiàn">Fu Jiàn</a>, who established <a href="/wiki/Former_Qin" title="Former Qin">Former Qin</a>. The southern provinces largely switched their allegiance to Jin. Meanwhile, Former Yan, which had already captured <a href="/wiki/Youzhou" title="Youzhou">Youzhou</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tianjin" title="Tianjin">Tianjin</a>, and northern <a href="/wiki/Hebei" title="Hebei">Hebei</a>) and moved its capital to <a href="/wiki/Jicheng_(Beijing)" title="Jicheng (Beijing)">Jicheng</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>), continued to advance south. Ran Min, who captured Xiangguo in early 352 and executed Liu Xian, decided to head north to face the Former Yan army, against advice of several officials who felt that his army needed a rest. The Former Yan general <a href="/wiki/Murong_Ke" title="Murong Ke">Murong Ke</a>, Murong Jun's brother, pretended to lose several skirmishes and then retreat, tricking Ran Min and his infantry into the open field, and then used his cavalry to surround Ran Min's, inflicting great losses. Ran Min's horse suddenly died, and he fell off and was captured. Former Yan forces delivered him to Murong Jun, and he insulted Murong Jun. Murong Jun had him whipped 300 times and then executed, although was soon fearful that his spirit was causing a drought, and therefore honored him with the <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_name" title="Posthumous name">posthumous name</a> Daowu. Ran Min's wife Empress Dong and her son Ran Zhi would hold out for several more months, but eventually surrendered later that year, ending Ran Wei's brief existence.</p>
<p>Ran Min is now mostly known for his order to execute all of the <a href="/wiki/Five_Barbarians" title="Five Barbarians">Wu Hu</a>, particularly the Jie. Then he fought with Hu armies in <a href="/wiki/Ji_Province" title="Ji Province">Jizhou</a>, lead to several millions of migrants of different races to flee, and in the way they attacked each other and only 2-3/10 people was able to go back to their origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The North however soon again fell under control of the Xianbei.</p>
