<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Goat meat</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Goat meat</b> or <b>goat's meat</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_goat" title="Domestic goat">domestic goat</a> <i>(Capra aegagrus hircus)</i>.</p>
<p>In France, goat meat from adults is often called <i>chevon</i> and <i>cabrito</i>, <i>capretto</i>, or <i>kid</i> when it is from young animals. While "goat" is usually the name for the meat found in common parlance, producers and marketers may prefer to use the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French-derived</a> word chevon (from <i>ch√®vre</i>), since <a href="/wiki/Market_research" title="Market research">market research</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> suggests that "chevon" is more palatable to consumers than "goat meat".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Cabrito, a word of <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> and Portuguese origin, refers specifically to young, milk-fed goat. In some parts of Asia, particularly <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>; the word "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutton" title="Mutton">mutton</a>" is sometimes used to describe both goat and sheep meat, despite its more specific meaning (limited to the meat of adult sheep) in the UK, US, Australia and other <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">English-speaking countries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HT_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-HT-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_cuisine">In cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goat_meat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_goat_dishes" title="List of goat dishes">List of goat dishes</a></div>
<p>Goat is a staple in Africa, Asia and South/Central America, and a delicacy in a few <a href="/wiki/European_cuisine" title="European cuisine">European cuisines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-love_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-love-4">[4]</a></sup> The cuisines best known for their use of goat include <a href="/wiki/African_cuisine" title="African cuisine">African cuisine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_Eastern_cuisine" title="Middle Eastern cuisine">Middle Eastern</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North African</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_cuisine" title="Indian cuisine">Indian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_cuisine" title="Indonesian cuisine">Indonesian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepalese_cuisine" title="Nepalese cuisine">Nepali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistani_cuisine" title="Pakistani cuisine">Pakistani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexican_cuisine" title="Mexican cuisine">Mexican</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_cuisine" title="Caribbean cuisine">Caribbean</a> (<a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfgate_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-sfgate-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cabrito" title="Cabrito">Cabrito</a>, or baby goat, is the typical food of <a href="/wiki/Monterrey" title="Monterrey">Monterrey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuevo_Le%C3%B3n" title="Nuevo Le√≥n">Nuevo Le√≥n</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in Italy it is called capretto.</p>
<p>Goat has historically been less commonplace in American, Canadian and Northern European cuisines but has become more popular in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niche_markets" title="Niche markets">niche markets</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-niman_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-niman-7">[7]</a></sup> including those that serve immigrants from Asia and Africa who prefer goat to other meat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap20140418_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap20140418-8">[8]</a></sup> As of 2011<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Goat_meat&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the number of goats slaughtered in the United States has doubled every 10 years for three decades, rising to nearly one million annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wp20110405_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-wp20110405-9">[9]</a></sup> While in the past goat meat in the West was confined to ethnic markets, it can now be found in a few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luxury_good" title="Luxury good">upscale</a> restaurants and purveyors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-love_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-love-4">[4]</a></sup> especially in cities such as <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfgate_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-sfgate-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brady,_Texas" title="Brady, Texas">Brady, Texas</a> has held its Annual World Championship BBQ Goat Cook-Off annually since 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Goat meat is savory and less sweet than beef<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wp20110405_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-wp20110405-9">[9]</a></sup> but slightly sweeter than lamb. It can be prepared in a variety of ways, such as being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stewed" title="Stewed">stewed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Curry" title="Curry">curried</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baked" title="Baked">baked</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grilling" title="Grilling">grilled</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbecue" title="Barbecue">barbecued</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minced" title="Minced">minced</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canning" title="Canning">canned</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frying" title="Frying">fried</a>, or made into <a href="/wiki/Sausage" title="Sausage">sausage</a>. Goat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jerky_(food)" title="Jerky (food)">jerky</a> is also another popular variety. In Okinawa (Japan), goat meat is served raw in thin slices as <i>yagisashi</i>. On the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, the rice dish mutton <i><a href="/wiki/Biryani" title="Biryani">biryani</a></i> uses goat meat as a primary ingredient to produce a rich taste. "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curry_Goat" title="Curry Goat">Curry goat</a>" is a common traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-Caribbean" title="Indo-Caribbean">Indo-Caribbean</a> dish. In <a href="/wiki/West_Bengal" title="West Bengal">West Bengal</a>, traditional meat dishes like kosha mangsho and rezala are prepared using meat from a "Khashi", a castrated goat with a meat that has richer taste and a milder, less gamey flavour. In <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_cuisine" title="Indonesian cuisine">Indonesia</a>, goat meat is popularly skewered and grilled as <i><a href="/wiki/Sate_kambing" title="Sate kambing">sate kambing</a></i>, or curried in soups such as <i><a href="/wiki/Sup_kambing" title="Sup kambing">sup kambing</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Gulai" title="Gulai">gulai kambing</a></i>.</p>
<p>Goat meat is also a major delicacy in <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, and both castrated <i>(Khashi-ko-masu)</i> and uncastrated <i>(Boka-ko-masu)</i> goats are sacrificed during <a href="/wiki/Dashain" title="Dashain">Dashain</a>, the largest annual celebrations in the country, as well as on other festive occasions. There are many separate dishes, which together include all edible parts of the animal. <i>Bhutun</i> is made from the gut, <i>Rakhti</i> from the blood, <i>Karji-marji</i> from the liver and lungs, and the feet ‚Äî <i>Khutti</i> ‚Äî are often made into soup. <a href="/wiki/Sukuti" title="Sukuti">Sukuti</a> is a kind of <a href="/wiki/Jerky" title="Jerky">jerky</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Sekuwa" title="Sekuwa">Sekuwa</a> is made from roasted meat and often eaten with alcoholic beverages. In addition to these dishes, goat meat is often eaten as part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Momo_(food)" title="Momo (food)">momos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thukpa" title="Thukpa">thukpa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chow_mein" title="Chow mein">chow mein</a> and other dishes in various parts of the country. Taas is another popular fried goat meat dish in Nepal, particularly popular in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chitwan" title="Chitwan">Chitwan</a> district of Nepal.</p>
<p>Cabrito, a specialty especially common in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> cuisines such as Mexican, <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_cuisine" title="Peruvian cuisine">Peruvian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_cuisine" title="Brazilian cuisine">Brazilian</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argentinian_cuisine" title="Argentinian cuisine">Argentine</a>, is usually slow roasted. <a href="/wiki/Italian_cuisine" title="Italian cuisine">Southern Italian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_cuisine" title="Greek cuisine">Greek</a> and Portuguese cuisines are also known for serving roast goat in celebration of <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>, with the North of Portugal serving it as well on Christmas day;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfgate_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-sfgate-5">[5]</a></sup> goat dishes are also an Easter staple in the alpine regions of central Europe, often braised (<a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>) or breaded and fried (<a href="/wiki/Tyrol_(state)" title="Tyrol (state)">Tyrol</a>).</p>
<p>In Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Chaga_people" title="Chaga people">Chaga people</a> of Tanzania, a ceremonial goat (locally called Ndafu) would be gutted and roasted as whole as part of tradition that spans hundreds of years. The ceremonial goat is the preferred replacement to the <a href="/wiki/Wedding_cake" title="Wedding cake">Wedding cake</a> used in many weddings around the world.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goat_meat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tajine_with_goat_meat_and_quinces.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1826" data-file-width="2820" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Tajine_with_goat_meat_and_quinces.jpg/220px-Tajine_with_goat_meat_and_quinces.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Tajine_with_goat_meat_and_quinces.jpg/330px-Tajine_with_goat_meat_and_quinces.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Tajine_with_goat_meat_and_quinces.jpg/440px-Tajine_with_goat_meat_and_quinces.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Goat has a reputation for strong, gamey flavour, but can be mild depending on how it is raised and prepared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-love_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-love-4">[4]</a></sup> Caribbean cultures often prefer meat from mature goats, which tend to be more pungent; the best meat comes from younger goats that are six to nine months old. Ribs, loins, and tenderloin goat meat are suitable for quick cooking, while other parts are best for long braising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wp20110405_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-wp20110405-9">[9]</a></sup> Despite being classified as red meat, goat is leaner and contains less <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> than both <a href="/wiki/Lamb_and_mutton" title="Lamb and mutton">lamb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beef" title="Beef">beef</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and less energy than beef or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicken_(food)" title="Chicken (food)">chicken</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wp20110405_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-wp20110405-9">[9]</a></sup> therefore, it requires low-heat, slow cooking to preserve tenderness and moisture.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goat_meat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Goats consume less <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a> than <a href="/wiki/Beef_cattle" title="Beef cattle">beef cattle</a>. An acre of <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pasture</a> can sustain 10 goats or more, compared to two <a href="/wiki/Cattle#Terminology" title="Cattle">steers</a>. A goat may produce 40 pounds of meat, however, which is much less than from cattle or pigs, often making goats unsuitable for modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meat_processor" title="Meat processor">meat processors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wp20110405_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-wp20110405-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Goat meat production is growing rapidly but is still less than 4% of total meat production (less than 13.6 -- ovine including sheep‚Äîout of 302 million tonnes in 2012).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (December 2013)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
