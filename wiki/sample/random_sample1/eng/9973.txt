<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Chlorate</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>The <b>chlorate anion</b> has the formula <span class="chemf nowrap">ClO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
3</span></span>. In this case, the <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> atom is in the +5 <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a>. "Chlorate" can also refer to <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> containing this anion; <a href="/wiki/Chlorate#Compounds_.28salts.29" title="Chlorate">chlorates</a> are the <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chloric_acid" title="Chloric acid">chloric acid</a>. "Chlorate", when followed by a Roman numeral in parentheses, e.g. chlorate(VII), refers to a particular <a href="/wiki/Chlorate#Other_oxyanions" title="Chlorate">oxyanion of chlorine</a>.</p>
<p>As predicted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VSEPR" title="VSEPR">VSEPR</a>, chlorate anions have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonal_pyramid_(chemistry)" title="Trigonal pyramid (chemistry)">trigonal pyramidal structures</a>.</p>
<p>Chlorates are powerful <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizers</a> and should be kept away from organics or easily oxidized materials. Mixtures of chlorate salts with virtually any combustible material (sugar, sawdust, charcoal, organic solvents, metals, etc.) will readily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deflagrate" title="Deflagrate">deflagrate</a>. Chlorates were once widely used in <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnics" title="Pyrotechnics">pyrotechnics</a> for this reason, though their use has fallen due to their instability. Most pyrotechnic applications that formerly used chlorates now use the more stable <a href="/wiki/Perchlorate" title="Perchlorate">perchlorates</a> instead.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_bonding">Structure and bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The chlorate ion cannot be satisfactorily represented by just one <a href="/wiki/Lewis_structure" title="Lewis structure">Lewis structure</a>, since all the Cl–O bonds are the same length (1.49 Å in <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chlorate" title="Potassium chlorate">potassium chlorate</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>), and the chlorine atom is <a href="/wiki/Hypervalent_molecule" title="Hypervalent molecule">hypervalent</a>. Instead, it is often thought of as a hybrid of multiple <a href="/wiki/Resonance_(chemistry)" title="Resonance (chemistry)">resonance structures</a>:</p>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chlorate-resonance-2D.png" title="Resonance structures of the chlorate ion"><img alt="Resonance structures of the chlorate ion" data-file-height="412" data-file-width="2150" height="77" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Chlorate-resonance-2D.png/400px-Chlorate-resonance-2D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Chlorate-resonance-2D.png/600px-Chlorate-resonance-2D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Chlorate-resonance-2D.png/800px-Chlorate-resonance-2D.png 2x" width="400"/></a></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory">Laboratory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Laboratory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Metal chlorates can be prepared by adding <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> to hot metal <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxides</a> like <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">KOH</a>:</p>
<dl>
<dd>3 Cl<sub>2</sub> + 6 KOH → 5 KCl + KClO<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
</dl>
<p>In this reaction, chlorine undergoes <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionation</a>, both reduction and oxidation. Chlorine, oxidation number 0, forms chloride Cl<sup>−</sup> (oxidation number −1) and chlorate(V) <span class="chemf nowrap">ClO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">−<br/>
3</span></span> (oxidation number +5). The reaction of cold aqueous metal hydroxides with chlorine produces the chloride and <a href="/wiki/Hypochlorite" title="Hypochlorite">hypochlorite</a> (oxidation number +1) instead.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial">Industrial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Industrial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The industrial scale synthesis for <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chlorate" title="Sodium chlorate">sodium chlorate</a> starts from aqueous <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> solution (brine) rather than chlorine gas. If the equipment for electrolysis allows for the mixing of the <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>, then the disproportionation reaction described above occurs. The heating of the reactants to 50–70 °C is performed by the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electrical power</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrence">Natural occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Natural occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A recent study has discovered the presence of natural chlorate deposits around the world, with relatively high concentrations found in arid and hyper-arid regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The chlorate was also measured in rainfall samples with the amount of chlorate similar to <a href="/wiki/Perchlorate" title="Perchlorate">perchlorate</a>. It is suspected that chlorate and perchlorate may share a common natural formation mechanism and could be a part of the chlorine biogeochemistry cycle. From a microbial standpoint, the presence of natural chlorate could also explain why there is a variety of microorganisms capable of reducing chlorate to chloride. Further, the evolution of chlorate reduction may be an ancient phenomenon as all perchlorate reducing bacteria described to date also utilize chlorate as a terminal electron acceptor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds_.28salts.29">Compounds (salts)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Compounds (salts)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Chlorates" title="Category:Chlorates">Category:Chlorates</a></div>
<p>Examples of chlorates include</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Potassium_chlorate" title="Potassium chlorate">potassium chlorate</a>, KClO<sub>3</sub></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sodium_chlorate" title="Sodium chlorate">sodium chlorate</a>, NaClO<sub>3</sub></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnesium_chlorate" title="Magnesium chlorate">magnesium chlorate</a>, Mg(ClO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub></li>
</ul>

<p>Using this convention, "chlorate" means <i>any</i> chlorine oxyanion. Commonly, "chlorate" refers only to chlorine in the +5 oxidation state.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chlorate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chlorates are relatively toxic, though they form generally harmless chlorides on reduction.</p>
