<p><b>Republicanism in the United Kingdom</b> is a movement that seeks to replace that country's <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">monarchy</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republic</a>. For those who want a head of state, the method by which one should be chosen is not agreed upon, with some favouring an elected president, some an appointed head of state with little power. Others support something akin to the Swiss model, without a head of state at all.</p>
<p>A republican government was briefly established in the mid-17th century, after the Parliamentarian victory in the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>.</p>
<p>The main lobby group that campaigns for the abolition of the monarchy is <a href="/wiki/Republic_(political_organisation)" title="Republic (political organisation)">Republic</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Context">Context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Within Britain, republican sentiment has largely focused on the abolition of the British monarch, rather than the dissolution of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_unionism" title="British unionism">British Union</a> or independence for its <a href="/wiki/Countries_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Countries of the United Kingdom">constituent countries</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, the term "republican" is usually used in the sense of <a href="/wiki/Irish_republicanism" title="Irish republicanism">Irish republicanism</a>. While also against monarchical forms of government, Irish republicans are against the presence of the British state in <i>any</i> form in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and advocate re-creating a <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">united</a>, all-island state, comprising the whole of Ireland. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unionism_(Ireland)" title="Unionism (Ireland)">Unionists</a> who support a British republic <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Northern_Ireland#Voting_patterns" title="Politics of Northern Ireland">also exist</a> in Northern Ireland.</p>
<p>There are republican members of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> (SNP) in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> who advocate independence for those countries as republics. The SNP's official policy is that the British monarch would remain head of state of an independent Scotland, unless the people of Scotland decided otherwise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salmond_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salmond-1">[1]</a></sup> Plaid Cymru have a similar view for Wales. The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Socialist_Party" title="Scottish Socialist Party">Scottish Socialist Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Green_Party" title="Scottish Green Party">Scottish Green Party</a> both support an independent Scottish republic.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the 1970s, early modern English republicanism has been extensively studied by historians, to the point where <a href="/wiki/Monarchism" title="Monarchism">monarchism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolutism</a> have now become neglected fields. <a href="/wiki/James_Harrington_(author)" title="James Harrington (author)">James Harrington</a> (1611–77) is generally considered to be the most representative republican writer of the era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cromwellian_Commonwealth">Cromwellian Commonwealth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cromwellian Commonwealth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth of England</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Protectorate" title="The Protectorate">The Protectorate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Interregnum_(England)" title="Interregnum (England)">Interregnum (England)</a></div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Wars of the Three Kingdoms">Wars of the Three Kingdoms</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Countries_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Countries of the United Kingdom">countries</a> that now make up the United Kingdom, together with the present <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, were briefly ruled as a republic in the 17th century, first under the Commonwealth consisting of the <a href="/wiki/Rump_Parliament" title="Rump Parliament">Rump Parliament</a> and the <a href="/wiki/English_Council_of_State" title="English Council of State">Council of State</a> (1649–53) and then under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protectorate_(English_history)" title="Protectorate (English history)">Protectorate</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a> (1653–58). The Commonwealth Parliament represented itself as a Republic on the classical model, with <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">John Milton</a> writing Latin justifications for use as propaganda in Continental Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Cromwell's Protectorate was less ideologically republican and was seen by Cromwell as restoring the mixed constitution of monarchy, aristocracy and democracy found in classical literature and English common law discourse.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>First <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">the Kingdom of England</a> was declared to be the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth of England</a> and then Scotland and Ireland were briefly forced into union with England by the army. This decision was later reversed when the monarchy was restored in 1660. In 1707 the Act of Union between England and Scotland was signed; the two countries' parliaments became one, and in return Scotland was granted access to the <a href="/wiki/English_overseas_possessions" title="English overseas possessions">English overseas possessions</a>.</p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cromwell_as_a_usurper.tiff"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1278" data-file-width="699" height="402" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Cromwell_as_a_usurper.tiff/lossy-page1-220px-Cromwell_as_a_usurper.tiff.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Cromwell_as_a_usurper.tiff/lossy-page1-330px-Cromwell_as_a_usurper.tiff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Cromwell_as_a_usurper.tiff/lossy-page1-440px-Cromwell_as_a_usurper.tiff.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cromwell_as_a_usurper.tiff" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>Many of Cromwell's actions upon gaining power were decried as "harsh, unwise, and tyrannical".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> He and General <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Fairfax" title="Thomas Fairfax">Thomas Fairfax</a> were often ruthless in putting down the mutinies which occurred within their own army towards the end of the civil wars (prompted by Parliament's failure to pay the troops). They showed little sympathy for the <a href="/wiki/Levellers" title="Levellers">Levellers</a>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egalitarian" title="Egalitarian">egalitarian</a> movement which had contributed greatly<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to Parliament's cause but sought representation for ordinary citizens. The Leveller point of view had been strongly represented in the <a href="/wiki/Putney_Debates" title="Putney Debates">Putney Debates</a>, held between the various factions of the Army in 1647, just prior to the King's temporary escape from army custody. Cromwell and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grandees" title="Grandees">Grandees</a> were not prepared to permit such a radical democracy and used the debates to play for time while the future of the King was being determined. <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Catholics</a> were persecuted zealously under Cromwell<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Although he personally was in favour of religious toleration – "liberty for tender consciences" – not all his compatriots agreed. The war led to much <a href="/wiki/Cromwellian_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Cromwellian conquest of Ireland">death and chaos in Ireland</a> where Irish Catholics and Protestants who fought for the Royalists were persecuted. There was a ban on many forms of entertainment, as public meetings could be used as a cover for conspirators; horse racing was banned, the <a href="/wiki/Maypole" title="Maypole">maypoles</a> were famously cut down, the theatres were closed, and Christmas celebrations were outlawed for being too ceremonial, Catholic, and "popish". When <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> eventually regained the throne, in 1660, he was widely celebrated for allowing his subjects to have "fun" again.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Much of Cromwell's power was due to the Rump Parliament, a Parliament purged of opposition to grandees in the <a href="/wiki/New_Model_Army" title="New Model Army">New Model Army</a>. Whereas <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> had been in part restrained by a Parliament that would not always do as he wished (the cause of the Civil War), Cromwell was able to wield much more power as only loyalists were allowed to become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Members_of_Parliament" title="Members of Parliament">MPs</a>, turning the chamber into a rubber-stamping organisation. This was ironic given his complaints about Charles I acting without heeding the "wishes" of the people. But even so he found it almost impossible to get his Parliaments to follow all his wishes. His executive decisions were often thwarted – most famously in the ending of the rule of the regional major generals appointed by himself.</p>
<p>In 1657 Cromwell was offered the crown by Parliament, presenting him with a dilemma since he had played a great role in abolishing the monarchy. After two months of deliberation, he rejected the offer. Instead, he was ceremonially re-installed as "<a href="/wiki/Lord_Protector" title="Lord Protector">Lord Protector</a>", with greater powers than he had previously held. It is often suggested that offering Cromwell the Crown was an effort to curb his power: as a King he would be obliged to honour agreements such as <a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">Magna Carta</a>, but under the arrangement he had designed he had no such restraints. This allowed him to preserve and enhance his power and the army's while decreasing Parliament's control over him, probably to enable him to maintain a well-funded army which Parliament could not be depended upon to provide.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The office of Lord Protector was not formally hereditary, although Cromwell was able to nominate his own successor in his son, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Cromwell" title="Richard Cromwell">Richard</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration_of_the_monarchy">Restoration of the monarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Restoration of the monarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although England, Ireland and Scotland became <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchies</a>, after the reigns of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> and his brother <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">James II &amp; VII</a>, and with the ascension of <a href="/wiki/William_and_Mary" title="William and Mary">William and Mary</a> to the English, Irish and Scottish thrones as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a> of 1688, there have been movements throughout the last few centuries whose aims were to remove the monarchy and establish a republican system. A notable period was the time in the late 18th century and early 19th century when many <a href="/wiki/Radicalism_(historical)" title="Radicalism (historical)">Radicals</a> such as the minister <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fawcett" title="Joseph Fawcett">Joseph Fawcett</a> were openly republican.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_and_French_Revolutions">American and French Revolutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: American and French Revolutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Paine_rev1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3451" data-file-width="2562" height="296" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Thomas_Paine_rev1.jpg/220px-Thomas_Paine_rev1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Thomas_Paine_rev1.jpg/330px-Thomas_Paine_rev1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Thomas_Paine_rev1.jpg/440px-Thomas_Paine_rev1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/Thomas_Paine" title="Thomas Paine">Thomas Paine</a> (1737–1809): "One of the strongest natural proofs of the folly of hereditary right in kings is that nature disproves it, otherwise she would not so frequently turn it into ridicule, by giving mankind <i>an ass for a lion</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foner_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foner-4">[4]</a></sup></div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> had a great impact on political thought in the British Isles. According to <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hitchens" title="Christopher Hitchens">Christopher Hitchens</a>, the British–American author, philosopher, politician and activist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Paine" title="Thomas Paine">Thomas Paine</a> was the 'moral author of the American Revolution', who posited in the soon widely read pamphlet <i><a href="/wiki/Common_Sense_(pamphlet)" title="Common Sense (pamphlet)">Common Sense</a></i> (January 1776) that the conflict of the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">Thirteen Colonies</a> with the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hanover" title="House of Hanover">Hanoverian monarchy</a> in London was best resolved by setting up a separate <a href="/wiki/Democratic_republic" title="Democratic republic">democratic republic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-5">[5]</a></sup> To him, republicanism was more important than independence. However, the circumstances forced the American revolutionaries to give up any hope of reconciliation with Britain, and reforming its 'corrupt' monarchial government, that so often dragged the American colonies in its European wars, from within.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foner_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Foner-4">[4]</a></sup> He and other British republican writers saw in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">Declaration of Independence</a> (4 July 1776) a legitimate struggle against the Crown, that violated people's freedom and rights, and denied them <a href="/wiki/Representation_(politics)" title="Representation (politics)">representation in politics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Revolution_Controversy" title="Revolution Controversy">Revolution Controversy</a></div>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> broke out in 1789, debates started in the British Isles on how to respond. Soon a pro-Revolutionary republican and anti-Revolutionary monarchist camp had established themselves amongst the intelligentsia, who waged a pamphlet war until 1795. Prominent figures of the republican camp were <a href="/wiki/Mary_Wollstonecraft" title="Mary Wollstonecraft">Mary Wollstonecraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Godwin" title="William Godwin">William Godwin</a> and again Thomas Paine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Paine would also play an important role inside the Revolution in France as an elected member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Convention" title="National Convention">National Convention</a> (1792–93), where he lobbied for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Britain" title="Invasion of Britain">invasion of Britain</a> to establish a republic after the example of the United States, <a href="/wiki/Proclamation_of_the_abolition_of_the_monarchy" title="Proclamation of the abolition of the monarchy">France</a> and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sister_Republic" title="Sister Republic">Sister Republics</a>, but also famously opposed the <a href="/wiki/Execution_of_Louis_XVI" title="Execution of Louis XVI">execution of Louis XVI</a>, which got him arrested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_French_Republic" title="First French Republic">First French Republic</a> would indeed stage an <a href="/wiki/Exp%C3%A9dition_d%27Irlande" title="Expédition d'Irlande">Expedition to Ireland</a> in December 1796 to help the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_United_Irishmen" title="Society of United Irishmen">Society of United Irishmen</a> set up an Irish republic in order to destabilise the United Kingdom, but this ended in a failure. The subsequent <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1798" title="Irish Rebellion of 1798">Irish Rebellion of 1798</a> was utterly crushed by the British Army. <a href="/wiki/Napoleon%27s_planned_invasion_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Napoleon's planned invasion of the United Kingdom">Napoleon also planned an invasion of Britain</a> since 1798 and more seriously since 1803, but in 1804 he relinquished republicanism by <a href="/wiki/Coronation_of_Napoleon_I" title="Coronation of Napoleon I">crowning himself Emperor of the French</a> and converting all Sister Republics into client kingdoms of the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">French Empire</a>, before calling off the invasion of Britain altogether in 1805.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revolutionary_republicanism_1800.E2.80.931848">Revolutionary republicanism 1800–1848</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Revolutionary republicanism 1800–1848">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_the_British_Republic.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="600" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Flag_of_the_British_Republic.svg/220px-Flag_of_the_British_Republic.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Flag_of_the_British_Republic.svg/330px-Flag_of_the_British_Republic.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Flag_of_the_British_Republic.svg/440px-Flag_of_the_British_Republic.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_the_British_Republic.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
British Republican Flag, originated in 1816, in use until at least 1935.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riot_City_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riot_City-8">[8]</a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>From the start of the French Revolution into the early 19th century, the revolutionary <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_France" title="Flag of France">blue-white-red tricolour</a> was used throughout England, Wales and Ireland in defiance of the royal establishment. During the 1816 <a href="/wiki/Spa_Fields_riots" title="Spa Fields riots">Spa Fields riots</a>, a 'green, white and red' horizontal flag appeared for the first time, soon followed by a red, white and green horizontal version allegedly in use during the 1817 <a href="/wiki/Pentrich_rising" title="Pentrich rising">Pentrich rising</a> and the 1819 <a href="/wiki/Peterloo_Massacre" title="Peterloo Massacre">Peterloo Massacre</a>. The latter would become known as the British Republican Flag. It may have been inspired by the French revolutionary tricolour, but this is unclear. It was however often accompanied by slogans consisting of three words such as "Fraternity – Liberty – Humanity" (a clear reference to <i><a href="/wiki/Libert%C3%A9,_%C3%A9galit%C3%A9,_fraternit%C3%A9" title="Liberté, égalité, fraternité">Liberté, égalité, fraternité</a></i>), and adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Chartism" title="Chartism">Chartist movement</a> in the 1830s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riot_City_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Riot_City-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Besides these skirmishes in Great Britain itself, separatist republican revolutions against the British monarchy during the <a href="/wiki/Rebellions_of_1837" title="Rebellions of 1837">Canadian rebellions of 1837–38</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Young_Irelander_Rebellion_of_1848" title="Young Irelander Rebellion of 1848">Young Irelander Rebellion of 1848</a> failed.</p>
<p>Parliament passed the <a href="/wiki/Treason_Felony_Act_1848" title="Treason Felony Act 1848">Treason Felony Act</a> in 1848. This act made advocacy of republicanism punishable by <a href="/wiki/Penal_transportation" title="Penal transportation">transportation</a> to Australia, which was later amended to life imprisonment. The law is still on the statute books; however in a 2003 case, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_Lords" title="Law Lords">Law Lords</a> stated that, "It is plain as a pike staff to the respondents and everyone else that no one who advocates the peaceful abolition of the monarchy and its replacement by a republican form of government is at any risk of prosecution."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LawLords_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-LawLords-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_19th_century">Late 19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Late 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the later years of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victoria_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Victoria of the United Kingdom">Queen Victoria's</a> reign, there was considerable criticism of her decision to withdraw from public life following the death of her husband, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Albert_of_Saxe-Coburg-Gotha" title="Prince Albert of Saxe-Coburg-Gotha">Prince Albert</a>. This resulted in a "significant incarnation" of republicanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olech190_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olech190-10">[10]</a></sup> During the 1870s, calls for Britain to become a republic on the American or French model were made by the politicians <a href="/wiki/Sir_Charles_Dilke,_2nd_Baronet" title="Sir Charles Dilke, 2nd Baronet">Charles Dilke</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bradlaugh" title="Charles Bradlaugh">Charles Bradlaugh</a>, as well as journalist <a href="/wiki/George_W._M._Reynolds" title="George W. M. Reynolds">George W. M. Reynolds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olech190_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Olech190-10">[10]</a></sup> This republican presence continued in debates and the Labour press, especially in the event of royal weddings, jubilees and births, until well into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interwar_Period" title="Interwar Period">Interwar Period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olech190_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Olech190-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Some members of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Keir_Hardie" title="Keir Hardie">Keir Hardie</a> (1856–1915), also held republican views.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century_republicanism">20th century republicanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 20th century republicanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1923, at the Labour Party's annual conference, two motions were proposed, supported by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Thurtle" title="Ernest Thurtle">Ernest Thurtle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emrys_Hughes" title="Emrys Hughes">Emrys Hughes</a>. The first was "that the Royal Family is no longer a necessary party of the British constitution", and the second was "that the hereditary principle in the British Constitution be abolished".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-km_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-km-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Lansbury" title="George Lansbury">George Lansbury</a> responded that, although he too was a republican, he regarded the issue of the monarchy as a "distraction" from more important issues. Lansbury added that he believed the "social revolution" would eventually remove the monarchy peacefully in the future. Both of the motions were overwhelmingly defeated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-km_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-km-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Following this event, most of the Labour Party moved away from advocating republican views.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-km_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-km-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1936, following the <a href="/wiki/Edward_VIII_abdication_crisis" title="Edward VIII abdication crisis">abdication of Edward VIII</a>, MP <a href="/wiki/James_Maxton" title="James Maxton">James Maxton</a> proposed a "republican amendment" to the Abdication Bill, which would have established a Republic in Britain. Maxton argued that while the Monarchy had benefited Britain in the past, it had now "outlived its usefulness." Five MPs voted to support the bill, including <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Salter" title="Alfred Salter">Alfred Salter</a>. However the bill was defeated by 403 votes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1991, Labour MP <a href="/wiki/Tony_Benn" title="Tony Benn">Tony Benn</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Britain_Bill" title="Commonwealth of Britain Bill">Commonwealth of Britain Bill</a>, which called for the transformation of the United Kingdom into a "democratic, federal and secular Commonwealth of Britain", with an elected President.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The monarchy would be abolished and replaced by a republic with a written constitution. It was read in Parliament a number of times until his retirement at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2001" title="United Kingdom general election, 2001">2001 election</a>, but never achieved a <a href="/wiki/Reading_(legislature)" title="Reading (legislature)">second reading</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Benn presented an account of his proposal in <i><a href="/wiki/Common_Sense_(book)" title="Common Sense (book)">Common Sense: A New Constitution for Britain</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century_republicanism">21st century republicanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 21st century republicanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MORI" title="MORI">MORI</a> Polls in the opening years of the 21st century showing support for retaining the monarchy stable at around 70% of people, but in 2005, during the time of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wedding_of_Charles,_Prince_of_Wales_and_Camilla_Parker_Bowles" title="Wedding of Charles, Prince of Wales and Camilla Parker Bowles">wedding</a> of <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Prince Charles</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camilla_Parker_Bowles" title="Camilla Parker Bowles">Camilla Parker Bowles</a>, support for the monarchy dipped with one poll showing that 65% of people would support keeping the monarchy if there were a referendum on the issue, with 22% saying they favoured a republic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipsos-mori.com_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipsos-mori.com-21">[21]</a></sup> In 2009 an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICM_(polling)" title="ICM (polling)">ICM</a> poll, commission by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>, found that 76% of those asked wanted the monarchy to continue after the Queen, against 18% of people who said they would favour Britain becoming a republic and 6% who said they did not know.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2011, a YouGov poll put support for ending the monarchy after the Queen's death at 13%, if Prince Charles becomes King.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> However, an ICM poll shortly before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wedding_of_Prince_William_and_Kate_Middleton" title="Wedding of Prince William and Kate Middleton">royal wedding</a> suggested that 26% thought Britain would be better off without the monarchy, with only 37% "genuinely interested and excited" by the wedding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In April 2011, in the lead up to the Royal Wedding, an Ipsos MORI poll of 1,000 British adults found that 75% of the public would like Britain to remain a monarchy, with 18% in favour of Britain becoming a republic, and in May 2012, in the lead up to the Queen's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamond_Jubilee_of_Queen_Elizabeth_II" title="Diamond Jubilee of Queen Elizabeth II">Diamond Jubilee</a>, a Ipsos MORI poll of 1,006 British adults found that 80% were in favour of the monarchy, with only 13% in favour of the United Kingdom becoming a republic. This was thought to be a record high figure in recent years in favour of the monarchy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipsos-mori.com_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipsos-mori.com-21">[21]</a></sup> In a recent survey, for the Guardian, support for the monarchy was shown to be at least 75%.</p>
<p>The main organisation campaigning for a republic in the United Kingdom is the campaign group <a href="/wiki/Republic_(political_organisation)" title="Republic (political organisation)">Republic</a>. Formed in 1987, Republic is frequently cited by much of the UK media on issues involving the royal family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title='"That'>not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In September 2015, <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Corbyn" title="Jeremy Corbyn">Jeremy Corbyn</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> MP with republican views, won <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)_leadership_election,_2015" title="Labour Party (UK) leadership election, 2015">his party's leadership election</a> and became both <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Opposition_(United_Kingdom)" title="Leader of the Opposition (United Kingdom)">Leader of the Opposition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Leader of the Labour Party (UK)">Leader of the Labour Party</a>. In 1991, Corbyn seconded the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Britain_Bill" title="Commonwealth of Britain Bill">Commonwealth of Britain Bill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> However, Corbyn stated during his 2015 campaign for the leadership that republicanism was "not a battle that I am fighting".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Proctor_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Proctor-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeremyrepublicanism_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-jeremyrepublicanism-29">[29]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_parties">Political parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Political parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2017<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> none of the three major nationwide British political parties, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Labour_Party" title="British Labour Party">the Labour Party</a>, the Conservative Party and the Liberal Democrat Party, had an official policy of republicanism. However, there are a number of individual politicians who favour abolition of the monarchy (<a href="#Advocates_of_republicanism_for_the_UK">see above</a>). <a href="/wiki/Tony_Benn" title="Tony Benn">Tony Benn</a> of the Labour Party introduced a <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Britain_Bill" title="Commonwealth of Britain Bill">Commonwealth of Britain Bill</a> in <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a> in 1991.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Corbyn" title="Jeremy Corbyn">Jeremy Corbyn</a>, who became leader of the Labour Party in 2015, is a republican, but has stated that he will not seek to abolish the monarchy whilst he remains leader. The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party of England and Wales</a>, with one MP in the 2015-2020 Parliament, has an official policy of republicanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> The Irish republican party <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a> has four MPs, but they do not take their seats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-belfasttele_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-belfasttele-77">[77]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Green_Party" title="Scottish Green Party">Scottish Green Party</a>, with six MSPs in the 2016-2021 <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>, supports having an elected Head of State in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_Scotland" title="Independent Scotland">Independent Scotland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sgreensindy_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-sgreensindy-78">[78]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Republic_Party_(UK)" title="Republic Party (UK)">Republic Party</a> also has an official policy of republicanism, whose main aim is to establish a republican government.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lobby_groups">Lobby groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Lobby groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Republic_(political_organisation)" title="Republic (political organisation)">Republic (political organisation)</a></div>
<p>The largest lobby group in favour of republicanism in the United Kingdom is the <a href="/wiki/Republic_(political_organisation)" title="Republic (political organisation)">Republic</a> campaign group, founded in 1983. The group has benefited from occasional negative publicity about the Royal Family, and Republic has reported a large rise in membership since the wedding of Prince Charles and Camilla Parker-Bowles. In June 2006 the group handed in a petition of over 3,000 names to <a href="/wiki/10_Downing_Street" title="10 Downing Street">10 Downing Street</a> calling for a serious national debate about the future of the monarchy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Since then Republic has lobbied on changes to the parliamentary oath of allegiance, royal finances and changes to the Freedom of Information Act relating to the monarchy, none of which have produced any change. However, Republic has been invited to Parliament to talk as witnesses on certain issues related to the monarchy such as conduct of the honours system in the country.</p>
<p>In 2009 Republic made news<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> by reporting Prince Charles's architecture charity to the Charity Commission, claiming that the Prince was effectively using the organisation as a private lobbying firm (the Commission declined to take the matter further). Republic has previously broken stories about royals using the Freedom of Information Act. The organisation is regularly called up to comment and provide quotes for the press, national and local radio and national TV programmes, with much criticism as to the portrayal of the monarchy by the BBC which has been accused of celebrating the monarchy rather than keeping its politically neutral stance on issues related to it.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">Observer</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">Independent</a></i> newspapers have all advocated the abolition of monarchy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewStat_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewStat-80">[80]</a></sup> In the wake of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Parliamentary_expenses_scandal" title="United Kingdom Parliamentary expenses scandal">2009 MPs' expenses scandal</a>, a poll of readers of the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">Guardian</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">Observer</a></i> newspapers placed support for abolition of the monarchy at 54%, although only 3% saw it as a top priority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuardianPoll_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianPoll-81">[81]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_in_favour_of_a_republic">Arguments in favour of a republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Arguments in favour of a republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_benefits_of_a_republic">The benefits of a republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: The benefits of a republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Republicans suggest that republicanism is a constitutional step which answers a number of key issues.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Advocacy_group" title="Advocacy group">advocacy group</a> <a href="/wiki/Republic_(political_organisation)" title="Republic (political organisation)">Republic</a> argues:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The monarchy is not only an unaccountable and expensive institution, unrepresentative of modern Britain, it also gives politicians almost limitless power.</p>
<p>It does this is in a variety of ways:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Royal_prerogative_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Royal prerogative in the United Kingdom">Royal Prerogative</a>: Royal powers that allow the Prime Minister to declare war or sign treaties (amongst other things) without a vote in Parliament.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Council</a>: A body of advisors to the monarch, now mostly made up of senior politicians, which can enact legislation without a vote in Parliament.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_in_Parliament" title="Crown in Parliament">Crown-in-Parliament</a>: The principle, which came about when Parliament removed much of the monarch's power, by which Parliament can pass any law it likes – meaning our liberties can never be guaranteed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-want_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-want-82">[82]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</blockquote>
<p>Republicans also want to see a constitution that they claim will inspire aspiration (by allowing anyone to become head of state) and political responsibility (by introducing popular sovereignty, the notion that the people are "in charge"). They also claim that they want what is "best for Britain", which includes the best democracy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-case_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-case-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_against_monarchy">Arguments against monarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Arguments against monarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Republicans assert that hereditary monarchy is unfair and elitist. They claim that in a modern and democratic society no one should be expected to defer to another simply because of their birth. Such a system, they assert, does not make for a society which is at ease with itself, and it encourages attitudes which are more suited to a bygone age of imperialism than to a "modern nation". Some claim that maintaining a privileged royal family diminishes a society and encourages a feeling of dependency in many people who should instead have confidence in themselves and their fellow citizens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-case_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-case-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>Further, republicans argue that 'the people', not the members of one family, should be sovereign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-case_83-2"><a href="#cite_note-case-83">[83]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Monarchy contradicts democracy</b>
<ul>
<li><b>Monarchy denies <i>the people</i> a basic right</b> – Republicans believe that it should be a fundamental right of the people of any nation to elect their head of state and for every citizen to be eligible to hold that office. It is argued such a head of state is more accountable to the people, and that such accountability to the people creates a better nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Monarchy devalues a parliamentary system</b> – Monarchical prerogative powers can be used to circumvent normal democratic process with no accountability, and such processes are more desirable than not for any given nation-state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-want_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-want-82">[82]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><b>Monarchy is gender-discriminative</b></li>
</ul>
<p>Succession to the crown has been decided by male <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">primogeniture</a>, which has meant that the crown has been inherited by the eldest son, and only passed to a daughter if the monarch had no sons. In October 2011 it was announced in the <a href="/wiki/Perth_Agreement" title="Perth Agreement">Perth Agreement</a> that succession laws in the UK and in other <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_realm" title="Commonwealth realm">Commonwealth realms</a> would be changed so that sons of any future monarch would no longer be preferred over daughters. The <a href="/wiki/Succession_to_the_Crown_Act_2013" title="Succession to the Crown Act 2013">Succession to the Crown Act</a> gained Royal Assent on 25 April 2013 but commencement depends on an order being made by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_President_of_the_Council" title="Lord President of the Council">Lord President of the Council</a> once all sixteen Commonwealth realms have passed similar legislation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>A monarchy demands deference</b></li>
</ul>
<p>It is argued by republicans that the way citizens are expected to address members, however junior, of the royal family is part of an attempt to keep subjects 'in their place'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>It is the enemy of merit and aspiration</b></li>
</ul>
<p>The order of succession in a monarchy specifies a person who will become head of state, regardless of qualifications. The highest titular office in the land is not open to "free and fair competition". Although monarchists argue that the position of Prime Minister, the title with real power, is something anyone can aspire to become, the executive and symbolically powerful position of Head of State is not.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>It devalues intellect and achievement</b></li>
</ul>
<p>Republicans argue that members of the royal family bolster their position with unearned symbols of achievement. Examples in the UK include the Queen's many honorary military titles of colonel-in-chief, regardless of her military experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> There is debate over the roles which the members of the monarchy have played in the military; many doubt that members of the Royal Family have served on the front line on the same basis as other members of the Armed Forces. Examples here include Prince Andrew, whose presence during the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a> was later criticised by the commander of the British Naval Force who stated that "special measures" had to be taken to ensure that the prince did not lose his life,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> and Prince Harry, who was moved to a safe room and placed under guard during an attack on the Camp Bastion base in Afghanistan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> It is seen to some as more of a PR exercise than military service. Members of the royal family are fast-tracked to higher ranks in the army.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>It harms the monarchs themselves</b></li>
</ul>
<p>Republicans argue that a hereditary system condemns each heir to the throne to an abnormal childhood. This was historically the reason why the anarchist <a href="/wiki/William_Godwin" title="William Godwin">William Godwin</a> opposed the monarchy. <a href="/wiki/Johann_Hari" title="Johann Hari">Johann Hari</a> has written a book <i>God Save the Queen?</i> in which he argues that every member of the royal family has suffered psychologically from the system of monarchy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Monarchs are not impartial, and lack accountability</b></li>
</ul>
<p>Republicans argue that monarchs are not impartial but harbour their own opinions, motives, and wish to protect their interests. Republicans claim that monarchs are not <i>accountable</i>. As an example, republicans argue that <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Prince Charles</a> has spoken and acted in ways that have widely been interpreted as taking a political stance, citing his refusal to attend, in protest of China's dealings with <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, a state dinner hosted by the Queen for the Chinese head of state; his strong stance on GM food; and the contents of certain memos which were leaked to the press regarding how people achieve their positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charles-tibet_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-charles-tibet-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>
<p>Republicans see a lack of important democratic accountability and transparency for such institutions.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>The monarchy is expensive</b></li>
</ul>
<p>Republicans argue that it costs a lot to have a monarchy. Republicans claim that the total costs to taxpayers including hidden elements (e.g., the Royal Protection security bill and lost rental income from palaces and state-owned land) of the monarchy are £334 million per annum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> claims the monarchy costs each adult in Britain around 62p a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R000062_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-R000062-96">[96]</a></sup> However, this figure does not take into account royal security, nor the money paid by regional councils to fund the costs of visits by members of the Royal Family, and assumes the "official" figure of £34m per annum to be divided between every man, woman and child in the land. Republic also argues that the Royal finances, which are exempt from the Freedom of Information Act, are shrouded in secrecy and should be subject to greater scrutiny. Although monarchists argue that this does not take into account the 'hereditary revenues' which generated £190.8 million for the treasury in 2007–2008, republicans assert that the Crown Estate, from which these revenues are derived, is national and State property, and that the monarch cannot surrender what they have never owned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> The monarchy is estimated to cost British taxpayers £202.4m, when costs such as security are included, making it the most expensive monarchy in Europe and 112 times more expensive than the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">presidency of the Republic of Ireland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> The argument that tourism benefits from the continued existence of the British monarchy is refuted by Republic, who suggest that the reverse may actually be true – if the palaces were open throughout the year, tourists would be better able to visit them (as has happened with the Tower of London).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>The monarchy makes the UK appear 'backward'</b></li>
</ul>
<p>Republicans argue that the monarchy is to be considered embarrassing: as a concept it is archaic, too reminiscent of medieval feudalism, and while the UK has a hereditary head of state it cannot claim to be a modern nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_in_favour_of_constitutional_monarchy">Arguments in favour of constitutional monarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Arguments in favour of constitutional monarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">
<tr>
<td class="mbox-image">
<div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div>
</td>
<td class="mbox-text"><span class="mbox-text-span">This article <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>. <span class="hide-when-compact">Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Republicanism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. </tr>
</table>
<ul>
<li><b>Monarchy can be complementary to rather than a replacement for democracy</b></li>
</ul>
<p>Some argue that the current system is still democratic as the Government and MPs of Parliament are elected by universal suffrage and as the Crown acts only on the advice of the Parliament, the people still hold power. Monarchy only refers to how the head of state is chosen and not how the Government is chosen. It is only undemocratic if the monarchy holds meaningful power, which it currently does not as government rests with Parliament. However, it was revealed in October 2011 that both the Queen and Prince Charles do have the power to veto government legislation which affects their private interests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> The Queen attended a cabinet meeting on 18 December 2012 – the first Monarch to have done so since <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">George III</a> in 1781.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Provides a safeguard against government instability</b></li>
</ul>
<p>Some argue that the Monarch's constitutional position (with the little-used power to dissolve or refuse a government) could safeguard against Britain ever becoming a dictatorship; however, Republic has denied claims that the monarchy has this sort of power. Examples of this argument being used often include the <a href="/wiki/Manoonkrit_Roopkachorn#Attempted_coup_of_1981" title="Manoonkrit Roopkachorn">1981 April Fool's Day Coup</a> in <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and the <a href="/wiki/23-F" title="23-F">El Tejerazo coup</a> in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> when <a href="/wiki/Bhumibol_Adulyadej" title="Bhumibol Adulyadej">King Bhumibol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I_of_Spain" title="Juan Carlos I of Spain">King Juan Carlos I</a> respectively stepped in to restore democracy in their countries.</p>
<p>According to the Democracy Index 2014, seven of the ten most democratic countries in the world are constitutional monarchies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>Safeguards the constitutional rights of the individual</b></li>
</ul>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_constitution" title="British constitution">British constitutional</a> system sets limits on Parliament and separates the executive from direct control over the police and courts. Constitutionalists argue<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> that this is because contracts with the monarch such as the <a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">Magna Carta</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bill_of_Rights_1689" title="Bill of Rights 1689">Declaration of Rights</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Settlement_1701" title="Act of Settlement 1701">Act of Settlement</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union</a> place obligations on the state and<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> confirm its citizens as sovereign beings. These obligations are re-affirmed at every monarch's coronation. These obligations, whilst at the same time placing limits on the power of the judiciary and the police, also confirm those rights which are intrinsically part of British and especially English culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> Examples are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_Law" title="Common Law">Common Law</a>, the particular status of ancient practices, <a href="/wiki/Jury_trial" title="Jury trial">jury trials</a>, legal <a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">precedent</a>, protection against non-judicial seizure and the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_protest" title="Right to protest">right to protest</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Provides a focal point for unity and tradition</b></li>
</ul>
<p>Monarchists argue that a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarch</a> with limited powers and non-partisan nature can provide a focus for national unity, national awards and honours, national institutions, and allegiance, as opposed to a president affiliated to a political party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>If there were a republic, the costs may remain the same</b></li>
</ul>
<p>Some argue that if there were a republic, the costs incurred in regards to the duties of the head of state would remain more or less the same. This includes the upkeep and conservation of the royal palaces and buildings which would still have to be paid for as they belong to the nation as a whole rather than the monarch personally. On top of that, the head of state would require a salary and security, state visits, banquets and ceremonial duties would still go ahead. In 2009, the monarchy claimed to be costing each person an estimated 69 pence a year (not including "a hefty security bill").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> However, the figure of 69p per person has been criticised for having been calculated by dividing the overall figure by approximately 60 million people, rather than by the number of British taxpayers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><b>A British Republic has already been tested and failed</b></li>
</ul>
<p>Even though no modern republicans advocate a republic modelled on Cromwell's Protectorate, some<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> point out that a Republican <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth of England</a>, Ireland and Scotland has already been tried when <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a> installed it on 30 January 1649. Yet by February 1657 some people<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> argued that Cromwell should assume <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_crown" title="The crown">the crown</a> as it would stabilise the constitution, limit his powers and restore <a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">precedent</a>. Cromwell declined. Within three years of his death the Republic had lost support and the monarchy was restored. Later, during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Glorious_Revolution" title="The Glorious Revolution">The Glorious Revolution</a> of 1688 caused partially by disillusionment with the absolutist rule of the Scottish <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_England" title="James II of England">James II of England</a> (VII of Scotland), Parliament and others, such as John Locke<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> argued that James had broken "the original contract" with the state. Far from pressing for a republic, which had been experienced within living memory, they instead argued that the best form of government was a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> with explicitly circumscribed powers. (This overlooks a few factors, however – Cromwell's republic was based on the ideals of puritanism and land-based privilege, whilst modern republican advocates have strong links to secularism and expansion of democracy. Cromwell was also not a republican in the strict sense, and opposed groups that were, such as the <a href="/wiki/Levellers" title="Levellers">Levellers</a>. Furthermore, the motivation behind the Glorious Revolution was not public opinion, but the need to overthrow the monarchy without causing a disturbance in public rest, and <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a> himself was a key thinker behind republicanism and an influence on the American revolutionaries.)</p>
