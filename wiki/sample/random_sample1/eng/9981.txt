<p>The <b>Corps of Royal Marines</b> (<b>RM</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Amphibious_warfare" title="Amphibious warfare">amphibious</a> <a href="/wiki/Light_infantry" title="Light infantry">light infantry</a> force, forming part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Service_(United_Kingdom)" title="Naval Service (United Kingdom)">Naval Service</a>, along with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup> The Royal Marines were formed in 1755 as the Royal Navy's <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry troops</a>. However, the marines can trace their origins back to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/English_Army" title="English Army">English Army</a>'s "Duke of York and Albany's maritime regiment of Foot" at the grounds of the <a href="/wiki/Honourable_Artillery_Company" title="Honourable Artillery Company">Honourable Artillery Company</a> on 28 October 1664.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Origins_of_Royal_Marines_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Origins_of_Royal_Marines-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>As a highly specialised and adaptable light infantry force, the Royal Marines are trained for rapid deployment worldwide and capable of dealing with a wide range of threats. The Royal Marines are organised into a light infantry brigade (<a href="/wiki/3_Commando_Brigade" title="3 Commando Brigade">3 Commando Brigade</a>) and a number of separate units, including <a href="/wiki/1_Assault_Group_Royal_Marines" title="1 Assault Group Royal Marines">1 Assault Group Royal Marines</a>, 43 Commando Royal Marines formerly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fleet_Protection_Group_Royal_Marines" title="Fleet Protection Group Royal Marines">Fleet Protection Group Royal Marines</a> (previously the Comacchio Group), and a company strength commitment to the <a href="/wiki/Special_Forces_Support_Group" title="Special Forces Support Group">Special Forces Support Group</a>. The Corps operates in all environments and climates, though particular expertise and training is spent on <a href="/wiki/Amphibious_warfare" title="Amphibious warfare">amphibious warfare</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_warfare" title="Arctic warfare">arctic warfare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain_warfare" title="Mountain warfare">mountain warfare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Expeditionary_warfare" title="Expeditionary warfare">expeditionary warfare</a>, and its commitment to the UK's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_Reaction_Force" title="Rapid Reaction Force">Rapid Reaction</a> Force.</p>
<p>Throughout its history, the Royal Marines have seen action in a number of major wars often fighting beside the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>Â â including the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. In recent times the Corps has been largely deployed in expeditionary warfare roles such as the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bosnian_War" title="Bosnian War">Bosnian War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kosovo_War" title="Kosovo War">Kosovo War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone_Civil_War" title="Sierra Leone Civil War">Sierra Leone Civil War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001âpresent)">War in Afghanistan</a>. The Royal Marines have close international ties with allied marine forces, particularly the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Marine_Corps" title="Netherlands Marine Corps">Netherlands Marine Corps</a> (Dutch: <span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl"><i>Korps Mariniers</i></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mod.uk_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-mod.uk-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royalnavy.mod.uk_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-royalnavy.mod.uk-5">[4]</a></sup> Today, the Royal Marines are an elite fighting force within the British Armed forces, having undergone many substantial changes over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Royal_Marines" title="History of the Royal Marines">History of the Royal Marines</a></div>
<p>The Royal Marines can trace its origins back as far as 28 October 1664 when at the grounds of the <a href="/wiki/Honourable_Artillery_Company" title="Honourable Artillery Company">Honourable Artillery Company</a> "the Duke of York and Albany's maritime regiment of foot" was first formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Origins_of_Royal_Marines_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Origins_of_Royal_Marines-3">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_British_Empire">Early British Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early British Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 5 April 1755, <i>His Majesty's Marine Forces</i>, fifty Companies in three Divisions, headquartered at <a href="/wiki/Chatham,_Kent" title="Chatham, Kent">Chatham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a>, were formed by Order of Council under <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Admiralty</a> control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Origins_of_Royal_Marines_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Origins_of_Royal_Marines-3">[2]</a></sup> Initially all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_officers" title="Field officers">field officers</a> were Royal Navy officers as the Royal Navy felt that the ranks of Marine field officers were largely honorary. This meant that the furthest a Marine officer could advance was to Lieutenant Colonel. It was not until 1771 that the first Marine was promoted to Colonel. This attitude persisted well into the 1800s. During the rest of the 18th century, they served in numerous landings all over the world, the most famous being the landing at <a href="/wiki/Belle_%C3%8Ele" title="Belle Ãle">Bellisle</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> coast in 1761.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Origins_of_Royal_Marines_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Origins_of_Royal_Marines-3">[2]</a></sup> They also served in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American War of Independence</a>, being particularly courageous in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bunker_Hill" title="Battle of Bunker Hill">Battle of Bunker Hill</a> led by Major <a href="/wiki/John_Pitcairn" title="John Pitcairn">John Pitcairn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1788 a detachment of four companies of marines, under Major <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ross_(Royal_Marines_officer)" title="Robert Ross (Royal Marines officer)">Robert Ross</a>, accompanied the <a href="/wiki/First_Fleet" title="First Fleet">First Fleet</a> to protect a new colony at <a href="/wiki/Botany_Bay" title="Botany Bay">Botany Bay</a> (<a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>). Due to an error the Fleet left Portsmouth without its main supply of ammunition, and were not resupplied until the Fleet docked in Rio de Janeiro midway through the voyage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> One scholar has claimed that the Marines deliberately spread smallpox among Australia's indigenous population in order to reduce its military effectiveness, but this is not corroborated by contemporaneous records of the settlement and most researchers attribute the indigenous smallpox outbreak to other causes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whowhenwhy_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-whowhenwhy-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1802, largely at the instigation of Admiral the Earl St. Vincent, they were titled the <i>Royal Marines</i> by <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">King George III</a>. The <i>Royal Marines Artillery</i> (RMA) was formed as a separate unit in 1804 to man the artillery in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bomb_ketch" title="Bomb ketch">bomb ketches</a>. These had been manned by the Army's <a href="/wiki/Royal_Artillery" title="Royal Artillery">Royal Regiment of Artillery</a>, but a lawsuit by a Royal Artillery officer resulted in a court decision that Army officers were not subject to Naval orders. As RMA uniforms were the blue of the Royal Regiment of Artillery they were nicknamed the "Blue Marines" and the Infantry element, who wore the scarlet uniforms of the British infantry, became known as the "Red Marines", often given the semi-derogatory nickname "Lobsters" by sailors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crest_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-crest-11">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> the Royal Marines participated in every <a href="/wiki/Category:Naval_battles_involving_the_United_Kingdom" title="Category:Naval battles involving the United Kingdom">notable naval battle</a> on board the Royal Navy's ships and also took part in multiple amphibious actions. Marines had a dual function aboard ships of the Royal Navy in this period; routinely, they ensured the security of the ship's officers and supported their maintenance of discipline in the ship's crew, and in battle, they engaged the enemy's crews, whether firing from positions on their own ship, or fighting in boarding actions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> In the Caribbean theatre volunteers from freed French slaves on <a href="/wiki/Marie-Galante" title="Marie-Galante">Marie-Galante</a> were used to form the <a href="/wiki/Corps_of_Colonial_Marines" title="Corps of Colonial Marines">1st Corps of Colonial Marines</a>. These men bolstered the ranks, helping the British to hold the island until reinforcements arrived. This practice was repeated during the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>, where escaped American slaves were formed into the <a href="/wiki/Corps_of_Colonial_Marines" title="Corps of Colonial Marines">2nd Corps of Colonial Marines</a>. These men were commanded by Royal Marines officers and fought alongside their regular Royal Marines counterparts at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bladensburg" title="Battle of Bladensburg">Battle of Bladensburg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> Throughout the war Royal Marines units raided up and down the east coast of America including up the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hampden" title="Battle of Hampden">Penobscot River</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>. They fought in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_New_Orleans" title="Battle of New Orleans">Battle of New Orleans</a> and later helped capture <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fort_Bowyer" title="Battle of Fort Bowyer">Fort Bowyer</a> in Mobile Bay in what was the last action of the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1855 the Infantry forces were renamed the <i>Royal Marines Light Infantry</i> (RMLI). During the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a> in 1854 and 1855, three Royal Marines earned the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Cross" title="Victoria Cross">Victoria Cross</a>, two in the Crimea and one in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cross_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-cross-15">[14]</a></sup> In 1862 the name was slightly altered to <i>Royal Marine Light Infantry</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> did not fight any other ships after 1850 and became interested in landings by Naval Brigades. In these Naval Brigades, the function of the Royal Marines was to land first and act as skimishers ahead of the sailor Infantry and Artillery. This skirmishing was the traditional function of Light Infantry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> For most of their history, British Marines had been organised as <a href="/wiki/Fusilier" title="Fusilier">fusiliers</a>. In the rest of the 19th Century the Royal Marines served in many landings especially in the <a href="/wiki/First_Opium_War" title="First Opium War">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Opium_War" title="Second Opium War">Second Opium Wars</a> (1839â1842 and 1856â1860) against the Chinese. These were all successful except for the landing at the Mouth of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hai_He" title="Hai He">Peiho</a> in 1859, where <a href="/wiki/James_Hope_(Royal_Navy_officer)" title="James Hope (Royal Navy officer)">Admiral Sir James Hope</a> ordered a landing across extensive mud flats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Royal Marines also played a prominent role in the <a href="/wiki/Boxer_Rebellion" title="Boxer Rebellion">Boxer Rebellion</a> in China (1900), where a Royal Marine earned a <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Cross" title="Victoria Cross">Victoria Cross</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cross_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-cross-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pursuing a career in the Marines had been considered social suicide through much of the 18th and 19th centuries since Marine officers had a lower standing than their counterparts in the Royal Navy. An effort was made in 1907 through the common entry or "Selborne Scheme" to reduce the professional differences between RN and RM officers through a system of common entry that provided for an initial period of service where both groups performed the same roles and underwent the same training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Wars">World Wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World Wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="First_World_War">First World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>, in addition to their usual stations aboard ship, Royal Marines were part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_Division" title="Royal Naval Division">Royal Naval Division</a> which landed in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> in 1914 to help defend <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> and later took part in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gallipoli" title="Battle of Gallipoli">amphibious landing at Gallipoli</a> in 1915. It also served on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a>. The Division's first two commanders were Royal Marine Artillery Generals. Other Royal Marines acted as landing parties in the Naval campaign against the Turkish fortifications in the Dardanelles before the Gallipoli landing. They were sent ashore to assess damage to Turkish fortifications after bombardment by British and French ships and, if necessary, to complete their destruction. The Royal Marines were the last to leave Gallipoli, replacing both British and <a href="/wiki/French_Army" title="French Army">French troops</a> in a neatly planned and executed withdrawal from the beaches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>The Royal Marines also took part in the <a href="/wiki/Zeebrugge_Raid" title="Zeebrugge Raid">Zeebrugge Raid</a> in 1918. Five Royal Marines earned the Victoria Cross in the First World War, two at Zeebrugge, one at Gallipoli, one at Jutland and one on the Western Front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cross_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-cross-15">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Between_the_wars">Between the wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Between the wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After the war Royal Marines took part in the <a href="/wiki/Allied_intervention_in_the_Russian_Civil_War" title="Allied intervention in the Russian Civil War">allied intervention in Russia</a>. In 1919, the 6th Battalion RMLI mutinied and was disbanded at Murmansk. The Royal Marine Artillery (RMA) and Royal Marine Light Infantry (RMLI) were amalgamated on 22 June 1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> Post-war demobilisation had seen the Royal Marines reduced from 55,000 (1918) to 15,000 in 1922 and there was <a href="/wiki/Treasury" title="Treasury">Treasury</a> pressure for a further reduction to 6,000 or even the entire disbandment of the Corps. As a compromise an establishment of 9,500 was settled upon but this meant that two separate branches could no longer be maintained. The abandonment of the Marine's artillery role meant that the Corps would subsequently have to rely on Royal Artillery support when ashore, that the title of Royal Marines would apply to the entire Corps and that only a few specialists would now receive gunnery training. As a form of consolation the dark blue and red uniform of the Royal Marine Artillery now became the full dress of the entire Corps. Royal Marine officers and SNCO's however continue to wear the historic scarlet in <a href="/wiki/Mess_dress" title="Mess dress">mess dress</a> to the present day. The ranks of <a href="/wiki/Private_(rank)" title="Private (rank)">Private</a>, used by the RMLI, and <a href="/wiki/Gunner_(rank)" title="Gunner (rank)">Gunner</a>, used by the RMA, were abolished and replaced by the rank of Marine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup></p>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>, a small party of Royal Marines were first ashore at <a href="/wiki/Namsos_Campaign" title="Namsos Campaign">Namsos in April 1940</a>, seizing the approaches to the Norwegian town preparatory to a landing by the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> two days later. The Royal Marines formed the Royal Marine Division as an amphibiously trained division, parts of which served at Dakar and in the capture of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>. After the assault on the French naval base at Antsirane in Madagascar was held up, fifty Sea Service Royal Marines from HMS Ramilles commanded by Captain Martin Price were landed on the quay of the base by the British destroyer HMS Anthony after it ran the gauntlet of French shore batteries defending Diego Suarez Bay. They then captured two of the batteries, which led to a quick surrender by the French.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition the Royal Marines formed Mobile Naval Base Defence Organisations (MNBDOs) similar to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a> Defense Battalions. One of these took part in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Crete" title="Battle of Crete">defence of Crete</a>. Royal Marines also served in <a href="/wiki/Malayan_Campaign" title="Malayan Campaign">Malaya</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Singapore" title="Battle of Singapore">Singapore</a>, where due to losses they were joined with remnants of the 2nd Battalion, <a href="/wiki/Argyll_and_Sutherland_Highlanders" title="Argyll and Sutherland Highlanders">Argyll and Sutherland Highlanders</a> to form the "Plymouth Argylls". The Royal Marines formed one Commando (A Commando) which served at <a href="/wiki/Dieppe_Raid" title="Dieppe Raid">Dieppe</a>. One month after Dieppe, most of the 11th Royal Marine Battalion was killed or captured in an ill staged amphibious landing at Tobruk in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Agreement" title="Operation Agreement">Operation Agreement</a>. Again, the Marines were involved with the Argyll and Sutherland Highlanders, this time the 1st Battalion. In 1942 the Infantry Battalions of the Royal Marine Division were re-organised as <a href="/wiki/Commando" title="Commando">Commandos</a>, joining the <a href="/wiki/British_Commandos" title="British Commandos">British Army Commandos</a>. The Division command structure became a <a href="/wiki/Special_Service_Brigade" title="Special Service Brigade">Special Service Brigade</a> command. The support troops became <a href="/wiki/Landing_craft" title="Landing craft">landing craft</a> crew and saw extensive action on <a href="/wiki/Normandy_landings" title="Normandy landings">D-Day</a> in June 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>

<p>A total of four Special Service Brigades (later Commando brigade) were raised during the war, and Royal Marines were represented in all of them. Special Service Brigade">1 Commando Brigade</a> had just one RM Battalion, <a href="/wiki/45_Commando" title="45 Commando">No 45 Commando</a>. <a href="/wiki/2nd_Special_Service_Brigade" title="2nd <p>In January 1945, two further RM Brigades were formed, 116th Brigade and 117th Brigade. Both were conventional Infantry, rather than in the Commando role. 116th Brigade saw some action in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, but 117th Brigade was hardly used operationally. In addition one Landing Craft Assault (LCA) unit was stationed in Australia late in the war as a training unit. In 1946 the Army Commandos were disbanded, leaving the Royal Marines to continue the Commando role (with supporting Army elements). A number of Royal Marines served as pilots during the Second World War. It was a Royal Marines officer who led the attack by a formation of <a href="/wiki/Blackburn_Skua" title="Blackburn Skua">Blackburn Skuas</a> that sank the <a href="/wiki/German_cruiser_K%C3%B6nigsberg" title="German cruiser KÃ¶nigsberg">KÃ¶nigsberg</a>. Eighteen Royal Marines commanded <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Air_Arm" title="Fleet Air Arm">Fleet Air Arm</a> squadrons during the course of the war, and with the formation of the <a href="/wiki/British_Pacific_Fleet" title="British Pacific Fleet">British Pacific Fleet</a> were well-represented in the final drive on Japan. Captains and Majors generally commanded squadrons, whilst in one case Lt. Colonel R.C. Hay on <a href="/wiki/HMS_Indefatigable_(R10)" title="HMS Indefatigable (R10)">HMS <i>Indefatigable</i></a> was Air Group Co-ordinator from <a href="/wiki/HMS_Victorious_(R38)" title="HMS Victorious (R38)">HMS <i>Victorious</i></a> of the entire <a href="/wiki/British_Pacific_Fleet" title="British Pacific Fleet">British Pacific Fleet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout the war Royal Marines continued in their traditional role of providing ships detachments and manning a proportion of the guns on <a href="/wiki/Cruiser" title="Cruiser">Cruisers</a> and Capital Ships. They also provided the crew for the UK's Minor <a href="/wiki/Landing_craft" title="Landing craft">Landing craft</a> and the Royal Marines Armoured Support Group manned <a href="/wiki/Cromwell_tank" title="Cromwell tank">Centaur IV</a> tanks on <a href="/wiki/Normandy_landings" title="Normandy landings">D Day</a> one of these is still on display at <a href="/wiki/Pegasus_Bridge" title="Pegasus Bridge">Pegasus Bridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup></p>
<p>Only one Marine (<a href="/wiki/Corporal" title="Corporal">Corporal</a> <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hunter_(VC)" title="Tom Hunter (VC)">Thomas Peck Hunter</a> of 43 Commando) was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Cross" title="Victoria Cross">Victoria Cross</a> in the Second World War for action at Lake Comacchio in <a href="/wiki/Italian_Campaign_(World_War_II)" title="Italian Campaign (World War II)">Italy</a>. Hunter was the most recent RM Commando to be awarded the medal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cross_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-cross-15">[14]</a></sup> The Royal Marines Boom Patrol Detachment under Blondie Haslar carried out <a href="/wiki/Operation_Frankton" title="Operation Frankton">Operation Frankton</a> and provided the basis for the post-war continuation of the <a href="/wiki/Special_Boat_Service" title="Special Boat Service">SBS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-colonial_era">Post-colonial era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-colonial era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Corps underwent a notable change after 1945 however, when the Royal Marines took on the main responsibility for the role and training of the <a href="/wiki/British_Commandos" title="British Commandos">British Commandos</a>. The Royal Marines have an illustrious history, and since their creation in 1942 Royal Marines Commandos have engaged on active operations across the globe, every year, except 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> Notably they were the first ever military unit to perform an air assault insertion by helicopter, during the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Crisis" title="Suez Crisis">Suez Crisis</a> in 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> They were also part of the land element during the 1982 <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personnel">Personnel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personnel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The Royal Marines are part of the Naval Service and under the full command of <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Commander" title="Fleet Commander">Fleet Commander</a>. The rank structure of the corps is similar to that of the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> with officers and other ranks recruited and initially trained separately from other naval personnel. Since 2017 women have been able to serve in all roles in the Royal Marines. On average, 1,200 recruits and 2,000 potential recruits, and 400 potential officers attend training courses and acquaint courses at CTCRM every year.</p>
<p>At its height in 1944 during the Second World War, more than 70,000 people served in the Royal Marines. Following the Allied victory the Royal Marines were quickly reduced to a post-war strength of 13,000. When <a href="/wiki/Conscription_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Conscription in the United Kingdom">National Service</a> finally came to an end in 1960, the Marines were again reduced, but this time to an all <a href="/wiki/British_Commandos" title="British Commandos">Commando-trained</a> force of 9,000 personnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> As of October 2014 the Royal Marines had a strength of 7,760 Regular<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> and 750 <a href="/wiki/Royal_Marines_Reserve" title="Royal Marines Reserve">Royal Marines Reserve</a>, giving a combined component strength of around 8,510 personnel. The Royal Marines are the only European marine force capable of conducting amphibious operations at <a href="/wiki/Brigade" title="Brigade">brigade</a> level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sdsr_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-sdsr-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Infantry</b> The basic infantry weapon of the Royal Marines is the <a href="/wiki/SA80#L85_Rifle" title="SA80">L85A2</a> assault rifle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rafpdf1_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-rafpdf1-34">[33]</a></sup> sometimes fitted with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heckler_%26_Koch_AG-C/GLM#Users" title="Heckler &amp; Koch AG-C/GLM">L123A3</a> underslung grenade launcher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rafpdf2_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-rafpdf2-35">[34]</a></sup> Support fire is provided by the <a href="/wiki/FN_Minimi" title="FN Minimi">L110A1</a> light machine gun,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rafpdf2_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-rafpdf2-35">[34]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L7_(machine_gun)" title="L7 (machine gun)">L7A2 General Purpose Machine Gun</a> (GPMG)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rafpdf3_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-rafpdf3-36">[35]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M2_Browning_machine_gun" title="M2 Browning machine gun">L111A1</a> heavy machine gun<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rafpdf4_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-rafpdf4-37">[36]</a></sup> (which is often mounted on an armoured vehicle); indirect fire by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L16_81mm_Mortar" title="L16 81mm Mortar">L16A2 81mm mortar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rafpdf4_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-rafpdf4-37">[36]</a></sup> Sniper rifles used include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L115A3" title="L115A3">L115A3</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rafpdf3_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-rafpdf3-36">[35]</a></sup> produced by <a href="/wiki/Accuracy_International" title="Accuracy International">Accuracy International</a>. More recently the <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Machine_and_Tool_Company#Notable_products" title="Lewis Machine and Tool Company">L129A1</a> has come into service as the <a href="/wiki/Designated_marksman_rifle" title="Designated marksman rifle">designated marksman rifle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rafpdf2_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-rafpdf2-35">[34]</a></sup> Other weapons include the <a href="/wiki/FGM-148_Javelin" title="FGM-148 Javelin">Javelin Anti-Tank missile</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rafpdf5_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-rafpdf5-38">[37]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SIG_P226#P228.2FP229" title="SIG P226">L107A1 pistol</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rafpdf1_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-rafpdf1-34">[33]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glock_17#Users" title="Glock 17">L131A1 pistol</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rafpdf1_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-rafpdf1-34">[33]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fairbairn-Sykes_Fighting_Knife" title="Fairbairn-Sykes Fighting Knife">Fairbairn-Sykes Fighting Knife</a>.</p>
<p><b>Armour</b> The Royal Marines maintain no heavy armoured units, instead, they operate a fleet of lightly armoured and highly mobile vehicles intended for amphibious landings or rapid deployment. The primary <a href="/wiki/Armoured_fighting_vehicle" title="Armoured fighting vehicle">armoured fighting vehicle</a> operated by the Armoured Support Group is the <a href="/wiki/BvS_10" title="BvS 10">BvS 10 Viking</a> All Terrain Armoured Vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> Other, lighter vehicles include the <a href="/wiki/Land_Rover_Wolf" title="Land Rover Wolf">Land Rover Wolf</a> Armoured Patrol Vehicle, the <a href="/wiki/Jackal_(vehicle)" title="Jackal (vehicle)">Jackal (MWMIK)</a> Armoured Vehicle and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinzgauer_High_Mobility_All-Terrain_Vehicle" title="Pinzgauer High Mobility All-Terrain Vehicle">Pinzgauer</a> High Mobility All Terrain Vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>Artillery</b> Field artillery support is provided by <a href="/wiki/29th_Commando_Regiment_Royal_Artillery" title="29th Commando Regiment Royal Artillery">29th Commando Regiment Royal Artillery</a> of the British Army using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L118_Light_Gun" title="L118 Light Gun">L118 Light Gun</a>, a 105Â mm towed <a href="/wiki/Howitzer" title="Howitzer">howitzer</a>. The regiment is <a href="/wiki/British_Commandos" title="British Commandos">Commando-trained</a>.</p>
<p><b>Aviation</b> The <a href="/wiki/Commando_Helicopter_Force" title="Commando Helicopter Force">Commando Helicopter Force</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Air_Arm" title="Fleet Air Arm">Fleet Air Arm</a> provides transport helicopters in support of the Royal Marines. It currently uses both <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_AW101" title="AgustaWestland AW101">Merlin HC4/4A</a> medium-lift transport and <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_AW159_Wildcat" title="AgustaWestland AW159 Wildcat">Wildcat AH1</a> attack <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a> to provide direct aviation support for the Corps. In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> provides <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Chinook_(UK_variants)" title="Boeing Chinook (UK variants)">Chinook</a> heavy-lift and <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9rospatiale_SA_330_Puma" title="AÃ©rospatiale SA 330 Puma">Puma HC2</a> medium-lift transport helicopters.</p>
<p><b>Vessels</b> The Royal Marines operate a varied fleet of <a href="/wiki/List_of_active_Royal_Marines_military_watercraft" title="List of active Royal Marines military watercraft">military watercraft</a> designed to transport troops and material from ship to shore or conduct river or estuary patrols. These include the 2000TDX <a href="/wiki/LCAC_(United_Kingdom)" title="LCAC (United Kingdom)">Landing Craft Air Cushion</a>, the Mk10 <a href="/wiki/Landing_Craft_Utility#United_Kingdom" title="Landing Craft Utility">Landing Craft Utility</a>, the <a href="/wiki/LCVP_(United_Kingdom)#LCVP_Mk5" title="LCVP (United Kingdom)">Mk5 Landing Craft Vehicle Personnel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/SEAL_Delivery_Vehicle" title="SEAL Delivery Vehicle">SDV Mk8 Mod 1</a> <a href="/wiki/Swimmer_Delivery_Vehicle" title="Swimmer Delivery Vehicle">Swimmer Delivery Vehicle</a> for special forces. Other smaller amphibious craft such as the <a href="/wiki/Offshore_Raiding_Craft" title="Offshore Raiding Craft">Offshore Raiding Craft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rigid_Raider" title="Rigid Raider">Rigid Raider</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inflatable_Raiding_Craft" title="Inflatable Raiding Craft">Inflatable Raiding Craft</a> are in service in much greater numbers.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_structure">Formation and structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Formation and structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The overall head of the Royal Marines is <a href="/wiki/Majesty" title="Majesty">Her Majesty</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a>, in her role as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commander-in-Chief" title="Commander-in-Chief">Commander-in-Chief</a> of the <a href="/wiki/British_Armed_Forces" title="British Armed Forces">British Armed Forces</a>. The ceremonial head of the Royal Marines is the <a href="/wiki/Captain_General_Royal_Marines" title="Captain General Royal Marines">Captain General Royal Marines</a> (equivalent to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonel-in-Chief" title="Colonel-in-Chief">Colonel-in-Chief</a> of a <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> regiment). The current Captain-General is <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh</a>. Full Command of the Royal Marines is vested in the <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Commander" title="Fleet Commander">Fleet Commander</a> (FLTCDR)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> with the <a href="/wiki/Commandant_General_Royal_Marines" title="Commandant General Royal Marines">Commandant General Royal Marines</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major-General" title="Major-General">Major-General</a>, embedded within the <a href="/wiki/Navy_Command_Headquarters" title="Navy Command Headquarters">Navy Command Headquarters</a> (NCHQ) as Commander UK Amphibious Force (COMUKAMPHIBFOR).</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="3_Commando_Brigade">3 Commando Brigade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 3 Commando Brigade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Operational Command of the five Commandos and the Commando Logistics Regiment is delegated to <a href="/wiki/3_Commando_Brigade" title="3 Commando Brigade">3 Commando Brigade Royal Marines</a>, of which they are a part. Based at <a href="/wiki/Stonehouse_Barracks" title="Stonehouse Barracks">Stonehouse Barracks</a>, the brigade exercises control as directed by either CINCFLEET or the <a href="/wiki/Permanent_Joint_Headquarters" title="Permanent Joint Headquarters">Permanent Joint Headquarters</a>. As the main combat formation of the Royal Marines, the brigade has its own organic capability to it in the field, 30 Commando Information Exploitation Group, a battalion sized formation providing information operations capabilities, life support and security for the Brigade Headquarters.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/43_Commando_Fleet_Protection_Group_Royal_Marines" title="43 Commando Fleet Protection Group Royal Marines">43 Commando Fleet Protection Group Royal Marines</a>, responsible for the security of the United Kingdom's nuclear deterrent and other security-related duties was originally outside the brigade however from April 2012 it moved into it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-navynews.co.uk_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-navynews.co.uk-44">[43]</a></sup> It also provides specialist boarding parties and snipers for the Royal Navy worldwide, for roles such as embargo enforcement, counter-narcotics, counter-piracy and counter-insurgency activities of the Royal Navy. It is the largest unit in the brigade at 790 strong<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-navynews.co.uk_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-navynews.co.uk-44">[43]</a></sup> with a different structure from the other Commandos.</p>

<p>In general a rifle company Marine will be a member of a four-man <a href="/wiki/Fireteam" title="Fireteam">fire team</a>, the building block of commando operations. This structure is a recent development, formerly Commandos were structured similarly to British Army light Infantry <a href="/wiki/Battalion" title="Battalion">Battalions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> During the restructuring of the United Kingdom's military services the Corps evolved from a <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> focus on NATO's Northern Flank towards a more expeditionary posture.</p>

<p>Formerly known as the Amphibious Ready Group, the Amphibious Task Group (or ATG) is a mobile, balanced <a href="/wiki/Amphibious_warfare" title="Amphibious warfare">amphibious warfare</a> force, based on a Commando Group and its supporting assets, that can be kept at high readiness to deploy into an area of operations. The ATG is normally based around specialist amphibious ships, most notably <a href="/wiki/HMS_Ocean_(L12)" title="HMS Ocean (L12)">HMSÂ <i>Ocean</i></a>, the largest ship in the British fleet. <i>Ocean</i> was designed and built to accommodate an embarked commando and its associated stores and equipment. The strategy of the ATG is to wait "beyond the horizon" and then deploy swiftly as directed by HM Government. The whole amphibious force is intended to be self-sustaining and capable of operating without host-nation support. The concept was successfully tested in operations in <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commando_Helicopter_Force">Commando Helicopter Force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Commando Helicopter Force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Commando_Helicopter_Force" title="Commando Helicopter Force">Commando Helicopter Force</a> (CHF) forms part of the <a href="/wiki/Fleet_Air_Arm" title="Fleet Air Arm">Fleet Air Arm</a>. It comprises three helicopter squadrons and is commanded by the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Helicopter_Command" title="Joint Helicopter Command">Joint Helicopter Command</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> It consists of both <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> (RN) and Royal Marines personnel. RN personnel need not be commando trained. The CHF is neither under the permanent control of <a href="/wiki/3_Commando_Brigade" title="3 Commando Brigade">3 Commando Brigade</a> nor that of the Commandant General Royal Marines, but rather is allocated to support Royal Marines units as required. It uses both <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_AW101" title="AgustaWestland AW101">Merlin HC4/4A</a> medium-lift and <a href="/wiki/AgustaWestland_AW159_Wildcat" title="AgustaWestland AW159 Wildcat">Wildcat AH1</a> light transport/reconnaissance <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopters</a> to provide aviation support for the Royal Marines.</p>

<p>Royal Marines are required to undergo one of the longest and most physically demanding specialist infantry training regimes in the world. Recruit training lasts for 32 weeks for Marines and 60 weeks for officers. Potential recruits must be male and aged 16 to 32 (18 to 25 for Commissioned Officers);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup> however by the end of 2018 women will be permitted to apply after the ban on women in Ground Close Combat roles was lifted in July 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> and they must first undertake a series of interviews, medical tests, an eye/sight test, psychometric tests and a PJFT (Pre-joining fitness test).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-52">[51]</a></sup> Once a potential recruit passes these, enlisted recruits undertake a 3-day selection course called PRMC (Potential Royal Marine Course) and potential officers undertake POC (Potential Officer Course) â both take place at the <a href="/wiki/Commando_Training_Centre_Royal_Marines" title="Commando Training Centre Royal Marines">Commando Training Centre Royal Marines</a> (CTCRM) in <a href="/wiki/Lympstone" title="Lympstone">Lympstone</a>, Devon. Officers must also take the <a href="/wiki/Admiralty_Interview_Board" title="Admiralty Interview Board">Admiralty Interview Board</a> (AIB).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup> Upon passing the 3-day course, recruits then start basic recruit training (RT) at CTCRM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-52">[51]</a></sup> Unlike in many countries, enlisted Marines and officer Marines often train together for the first 32 weeks. A large proportion of training is carried out on <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a>'s inhospitable terrain and <a href="/wiki/Woodbury_Common,_Devon" title="Woodbury Common, Devon">Woodbury Common</a> woodland. The culmination of their training ends with their infamous commando courses which they initially pre-train for. The commando courses are a series of physical and mental endurance tests that highlight their military professionalism.</p>
<p>Throughout the recruit training, Royal Marines learn and develop many military skills such as weapons handling, marksmanship and proficiency with different firearms, personal administration, marching and parade ground skills, map reading and navigation, physical fitness and mental toughness development, <a href="/wiki/Fieldcraft" title="Fieldcraft">fieldcraft</a> skills such as camouflage and stalking, basic survival techniques, patrolling and sentry duty development, unarmed and armed <a href="/wiki/Close_combat" title="Close combat">close quarters combat</a> (CQC), first aid, underwater escape, chemical biological radiological nuclear (CBRN) training, military communications and signals, teamwork skills, amphibious landings training, and leadership skills for officers to name a few.</p>
<p>The best recruit to finish training is awarded the Kings Badge. King George V directed that his Royal Cypher, surrounded by a laurel wreath, would be known as the King's Badge, and would be awarded to the best all round recruit in the King's Squad, provided that he was worthy of the honour. The badge was to be carried on the left shoulder, and worn in every rank. The King's Badge is not awarded to every squad, and is only presented if a recruit measures up to the very exacting standards required.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout his career, a Marine can specialise in a number of different roles upon completion of their respective courses after spending 1â2 years as a general duties (GD) Marine. Examples of some specialisations and different courses includes the mountain leader (ML), physical training instructor (PTI), Assault Engineer (AE), military police (MP), sniper course, medical assistant, pilot, reconnaissance operator (RO), drill instructor, driver, clerk, chef, signaller, combat intelligence, armourer, and heavy weapons training. Royal Marines can also apply for swimmer canoeist/<a href="/wiki/Special_Boat_Service" title="Special Boat Service">Special Boat Service</a> selection (SBS) or any other branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UKSF" title="UKSF">UKSF</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup> All Royal Marines will also conduct training exercises on differing military skills on a regular basis including development in mountain, arctic, jungle, amphibious and desert warfare. They can also be involved in exchange training programs with other countries forces â particularly the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mod.uk_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-mod.uk-4">[3]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Marine_Corps" title="Netherlands Marine Corps">Netherlands Marine Corps</a>/Korps Mariniers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royalnavy.mod.uk_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-royalnavy.mod.uk-5">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Customs_and_traditions">Customs and traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Customs and traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Royal Marines have a proud history and unique traditions. With the exceptions of "Gibraltar" and the laurel wreath for the Battle of Belle Island, their colours (flags) do not carry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_honours" title="Battle honours">battle honours</a> in the manner of the regiments of the British Army or of the US Marine Corps, but rather the "globe itself" as a symbol of the Corps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nato2009_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-nato2009-56">[55]</a></sup></p>

<p>The heraldic crest of the Royal Marines commemorates the history of the Corps. The Lion and Crown denotes a Royal regiment. <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">King George III</a> conferred this honour in 1802 "in consideration of the very meritorious services of the Marines in the late war." The "Great Globe itself" was chosen in 1827 by <a href="/wiki/George_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George IV of the United Kingdom">King George IV</a> in place of <a href="/wiki/Battle_honour" title="Battle honour">Battle honours</a> to recognise the Marines' service and successes in multiple engagements in every quarter of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crest_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-crest-11">[10]</a></sup> The laurels are believed to honour the gallantry they displayed during the investment and capture of <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Belle_%C3%8Ele" title="Capture of Belle Ãle">Belle Isle</a>, off <a href="/wiki/Lorient" title="Lorient">Lorient</a>, in AprilâJune 1761. The word <i><a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a></i> refers to the <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Gibraltar" title="Capture of Gibraltar">Capture of Gibraltar</a> by a force of Anglo-Dutch Marines in 1704 and the subsequent defence of the strategic fortress throughout a nine-month <a href="/wiki/Twelfth_Siege_of_Gibraltar" title="Twelfth Siege of Gibraltar">siege</a> against a numerically superior Franco-Spanish force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crest_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-crest-11">[10]</a></sup> Their determination and valour throughout the siege led to a contemporary report published in <i>The Triumphs of Her Majesty's Arms</i> in 1707 to announce:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Encouraged by the Prince of Hesse, the garrison did more than could humanly be expected, and the English Marines gained an immortal glory</p>
<div class="templatequotecite"><cite>ââreferred to by Paul Harris Nicolas, Historical record of the Royal marine forces<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>There are no other battle honours displayed on the <a href="/wiki/Colours,_standards_and_guidons" title="Colours, standards and guidons">colours</a> of the four battalion-sized units of the current Corps. The Latin motto "Per Mare Per Terram" translates into English as "By Sea By Land". Believed to have been first used in 1775 this motto describes the Royal Marines ability in fighting both afloat on-board ships of the Royal Navy, as well as ashore in their many land engagements. The fouled anchor, incorporated into the emblem in 1747, is the badge of the <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Lord High Admiral</a> and shows that the Corps is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Service_(United_Kingdom)" title="Naval Service (United Kingdom)">Naval Service</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nato2009_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-nato2009-56">[55]</a></sup></p>
<p>The regimental quick march of the Corps is "<a href="/wiki/A_Life_on_the_Ocean_Wave" title="A Life on the Ocean Wave">A Life on the Ocean Wave</a>", while the slow march is the <a href="/wiki/Preobrazhensky_Regiment#March" title="Preobrazhensky Regiment">march</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Preobrazhensky_Regiment" title="Preobrazhensky Regiment">Preobrazhensky Regiment</a>, awarded to the Corps by Admiral of the Fleet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earl_Mountbatten" title="Earl Mountbatten">Earl Mountbatten</a> of Burma on the occasion of the Corps's tercentenary in 1964. Lord Mountbatten was Life Colonel Commandant of the Royal Marines until his murder by the IRA in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup></p>

<p>The Royal Marines are allowed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor_of_the_City_of_London" title="Lord Mayor of the City of London">Lord Mayor of the City of London</a> to march through the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City</a> as a regiment in full array. This dates to the charter of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> that allowed recruiting parties of the Admiral's Regiment of 1664 to enter the City with drums beating and colours flying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uniforms">Uniforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Uniforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The modern Royal Marines retain a number of distinctive uniform items. These include the green "Lovat" service dress worn with the <a href="/wiki/Green_beret" title="Green beret">green beret</a>, the dark blue parade dress worn with either the white Wolseley Pattern Helmet (commonly referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Pith_helmet" title="Pith helmet">pith helmet</a>") or white and red peaked cap, the scarlet and blue <a href="/wiki/Mess_dress" title="Mess dress">mess dress</a> for officers and senior non-commissioned officers and the white hot-weather uniform of the Band Service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup></p>
<p>For historical information regarding Marine uniforms, see <i><a href="/wiki/Uniforms_of_the_Royal_Marines" title="Uniforms of the Royal Marines">Uniforms of the Royal Marines</a></i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Order_of_precedence">Order of precedence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Order of precedence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the descendant of the old marine regiments of the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>, the Royal Marines used to have a position in the <a href="/wiki/British_Army_order_of_precedence" title="British Army order of precedence">order of precedence</a> of the infantry; this was after the 49th Regiment of Foot, the final lineal descendant of which was the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Gloucestershire,_Berkshire_and_Wiltshire_Regiment" title="Royal Gloucestershire, Berkshire and Wiltshire Regiment">Royal Gloucestershire, Berkshire and Wiltshire Regiment</a> (RGBW). Therefore, the Royal Marines would have paraded after the RGBW. This is because the 49th Foot was the last regiment raised prior to the formation of the Corps of Marines as part of the Royal Navy in 1755. In 2007, the RGBW was amalgamated into a large regiment â this new regiment is placed last in the order of precedence, as it is a regiment of rifles. However, as a result of new Army amalgamations the Royal Marines have now been removed from the infantry order of precedence and now always take post, as a constituent part of the Royal Navy (the Senior Service), at the head of the parade alongside the Navy, or alone if the Navy are not represented. Thus, if only the infantry is represented, the Royal Marines would parade before the <a href="/wiki/Grenadier_Guards" title="Grenadier Guards">Grenadier Guards</a>, the senior infantry regiment of the Army.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Affiliations">Affiliations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Marines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Affiliations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Argyll and Sutherland Highlanders</b></p>
<p>Early connections date from Balaclava in the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a> and Lucknow during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Mutiny" title="Indian Mutiny">Indian Mutiny</a>, but the main association stems from World War II. In July 1940, after the fall of Dunkirk, the 5th Battalion, <a href="/wiki/Argyll_and_Sutherland_Highlanders" title="Argyll and Sutherland Highlanders">Argyll and Sutherland Highlanders</a> served with the Royal Marine Brigade for over a year. When the battleships <a href="/wiki/HMS_Prince_of_Wales_(53)" title="HMS Prince of Wales (53)">HMSÂ <i>Prince of Wales</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/HMS_Repulse_(1916)" title="HMS Repulse (1916)">HMSÂ <i>Repulse</i></a> were <a href="/wiki/Sinking_of_Prince_of_Wales_and_Repulse" title="Sinking of Prince of Wales and Repulse">sunk in December 1941</a>, the Royal Marines survivors joined up with the remnants of the 2nd Battalion, in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fall_of_Singapore" title="Fall of Singapore">defence of Singapore</a>. They formed what became known as 'The Plymouth Argylls', after the association football team, since both ships were Plymouth manned. Most of the Highlanders and Marines who survived the bitter fighting were taken prisoner by the Japanese. The Royal Marines inter-unit rugby football trophy is the 'Argyll Bowl', presented to the Corps by the Regiment in 1941. A message of greetings is sent to the Regiment each year on their Regimental Day, 25 October, the anniversary of the Battle of Balaclava in 1854.</p>
<p><b>The Princess of Wales's Royal Regiment</b></p>
<p>The fore-bearer regiments of the <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Princess_of_Wales%27s_Royal_Regiment" title="The Princess of Wales's Royal Regiment">The Princess of Wales's Royal Regiment</a></b>, The East Surrey Regiment (Villier's Marines) was initially raised as amphibious troops. They served as Marines for a period. To this day one officer from the Royal Marines serves with the PWRR and Vice Versa. Also the Royal Marine Lanyard is worn by all ranks in Service Dress and Number 2 Dress uniform and barrack dress of PWRR</p>
<p><b>Netherlands Marine Corps</b></p>
<p>The Royal Marines have close links with the Royal <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Marine_Corps" title="Netherlands Marine Corps">Netherlands Marine Corps</a>, with whom they conduct NATO exercises throughout the year. Formed during the Anglo-Dutch Wars in 1665, the Dutch Marines distinguished themselves in raids on the English coast, where it is likely they met their future counterparts. Units of the Royal Netherlands Marine Corps work in close co-operation with 3 Commando Brigade of the Royal Marines. Operational units of the Royal Netherlands Marine Corps are fully integrated into this brigade. This integration is known as the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Marine_Corps#UK.2FNL_Landing_Force" title="Netherlands Marine Corps">United Kingdom-Netherlands Landing Force</a> and is a component of the United Kingdom-Netherlands Amphibious Force as a key strike force during the Cold War to strengthen the Nordic area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup></p>
