<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">French and Indian Wars</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="hatnote" role="note">Not to be confused with the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a>, the name given to the fourth conflict of the following wars.</div>
<p>The <b>French and Indian Wars</b> is a name used in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> for a series of intermittent conflicts between the years 1688 and 1763 in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> that represented colonial events related to the European dynastic wars. The title <i>French and Indian War</i>, in the singular, is used in the United States specifically for the warfare of 1754–1763, the North American colonial counterpart to the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a> in Europe. The French and Indian Wars were preceded by the <a href="/wiki/Beaver_Wars" title="Beaver Wars">Beaver Wars</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, a former French colony, the wars are generally referred to as the War of the Conquest. While some conflicts involved <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> forces, all pitted the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a>, its <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">colonies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native American</a> allies on one side against <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, its colonies and Native American allies on the other.</p>
<p>A major cause of the wars was the desire of each country to take control of the interior territories of North America, as well as the region around <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a>; both were deemed essential to domination of the <a href="/wiki/Fur_trade" title="Fur trade">fur trade</a>. Whenever the European countries went to war, military conflict also occurred in North America in their colonies, although the dates of the conflicts did not necessarily exactly coincide with those of the larger conflicts.</p>

<p>The naming of conflicts after the British monarch of the day is a convention in United States history related to its early European settlement as majority-English colonies. Canadian convention uses the name of the larger European conflict (e.g. the "War of the Grand Alliance" rather than "King William's War") or refers to the wars as the Intercolonial Wars.</p>
<p>As the wars proceeded, the military advantage moved toward the British side. This was chiefly the result of the greater population and productive capacity of the British colonies, compared with those of France. In addition, the British had the greater ability to resupply their colonies and project military power by sea. In the first three conflicts, the French were able to offset these factors largely by more effective <a href="/wiki/Mobilization" title="Mobilization">mobilization</a> of Native American allies, but they were finally overwhelmed in the fourth and last war.</p>
<p>The overwhelming victory of the British played a role in eventual loss of their thirteen American colonies. Without the threat of French invasion, the American colonies saw little need for British military protection. In addition, the people resented British efforts to limit their colonization of the new French territories to the west of the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a>, as stated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proclamation_of_1763" title="Proclamation of 1763">Proclamation of 1763</a>, in an effort to relieve encroachment on Native American territory. These pressures contributed to the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>.</p>
<p>The first three of the French and Indian Wars followed the same basic pattern: they all started in Europe and then moved to North America. Once the conflict broke out in North America, it was mostly fought by colonial militias'. The final conflict broke this pattern by beginning in North America. In addition, the British used more regular troops alongside colonial militia. They returned almost none of the French territory seized during the war. France was forced to cede its extensive territory in present-day Canada and <i>Louisiane</i>. The British victory in the French and Indian Wars reduced France's New World empire to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Pierre_and_Miquelon" title="St. Pierre and Miquelon">St. Pierre and Miquelon</a>, two islands off <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>; a few <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indian</a> islands; and <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>.</p>
