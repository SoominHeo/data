<p><b>British South American Airways</b> (BSAA) was a state-run airline in the United Kingdom in the late 1940s responsible for services to the Caribbean and South America. Originally named <b>British Latin American Air Lines</b> it was renamed before services started in 1946. BSAA operated mostly <a href="/wiki/Avro" title="Avro">Avro</a> aircraft: <a href="/wiki/Avro_York" title="Avro York">Yorks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancastrian" title="Avro Lancastrian">Lancastrians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avro_Tudor" title="Avro Tudor">Tudors</a> and flew to <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>, Mexico and the western coast of South America. After two high-profile aircraft disappearances it was merged into the <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Airways_Corporation" title="British Overseas Airways Corporation">British Overseas Airways Corporation</a> at the end of 1949.</p>
<p>Most of BSAA's aircraft were given individual aircraft names beginning with "<a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">Star</a>", the heavenly bodies used in long-range <a href="/wiki/Celestial_navigation" title="Celestial navigation">celestial navigation</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_South_American_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><big><b>1945</b></big></p>
<p>British Latin American Air Lines (BLAIR) was formed on 25 January 1944 by shipping interests (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Mail_Lines" title="Royal Mail Lines">Royal Mail Lines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Steam_Navigation_Company" title="Pacific Steam Navigation Company">Pacific Steam Navigation Company</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lamport_%26_Holt_Line&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lamport &amp; Holt Line (page does not exist)">Lamport &amp; Holt Line</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Booth_Steamship_Company" title="Booth Steamship Company">Booth Steamship Company</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blue_Star_Line" title="Blue Star Line">Blue Star Line</a>) to complement the shipping services to South America, at the end of 1945 the company was renamed British South American Airways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Chairman of the new company was W J Booth. The initial aircraft - until the Avro Tudor II was available - would be <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancaster" title="Avro Lancaster">Avro Lancasters</a> converted by Avro into the same configuration as Lancastrians and the crews were being sought from former <a href="/wiki/Pathfinder_(RAF)" title="Pathfinder (RAF)">Pathfinder Force</a> members: the general manager <a href="/wiki/Don_Bennett" title="Don Bennett">Don Bennett</a> had been the force commander during the war. The single route to be flown was Hurn- Lisbon-Bathurst-Natal-Rio de Janeiro-Montevideo-Buenos Aires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p><big><b>1946</b></big></p>
<p>On 1 January 1946 the airlines first <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancastrian" title="Avro Lancastrian">Avro Lancastrian</a> <i>Star Light</i> flown by Don Bennett and R. Clifford Alabaster undertook the first flight from the newly opened Heathrow Airport, it was on a proving flight to South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The first commercial flight followed ten weeks later.</p>
<p>With the approaching nationalisation of British airlines, the airline came under the control of the <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Airways_Corporation" title="British Overseas Airways Corporation">British Overseas Airways Corporation</a> and with the passing of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Aviation_Act_1946&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Civil Aviation Act 1946 (page does not exist)">Civil Aviation Act 1946</a> - which set up three nationally owned corporations - BSAA became a government-owned corporation on 1 August 1946 charged with developing services from the UK to South America. This responsibility was then expanded later in 1946 to routes to the West Indies, Central America and the west coast of South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><big><b>1947</b></big></p>
<p>In January 1947 the airline reached an agreement with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_West_Indian_Airways" title="British West Indian Airways">British West Indian Airways</a> which would become an associate. BSAA would buy the majority of shares in BWIA and provide technical advice and general supervision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In May 1947 the airline started a series of test flights to Bermuda using a converted <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancaster" title="Avro Lancaster">Avro Lancaster</a> which was refuelled in mid-air over the Azores to complete the flight in 20 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On 2 August 1947 <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancastrian" title="Avro Lancastrian">Avro Lancastrian</a> <i>Star Dust</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BSAA_Star_Dust_accident" title="BSAA Star Dust accident">crashed in Argentina</a> with the loss of all on board.</p>
<p>In the first financial year (August 1946-March 1947) under government control the airline made a surplus of £20,507. The two other airline corporations BOAC and BEA made a combined loss of £10,234,781.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-profit_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-profit-7">[7]</a></sup> On 31 March 1947 the corporation had a staff of 1,031 and had carried 5,397 passengers since August 1946.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-profit_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-profit-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>For navigation purposes, the Lancastrians and Yorks were using military <a href="/wiki/Gee_(navigation)" title="Gee (navigation)">Gee</a> radar over Europe, and <a href="/wiki/Rebecca/Eureka_transponding_radar" title="Rebecca/Eureka transponding radar">Rebecca</a> on the other side of the Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><big><b>1948</b></big></p>
<p>On 30 January 1948 Avro Tudor <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_Tiger" title="Star Tiger">Star Tiger</a></i> with a crew of six and 25 passengers bound for Bermuda <a href="/wiki/BSAA_Star_Tiger_disappearance" title="BSAA Star Tiger disappearance">disappeared over the Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The remaining Tudors were grounded while an investigation was undertaken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In February the chief executive, Don Bennett was dismissed by the board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In March the Tudor aircraft were allowed to fly initially as freighters but not to carry passengers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In the financial year April 1947 to March 1948 the Corporation made a loss of £421,481.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 18 August the Tudor returned to passenger service with a new service to Kingston, Jamaica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In December the Tudors replaced the Avro Lancastrian on routes to Havana, Cuba and the west coast of South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>From September 1948 the airline based <a href="/wiki/Avro_Tudor" title="Avro Tudor">Avro Tudors</a> at Wunstorf to support the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berlin_Airlift" title="Berlin Airlift">Berlin Airlift</a> mainly by each carrying 2,300 gallons of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrol" title="Petrol">Petrol</a> or 2,100 gallons of fuel oil, by April 1949 the airline had five aircraft operating the Air Lift.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> By December 1948 the airline had transported over 1,000,000 gallons of petrol into Berlin from Wunstorf in over 700 flights using mostly Tudor V tankers each fitted with five tanks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p><big><b>1949</b></big></p>
<p>In January 1949 the airline acquired <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bahamas_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bahamas Airways (page does not exist)">Bahamas Airways</a>. With <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_West_Indian_Airways" title="British West Indian Airways">British West Indian Airways</a> it would be used as a feeder airline for BSAA services in the Caribbean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<p>On 17 January in a repeat of <i>Star Tiger</i> incident the Tudor <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_Ariel" title="Star Ariel">Star Ariel</a></i> <a href="/wiki/BSAA_Star_Ariel_disappearance" title="BSAA Star Ariel disappearance">disappeared over the Atlantic</a> on a flight from Bermuda. It had seven crew and 13 passengers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The Tudors were withdrawn from service by the airline pending investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>By March 1949 with the loss of the <i>Star Ariel</i> unexplained, the permanent grounding of the Tudor IVs for passenger flying, and the lack of other long-range aircraft, the government proposed amalgamating the airline with BOAC. BSAA passengers to Bermuda were already being carried by BOAC aircraft via New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-integrate_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-integrate-21">[21]</a></sup> The airline did have <a href="/wiki/Saunders-Roe_Princess" title="Saunders-Roe Princess">Saunders-Roe Princess</a> flying-boats on order but they would not be delivered until 1951 and the transfer of <a href="/wiki/Canadair_North_Star" title="Canadair North Star">Canadair North Star</a> which were on order for BOAC was considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-integrate_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-integrate-21">[21]</a></sup> On 15 March the Minister of Civil Aviation announced that BSAA and BOAC would be amalgamated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>
<p>On the passing of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Air_Corporations_Act_1949&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Air Corporations Act 1949 (page does not exist)">Air Corporations Act 1949</a>, British South American Airways Corporation became the South American Division of BOAC, the change became effective from 1 January 1950.</p>

<p>The airline used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langley_Airfield" title="Langley Airfield">Langley Airfield</a> for maintenance before transferring all operations to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Airport" title="London Heathrow Airport">London Heathrow Airport</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Star_Dust"><i>Star Dust</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_South_American_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Star Dust">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1947_BSAA_Avro_Lancastrian_Star_Dust_accident" title="1947 BSAA Avro Lancastrian Star Dust accident">1947 BSAA Avro Lancastrian Star Dust accident</a></div>
<p>On 2 August 1947 the <a href="/wiki/1947_BSAA_Avro_Lancastrian_Star_Dust_accident" title="1947 BSAA Avro Lancastrian Star Dust accident">Avro Lancastrian</a> "<a href="/wiki/1947_BSAA_Avro_Lancastrian_Star_Dust_accident" title="1947 BSAA Avro Lancastrian Star Dust accident">Star Dust</a>" disappeared on a flight from Buenos Aires, Argentina to Santiago, Chile; fifty years later remains of the aircraft were found to have crashed into an Argentine mountain.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Star_Tiger_and_Star_Ariel"><i>Star Tiger</i> and <i>Star Ariel</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_South_American_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Star Tiger and Star Ariel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Star Tiger</i> and <i>Star Ariel</i> were <a href="/wiki/Avro_Tudor" title="Avro Tudor">Avro Tudor IV</a> aircraft lost over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a>. The loss of each without a trace, plus the unexplained disappearance of a <a href="/wiki/1948_Airborne_Transport_DC-3_(DST)_disappearance" title="1948 Airborne Transport DC-3 (DST) disappearance">DC-3</a> south of <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a> on 27 December 1948, and the loss of <a href="/wiki/Flight_19" title="Flight 19">Flight 19</a> on 5 December 1945, led to the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_Triangle" title="Bermuda Triangle">Bermuda Triangle</a> myth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p><big><i><b>Star Tiger</b></i></big></p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G-AHNP_%22Star_Tiger%22" title='G-AHNP "Star Tiger"'>G-AHNP "Star Tiger"</a></div>
<p>On 30 January 1948, <i>Star Tiger</i> was flying from England to Bermuda. It stopped for fuel in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>. Early on 31 January the captain asked for a bearing for Bermuda. The request was routine, and there was no cause for alarm. He then gave an estimated arrival at 05:00. That was the last contact. Azores to Bermuda is 2,230 miles (3,588 km). At 05:00 a <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue" title="Search and rescue">search</a> was launched from Bermuda but the aircraft was not found.</p>
<p><big><i><b>Star Ariel</b></i></big></p>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G-AGRE_%22Star_Ariel%22" title='G-AGRE "Star Ariel"'>G-AGRE "Star Ariel"</a></div>
<p><i>Star Ariel</i> G-AGRE left Bermuda for <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica" title="Kingston, Jamaica">Kingston, Jamaica</a> on 17 January 1949. Soon after take-off captain John McPhee radioed a standard departure message including an estimate at Kingston of 14:10. This was followed by a position report "I was over 30° N at 9:37 I am changing frequency to MRX." Star Ariel was never heard from again. Over 70 aircraft and many ships searched between up to 500 miles south of Bermuda, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aircraft_carriers" title="Aircraft carriers">aircraft carriers</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Kearsarge_(CV-33)" title="USS Kearsarge (CV-33)">USS <i>Kearsarge</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/USS_Leyte_(CV-32)" title="USS Leyte (CV-32)">USS <i>Leyte</i></a>, and the battleship <a href="/wiki/USS_Missouri_(BB-63)" title="USS Missouri (BB-63)">USS <i>Missouri</i></a>, involving more than 13,000 men. No debris, oil slicks, or wreckage were found. The Tudor IV was later discontinued.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_accidents_and_incidents">Further accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_South_American_Airways&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Further accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>7 September 1946: an <a href="/wiki/Avro_York" title="Avro York">Avro 685 York I</a> registration G-AHEW named "Star Leader" flying from <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> to <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lisbon_Portela_Airport" title="Lisbon Portela Airport">Lisbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banjul" title="Banjul">Bathurst (Banjul)-Jeshwang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Augusto_Severo_International_Airport" title="Augusto Severo International Airport">Natal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santos_Dumont_Airport" title="Santos Dumont Airport">Rio de Janeiro-Santos Dumont</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carrasco_International_Airport" title="Carrasco International Airport">Montevideo</a> lost control and crashed shortly after takeoff from Bathurst. The cause of the loss of control cannot be determined with certainty, but a mishandling of the controls by the captain is the most likely explanation. All 24 occupants died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>30 August 1946 G-AGWJ swung off the runway on landing and was written off</li>
<li>13 April 1947 G-AHEZ crash-landed killing 6 in poor visibility at Dakar Airport</li>
<li>5 January 1949 Avro York G-AHEX <i>Star Venture</i> suffered engine fire and crash landed killing 3 passengers</li>
<li>BSAA Avro York G-AHFA <i>Star Dale</i> was sold to Skyways suffered 2 February <a href="/wiki/1953_Skyways_Avro_York_disappearance" title="1953 Skyways Avro York disappearance">1953 Skyways Avro York disappearance</a></li>
</ul>
