<p><b>British Guiana</b> (also spelled <b>Guyana</b>) was the name of the British colony on the northern coast of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, since 1966 known as the independent nation of <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>. Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people" title="Indigenous people">indigenous people</a> are the <a href="/wiki/Arawak" title="Arawak">Arawak</a>-speaking <a href="/wiki/Lucayan_people" title="Lucayan people">Lucayan</a>, part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taino_people" title="Taino people">Taino people</a>.</p>
<p>The first European to discover Guiana was Sir <a href="/wiki/Walter_Raleigh" title="Walter Raleigh">Walter Raleigh</a>, an English explorer. The <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Empire" title="Dutch Empire">Dutch</a> were the first Europeans to settle there, starting in the early 17th century, when they founded the colonies of <a href="/wiki/Essequibo_(colony)" title="Essequibo (colony)">Essequibo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berbice" title="Berbice">Berbice</a>, adding <a href="/wiki/Demerara" title="Demerara">Demerara</a> in the mid-18th century. In 1796, Great Britain took over these three colonies during hostilities with the French, who had occupied the Netherlands. Britain returned control to the <a href="/wiki/Batavian_Republic" title="Batavian Republic">Batavian Republic</a> in 1802, but captured the colonies a year later during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>. The colonies were officially ceded to the United Kingdom in 1814, and consolidated into a single colony in 1831. The colony's capital was at <a href="/wiki/Georgetown,_Guyana" title="Georgetown, Guyana">Georgetown</a> (known as Stabroek prior to 1812).</p>
<p>As the British developed the colony for <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> <a href="/wiki/Sugar_plantations_in_the_Caribbean" title="Sugar plantations in the Caribbean">plantations</a>, they imported many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Africans" title="Africans">Africans</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave" title="Slave">slave</a> labour. The economy became more diversified since the late 19th century, but has relied on resource exploitation. Guyana became independent of the United Kingdom on 26 May 1966.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment">Establishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Guiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Establishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English had made at least two unsuccessful attempts in the 17th century to colonise the lands that would later be known as British Guiana, at which time the Dutch had established two colonies in the area: <a href="/wiki/Essequibo_(colony)" title="Essequibo (colony)">Essequibo</a>, administered by the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_West_India_Company" title="Dutch West India Company">Dutch West India Company</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Berbice" title="Berbice">Berbice</a>, administered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berbice_Association" title="Berbice Association">Berbice Association</a>. The Dutch West India Company founded a third colony, <a href="/wiki/Demerara" title="Demerara">Demerara</a>, in the mid-18th century. During the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">French Revolutionary Wars</a> of the late 18th century, when the Netherlands were occupied by the French, and Great Britain and France were at war, Britain took over the colony in 1796. A British expeditionary force was dispatched from its colony of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> to seize the colonies from the French-dominated <a href="/wiki/Batavian_Republic" title="Batavian Republic">Batavian Republic</a>. The colonies surrendered without a struggle. Initially very little changed, as the British agreed to allow the long-established laws of the colonies to remain in force.</p>
<p>In 1802 Britain returned the colonies to the Batavian Republic under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Amiens" title="Treaty of Amiens">Treaty of Amiens</a>. But, after resuming hostilities with France in the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> in 1803, Britain seized the colonies again less than a year later. The three colonies were officially ceded to the United Kingdom in the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Dutch_Treaty_of_1814" title="Anglo-Dutch Treaty of 1814">Anglo-Dutch Treaty of 1814</a>. The UK continued separate administration of the individual colonies until 1822, when the administration of Essequibo and Demerara was combined. In 1831, the administration Essequibo-Demerara and Berbice was combined, and the united colony became known as British Guiana.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Guiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The economy of British Guiana was completely based on sugarcane production until the 1880s, when falling cane sugar prices stimulated a shift toward rice farming, mining and forestry. However, sugarcane remained a significant part of the economy (in 1959 sugar still accounted for nearly 50% of exports). Under the Dutch, settlement and economic activity was concentrated around sugarcane plantations lying inland from the coast. Under the British, cane planting expanded to richer coastal lands, with greater coastline protection. Until the <a href="/wiki/Slavery_Abolition_Act_1833" title="Slavery Abolition Act 1833">abolition of slavery in the British Empire</a>, sugar planters depended almost exclusively on slave labour to produce sugar. Georgetown was the site of <a href="/wiki/Demerara_rebellion_of_1823" title="Demerara rebellion of 1823">a significant slave rebellion in 1823</a>.</p>

<p>In the 1880s gold and diamond deposits were discovered in British Guiana, but they did not produce significant revenue. <a href="/wiki/Bauxite" title="Bauxite">Bauxite</a> deposits proved more promising and would remain an important part of the economy. The colony did not develop any significant manufacturing industry, other than sugar factories, rice mills, <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">sawmills</a>, and certain small-scale industries (including a brewery, a soap factory, a biscuit factory and an oxygen-acetylene plant, among others).</p>
<p>The London-based <a href="/wiki/Booker_Group" title="Booker Group">Booker Group</a> of companies (Booker Brothers, McConnell &amp; Co., Ltd.) dominated the economy of British Guiana. The Bookers had owned sugar plantations in the colony since the early 19th century; by the end of the century they owned a majority of them; and by 1950 owned all but three. With the increasing success and wealth of the Booker Group, they expanded internationally, and diversified by investing in rum, pharmaceuticals, publishing, advertising, retail stores, timber, and petroleum, among other industries. The Booker Group became the largest employer in the colony, leading some to refer to it as "Booker's Guiana".</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railways">Railways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Guiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Railways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>British colonists built the first railway system in South America in British Guiana, 61 miles of standard gauge, from Georgetown to Rosignol, and 19 miles of 3'6" line between Vreeden Hoop and Parika; it opened in 1848. Several narrow-gauge lines were built to serve the sugar industry and others were built to serve the later mines.</p>
<p>In 1948, when the <a href="/wiki/Bermuda_Railway" title="Bermuda Railway">railway in Bermuda</a> was closed down, the locomotives, rolling stock, track, sleepers and virtually all the associated paraphernalia of a railway were shipped to British Guiana to renovate the aged system.</p>
<p>The lines ceased to operate in 1972, but the large Central Station is still standing in Georgetown. Some of the inland mines still operate narrow-gauge lines.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Guiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_British_Guiana" title="List of Governors of British Guiana">List of Governors of British Guiana</a></div>
<p>The British long continued the forms of Dutch colonial government in British Guiana. A Court of Policy exercised both <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislative</a> and executive functions under the direction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_British_Guiana" title="List of Governors of British Guiana">colonial Governor</a> (which existed from 1831 to 1966). A group known as the Financial Representatives sat with the Court of Policy in a Combined Court to set tax policies. A majority of the members of the Courts was appointed by the Governor; the rest were selected by a College of Kiezers (Electors). The Kiezers were elected, with the restrictive <a href="/wiki/Suffrage" title="Suffrage">franchise</a> based on property holdings and limited to the larger landowners of the colony. The Courts were dominated in the early centuries by the sugar planters and their representatives.</p>
<p>In 1891 the College of Kiezers was abolished in favour of direct election of the elective membership of the Courts. Membership of the Court of Policy became half elected and half appointed, and all of the Financial Representatives became elective positions. The executive functions of the Court of Policy were transferred to a new Executive Council under the control of the Governor. Property qualifications were significantly relaxed for voters and for candidates to the Courts.</p>
<p>In 1928 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Government" title="British Government">British Government</a> abolished the Dutch-influenced constitution and replaced it with a <a href="/wiki/Crown_colony" title="Crown colony">Crown colony</a> constitution. A Legislative Council with an appointed majority was established, and the administrative powers of the Governor were strengthened. These constitutional changes were not popular among the Guyanese, who viewed them as a step backward. The franchise was extended to women.</p>
<p>In 1938 the <a href="/wiki/Report_of_West_India_Royal_Commission_(Moyne_Report)" title="Report of West India Royal Commission (Moyne Report)">West India Royal Commission</a> ("The Moyne Commission") was appointed to investigate the economic and social condition of all the British colonies in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> region after a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_West_Indian_labour_unrest_of_1934%E2%80%931939" title="British West Indian labour unrest of 1934–1939">civil and labour disturbances</a>. Among other changes, the Commission recommended some constitutional reforms. As a result, in 1943 a majority of the Legislative Council seats became elective, the property qualifications for voters and for candidates for the Council were lowered, and the bar on women and clergy serving on the Council was abolished. The Governor retained control of the Executive Council, which had the power to veto or pass laws against the wishes of the Legislative Council.</p>
<p>The next round of constitutional reforms came in 1953. A <a href="/wiki/Bicameralism" title="Bicameralism">bicameral legislature</a>, consisting of a lower House of Assembly and an upper State Council, was established. The voting membership of the House of Assembly was entirely elective. The membership of the State Council was appointed by the Governor and the House of Assembly, and possessed limited revisionary powers. A Court of Policy became the executive body, consisting of the Governor and other colonial officials. Universal adult suffrage was instituted, and the property qualifications for office abolished.</p>
<p>The election of 27 April 1953 under the new system provoked a serious constitutional crisis. The <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Progressive_Party_(Guyana)" title="People's Progressive Party (Guyana)">People's Progressive Party</a> (PPP) won 18 of the 24 seats in the House of Assembly. This result alarmed the British Government, which was surprised by the strong showing of the PPP. It considered the PPP as too friendly with communist organisations.</p>
<p>As a result of its fears of communist influence in the colony, the British Government suspended the constitution, declared a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a>, and militarily occupied British Guiana on 9 October 1953. Under the direction of the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_the_Colonies" title="Secretary of State for the Colonies">British Colonial Office</a>, the Governor assumed direct rule of the colony under an Interim Government, which continued until 1957. On 12 August 1957, elections were held and the PPP won nine of fourteen elective seats in a new legislature.</p>
<p>A constitutional convention convened in London in March 1960 reached agreement on another new legislature, to consist of an elected House of Assembly (35 seats) and a nominated Senate (13 seats). In the ensuing election of 21 August 1961, the PPP won 20 seats in the House of Assembly, entitling it as the majority party to appoint eight senators. Upon the 1961 election, British Guiana also became <a href="/wiki/Self-governance" title="Self-governance">self-governing</a>, except as to defence and external matters. The leader of the majority party became Prime Minister, who then named a Council of Ministers, replacing the former Executive Council.</p>
<p>From 1962 to 1964, riots, strikes and other disturbances stemming from racial, social and economic conflicts delayed full independence for British Guiana. The leaders of the political parties reported to the British Colonial Secretary that they were unable to reach agreement on the remaining details of forming an independent government. The British Colonial Office intervened by imposing its own independence plan, in part requiring another election under a new <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> system. Britain expected that this system would reduce the number of seats won by the PPP and prevent it from obtaining a majority.</p>
<p>The December 1964 elections for the new legislature gave the PPP 45.8% (24 seats), the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_National_Congress_(Guyana)" title="People's National Congress (Guyana)">People's National Congress</a> (PNC) 40.5% (22 seats) and the United Force (UF) 12.4% (7 seats). The UF agreed to form a <a href="/wiki/Coalition_government" title="Coalition government">coalition government</a> with the PNC, and accordingly, the PNC leader became the new Prime Minister. In November 1965 an independence conference in London quickly reached agreement on an independent constitution; it set the date for independence as 26 May 1966. On that date, at 12 midnight, British Guiana became the new nation of <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_boundary_with_Venezuela">Western boundary with Venezuela</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Guiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Western boundary with Venezuela">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1840, the British Government assigned <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hermann_Schomburgk" title="Robert Hermann Schomburgk">Robert Hermann Schomburgk</a> to survey and mark out the western boundary of British Guiana with newly independent <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>. Venezuela did not accept the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schomburgk_Line" title="Schomburgk Line">Schomburgk Line</a>, which placed the entire <a href="/wiki/Cuyuni_River" title="Cuyuni River">Cuyuni River</a> basin within the colony. Venezuela claimed all lands west of the <a href="/wiki/Essequibo_River" title="Essequibo River">Essequibo River</a> as its territory (see map above).</p>
<p>The dispute continued on and off for half a century, culminating in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venezuela_Crisis_of_1895" title="Venezuela Crisis of 1895">Venezuela Crisis of 1895</a>, in which Venezuela sought to use the United States' <a href="/wiki/Monroe_Doctrine" title="Monroe Doctrine">Monroe Doctrine</a> to win support for its position. US President <a href="/wiki/Grover_Cleveland" title="Grover Cleveland">Grover Cleveland</a> used diplomatic pressure to get the British to agree to arbitration of the issue, ultimately agreeing terms for the arbitration that suited Britain. An <a href="/wiki/Arbitral_tribunal" title="Arbitral tribunal">arbitration tribunal</a> convened in Paris in 1898, and issued its <a href="/wiki/Arbitration_award" title="Arbitration award">award</a> in 1899. The tribunal awarded about 94% of the disputed territory to British Guiana. A commission surveyed a new border according to the award, and the parties accepted the boundary in 1905.</p>
<p>There the matter rested until 1962, when Venezuela renewed its 19th-century claim, alleging that the arbitral award was invalid. After his death, Severo Mallet-Prevost, legal counsel for Venezuela and a named partner in the New York law firm <a href="/wiki/Curtis,_Mallet-Prevost,_Colt_%26_Mosle" title="Curtis, Mallet-Prevost, Colt &amp; Mosle">Curtis, Mallet-Prevost, Colt &amp; Mosle</a> published a letter alleging that the judges on the tribunal acted improperly as a result of a back room deal between Russia and Great Britain. The British Government rejected this claim, asserting the validity of the 1899 award. The British Guiana Government, then under the leadership of the PPP, also strongly rejected this claim. Efforts by all the parties to resolve the matter on the eve of Guyana's independence in 1966 failed. As of today the dispute remains unresolved.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_boundary_with_Suriname">Eastern boundary with Suriname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Guiana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Eastern boundary with Suriname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Borders_of_Suriname" title="Borders of Suriname">Borders of Suriname</a></div>
<p>Robert Schomburgk's 1840 commission also included a survey of the colony's eastern boundary with the Netherlands' <a href="/wiki/Surinam_(Dutch_colony)" title="Surinam (Dutch colony)">colony of Suriname</a>, now the independent nation of <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>. The 1899 arbitration award settling the British Guiana—Venezuela border made reference to the border with Suriname as continuing to the source of the <a href="/wiki/Courantyne_River" title="Courantyne River">Courantyne River</a>, which it named as the Kutari River. The Netherlands raised a diplomatic protest, claiming that the New River, and not the Kutari, was to be regarded as the source of the Courantyne and the boundary. The British government in 1900 replied that the issue was already settled by the long acceptance of the Kutari as the boundary.</p>
<p>In 1962, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands" title="Kingdom of the Netherlands">Kingdom of the Netherlands</a>, on behalf of Suriname, which had become a <a href="/wiki/Suriname_(Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands)" title="Suriname (Kingdom of the Netherlands)">constituent country</a> of the Kingdom, finally made formal claim to the "New River Triangle", the triangular-shaped region between the New and Kutari rivers that was in dispute. The Suriname colonial government, and after 1975 the independent Suriname government, maintained the Dutch position; while the British Guiana Government, and later the independent Guyanese government, maintained the British position.</p>

<p>British Guiana is famous among <a href="/wiki/Philately" title="Philately">philatelists</a> for its early postage stamps that were first issued in 1850. These stamps include some of the rarest, most expensive stamps in the world, such as the unique <a href="/wiki/British_Guiana_1c_magenta" title="British Guiana 1c magenta">British Guiana 1c magenta</a> from 1856 which was sold in 2014 for US$9.5 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
