<p><b>Sudachi</b> (<i>Citrus sudachi</i>; <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: スダチ or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%85%A2" title="wikt:酢">酢</a><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%A9%98" title="wikt:橘">橘</a>) is a small, round, green, Japanese <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">citrus</a> fruit that is a specialty of <a href="/wiki/Tokushima_Prefecture" title="Tokushima Prefecture">Tokushima Prefecture</a> in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schmadel_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-schmadel-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is a sour citrus, not eaten as fruit, but used as food flavoring in place of <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lime_(fruit)" title="Lime (fruit)">lime</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iuchi-etal_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-iuchi-etal-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sudachi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cultivated for centuries in parts of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and perhaps nearly as well known as another sour citrus, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuzu" title="Yuzu">yuzu</a> in that country,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanaka-taninaka10_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-tanaka-taninaka10-5">[5]</a></sup> it has been considered an "indispensable companion" to eating <a href="/wiki/Matsutake" title="Matsutake">matsutake</a> mushroom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Ponzu" title="Ponzu">ponzu</a> <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">ポン酢</span><sup class="t_nihongo_help noprint"><a href="/wiki/Help:Installing_Japanese_character_sets" title="Help:Installing Japanese character sets"><span class="t_nihongo_icon" style="color: #00e; font: bold 80% sans-serif; text-decoration: none; padding: 0 .1em;">?</span></a></sup>, "pon vinegar")</span>, the squeezed citrus juice used as an alternative to vinegar uses <i>sudachi</i> among other bitter oranges (<i><a href="/wiki/Kabosu" title="Kabosu">kabosu</a></i> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuzu" title="Yuzu">yuzu</a> juices, and <i><a href="/wiki/Daidai" title="Daidai">daidai</a></i>) as an ingredient.</p>
<p>The fruit is also the symbol of the prefecture of <a href="/wiki/Tokushima_Prefecture" title="Tokushima Prefecture">Tokushima</a> where the majority of the fruit is cultivated. Cultivation is centered mainly in the communities of Sanagouchi-son, Kamikatsu-cho and Kamiyama-cho.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The plant has white flowers which bloom in May and June. The fruits form in bunches, or tight clusters, and are harvested in the fall. Though <i>sudachi</i> fruits will eventually develop a yellow-orange rind color, they are normally harvested and used while still green.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wardowski-etal_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-wardowski-etal-8">[8]</a></sup> They contain large smooth seeds, containing a green <a href="/wiki/Polyembryony" title="Polyembryony">polyembryo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tanaka-taninaka12_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-tanaka-taninaka12-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>A half-slice of this fruit is served as garnish<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wardowski-etal_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-wardowski-etal-8">[8]</a></sup> with many traditional <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cuisine" title="Japanese cuisine">Japanese dishes</a> which include fish, <a href="/wiki/Soba" title="Soba">soba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Udon" title="Udon">udon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nabemono" title="Nabemono">nabe</a>, and even some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a>. It is considered to have a zestier flavor and aroma than <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemons</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lime_(fruit)" title="Lime (fruit)">limes</a>. It also boasts a higher <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascorbic_acid" title="Ascorbic acid">ascorbic acid</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a>) content than lemons. Sudachi flavored products (such as <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vodka" title="Vodka">vodka</a> coolers, <a href="/wiki/Ice_pop" title="Ice pop">ice pops</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drinks</a>) can also be found in Japan, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Tokushima_Prefecture" title="Tokushima Prefecture">Tokushima Prefecture</a>, where the fruit is sold cheaply. The actual fruit is regarded as a delicacy in other parts of Japan, and is often expensive. Compared to the related <i>kabosu</i>, <i>sudachi</i> is much smaller at 20-25<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wardowski-etal_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-wardowski-etal-8">[8]</a></sup> to 40 grams as compared to 100 to 140 grams.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sudachi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Citrus_taxonomy#Citrus_relatives" title="Citrus taxonomy">Citrus taxonomy § Citrus relatives</a></div>
<p><i>Sudachi</i> is thought to be an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ichang_papeda" title="Ichang papeda">ichang papeda</a> x <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_orange" title="Mandarin orange">mandarin orange</a> hybrid.</p>
<p>Its species name was published by <a href="/wiki/Mitsutaro_Shirai" title="Mitsutaro Shirai">Mitsutaro Shirai</a> (1933), but most modern scientists, even while still using this nomenclature by habit or as legacy, do not consider it as a bona fide species, but regard it as a <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivar</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iuchi-etal_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-iuchi-etal-4">[4]</a></sup> of unknown parentage, or a <a href="/wiki/Cultigen" title="Cultigen">cultigen</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuzu" title="Yuzu">yuzu</a></p>
<p>The sudachi is classed within the <a href="/wiki/Citrus_subg._Papeda" title="Citrus subg. Papeda">Papeda</a> subgenus in the <a href="/wiki/Walter_Tennyson_Swingle" title="Walter Tennyson Swingle">Swingle</a> scheme, and in the more complicated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyozaburo_Tanaka" title="Tyozaburo Tanaka">Tanaka</a> scheme within the <i>Eusmocitrus</i> or true yuzu subgenus, under the <i>Osmocitrus</i> or yuzu section.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phytochemistry">Phytochemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sudachi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phytochemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sudachi_seedling_1_yr_old.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="856" data-file-width="1224" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Sudachi_seedling_1_yr_old.jpg/220px-Sudachi_seedling_1_yr_old.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Sudachi_seedling_1_yr_old.jpg/330px-Sudachi_seedling_1_yr_old.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Sudachi_seedling_1_yr_old.jpg/440px-Sudachi_seedling_1_yr_old.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sudachi_seedling_1_yr_old.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Sudachi seedling 1 year old</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Flavonoid" title="Flavonoid">flavonoid</a> <a href="/wiki/Eriocitrin" title="Eriocitrin">eriocitrin</a> abundant in lemon and lime juices is present in comparable concentrations in sudachi juice, but is lacking in yuzu or kabosu juices. Also, <a href="/wiki/Neoeriocitrin" title="Neoeriocitrin">neoeriocitrin</a> (characteristic in <a href="/wiki/Bergamot_orange" title="Bergamot orange">bergamot</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Daidai" title="Daidai">daidai</a></i>) is found in sudachi juice and rind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miyake2006_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-miyake2006-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Eriocitrin is an <a href="/wiki/Antioxidant" title="Antioxidant">antioxidant</a> reported to combat <a href="/wiki/Lipid_peroxidation" title="Lipid peroxidation">lipid peroxidation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and like <a href="/wiki/Neoeriocitrin" title="Neoeriocitrin">neoeriocitrin</a> is said to block the formation of <a href="/wiki/Lipoxygenase" title="Lipoxygenase">lipoxygenases</a> involved in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allergies" title="Allergies">allergies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atherosclerosis" title="Atherosclerosis">atherosclerosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>。</p>
<p>Also, the flavonoid <a href="/wiki/Narirutin" title="Narirutin">narirutin</a> is said to be the active chemical in the <a href="/wiki/Wakayama_Prefecture" title="Wakayama Prefecture">Wakayama Prefecture</a> specialty <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Citrus_jabara" title="Citrus jabara">citrus jabara</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Juice" title="Juice">juice</a> (<a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%82%B8%E3%83%A3%E3%83%90%E3%83%A9" title="ja:ジャバラ">ja:ジャバラ</a>) that is said to diminish the effects of <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> <a href="/wiki/Allergic_rhinitis" title="Allergic rhinitis">allergies</a>. The <i>jabara</i> advertises that it contains 6 times the narirutin in yuzu, but <i>sudachi</i> juice also contains about 3 times as much as yuzu juice (20.1 mg per 100 ml, vs. 6.6 mg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miyake2006_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-miyake2006-11">[11]</a></sup> But it should be noted this substance is quite abundant in the rind of yuzu and kabosu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miyake2006_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-miyake2006-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>The ability of the <i>sudachi</i> to promote the body's calcium absorption has been studied as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>。</p>
<p>In 2006, a <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokushima" title="University of Tokushima">Tokushima University</a> research team published a report which suggests that the fruit may be effective in lowering <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> levels in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetic" title="Diabetic">diabetic</a> patients.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The team gave rats sudachi zest over a one-year period and found the glucose levels fell and the health of the rats to improve.）<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
