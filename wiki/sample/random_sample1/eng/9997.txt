<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Nilmanifold</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, a <b>nilmanifold</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Differentiable_manifold" title="Differentiable manifold">differentiable manifold</a> which has a transitive <a href="/wiki/Nilpotent_group" title="Nilpotent group">nilpotent</a> group of diffeomorphisms acting on it. As such, a nilmanifold is an example of a <a href="/wiki/Homogeneous_space" title="Homogeneous space">homogeneous space</a> and is diffeomorphic to the <a href="/wiki/Quotient_space_(topology)" title="Quotient space (topology)">quotient space</a> <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mi>N</mi>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mo>/</mo>
</mrow>
<mi>H</mi>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle N/H}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="{\displaystyle N/H}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/827a971e9540e70c03c48321e8d679ccd6589de0" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:5.321ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span>, the quotient of a nilpotent <a href="/wiki/Lie_group" title="Lie group">Lie group</a> <i>N</i> modulo a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closed_(topology)" title="Closed (topology)">closed</a> <a href="/wiki/Subgroup" title="Subgroup">subgroup</a> <i>H</i>. This notion was introduced by <a href="/wiki/Anatoly_Maltsev" title="Anatoly Maltsev">A. Mal'cev</a> in 1951.</p>
<p>In the Riemannian category, there is also a good notion of a nilmanifold. A <a href="/wiki/Riemannian_manifold" title="Riemannian manifold">Riemannian manifold</a> is called a <b>homogeneous nilmanifold</b> if there exist a nilpotent group of isometries acting transitively on it. The requirement that the transitive nilpotent group acts by isometries leads to the following rigid characterization: every homogeneous nilmanifold is isometric to a nilpotent Lie group with left-invariant metric (see Wilson <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>).</p>
<p>Nilmanifolds are important geometric objects and often arise as concrete examples with interesting properties; in Riemannian geometry these spaces always have mixed curvature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Almost_flat_manifold" title="Almost flat manifold">almost flat spaces</a> arise as quotients of nilmanifolds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and compact nilmanifolds have been used to construct elementary examples of collapse of Riemannian metrics under the Ricci flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to their role in geometry, nilmanifolds are increasingly being seen as having a role in <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic_combinatorics" title="Arithmetic combinatorics">arithmetic combinatorics</a> (see Green–Tao <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>) and <a href="/wiki/Ergodic_theory" title="Ergodic theory">ergodic theory</a> (see, e.g., Host–Kra <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compact_nilmanifolds">Compact nilmanifolds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nilmanifold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Compact nilmanifolds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Complex_nilmanifolds">Complex nilmanifolds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nilmanifold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Complex nilmanifolds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, a <b>complex nilmanifold</b> meant a quotient of a complex nilpotent Lie group over a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cocompact_lattice" title="Cocompact lattice">cocompact lattice</a>. An example of such a nilmanifold is an <a href="/wiki/Iwasawa_manifold" title="Iwasawa manifold">Iwasawa manifold</a>. From the 1980s, another (more general) notion of a complex nilmanifold gradually replaced this one.</p>
<p>An <b>almost complex structure</b> on a real Lie algebra <i>g</i> is an endomorphism <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

It is easy to see that every connected complex <a href="/wiki/Homogeneous_space" title="Homogeneous space">homogeneous manifold</a> equipped with a free, transitive, holomorphic action by a real Lie group is obtained this way.</p>
<p>Let <i>G</i> be a real, nilpotent Lie group. A <b>complex nilmanifold</b> is a quotient of a complex group manifold (<i>G</i>,<i>I</i>), equipped with a left-invariant complex structure, by a discrete, cocompact lattice, acting from the right.</p>
<p>Complex nilmanifolds are usually not homogeneous, as complex varieties.</p>
<p>In complex dimension 2, the only complex nilmanifolds are a complex torus and a <a href="/wiki/Kodaira_surface" title="Kodaira surface">Kodaira surface</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nilmanifold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Compact nilmanifolds (except a torus) are never <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formal_space" title="Formal space">homotopy formal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This implies immediately that compact nilmanifolds (except a torus) cannot admit a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%A4hler_structure" title="Kähler structure">Kähler structure</a> (see also <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>).</p>
<p>Topologically, all nilmanifolds can be obtained as iterated torus bundles over a torus. This is easily seen from a filtration by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_central_series" title="Upper central series">ascending central series</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nilmanifold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nilpotent_Lie_groups">Nilpotent Lie groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nilmanifold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nilpotent Lie groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From the above definition of homogeneous nilmanifolds, it is clear that any nilpotent Lie group with left-invariant metric is a homogeneous nilmanifold. The most familiar nilpotent Lie groups are matrix groups whose diagonal entries are 1 and whose lower diagonal entries are all zeros.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abelian_Lie_groups">Abelian Lie groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nilmanifold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Abelian Lie groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A simpler example would be any abelian Lie group. This is because any such group is a nilpotent Lie group. For example, one can take the group of real numbers under addition, and the discrete, cocompact subgroup consisting of the integers. The resulting 1-step nilmanifold is the familiar circle <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generalizations">Generalizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nilmanifold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Generalizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A parallel construction based on <a href="/wiki/Solvable_group" title="Solvable group">solvable</a> Lie groups produces a class of spaces called <b>solvmanifolds</b>. An important example of a solvmanifolds are <a href="/wiki/Inoue_surface" title="Inoue surface">Inoue surfaces</a>, known in <a href="/wiki/Complex_geometry" title="Complex geometry">complex geometry</a>.</p>
